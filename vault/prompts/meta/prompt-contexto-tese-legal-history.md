## Theologico-Political Simulacrum: The January 8th Attacks in Brasília as a Crisis of Legal Allegory and Patriotic Embodiment

**A Framework for Legal History and Visual Culture Analysis**

### Executive Summary

The January 8, 2023 attacks on Brazil's Three Powers Square represent far more than a failed coup attempt. They constitute a complex theologico-political *simulacrum*—in Baudrillard's sense—wherein far-right Christian nationalist actors attempted to replace democratic constitutional reality with a "map" of purified national symbols: the Flag, the Motto "Order and Progress," and the yellow national team shirt worn as patriotic armor. This report develops a theoretical framework connecting four analytical dimensions: (1) the precession of simulacra in the fetishization of Article 142; (2) neo-Pentecostal spiritual warfare as ritualized bodily performance; (3) gendered allegory and the attack on the feminized body of Justice; and (4) the masculine patriotic body as a substitute for actual democratic institutions. The analysis positions January 8 not as spontaneous violence but as orchestrated symbolic performance aimed at purifying the "contaminated" state body through bodily violation and iconoclasm.

### I. Baudrillard's Precession of Simulacra: The Map Replaces the Territory

#### The Logic of Simulation in Brazilian Authoritarian Politics

Jean Baudrillard's concept of *simulacrum*—the generation of a real without origin or reality—provides the theoretical backbone for understanding January 8 as a performative attempt to replace constitutional democracy with hyperreality. The territory no longer precedes the map; instead, the map precedes and generates the territory. In Brazil's far-right imaginary, this manifests in a triple collapse: the Constitution as living legal document becomes indistinguishable from the Constitution as fetishized object (the burned copy); Article 142 becomes pure performance divorced from constitutional meaning; and the patriot body in yellow shirt becomes the only "real" nation, displacing actual citizenship.[^1][^2]

Baudrillard observes that "the models come first, their circulation, orbital like that of the bomb, constitutes the genuine magnetic field of the event. The facts no longer have a specific trajectory, they are born at the intersection of models." In this frame, the January 8 attack was not motivated by rational political calculation but by the circulation of hyperreal models: a model of a military intervention, a model of spiritual purification, a model of national reclamation. The facts—the actual state of democratic institutions, constitutional law, electoral legitimacy—became irrelevant. What mattered was the orbital circulation of these models through evangelical networks, social media algorithms, and military WhatsApp groups.[^3]

#### Article 142 as Constitutional Mutation and Fetish

The fetishization of Article 142 of the 1988 Constitution exemplifies this simulacral collapse. Article 142 states: "The Armed Forces, constituted by the Navy, Army, and Air Force, are permanent and regular national institutions, organized on the basis of hierarchy and discipline, under the supreme authority of the President of the Republic, and are intended to defend the Fatherland, guarantee the constitutional powers of the Union... and maintain law and order."[^4][^5]

The text contains no reference to military "moderation" between branches, no role for the armed forces as a fourth power. Yet Bolsonaro's legal theorists—particularly congressman Bia Kicis and federal judge Kassio Nunes Marques—promoted an "abusive interpretation" of Article 142 as authorizing military intervention to "restore order" when the Supreme Court allegedly overreached. This was not a reading of law; it was a *simulation of legality*. The text remained unchanged, but its meaning was inverted through what Brazilian constitutional scholars term a "constitutional mutation"—a change in constitutional meaning without a corresponding change in text.[^6][^7]

This mutation reveals the simulacral structure: Article 142 functions less as a legal norm than as a sacred text to be inverted and perverted. It becomes a *fetish object*—revered precisely because its meaning is indeterminate and malleable, available for endless reinterpretation. Like patriotic symbols studied by Marxist scholars, Article 142 operates as an alienated product of political activity, a stand-in for actual military authority, now reclaimed and manipulated by a political elite. The text is treated with liturgical reverence; its actual legal meaning is irrelevant.[^8]

#### The Flag, the Motto, the Shirt: Chain of Hyperreal Substitutions

The January 8 attackers wore yellow shirts, carried Brazilian flags, and chanted "Order and Progress"—the motto from the national flag. This was not accidental symbolism; it represented a deliberate substitution of hyperreal markers for actual political content.

The "Order and Progress" (*Ordem e Progresso*) motto originates in Auguste Comte's positivism: "L'amour pour principe et l'ordre pour base; le progrès pour but" (Love as principle and order as foundation; progress as goal). The motto was designed in 1889 by positivist theorists Raimundo Teixeira Mendes and Miguel Lemos to encode republican modernization ideology onto the national flag itself. In Baudrillard's terms, the motto was already a simulacrum—a sign substituting for actual progress, order, and love that never materialized under the First Republic or any subsequent regime.[^9][^10]

By January 2023, the yellow-shirt-wearing patriot had appropriated this already-hyperreal motto, emptying it further of meaning. "Order and Progress" was now invoked while destroying art, urinating in legislative chambers, and vandalizing the Supreme Court building. The gap between sign and referent had expanded to infinity. The motto signified nothing except the mob's claim to patriotic authenticity against the "contaminated" democratic state.

The yellow national team shirt underwent parallel transformation. Historically, the yellow jersey symbolized inclusive national identity—a space where Afro-Brazilian athletes could achieve iconic status and transcend social inequality. By 2013-2018, right-wing movements had appropriated the shirt as a patriotic uniform, transforming it into what scholars term a "political dispute" over national symbols. By January 8, the yellow shirt had become a *pure signifier*—carrying no meaning except the wearer's claim to embody the nation against an allegedly "communist" state. The shirt was the nation; the person wearing it was the nation; therefore, any violence committed by that person was justified in the name of national defense.[^11][^12]

This is simulacrum in its purest form: the sign (the yellow shirt) has preceded and replaced the referent (actual Brazilian nationhood, which includes all citizens regardless of politics). The hyperreal patriot body *is* the nation; the democratic state is contamination to be purified.

### II. Neo-Pentecostal Spiritual Warfare: Embodied Performance of Purification Ritual

#### The Logic of Spiritual Combat and Political Violence

The January 8 attack was saturated with religious performance and rhetoric. Attackers wore shirts emblazoned with "Deus Acima de Todos" ("God Above All"); they knelt in prayer, held hands in prayer circles, and used rosaries as spiritual armor against police. Gospel singers Salomão Vieira de Jesus and Fernanda Ôliver served as organizers and livestreamers; evangelical pastors recorded themselves claiming "the Brazilian people are waking up."[^13][^14][^15]

This was not incidental religiosity. Rather, it represented a deliberate transfer of neo-Pentecostal *spiritual warfare* theology into explicit political performance. Neo-Pentecostalism teaches that believers are locked in cosmic combat against demonic forces; that prayer and ritual performance can expel evil from society; that the faithful must engage in "spiritual warfare" to reclaim territory (physical and spiritual) from demonic occupation.[^16]

The theological root lies in American Pentecostal militancy traditions, particularly the "Joel's Army" doctrine developed from a distorted reading of Joel 2, describing locusts devouring the land as an army of God preparing for End Times. This hermeneutics—treating biblical texts as literal military blueprints—has been adopted and amplified by Brazilian neo-Pentecostal pastors aligned with Bolsonaro. The result is a directly militarized theology: *spiritual warfare becomes political violence against those identified as demonic forces*.[^17][^18]

In the Brazilian context, "demons" are identified as progressive sectors of society, the Supreme Court (associated with "communist" interpretations of rights), and LGBTQ+ communities. The Pentecostal logic treats political opponents not as fellow citizens with whom one disagrees, but as literal manifestations of evil requiring ritual expulsion.[^19]

#### Embodied Practice: Purity Through Violation

Mary Douglas's anthropological framework on purity and danger illuminates the January 8 attackers' bodily performance. Douglas demonstrates that "dirt" is "matter out of place"—categories that threaten systematic ordering are treated as polluting and dangerous. Pollution anxiety arises not from biology but from category confusion; the ritual response is to reinscribe boundaries through elaborate purification ceremonies.[^20]

What the January 8 attackers did in the Supreme Court, National Congress, and Presidential Palace represents precisely this ritual logic: they defaced, destroyed, and—crucially—*desecrated* through bodily violation. Rioters urinated and defecated in the Supreme Court press areas, Congress chambers, and presidential palace. They defaced Alfredo Ceschiatti's 1961 granite sculpture *A Justiça*, which depicts a blindfolded female figure holding a sword—the allegorical embodiment of Brazilian judicial power.[^21][^22]

These were not random acts of vandalism. The *pattern* of bodily violation—fecal matter, urination, the specific targeting of Justice imagery—reveals a ritual purification logic. In anthropological terms, these actors were treating the democratic state's body as *polluted*, requiring ritual cleansing through abusive contact. The female body of Justice, already symbolically feminized in Western legal iconography, became the target for reassertion of masculine patriotic order.

Douglas notes: "Ritual pollution arises from the interplay of form and surrounding formlessness. Pollution dangers strike when form has been attacked." The January 8 actors attacked the *form* of constitutional democracy—its buildings, its symbols, its artistic representations—precisely because they experienced it as polluted, compromised, infiltrated by "leftist," "communist," and demonic forces. Their bodily violations were acts of ritual purification: by covering Justice with their bodily waste, they symbolically transferred the pollution from their own patriotic body onto the corrupted state body.[^23]

#### The Aesthetic of Power: Neo-Pentecostal Ritual Energy

Brazilian neo-Pentecostalism, particularly the theology practiced by the IURD (Igreja Universal do Reino de Deus), deploys what scholars term an "aesthetic of power"—a ritualized production of charismatic energy that "directly deploys divine power" and disperses it among followers. The massive church gatherings, the invocation of divine blessing by pastors, the circulation of blessed objects—all generate an efficacious ritual energy distinct from ordinary religious practice.[^24]

On January 8, this neo-Pentecostal aesthetic of power was transferred into explicitly political performance. The chainsaw-wielding rioters, the defacing of presidential chairs, the destruction of a 17th-century Balthazar Martinot clock—these violent acts became ritualized performances of divine power reclaiming territory from demonic occupation. Each act of destruction was simultaneously an invocation of spiritual warfare theology and a performance of patriotic purification.

One pastor livestreaming from the riot claimed that "the Brazilian people are waking up"—language identical to Christian nationalist discourse in the United States linking political action to spiritual awakening and divine will. The January 8 attackers were not simply engaging in political violence; they were performing *liturgy*—embodied acts of ritual communication with the divine, designed to effect cosmic reordering through destruction of the polluted institutional form.[^25]

### III. Gendered Allegory: The Feminized Body of Justice and Masculine Patriotic Substitution

#### Justice as Feminine Personification: Historical Genealogy

The personification of Justice as a female figure originates in Roman legal culture: the Latin noun *iūstitia* is grammatically feminine, leading Romans to adopt the Greek goddess Dike and Latinize her as Iūstitia. Medieval and Renaissance legal culture elaborated her iconography: scales representing balanced judgment, a sword representing the enforcement of justice, and increasingly (from the 16th century) a blindfold representing impartiality.[^26][^27]

This gendering of Justice is not accidental or ornamental. Feminist legal historians argue that the feminization of abstract legal concepts served to displace masculine legal authority from the realm of visible institutional power into a realm of allegorical abstraction. The male judge or ruler maintains actual power; the female Justice figure provides moral legitimacy for that power. She is simultaneously all-powerful (representing eternal justice) and powerless (confined to allegorical representation, never actually making decisions).[^28]

The European 18th-century visual culture elaborated this paradox. During the Enlightenment, Justice replaced Christ in visual representations of ultimate authority—the secularization of eschatology made the judiciary itself the source of salvation. Yet this newly-powerful Justice remained feminized, often depicted nude or semi-nude, her body vulnerable to interpretation and violation. Early modern engravings by Hans Burgkmair and other Northern Renaissance artists depicted Justice as an ambivalent virgin—simultaneously powerful in her command of scales and sword, yet always potentially vulnerable to sexualized interpretation.[^29]

#### The Ceschiatti Sculpture: Justice in the Brazilian Republic

Alfredo Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* (1961), positioned in front of the Supreme Federal Court in Brasília's Praça dos Três Poderes, represents the culmination of this long allegorical tradition applied to Brazilian constitutional law. Sculpted in a single monolithic block of granite from Petrópolis, standing 3.3 meters tall, Ceschiatti's Justice depicts the seated female figure with blindfold (*vendada*), sword in lap, no scales.[^30][^31]

The artist's choice to depict Justice without scales is significant. Scales traditionally symbolize the weighing of evidence, the balancing of competing claims. Their absence in Ceschiatti's work emphasizes instead the *condition of justice itself*: the blindness that prevents partial vision, the sword that enforces law equally for all. The sculpture was created in 1961, coinciding with the founding of Brasília as the new capital under President Juscelino Kubitschek, and it became one of the primary visual symbols of Brazilian judicial authority for over 60 years.

On January 8, 2023, this female Justice figure—emblematic of judicial independence and impartial law—became a target for ritualized violence and defacement. The vandalism was not random. As one governmental curator noted: "The damage was not random, it was obviously deliberate. There is a movement of intolerance toward what this palace represents."[^32][^33]

The targeting of Ceschiatti's Justice reveals the deep gendered logic of the January 8 attack: the far-right attackers were not simply destroying symbols of democratic institutions; they were performing a symbolic *castration* or *enucleation* of the feminized judicial body. The blindfold represents the Supreme Court's refusal to see—its rejection of Bolsonaro's claims to special insight and authority. The sword represents law applied equally, without exception for patriotic petitioners. The female body represents the Court's impartial distance from masculine political power.

By vandalizing Justice, the January 8 attackers were symbolically reasserting masculine patriotic power against this feminized judicial authority. They were saying: *we will see what you refuse to see (Bolsonaro's legitimacy); we will wield the sword (military power); we will restore patriotic order (masculine dominion).*

#### The Yellow-Shirted Patriot as Hypermasculine National Body

The substitute for feminized Justice was the yellow-shirted patriot—embodying what scholars term "hegemonic masculinity" aligned with nationalist ideology. The patriot in yellow is not blindfolded; he sees clearly (or believes he does). He is not restrained by law; he wields violence as patriotic duty. He does not stand apart from political power; he *is* political power, reclaimed from corrupt elites and judges.[^34]

Joane Nagel's analysis of nationalism and masculinity illuminates this gendered substitution. Nationalism is fundamentally organized around "the construction of patriotic manhood and exalted motherhood as icons of nationalist ideology." The male patriot must be prepared for violence; the female must reproduce and preserve cultural tradition. The nation-state, despite its elaborate symbolic feminine personifications, remains a fundamentally masculine institution organized around martial power and hierarchical authority.[^35]

On January 8, the yellow-shirted attackers were performing an idealized hegemonic masculinity: aggressive, militant, willing to destroy "feminine" legal restraints (represented by the blindfolded Justice), and claiming to reclaim the nation for "patriotic" (masculine) values. The chants of "God, Nation, Family, Freedom" encode this gender ideology: God above all (patriarchal cosmic order); Nation (belonging through patriotic violence); Family (heteronuclear, patriarchally-ordered); Freedom (the masculine prerogative to act without legal restraint).

The substitution of yellow-shirted masculine patriot for blindfolded feminine Justice represents a *remasculinization* of political order. The Supreme Court's justice—impartial, blind, universalist—is dismissed as insufficient, corrupted, emasculating. In its place must come the warrior patriot, seeing clearly, acting decisively, embodying the nation through his own masculinized body.

#### The Body Politic in Gendered Crisis

Political theorists dating back to Thomas Hobbes have conceptualized the nation-state metaphorically as a "body politic," with the monarch or sovereign as its head. This somatic metaphor reaches further: the nation is often depicted as a feminine body (the "body of the nation"), while sovereign authority is organized through masculine institutional forms (the king, the president, the military, the judiciary).[^36]

January 8 reveals the crisis in this gendered body politic. The feminized Justice—representing the rule of law and constitutional restraint—has become unmoored from the actual masculine power that sustains it (the military, executive authority). The yellow-shirted patriot seeks to restore masculine dominion by literally violating the female Justice figure, by urinating in the spaces she symbolizes, by asserting that patriotic violence supersedes legal restraint.

The desecration through bodily fluids—fecal matter and urine in the Supreme Court chambers—represents the ultimate assertion of masculine bodily sovereignty over the feminized legal body. It is an act of dominance over the female body; it is the assertion that patriotic/masculine bodily reality supersedes abstract legal form.

### IV. Legal Fetishism: The Constitution as Sacred Object

#### Hyperreality of Constitutional Veneration

The January 8 attackers deliberately stole and then recovered a copy of the 1988 Constitution. This was not incidental larceny; it was a ritualized appropriation of the nation's foundational legal text. The Constitution, printed and bound, became the fetish object—sacred, powerful, and essentially meaningless.[^37]

This represents what we might term *fetishized legal literacy*—the obsessive investment in legal texts as objects of worship while simultaneously draining them of interpretive meaning. The Constitution is revered precisely because it can be interpreted infinitely, exactly as Article 142 was inverted and perverted to authorize military intervention.

Baudrillard observes that simulacra operate by erasing the distinction between the representation and the real. A copy of the Constitution becomes *identical* to Constitutional law itself. The physical object—leather-bound, officially-printed—possesses an aura of sacred authority that makes it available for endless reinterpretation. By stealing the Constitution, the attackers were asserting: *this text belongs to us; we embody its true meaning.*[^38]

#### Legal Texts as Sites of Liturgical Performance

Constitutional texts in late modernity function increasingly as what scholars term "symbolic constitutions"—representations of political unity that conceal the complexity and autonomy of actual legal and political systems. The Constitution serves as a "civil religion," offering legitimacy to political and legal systems by providing an idealized representation of national values: popular sovereignty, human dignity, rule of law.[^39]

Fetishized legal literacy treats constitutional texts with religious reverence while ignoring (or inverting) their actual normative content. Bolsonaro supporters cited Article 142 obsessively, as if the mere recitation of the text created magical legal effect. Article 142 was invoked like a liturgical formula, a spell to be uttered to conjure military intervention into being.

This represents what Victor Turner, analyzing ritual symbolism, termed the collapse of "meaning" into "performance." The Constitution is no longer read for meaning; it is performed. Article 142 is not interpreted according to constitutional jurisprudence; it is chanted, it is cited in speeches, it is treated as a performative utterance that itself creates the reality it describes (military moderation).[^40]

Neo-Pentecostal theology encourages precisely this collapse of meaning into performance. The spoken word (*a palavra*) possesses creative power; prayer is not petition but creation. Applied to constitutional law, this theology transforms Article 142 from a descriptive legal norm into a performative invocation of divine will—the belief that by speaking/citing the article, one brings military intervention into being.[^41]

### V. Integrative Analysis: The Semiotic Chain of Simulacra

To synthesize these four dimensions into a coherent theoretical framework:

**The Precession of Simulacra → Neo-Pentecostal Performance → Gendered Bodily Violence → Legal Fetishism**

January 8 represents the full collapse of distinction between sign and reality, map and territory, simulation and authentic being. The event unfolds in this semiotic chain:

1. **Simulation precedes reality**: Article 142 is cited as legal authorization for military intervention that hasn't occurred (yet). The citation creates the model; actual constitutional meaning is irrelevant.
2. **Religious performance energizes simulation**: Neo-Pentecostal spiritual warfare theology treats political action as cosmic combat against demonic forces. Prayer, ritual, and bodily performance *activate* the simulacra, making them efficacious.
3. **Gendered bodily violation enacts symbolic purification**: The feminized Justice figure becomes the target for ritualized desecration. By violating the female body of the Supreme Court (urinating, defacing, vandalizing), the patriotic attackers assert masculine dominion and symbolically transfer pollution from their own bodies to the institutional order.
4. **Constitutional fetishism provides sacred legitimacy**: The Constitution is simultaneously stolen (fetishized as sacred object) and inverted (Article 142 reinterpreted as military authorization). The text becomes a blank screen for projection of patriotic will.

The result is a *theologico-political simulacrum*: a hyperreal performance in which the map (the Flag, the Motto, the yellow shirt, the chanted Article 142) has completely preceded and displaced the territory (actual democratic institutions, constitutional law, electoral legitimacy).

The yellow-shirted patriot in spiritual warfare has replaced the blindfolded Justice. The Constitution as fetish object has replaced the Constitution as living legal document. The "purified" patriotic body has replaced the "contaminated" democratic state body.
