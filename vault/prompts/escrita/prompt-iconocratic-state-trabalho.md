The Iconocratic State and the Vandalized Goddess: did January 8th Brasília attacks constitute a rupture in the (visual) continuity of the Brazilian republic?

1. The Crisis of Representation: From the King’s Body to the Abstract State
Did the storming of the Praça dos Três Poderes in Brasília on January 8, 2023, constitute a rupture in the visual continuity of the Brazilian republic? 
When rioters ascended the ramp of the Supreme Federal Court (STF) and defaced Alfredo Ceschiatti’s monumental sculpture A Justiça (1961), they engaged in a violent renegotiation of the state’s visual infrastructure. The graffiti scrawled across the statue’s chest—“Perdeu, mané” (You lost, fool)—was not merely an act of vandalism; it was a demonstration of "counter-literacy," a brutal reading that stripped the allegorical figure of her universal pretensions and reduced her to a partisan combatant in a localized political struggle.1 This event serves as the fulcrum for a comprehensive research project on legal literacy, necessitating a shift from the study of textual competence to an investigation of how legitimacy is staged, maintained, and destroyed through images.
This report establishes the architectural and theoretical specifications for such a project. It posits that legal literacy must be redefined as an "infrastructural and visual competence"—the historically situated capacity to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material cues, spatial arrangements, and iconographic systems.3 Modern legal orders, despite their claims to be impersonal, code-based, and bureaucratic, have historically depended on dense iconographic networks to make their authority legible. From the French Revolution’s reinvention of sovereignty to the high modernist construction of Brasília, the abstract principles of the state have been consistently embodied in the female form.
The central paradox to be explored is the "visual sexual contract".3 Precisely during the historical periods when women were most systematically excluded from political agency, civil capacity, and the legal profession, the female body proliferated as the omnipresent official face of the Law and the Nation. Figures such as Marianne, Britannia, Germania, and Ceschiatti’s A Justiça functioned as conduits for legal knowledge, teaching the public "what law looks like" while simultaneously masking the gendered exclusions of the actual legal order. The project summary detailed here seeks to reconstruct this background knowledge, tracing the genealogy of the female allegory from the 18th-century revolutionary Atlantic to the vandalized modernist plazas of 21st-century Brazil.
1.1 The Representational Vacuum
To understand the gravity of the January 8 attacks, one must first understand the historical problem the statue was meant to solve. The transition to political modernity, marked by the Atlantic Revolutions of the late 18th century, created a profound crisis of representation. Under the Old Regime, the state was embodied. Ernst Kantorowicz’s theory of the "King's Two Bodies" elucidates this pre-modern logic: the Monarch possessed a body natural (mortal and fallible) and a body politic (immortal and infallible).3 The physical presence of the King was the law; his seal was the state’s guarantee; his image on a coin was a shard of his sovereignty.
The decapitation of Louis XVI—symbolic of the broader republican turn—destroyed the "natural body" of the state. This created a terrifying representational vacuum. The new sovereigns were abstractions: "The People," "The Nation," "The Constitution," "The Law." These concepts possess no physical form. One cannot shake hands with the General Will; one cannot bow to the Civil Code. Yet, the psychological need for authority to appear in a recognizable, human form persisted. The state required a face to be loved, feared, and obeyed.
The solution found by the 18th and 19th-century republicans was the female allegory. This choice was driven by a convergence of grammatical accident and political strategy. In Latin and Romance languages, the abstract nouns for virtues are inherently feminine: Libertas (Liberty), Justitia (Justice), La République (The Republic), A Justiça (The Justice). It was linguistically natural to personify them as women.5 However, the political utility of the female form was paramount. As historians Maurice Agulhon and Marina Warner have argued, a male figure represents a ruler. A statue of a man inevitably resembles a specific King, General, or President—a competitor for power. A female figure, historically excluded from ruling and legally defined as a minor, implies ideals rather than personal power. She represents the state without threatening to usurp it. She is the "Mother" of the citizens, not their master.5
1.2 The Scope of the Inquiry
This project tracks how this solution hardened into a global visual system. By the late 19th century, a transnational visual literacy had emerged. An illiterate immigrant arriving in Rio de Janeiro, New York, or Paris could navigate the state through its female avatars. The woman with the scales was the court; the woman with the cap was the Republic; the woman with the trident was the Empire.
The research proposed here spans from the "representational crisis" of 1789 to the "iconoclastic crisis" of 2023. It connects the "scythe of equality" described by Paolo Grossi, which leveled the legal subject into a naked abstraction, with the modernist abstraction of Ceschiatti’s sculpture, which stripped Justice of her scales, leaving her only with a sword and a blindfold.3 By examining the "background knowledge" of legality, we can understand why the attack on the statue was interpreted not as property damage, but as an assassination of the state itself.
2. Redefining Legal Literacy: Beyond the Textual
Traditional definitions of legal literacy focus on "black-letter law"—the ability to read statutes, understand rights, and navigate procedural complexities.9 This text-centric view, however, excludes the vast majority of historical actors. For the illiterate peasant in 19th-century France, the enslaved African in Brazil, or the working-class citizen in Victorian London, the law was not encountered in books. It was encountered in the coin exchanged in the market, the stamp on a letter, the relief on a courthouse façade, and the statue in the public square.

The January 8 attacks demonstrate that this third form of literacy is not passive. The rioters possessed a high degree of visual competence; they knew exactly which symbols embodied the power they sought to dismantle. They did not burn the library of the court first; they attacked the image of the court. The destruction of the A Justiça statue, alongside the slashing of Di Cavalcanti’s As Mulatas and the smashing of the Araguaia stained glass, reveals that the "war on images" is a central front in contemporary political conflict.2
2.2 Background Knowledge as Legal Infrastructure
The concept of "background knowledge" is crucial here. It refers to the tacit, shared understandings that make a legal system functional. It is the instinct that tells a citizen that a building with classical columns and a statue of a blindfolded woman is a place of judgment, not a place of commerce. This knowledge is "infrastructural" because it undergirds the textual law. Without the visual cues that generate awe, respect, or fear, the text of the law remains inert.
Research indicates that in the 19th century, states invested heavily in producing this background knowledge. They minted coins with the face of the Republic to ensure that the image of the state circulated in the most intimate sphere of daily life—the market. They placed statues of Justice on courthouse pediments to "teach" the public that the violence that happened inside was not brute force, but "Justice".3 This project argues that legal historians must reconstruct this visual pedagogy to understand how the law was lived by those who could not read it.
3. The Visual Sexual Contract: Theory and Mechanism
The core critical innovation of this project is the concept of the "visual sexual contract." This theoretical framework integrates legal history with feminist political theory to explain the gendered nature of state iconography.
3.1 The Contractual Paradox
Carole Pateman famously argued in The Sexual Contract that the social contract is underpinned by a prior fraternal settlement that organizes women’s subordination.3 The modern state is a pact among brothers (men) that grants them equal political rights while maintaining their patriarchal right over women. This project extends Pateman's analysis to the visual field.
The "visual sexual contract" describes a regime of symbolic hypervisibility that acts as a substitute for political inclusion. In the 19th and early 20th centuries, precisely when women were most rigorously excluded from the franchise, the jury box, and the legislature, the female body was omnipresent as the symbol of the state. This was not a contradiction; it was a mechanism of the contract.
3.2 Mechanisms of Operation
The visual sexual contract operates through three distinct mechanisms, which the research will track across national contexts:
Substitution: The allegorical woman occupies the symbolic space where real women are absent. Marianne represents the French Republic, but French women cannot vote until 1944. The image serves as a "phantom" presence, allowing the state to claim it represents "humanity" while only empowering men.
Sacralization: The feminine figure is elevated to a plane of moral purity precisely because she is removed from the "grubby" reality of political bargaining. Justice is pure because she does not participate in the dirty work of politics. She sits above it, frozen in stone.
De-sexualization and Disciplining: To function as a state symbol, the female body must be disciplined. It is often draped in heavy classical robes, maternalized, or monumentalized to strip it of erotic or threatening agency. It is the "orderly" body that replaced the "disorderly" body of the witch or the revolutionary virago.1
3.3 The Scythe of Equality
Paolo Grossi’s metaphor of the "scythe of equality" (falce dell’uguaglianza) provides the legal-historical context for this aesthetic. The French Revolution sought to eliminate the intermediate bodies of the Old Regime (guilds, estates, corporations) to create a direct relationship between the State and the Individual.1 This produced the "socially naked" legal subject—an abstract unit devoid of specific history or status.
Female allegory was the perfect aesthetic correlate to this legal doctrine. Just as the Code Civil created an abstract citizen, the statue of the Republic created an abstract body—one that was "nobody in particular" and therefore could be "everybody." A statue of a specific male hero divides the populace (some love him, some hate him). A statue of an abstract woman unites them, precisely because she has no biography, no history, and no political ambition. The image bridged the gap between the messy reality of society and the pristine unity of the legal order.
4. Genealogy of the Allegorical Body (1789–1945)
To understand the target of the January 8 attacks, the project must reconstruct the lineage of the weaponized female form. This section of the research traces the transatlantic circulation of these icons, focusing on the "Golden Age" of allegory from the French Revolution to the end of World War II.
4.1 The Revolutionary Split: Marianne’s Two Bodies
The decapitation of Louis XVI created the need for a new sovereign face. The choice of Marianne was immediate, but contested. The research identifies a split in the iconography of Marianne that reflects the tension between revolutionary violence and state order.11
The Radical Marianne (The Virago): Exemplified by Delacroix’s Liberty Leading the People (1830). She is bare-breasted, active, dirty, and violent. She wears the Phrygian cap (symbol of the freed slave). She holds a pike or a flag. She is leading a mob. This figure is dangerous to the established order.
The Conservative Marianne (The Statue): This became the official seal of the Republic. She is seated (static), fully clothed (decorous), and holds the scales of justice or the tablets of the law. She wears a diadem or olive wreath instead of the revolutionary cap. She is the state stabilized.
The project tracks how the "Conservative Marianne" became the dominant model for legal iconography, engaging in a "disciplining" of the image that paralleled the disciplining of the working class and women in the Napoleonic Code.
4.2 Transatlantic Variations: Britannia, Germania, Helvetia
This visual vocabulary became the global lingua franca of sovereignty.
Britannia: In Britain, the female allegory coexisted with a living female monarch (Victoria). Yet, Britannia served a distinct function: she represented the Empire and maritime dominance (trident/shield). While Victoria was the head of state, Britannia was the spirit of the state’s reach. She allowed the monarchy to remain personal while the state became imperial and abstract.5
Germania: In the German lands, the allegory shifted from a romantic figure of cultural unity to a martial figure of imperial defense after the 1871 unification. She stands guard on the Rhine, holding the imperial sword. This shift militarized the female body to serve a patriarchal state, transforming the "mother" into the "valkyrie".5
Helvetia: In Switzerland, Helvetia served to unite a linguistically and religiously fractured confederation. She appears on coins as a unifying "mother" figure, smoothing over the differences between cantons.
4.3 The Brazilian Reception and Racial Whitening
When Brazil declared itself a Republic in 1889, it imported the French iconographic kit almost wholesale. The "Effigy of the Republic" (still seen on the Real currency) wears the Phrygian cap and laurel wreath. However, the project must address the specific racial dimension of this reception in a post-slavery society.
The Brazilian Republic’s image was aggressively Europeanized. Despite the country’s demographic reality, the official face of the state was a white, Greco-Roman woman. This "whitening" of the allegory served to project an image of "civilization" to Europe while masking the reality of the Afro-Brazilian population. The "Visual Sexual Contract" in Brazil was thus also a "Visual Racial Contract," offering symbolic inclusion only through a whitened, Europeanized avatar.9

5. Brasília and the Modernist Goddess: The Case of A Justiça
The research pivots from the 19th century to the 20th century with the construction of Brasília. Here, the European allegorical tradition meets High Modernism, resulting in the specific artifact that was attacked on January 8.
5.1 The Modernist Context
Brasília, inaugurated in 1960, was designed as a "total work of art" (Gesamtkunstwerk). Oscar Niemeyer’s architecture sought to express the rationality, transparency, and progress of the Brazilian state. The buildings are characterized by glass façades, suggesting that power is transparent and accessible to the people. However, as the project will argue, this transparency is a form of "simulacrum".12 It offers visual access without political access. The citizen can see the workings of power through the glass, but cannot touch them. The statue of A Justiça mediates this relationship: she sits outside, a stone guard, marking the boundary between the profane public square and the sacred space of the Court.
5.2 Alfredo Ceschiatti’s A Justiça (1961)
Alfredo Ceschiatti’s sculpture is the central artifact of the project. Carved from a monolithic block of Petrópolis granite, measuring 3.3 meters in height, it sits before the Supreme Federal Court.13 The statue represents a critical evolution in the iconography of justice. Unlike the baroque or neoclassical figures of Europe, Ceschiatti’s Justice is stripped of ornamentation. She is smooth, geometric, and imposing.

5.3 The Mystery of the Missing Scales
The most significant iconographic intervention by Ceschiatti is the omission of the scales. In traditional iconography, the scales represent the weighing of evidence, the deliberation, the "intellectual" side of judgment. The sword represents the executive power to punish. By removing the scales, Ceschiatti creates a figure that is "all execution."
Interpretations of this omission vary:
The "Unfinished" Justice: Some suggest it implies that justice in Brazil is not yet weighed, or that the task is incomplete.14
The Court as Scale: A more institutional reading suggests that the building itself (the STF) is the scale, and the statue is merely the guardian.8
Authoritarianism: A critical reading posits that a Justice with a sword but no scales represents a legal system defined by force rather than equity.
This iconographic simplification 1 fits the modernist ethos of Brasília—rational, uncluttered, direct. However, it also creates a symbolic vulnerability. A traditional Justice with scales reads clearly as a "neutral weigher." Ceschiatti’s Justice, enigmatic and armed, provided a blanker screen onto which the rioters of January 8 could project their narrative of a "tyrannical" judiciary.
6. The January 8 Iconoclasm: Anatomy of an Attack
The events of January 8, 2023, provide the empirical climax of the project. The attack on the statue was not collateral damage; it was a targeted assassination of a symbol. The rioters did not merely break things; they engaged in a complex semiotic performance.
6.1 The Act of Vandalism
Images and reports from the event reveal the specific nature of the violence against the statue:
The Graffiti: The phrase "Perdeu, mané" (You lost, fool/dude) was written in red (using lipstick or marker found in government offices) across the chest of the statue.2
The Physicality: Rioters climbed onto the statue, sitting on her lap and hanging Brazilian flags around her neck.8 This physical domination reverses the hierarchy where the citizen stands in awe below the pedestal.
The Context of the Phrase: "Perdeu, mané" was a direct quote from Supreme Court Justice Luís Roberto Barroso. In November 2022, while in New York, Barroso was heckled by a bolsonarista questioning the election results. Barroso retorted, "Perdeu, mané, não amola" (You lost, fool, don't bother me).17
6.2 Counter-Literacy and Re-Captioning
The project interprets this act through the lens of "counter-literacy." The rioters demonstrated a sophisticated understanding of the "visual contract." They knew that the Court’s legitimacy rested on its claim to be the neutral, abstract arbiter (the blindfolded woman). To destroy that legitimacy, they engaged in an act of "ventriloquism."
By writing Barroso’s dismissive, partisan words onto the chest of the abstract Goddess, they collapsed the distinction between the "Two Bodies of the Law." They forced the "Body Natural" of the Judge (Barroso, seen as biased and arrogant) to merge with the "Body Politic" of the Statue (Justice). They effectively captioned the statue, changing its meaning from "I am Eternal Justice" to "I am a Political Player who Mocks You."
This was an act of iconoclastic unmasking. It rejected the "allegorical universalization" 1 by asserting that the statue was a lie. The use of lipstick—a gendered tool—to deface the gendered allegory adds another layer of complexity, reclaiming the "female" surface for a raw, visceral message of rejection.15
6.3 The Legal and Curatorial Aftermath
The state's response confirms the importance of the symbol.
Sentencing: The Supreme Federal Court (STF) treated the vandalism with extreme severity. Débora Rodrigues dos Santos, the woman identified as writing the graffiti, was sentenced to 14 years in prison for crimes including the violent abolition of the democratic rule of law and damage to protected heritage.15 This harsh sentence underscores that attacking the image of the state is legally equivalent to attacking the body of the state.
Restoration: The statue was swiftly cleaned and restored. The promptness of this "healing" was a political necessity. The state could not allow the "scar" of the graffiti to remain, as it would permanently fix the partisan meaning onto the universal symbol. The restoration was a ritual of re-consecration, attempting to erase the counter-literacy and restore the silent authority of the stone.18
7. Comparative Case Studies: Living vs. Stone Allegories
To fully understand the tension inherent in the female allegory, the project compares the "Stone Justice" of Brasília with incidents where the allegory "came to life."
7.1 The Marianne Stamp Controversy (2013)
In 2013, France released a new stamp depicting Marianne. The artist, Olivier Ciappa, revealed that the model for the new Marianne was Inna Shevchenko, a leader of the radical feminist group FEMEN.20 Shevchenko was famous for topless protests, including sawing down crosses and disrupting political events.
This sparked a massive controversy. Conservative critics were outraged that the symbol of the Republic was modeled on a "radical" and "disorderly" woman. This incident perfectly illustrates the "Visual Sexual Contract":
The state needs the female image (Marianne).
But the state fears the real female political agent (Shevchenko).
When the "Stone Marianne" is revealed to be the "Living Shevchenko," the contract snaps. The allegory only works when the woman is abstract, silent, and contained. When she is a real, political actor, she becomes a threat to the state's dignity.
7.2 The Thatcher/Britannia Satire
Similarly, the project examines how Margaret Thatcher was depicted as Britannia during the Falklands War.20 Initially, the association empowered her (The Iron Lady). However, as her popularity waned, the comparison became satirical and grotesque. This demonstrates that the allegory is a double-edged sword: it can confer authority, but it can also be used to mock women who step out of the "symbolic" realm into the "political" realm.
8. Theoretical Synthesis and Project Methodology
8.1 Methodological Protocol: Iconographic Reconstruction
The report proposes a rigorous methodological protocol entitled "Iconographic Reconstruction for Legal History" to govern the research.3 This protocol treats images as legal texts that must be "read" with the same rigor as a statute.
Protocol Steps:
Iconographic Description: Cataloging attributes (sword, scales, cap, breast, posture). What is present? What is missing (e.g., the scales in Ceschiatti)?
Pedagogy of Encounter: Analyzing the setting. Who sees this image? Is it held in the hand (coin) or viewed from below (statue)? What does it teach about the user's relationship to the state? (Intimacy vs. Awe).
Intervisual Genealogy: Tracing the motif. Does this Brazilian coin copy a French coin? Does Ceschiatti’s statue cite Michelangelo or Egyptian Ma’at?
Gendered Operation: How does the femininity of the figure function? Does it solicit love? Does it promise protection? Does it mask violence?
Contestation Analysis: How has the image been mocked, satirized, or vandalized? (e.g., Suffragette defacement of pennies, the Jan 8 graffiti).
8.2 The Corpus
The project will curate a comparative corpus of visual legal artifacts from 1789 to the present, focusing on four "supports" with distinct circulation logics:
Monetary Iconography: Coins and banknotes. These represent the "intimate ubiquity" of the state. Every time a citizen buys bread, they touch the allegory.
Postal/Philatelic Iconography: Stamps. These are "global communicators" of national virtues.
Monumental Programs: Statues and courthouse pediments. These operate through "monumental verticality," dominating public space.
Legal Paratexts: Official seals, letterheads, and code frontispieces. These are the "authenticating images" that give documents legal force.
9. Conclusion: The Fragility of the Stone State
The January 8 attacks revealed that the "infrastructural competence" of the modern state is fragile. The background knowledge that sustains the rule of law—the shared belief that the statue is Justice—can evaporate in a moment of political crisis.
The research concludes that the "Visual Sexual Contract" is fraying. The 19th-century solution of using an abstract female body to mask political conflict is no longer effective in a 21st-century polarized environment. When the public possesses "counter-literacy"—the ability to read the statue not as a universal mother but as a partisan trick—the simulacrum dissolves. The statue becomes just a rock. The court becomes just a building. The law becomes just words.
This project proposes that the task of legal history in the 21st century is to understand how these images were built, how they held the world together for two centuries, and why they are now cracking. It is a project about the "ghosts" in the machine of the law—the female ghosts that haunt the marble halls of power, silent until the moment someone climbs up and writes words of rage across their chest.
By integrating the deep history of the Visual Sexual Contract with the sharp shock of the January 8 Iconoclasm, this research offers a vital new lens for understanding the crisis of democratic legitimacy in Brazil and beyond. It defines legal literacy not just as the ability to read the law, but as the power to see the structures that make the law visible—and to recognize when those structures are under attack.

# The Iconocratic State and the Vandalized Goddess: Toward a Theory of Legal Literacy Beyond the Text

The inscription "Perdeu, mané" (You lost, fool) scrawled in red lipstick across the granite chest of Alfredo Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* on January 8, 2023, represents far more than an act of political vandalism. When supporters of former Brazilian President Jair Bolsonaro stormed the Praça dos Três Poderes in Brasília, they engaged in what this report identifies as a sophisticated form of "counter-literacy"—a deliberate unmasking of the visual apparatus through which modern states have historically constructed and maintained legal legitimacy. The graffiti transformed the 3.3-meter monumental sculpture from a silent embodiment of abstract justice into a ventriloquist's dummy speaking the partisan words of Supreme Court Justice Luís Roberto Barroso, thereby collapsing the careful distinction between the state's immortal "body politic" and the mortal, fallible bodies of individual judges.[^1][^2][^3]

This event serves as the empirical fulcrum for a comprehensive reconceptualization of legal literacy itself. The female allegory, proliferating across coins, stamps, courthouse pediments, and public monuments from the late 18th century onward, constituted the primary visual vocabulary through which citizens learned "what law looks like". ## Redefining Legal Literacy: The Tripartite Model

Traditional scholarship on legal literacy has concentrated overwhelmingly on textual competence—the ability to read legislation, comprehend rights, and deploy legal doctrine. This text-centric paradigm, however, systematically excludes the vast majority of historical actors who encountered law not through written codes but through material encounters: the coin pressed into a peasant's palm at market, the stamp affixed to an official letter, the statue dominating the courthouse square, the classical pediment signaling that violence enacted within a particular building was "justice" rather than brute force.[^1][^2][^5][^10][^11][^12][^13]

The tripartite model of legal literacy expands traditional understanding beyond textual competence to include the infrastructural and visual dimensions through which most citizens encounter the law. The third category—visual/symbolic competence—is particularly crucial for understanding how the state maintained legitimacy among illiterate populations through iconographic systems.

This report advances a tripartite reconceptualization that recognizes three distinct but interlocking forms of legal competence. **Textual competence** remains the domain of legal professionals—lawyers, judges, and educated elites who possess mastery over codes, statutes, and doctrines. **Infrastructural competence** describes the practical knowledge required to navigate legal institutions: knowing which office to approach, which forms to file, how to wait appropriately, whom to bribe. But it is the third category—**visual/symbolic competence**—that has been most systematically neglected by legal historians despite constituting the primary mode through which subaltern populations accessed legal knowledge.[^1][^2][^10][^14]

Visual competence operates through what we might call "background knowledge": the tacit, shared understandings that make a legal system functional before a single statute is read. It is the instinctive recognition that a building adorned with classical columns and a blindfolded female figure houses a court rather than a marketplace; that the woman with scales represents adjudication while the woman with a sword represents enforcement; that the Phrygian cap signals republican liberty while the crown signals monarchical authority. This knowledge is "infrastructural" in the deepest sense—it undergirds the textual law, providing the affective and cognitive scaffolding without which legal texts remain inert marks on paper.[^2][^4][^5][^6][^15][^16][^17][^13]

The January 8 attacks demonstrate conclusively that visual literacy is not passive reception but active interpretation. The Brasília rioters possessed a sophisticated visual competence; they knew precisely which symbols embodied the power they sought to delegitimize. They did not burn the Supreme Court's law library first—they attacked its image. The systematic targeting of Ceschiatti's *A Justiça*, Di Cavalcanti's *As Mulatas*, and Marianne Peretti's *Araguaia* stained glass reveals that contemporary political conflict increasingly centers on contests over visual legitimacy.[^18][^19][^20][^21][^22][^1][^2]

## The Visual Sexual Contract: Theory and Historical Genealogy

To understand why the female form became the universal visual language of state authority requires synthesizing legal history with feminist political theory. Building upon Carole Pateman's foundational concept of "the sexual contract," this research proposes the existence of a **visual sexual contract**—a regime of symbolic hypervisibility that functioned as a sophisticated substitute for political inclusion.[^8][^9][^23][^24]

Pateman demonstrated that the Enlightenment's celebrated "social contract" was undergirded by a prior fraternal settlement organizing women's systematic subordination. The modern liberal state emerged not from a universal contract among equals but from a pact among brothers (men) granting them equal political rights while maintaining their patriarchal right over women. This report extends Pateman's analysis into the visual field, arguing that the omnipresent female allegory was not incidental decoration but rather a core mechanism through which patriarchal modernity operated.[^9][^23][^8]

The visual sexual contract operated through three interlocking mechanisms. First, **substitution**: the allegorical woman occupied the symbolic space where real women were absent. Marianne represented the French Republic, but French women could not vote until 1944—152 years after Marianne's consolidation as the official face of the state. The image served as a "phantom presence," allowing the state to claim it represented universal humanity while empowering only half the population.[^5][^6][^25][^26][^27][^8]

Second, **sacralization**: the feminine figure was elevated to a plane of moral purity precisely because she was removed from the "grubby" reality of political bargaining. Justice remained pure because she did not participate in the messy work of politics—she sat above it, frozen in stone, eternal and incorruptible. This sacralization paradoxically depended upon women's exclusion from actual political power; a Justice who could speak, vote, or legislate would threaten the very purity she was meant to embody.[^5][^6][^8][^9][^15][^16]

Third, **disciplining and de-sexualization**: to function as a state symbol, the female body required careful management. Allegories were typically draped in heavy classical robes, maternalized, or monumentalized to strip them of erotic or threatening agency. The "orderly" allegorical body replaced the "disorderly" body of the witch, the revolutionary virago, or the uncontrolled woman. The contrast between the "radical Marianne" of Delacroix's *Liberty Leading the People* (1830)—bare-breasted, active, violent, leading a mob—and the "conservative Marianne" adopted for official state seals—seated, clothed, holding tablets of law—illustrates this disciplining process.[^6][^28][^25][^27][^5]

The data reveal a striking correlation: nations that most heavily relied on the female form to conceptualize sovereignty were among the slowest to grant women political agency. This pattern suggests that the allegorical image did not merely reflect women's exclusion—it actively enabled and prolonged it. The statue served as a powerful mask, creating the illusion of feminine presence and power while the actual legal and political order systematically denied women agency. The image was not a placeholder awaiting replacement by real women; it was a substitute designed to make their absence tolerable, even invisible.[^6][^8][^9][^5]

## From the King's Two Bodies to the Goddess's Two Bodies

The representational crisis that necessitated the female allegory emerged from a profound rupture in political theology. Under the Old Regime, sovereignty was embodied. Ernst Kantorowicz's theory of "the King's Two Bodies" elucidates the pre-modern logic: the Monarch possessed both a body natural (mortal, subject to infirmity and death) and a body politic (immortal, infallible, continuous across individual reigns). The physical presence of the King *was* the law; his image on a coin constituted a fragment of his sovereignty; his seal authenticated documents through the transfer of his body into wax.[^31][^32][^33][^34]

The decapitation of Louis XVI in 1793 destroyed the "natural body" of the French state, creating a terrifying representational vacuum. The new sovereigns were pure abstractions: "The People," "The Nation," "The Constitution," "The Law". These concepts possessed no physical form. One cannot shake hands with the General Will; one cannot bow before the Civil Code. Yet the psychological and political need for authority to appear in recognizable, human form persisted. Without a visible sovereign, how could citizens recognize legitimate power? How could the state's commands be distinguished from the demands of mere men?[^5][^6][^35][^36][^31]

The female allegory emerged as the solution to this crisis. The choice was overdetermined by both linguistic and political factors. In Latin and Romance languages, abstract nouns for civic virtues are grammatically feminine: *Libertas* (Liberty), *Justitia* (Justice), *La République* (The Republic), *A Justiça* (The Justice). It was linguistically natural to personify them as women. But the political calculation was paramount: a male figure inevitably resembles a specific ruler—a potential competitor for power. A statue of a man divides the populace (some identify with him, others oppose him). A female figure, historically excluded from ruling and legally defined in many jurisdictions as a perpetual minor, implies ideals rather than personal ambition. She represents the state without threatening to usurp it. She is the "Mother" of citizens, not their master.[^6][^8][^28][^9][^37][^16][^38][^5]

This insight allows us to theorize what might be called the **Goddess's Two Bodies**—a gendered reconfiguration of Kantorowicz's medieval concept. The allegorical woman possesses a body natural (the specific statue, painting, or coin that can be touched, damaged, or destroyed) and a body politic (the abstract ideal of Justice, Liberty, or the Republic that transcends any individual representation). The January 8 attacks targeted precisely this dualism. By writing Barroso's partisan words onto Ceschiatti's universal Justice, the rioters forced a catastrophic merger of the two bodies. They revealed that the "body politic" was not, in fact, transcendent—it was vulnerable, partisan, located in specific mortal judges who could be mocked, dismissed, and defeated.[^1][^2][^32][^3][^39][^31]

## Brasília, Modernism, and the Mystery of the Missing Scales

The specific artifact vandalized on January 8 warrants close iconographic analysis, for Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* represents a critical evolution—and some would argue, a distortion—of the traditional allegorical vocabulary. Carved in 1961 from a monolithic block of Petrópolis granite, the 3.3-meter statue sits before the Supreme Federal Court as part of Oscar Niemeyer's comprehensive modernist program for Brazil's new capital.[^2][^40][^41][^42][^43]

Brasília itself constituted a utopian experiment in making legibility visible. Inaugurated in 1960, the city was designed as what the Germans call a *Gesamtkunstwerk*—a total work of art in which architecture, urban planning, and political symbolism fused into a unified expression of the rational, transparent, modern state. Niemeyer's buildings are characterized by glass façades, suggesting that power itself has become transparent and accessible to the people. The citizen can *see* the workings of government through crystalline walls. Yet scholars have noted that this transparency functions as "simulacrum"—it offers visual access without political access. The glass allows the citizen to observe power but not to touch it. The statue mediates this relationship: *A Justiça* sits outside, a stone sentinel marking the boundary between the profane public square and the sacred interior space of juridical authority.[^44][^41][^42][^43][^45][^2]

Ceschiatti's Justice departs significantly from her European predecessors. Where traditional representations depicted Justice standing or seated, holding scales in one hand (representing deliberation and the weighing of evidence) and a sword in the other (representing executive power to enforce judgment), Ceschiatti's figure is notably **missing the scales entirely**. She sits, blindfolded, with the sword resting dormant across her lap. The omission is not incidental—it constitutes the sculpture's most significant iconographic intervention.[^40][^15][^46][^16][^17][^2]

Multiple interpretations circulate. Some suggest the absence signals that justice in Brazil is "unfinished"—the weighing has not yet occurred. Others propose an institutional reading: the Supreme Court building itself functions as the scale, rendering a separate sculptural scale redundant. The most critical interpretation, however, reads the missing scales as symptomatic of authoritarianism: a Justice armed with a sword but lacking scales represents a legal system defined by force rather than equity, by punishment rather than deliberation.[^47][^46][^2][^40]

This modernist simplification—reducing the allegory to its barest formal elements—created a symbolic vulnerability. Traditional Justice, holding scales, reads unambiguously as a neutral arbiter carefully weighing competing claims. Ceschiatti's Justice, enigmatic and armed, provided a blanker screen onto which the January 8 rioters could project their narrative of a "tyrannical" judiciary that had already decided against them without weighing the evidence. The statue's modernist abstraction, intended to convey timeless rationality, became instead a site for contested meaning-making.[^1][^3][^39][^15][^48][^46][^49][^16][^50][^17][^12][^2][^40]

They scrawled "Perdeu, mané" across her chest using red lipstick—a gendered tool that added layers of meaning to an already gendered artifact.[^51][^1][^2][^18][^3][^39][^52]

The phrase itself is critical. "Perdeu, mané" (You lost, fool/dude) was a direct quotation from Justice Barroso. In November 2022, while in New York, Barroso was accosted by a Bolsonaro supporter questioning the election results. By inscribing Barroso's partisan, contemptuous words onto the body of the abstract Goddess, the rioters performed what this report theorizes as **iconoclastic unmasking**—a violent revelation that the universal pretensions of the allegory were a lie.[^1][^2][^3]

This act constitutes "counter-literacy" in its most sophisticated form. The rioters demonstrated that they could read the visual contract perfectly—they understood that the Court's legitimacy rested on its claim to be the neutral, abstract arbiter represented by the blindfolded woman. To destroy that legitimacy, they engaged in an act of *ventriloquism*, forcing the statue to speak in Barroso's voice. They collapsed the distinction between the "Two Bodies" of the Law—the abstract Body Politic (Justice herself, blind and impartial) and the Body Natural (specific judges with biases, contempt, partisan alignments).[^2][^31][^32][^3][^10][^53][^54][^1]

This was not illiteracy—it was hyperliteracy. The rioters re-captioned the statue, changing its meaning from "I am Eternal Justice" to "I am a partisan player who mocks you". The use of lipstick to inscribe this message added another semiotic layer: lipstick as feminine adornment, lipstick as tool of writing, lipstick's red color echoing blood or revolutionary violence. The choice to write on a gendered allegory using a gendered instrument created a dense palimpsest of meanings—reclaiming the disciplined feminine surface for a raw, visceral message of rejection.[^51][^3][^53][^54][^1][^2]

The Brazilian state's response confirms the statue's centrality to legal legitimacy. Débora Rodrigues dos Santos, identified as the woman who wrote the graffiti, was sentenced to 14 years in prison for crimes including "violent abolition of the democratic rule of law," "coup d'état," "damage to protected heritage," and "deterioration of listed property". This extraordinarily harsh sentence—far exceeding penalties for many violent crimes—underscores that attacking the *image* of the state is legally equivalent to attacking the state itself. The statue was swiftly cleaned and restored; the Brazilian government could not allow the "scar" of the graffiti to remain, as it would permanently inscribe the partisan meaning onto the universal symbol. The restoration was a ritual of re-consecration, attempting to erase the counter-literacy and restore the silent authority of stone.[^4][^55][^3][^39][^52][^22][^51]

## Comparative Iconoclasms: When the Allegory Comes to Life

The tension inherent in the female allegory—the paradox of representing power through figures systematically excluded from power—becomes most visible in moments when the "stone allegory" and the "living woman" collide. Two comparative cases illuminate this dynamic.

In 2013, France released a new postage stamp depicting Marianne, the embodiment of the French Republic. The artist, Olivier Ciappa, revealed that the model for the stamp was Inna Shevchenko, leader of the radical feminist group FEMEN. Shevchenko was internationally notorious for topless protests, including sawing down Orthodox crosses in Ukraine and disrupting political events across Europe. Conservative critics erupted in fury. How could the symbol of the Republic be modeled on a "radical," "disorderly," "indecent" woman?[^56][^57][^58][^59]

This incident perfectly crystallizes the visual sexual contract. The state *needs* the female image (Marianne must appear on stamps, coins, and official seals to make the Republic visible). But the state *fears* the real female political agent. When the "Stone Marianne" is revealed to be the "Living Shevchenko," the contract fractures. The allegory only functions when the woman is abstract, silent, contained, and compliant. A real woman who is vocal, political, and transgressive—who uses her body as a weapon of protest rather than a canvas for state symbolism—threatens the entire system. As one commentator noted, Shevchenko was deemed "too dangerous" to represent France precisely because she refused to remain safely frozen in symbolic space.[^5][^25][^26][^57][^58][^59][^56]

## Brasília's Other Wounded Bodies: *As Mulatas* and *Araguaia*

The January 8 attacks targeted not only Ceschiatti's Justice but also a constellation of modernist artworks that collectively constituted Brasília's visual infrastructure of legitimacy. Di Cavalcanti's *As Mulatas* (1962), displayed on the third floor of the Planalto Presidential Palace, was stabbed repeatedly—at least five individual puncture wounds tore through the canvas. The painting, depicting mixed-race women conversing by the sea, represented Brazilian modernism's attempt to construct a national identity through idealized representations of mestiçagem (racial mixture).[^19][^21][^22][^63]

The targeting of *As Mulatas* reveals the racial dimension of Brazil's visual sexual contract. The 1889 proclamation of the Republic imported France's iconographic template almost wholesale—the official effigy of the Republic wore the Phrygian cap and laurel wreath of Marianne. Yet this created a profound contradiction in a post-slavery society where the majority population was of African or mixed descent. The official face of the Brazilian state was aggressively Europeanized: a white, Greco-Roman woman projecting an image of "civilization" to Europe while masking the demographic reality of Afro-Brazilian citizens. The visual sexual contract in Brazil was simultaneously a **visual racial contract**, offering symbolic inclusion only through a whitened avatar aligned with the state's explicit "branqueamento" (whitening) ideology.[^64][^65][^66][^67]

Marianne Peretti's *Araguaia* stained glass (1977), installed in the Green Hall of the National Congress, was also damaged. Peretti, the only woman on Oscar Niemeyer's team, created abstract geometric patterns suggesting the movement of a river—bringing the natural landscape into the architectural space of lawmaking. The destruction of this work, alongside the statue and the painting, reveals that the rioters understood Brasília's artistic program as a unified system. They attacked not just individual objects but the comprehensive visual ideology that legitimated the state.[^1][^18][^20][^21][^68][^22][^69][^70][^19]

## Conclusion: The Fragility of the Stone State

The January 8 Brasília attacks revealed what scholars of iconoclasm have long understood: images do not merely reflect power—they constitute it. When rioters defaced Ceschiatti's *A Justiça*, they demonstrated that the "infrastructural competence" sustaining modern legal orders is profoundly fragile. The background knowledge that causes citizens to trust, obey, and navigate the state—the shared belief that the statue *is* Justice, that the building *is* the Court, that violence enacted within those walls is legitimate rather than criminal—can evaporate in moments of political crisis.[^1][^2][^71][^72][^73][^74][^75][^76]

The research presented here argues that the "visual sexual contract" that sustained republican legitimacy for two centuries is now visibly fraying. The 19th-century solution—using an abstract female body to mask political conflict and patriarchal exclusion—no longer functions effectively in a 21st-century environment characterized by counter-literacy, digital circulation of contested images, and populations capable of reading state iconography against the grain. When the public possesses sophisticated visual competence, the statue ceases to be a sacred embodiment of justice and becomes legible as a political trick, a gendered performance, a simulacrum concealing rather than revealing the operations of power.[^2][^44][^41][^10][^53][^54][^77][^78][^1]

The rioters' inscription transformed *A Justiça* from a monument into a palimpsest—a surface bearing the traces of multiple, contested meanings. This transformation cannot be fully erased, even after physical restoration. The image of the defaced statue circulates globally, teaching new generations to read state iconography suspiciously. The "ghost" of the graffiti haunts the cleaned stone, suggesting that future attacks—physical or semiotic—remain possible.[^4][^18][^55][^22][^53][^54][^1][^2]

This report has proposed that legal historians must fundamentally reconceptualize their object of study. Legal literacy is not merely the capacity to read statutes—it is the historically situated ability to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material cues, spatial arrangements, and iconographic systems. The female allegory was not decorative but foundational, teaching citizens "what law looks like" while simultaneously masking the gendered exclusions of the actual legal order. The task now is to understand how these visual infrastructures were built, how they held the political world together for two centuries, and why they are now cracking under the pressure of populations who have learned to read the statues back.[^5][^6][^8][^9][^71][^10][^14][^15][^16][^17][^12][^75][^13][^76][^1][^2]

The vandalized goddess stands as testament to a profound truth: justice, like sovereignty itself, requires more than texts and institutions. It requires belief—belief materialized in stone, circulated on coins, stamped on letters, carved into courthouse pediments. When that belief fractures, the statue becomes just a rock. The court becomes just a building. The law becomes just words. And the question that haunted the revolutionaries of 1789 returns with renewed urgency: without a visible sovereign, how can power be recognized as legitimate? The answer remains uncertain, but the January 8 attacks demonstrated conclusively that the old solutions—the stone goddesses who stood guard for two centuries—can no longer bear the weight of the question.
