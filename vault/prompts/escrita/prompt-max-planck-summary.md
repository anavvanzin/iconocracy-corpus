# **PROJECT SUMMARY**

## **The Iconocratic State and the Vandalized Goddess: Visual Literacy, Counter-Literacy, and the Crisis of Allegorical Legitimacy**

**Max Planck Summer Academy for Legal History 2026**
**Theme: Legal Literacy**
**Dates: 6–17 July 2026, Frankfurt am Main**

***

### **I. Research Question and Theoretical Framework**

This project investigates a fundamental paradox in the historical construction of legal legitimacy: How did modern states make law legible to populations who could not read? The proposed research redefines legal literacy beyond textual competence to encompass **visual/symbolic literacy**—the historically situated capacity to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material cues, spatial arrangements, and iconographic systems.[^1][^2][^3]

The empirical fulcrum is the January 8, 2023 attack on Brazil's Supreme Federal Court, when rioters defaced Alfredo Ceschiatti's monumental statue *A Justiça* (1961) with the graffiti "Perdeu, mané" (You lost, fool)—a phrase quoted from Justice Luís Roberto Barroso. This act of **iconoclastic unmasking** demonstrates that visual literacy is not passive reception but active interpretation and contestation. The rioters possessed sophisticated visual competence; they understood that the Court's legitimacy rested on the allegorical statue's claim to transcendent neutrality, and they systematically destroyed that claim by forcing the "universal goddess" to speak in a partisan judge's voice.[^4][^5][^6][^7][^8]

Building upon Carole Pateman's concept of "the sexual contract", this research proposes the existence of a **visual sexual contract**—a regime of symbolic hypervisibility functioning as substitute for political inclusion. Precisely when women were most rigorously excluded from political agency (franchise, jury service, legal profession), the female body proliferated as the omnipresent face of Law, Justice, and the Nation. Marianne, Britannia, Germania, Columbia, and Brazil's Republican effigy taught citizens across the Atlantic world "what law looks like" while masking the gendered exclusions of actual legal orders.[^2][^3][^9][^10][^11][^12][^13][^1]

***

### **II. Legal Literacy Reconceptualized: A Tripartite Model**

Traditional scholarship concentrates on "black-letter literacy"—mastery of codes and statutes accessible only to legal professionals. This project advances a comprehensive **tripartite definition** aligning with the Max Planck Academy's call to reconstruct diverse forms of legal knowledge managed by "laypeople, subaltern actors, or members of the elite":[^3][^14][^15][^1][^2]

**1. Textual Competence**: Mastery of codes, statutes, doctrines
*Media*: Law books, case reports, gazettes
*Actors*: Lawyers, judges, educated elites

**2. Infrastructural Competence**: Practical navigation of institutions
*Media*: Forms, counters, waiting rooms, bureaucratic procedures
*Actors*: Litigants, petitioners, bureaucrats

**3. Visual/Symbolic Competence**: Recognition of authority's "face"
*Media*: Statues, coins, stamps, courthouse architecture, seals
*Actors*: The general public, including illiterate and subaltern populations[^16][^17][^5][^14][^1][^3][^4]

The third category constitutes the project's primary contribution. For the 19th-century illiterate peasant, the enslaved African in Brazil, or the working-class citizen in Victorian London, law was not encountered in books but in the coin pressed into the palm at market, the stamp on official correspondence, the relief on the courthouse pediment, and the statue dominating the public square. This **background knowledge**—the tacit understanding that a blindfolded woman signals "court" rather than "marketplace," that classical columns denote state authority—undergirded textual law, providing the affective and cognitive infrastructure without which legal texts remain inert.[^5][^11][^18][^19][^20][^1][^2][^3][^4]

***

### **III. The Visual Sexual Contract: Mechanisms and Historical Evidence**

The female allegory was not incidental decoration but a core mechanism through which patriarchal modernity operated. The visual sexual contract functioned through three mechanisms:[^9][^10][^11][^12]

**Substitution**: The allegorical woman occupied symbolic space where real women were absent. Marianne represented the French Republic from 1792, but French women gained suffrage only in 1944—a **152-year lag**. Switzerland's Helvetia unified the confederation from 1850; Swiss women voted from 1971 (121-year lag). The United States deployed Columbia/Liberty from the 1790s; women's suffrage arrived in 1920 (130-year lag).[^11][^21][^22][^23][^24][^25]

**Sacralization**: The feminine figure was elevated to moral purity precisely through removal from political reality. Justice remained pure because she did not participate in politics—she sat above it, frozen in stone.[^12][^26][^27][^11]

**Disciplining**: To function as state symbol, the female body required control. Allegories were draped in classical robes, maternalized, monumentalized—stripped of erotic or threatening agency. The "conservative Marianne" (seated, clothed, holding law tablets) replaced the "radical Marianne" of Delacroix (bare-breasted, violent, leading mobs).[^13][^21][^11][^12]

This project employs **"Lag Analysis"**—measuring temporal gaps between allegorical consolidation and women's political rights—to provide quantitative evidence. Nations most heavily relying on female imagery (France 152 years, Switzerland 121 years, USA 130 years) were slowest to grant suffrage, suggesting that symbolic hypervisibility actively enabled and prolonged exclusion rather than anticipating inclusion.[^10][^23][^9][^11][^12]

***

### **IV. Methodological Innovation: Iconographic Reconstruction and Counter-Literacy**

The project proposes an **Iconographic Reconstruction Protocol** treating images as legal texts requiring rigorous "reading":[^28][^29][^30]

1. **Iconographic Description**: Cataloging attributes (scales, sword, blindfold, cap). What is present? What is *missing*? (Ceschiatti's statue notably lacks scales—only sword and blindfold).[^26][^27][^31][^32][^33][^28][^5]
2. **Pedagogy of Encounter**: Analyzing the setting. Who sees this image? From what position (hand-held coin vs. monumental statue viewed from below)? What does it teach about the citizen's relationship to the state?[^19][^20][^1][^28][^5]
3. **Intervisual Genealogy**: Tracing motifs across borders. Does this Brazilian coin copy a French model? Does Ceschiatti cite Michelangelo or Egyptian Ma'at?[^28][^5][^11][^12]
4. **Gendered Operation**: How does femininity function? Does it solicit love, promise protection, or mask violence?[^9][^10][^11][^12][^28]
5. **Contestation Analysis**: How has the image been mocked, satirized, or vandalized?[^34][^35][^36][^7][^37][^38][^4][^5]

The fifth dimension is critical. The January 8 attacks reveal the emergence of **counter-literacy**—sophisticated visual competence deployed to delegitimize rather than sustain state authority. Rioters engaged in **iconoclastic ventriloquism**, forcing the statue to speak Barroso's partisan words, thereby collapsing the distinction between the Law's immortal "body politic" (abstract Justice) and its mortal "body natural" (fallible judges). The use of red lipstick—a gendered tool—to deface a gendered allegory created a dense palimpsest of meanings, reclaiming the disciplined feminine surface for visceral rejection.[^35][^36][^6][^7][^39][^40][^41][^42][^43][^4][^5]

The Brazilian state's response confirms the statue's centrality: Débora Rodrigues dos Santos received a **14-year prison sentence** for the graffiti, treated legally as equivalent to attacking the state itself. The statue was swiftly restored—the government could not allow the partisan "scar" to remain permanently inscribed on the universal symbol.[^6][^8][^20][^43][^44][^45][^46]

***

### **V. Research Corpus and Comparative Framework**

The project constructs a **transatlantic comparative corpus** (1789–2023) examining four "supports" with distinct circulation logics:

**Monetary Iconography**: Coins/banknotes representing "intimate ubiquity"—every market transaction touches the allegory.

**Postal/Philatelic Iconography**: Stamps as "global communicators" of national virtues.

**Monumental Programs**: Statues and courthouse pediments operating through "vertical domination" of public space.[^30][^18][^19][^28]

**Legal Paratexts**: Official seals, letterheads, code frontispieces—the "authenticating images" giving documents legal force.

**Comparative case studies** illuminate tensions when "stone allegory" meets "living woman":

- **France 2013**: The new Marianne stamp modeled on FEMEN activist Inna Shevchenko sparked outrage. The state *needs* the female image but *fears* the real female political agent. When "Stone Marianne" is revealed as "Living Shevchenko," the contract fractures.[^37][^38][^47][^48]
- **Brazil's Racial Dimension**: The 1889 Republican allegory imported France's iconographic template, creating a white, Greco-Roman goddess in a post-slavery society. The visual sexual contract was simultaneously a **visual racial contract**, offering symbolic inclusion only through a whitened avatar aligned with "branqueamento" ideology.[^49][^50][^51][^52]

***

### **VI. Contribution to Legal History and Engagement with Max Planck Themes**

This research directly addresses the Academy's core questions:[^1][^2][^3]

**"What constitutes legal knowledge?"** Not only doctrinal mastery but the tacit visual competence enabling subaltern populations to recognize legitimate authority.

**"How can it be reconstructed?"** Through systematic analysis of material culture (coins, stamps, statues, architecture) as legal sources equivalent in importance to written codes.[^29][^30][^1][^28]

**"Is it black-letter law, practitioners' law, or background knowledge managed by laypeople and subaltern actors?"** All three, but traditional scholarship systematically neglects the third category despite its constituting the primary mode through which most historical populations accessed law.[^17][^2][^3][^16][^1]

**"How can legal historians approach diverse media transmitting knowledge?"** The Iconographic Reconstruction Protocol provides a rigorous methodology treating visual artifacts as legal texts.[^29][^30][^28]

The project contributes to current scholarly emphases on:

- **Material turn in legal history**: Recognizing law's embodiment in objects, spaces, and images[^30][^1][^28][^29]
- **Global/comparative legal history**: Tracking transatlantic circulation of iconographic vocabularies[^53][^54][^11][^12][^1]
- **Subaltern legal knowledge**: Reconstructing how populations excluded from textual literacy nevertheless "read" and contested law[^55][^56][^16][^17][^1]
- **Gender and law**: Exposing how visual culture masked exclusions while claiming universality[^16][^10][^1][^9]

***

### **VII. Contemporary Relevance: The Fragility of Visual Legitimacy**

The January 8 Brasília attacks reveal that 19th-century solutions no longer function in 21st-century polarized environments. When publics possess counter-literacy—the ability to read statues "against the grain"—the simulacrum dissolves. The statue becomes legible not as universal justice but as a partisan trick, a gendered performance concealing rather than revealing power operations.[^7][^39][^40][^57][^58][^59][^60][^61][^62][^35][^4][^5]

The defaced *A Justiça* now circulates globally as a palimpsest bearing contested meanings. Even after physical restoration, the "ghost" of the graffiti haunts the cleaned stone, teaching new generations suspicious readings of state iconography. This represents a crisis not merely of Brazilian legitimacy but of the entire modern legal-visual infrastructure built over two centuries.[^20][^39][^45][^46][^57][^4][^5][^7]

***

### **VIII. Conclusion: Legal History's Visual Turn**

This project argues that legal historians must fundamentally reconceptualize their object of study. Legal literacy extends far beyond reading statutes—it encompasses the historically situated ability to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material encounters. The female allegory was not decorative but **foundational**, teaching citizens "what law looks like" while masking who law served.[^14][^18][^27][^33][^63][^2][^3][^4][^5][^10][^11][^12][^19][^26][^1][^9]

The task now is to understand how these visual infrastructures were built, how they sustained political orders for two centuries, and why they are fracturing under pressure from populations who have learned to read the goddesses back. By integrating legal history, feminist theory, art history, and iconoclastic studies, this research offers vital new perspectives on the crisis of democratic legitimacy in Brazil and beyond—revealing that justice, like sovereignty itself, requires more than texts and institutions. It requires belief materialized in stone, circulated on coins, stamped on letters. When that belief fractures, the statue becomes just a rock, the court just a building, the law just words.[^58][^59][^60][^3][^4][^5][^11][^12][^1]
