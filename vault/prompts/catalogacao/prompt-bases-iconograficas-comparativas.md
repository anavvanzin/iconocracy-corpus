# Assim como o iconclass - quais bases de dados posso utilizar para a pesquisa e construção do corpus iconográfico?

To build an iconographic corpus in a way comparable to what Iconclass allows, it is useful to think in three layers:

1. Iconographic vocabularies and authorities (to give you stable concepts and codes).
2. Image databases and aggregators that are indexed by subject/iconography.
3. Legal‑iconography–specific collections and portals.

Unless indicated in quotation marks, the formulations below are my own interpretation based on the cited sources (my interpretation).

***

## 1. Iconographic vocabularies and authorities (beyond Iconclass)

### 1.1. Iconclass itself (as vocabulary and as access point)

Even though you already know Iconclass, it is worth underlining two aspects that matter for corpus construction:

- Iconclass is “a classification system designed for art and iconography” and is described as “the most widely accepted scientific tool for the description and retrieval of subjects represented in images” (direct quotation from an ICOM CIDOC working group page).[^1_1]
- It currently includes c. 28,000 hierarchically ordered definitions of subjects and about 14,000 keywords, divided into ten main divisions (0–9) from abstract art to classical mythology and ancient history.[^1_2][^1_1]

The official Iconclass website lists multiple external databases that can be searched via Iconclass codes (see section 2). For building a corpus, this means:[^1_3]

- You can use the Iconclass Browser to stabilize your subject terms (e.g. 11MM44 for “Justice, Justitia, as one of the four cardinal virtues” as used in a Tübingen “Allegorie der Gerechtigkeit” print).[^1_4]
- The “Iconclass Artificial Intelligence Test Set” offers 87,749 images already indexed with Iconclass concepts, downloadable as a combined image–metadata dataset. This is effectively a ready‑made, structured iconographic corpus that you can re‑filter around justice, law, sovereignty, nation, etc.[^1_3]

(Interpretation, my formulation based on these sources.)

***

### 1.2. Getty vocabularies and the Getty Iconography Authority (IA)

The Getty Research Institute maintains a family of controlled vocabularies (AAT, TGN, ULAN, CONA) and, more recently, the Getty Iconography Authority (IA).[^1_5][^1_6]

- The IA “includes the proper names of subjects not covered by other Getty Vocabularies,” with scope covering “named historical events, religious or mythological iconography, religious and fictional characters and places, themes from literature” (direct quotation).[^1_6][^1_5]
- It is explicitly designed as a subject thesaurus that can be linked to local catalogues and to other resources such as Iconclass and Library of Congress subject authorities.[^1_5]

For corpus work, the practical implication is:

- You can adopt Iconclass as your *spine* for detailed iconographic coding, and use Getty IA + AAT for more generic subject tags (e.g. “allegories,” “justice (concept),” “national personifications”).
- Many museum databases already index depicted subjects with Getty vocabularies; this makes cross‑institution queries more coherent.

(Interpretation, my formulation based on these sources.)

***

## 2. Multi‑institution image databases with iconographic indexing

These are the closest analogues to “Iconclass‑like” bases de dados: large image collections whose access is organized by subjects, often using Iconclass.

### 2.1. Arkyves (Iconclass‑based aggregator)

The Iconclass site lists Arkyves as an “aggregator site, bringing together sources like medieval illuminated manuscripts, printer’s devices and emblems, paintings and graphic material from collections such as the Rijksmuseum, Folger Shakespeare Library, Herzog August Bibliothek, the Getty Institute, and Glasgow University Library” (direct quotation). Access is by subscription.[^1_3]

- Arkyves organizes these heterogeneous collections through Iconclass, enabling you to search directly by Iconclass code or by the associated keywords.[^1_7][^1_3]
- For a corpus on female allegories of Justice, Nation, Liberty, etc., this is especially powerful because emblem books and early modern prints are heavily represented there (my interpretation).


### 2.2. Bildindex der Kunst und Architektur (Foto Marburg)

The Bildindex der Kunst und Architektur is a free image database with around 3.2 million photographs for about 1.9 million works of art and architecture in Germany and Europe.[^1_8]

- It aggregates holdings from the Bildarchiv Foto Marburg and roughly 90 partner institutions (including Bibliotheca Hertziana, Kunsthistorisches Institut in Florenz, Deutsche Fotothek, national libraries, and heritage agencies).[^1_8]
- The Iconclass project explicitly lists Bildindex as one of the online databases offering Iconclass search capabilities.[^1_3]

For your purposes:

- Bildindex is extremely strong for sculptural programs, court buildings, civic monuments, and allegorical figures in situ across Europe—ideal for tracing how Justice, the Nation, or Law are staged in architectural contexts (my interpretation).


### 2.3. Warburg Institute Iconographic Database

The Warburg Institute’s Photographic Collection holds about 400,000 photographs of artworks (sculptures, paintings, drawings, prints, tapestries, etc.). A growing portion is accessible in the Warburg Institute Iconographic Database.[^1_9]

- The collection is historically organized *iconographically*, following Aby Warburg’s card‑file and Mnemosyne‑atlas logic.[^1_10][^1_9]
- The database lets you browse images according to iconographic themes (my interpretation).

For allegories of Justice and the Nation, Warburg is valuable because:

- It foregrounds thematic and symbolic connections across time and geography (e.g. between civic rituals, astrological emblems, and legal allegories), which fits a post‑colonial, comparative legal‑iconography agenda (my interpretation).


### 2.4. RKD Research and RKDimages (Netherlands Institute for Art History)

RKD Research is the unified interface to nine RKD databases (RKDartists, RKDimages, RKDimages Lite, RKDlibrary, RKDarchives, etc.).[^1_11]

Key points:

- RKDimages contains images of over 250,000 mainly early modern Dutch paintings, drawings, prints, and photographs, with validated information on provenance, exhibitions, and literature.[^1_11]
- The visual documentation collections include more than five million digital and analogue images, with strong emphasis on Dutch and Flemish art but also “other Western art”.[^1_12]
- RKDimages and related resources emerge from a tradition that used Iconclass as a core index system (e.g. Decimal Index to the Art of the Low Countries, D.I.A.L.) and are widely used for iconographic research.[^1_13][^1_14]

In practice:

- RKDimages lets you search by subject fields and, in many cases, by Iconclass number (especially for Dutch painting and prints).[^1_14]
- For female allegories in legal and political contexts (e.g. personifications of the Republic, Liberty, Justice), RKD’s holdings on the Dutch Republic and early modern Europe can be systematically mined (my interpretation).


### 2.5. Europe‑wide and national digital heritage portals

These portals are not as tightly iconography‑driven as Arkyves or Bildindex, but are crucial to widen your corpus geographically and chronologically.

- **Europeana** aggregates digital cultural heritage (art, books, films, music, museum objects) from thousands of European institutions, with thematic entry points such as “Art” collections. Subject metadata is uneven, but many partner museums use controlled vocabularies.[^1_15][^1_16]
- **Gallica** (Bibliothèque nationale de France) has evolved from an experimental digital library to a large‑scale repository that, by 1999, aimed at 100,000 volumes and 300,000 digitized images, and now includes significant iconographic content, including legal and economic materials.[^1_17]
- **Biblioteca Digital Hispánica** (Biblioteca Nacional de España) offers free access to more than 220,000 digitized documents, including many illustrated works relevant for legal and political iconography.[^1_18][^1_19]

(Interpretation, my formulation based on these sources.)

These portals are especially useful when combined with an external iconographic vocabulary: you search by textual keywords (“Justicia,” “Justitia,” “allégorie de la Justice,” “República”), then *re‑code* the relevant images according to Iconclass or your chosen scheme in your own corpus.

## 3. Legal‑iconography–specific collections

For a doctoral project in legal history and iconography, legal‑specific resources are central because they already frame images as “juridical.”

### 3.1. Karl Frölich image collection (Max Planck Institute, Frankfurt)

The Max Planck Institute for Legal History and Legal Theory hosts a digital library that includes “Legal iconography and legal archaeology: The Karl Frölich image collection”.[^1_20][^1_21][^1_22]

- Karl Frölich (1877–1953), professor of legal history in Gießen, devoted himself to “legal folklore and legal archaeology” and assembled a large photographic collection of “old legal monuments” (Rechtsdenkmäler), covering places and objects of jurisdiction, punishment, and symbols of authority in Germany and other European countries (direct quotation and paraphrase).[^1_21][^1_23]
- These images—pillories, gallows, court buildings, emblems of authority—are now digitized and made accessible for research through the Max Planck digital library.[^1_20][^1_21]

For corpus building:

- The Frölich collection gives you a dense, spatially grounded corpus of legal monuments and symbols that can be linked to allegorical representations in painting, sculpture, and print (my interpretation).


### 3.2. Dutch Database for Legal Iconography (NCRD)

The Yale Law Library’s “Legal iconography resources” page notes “the Dutch Database for Legal Iconography (NCRD) at the National Library of the Netherlands,” originally restricted to library passholders but intended to be opened more widely.[^1_24][^1_25]

- NCRD (Netherlands Documentation Center for Legal History and Legal Iconography) collects information on the legal history of the Netherlands and legal iconography, with a focus on images connected to law, justice, and legal culture.[^1_26][^1_25]
- Although access conditions have varied, it remains one of the few databases explicitly dedicated to legal iconography.

(Interpretation, my formulation based on these sources.)

### 3.3. Rechtshistorie / Rechtshistorie digital image collections

The Rechtshistorie website (by Otto Vervaart) maintains a curated “Digital collections” overview for legal history, with a dedicated section on “image collections.” It explicitly states that “the classic collections for legal iconography are present here” and mentions special subjects such as the Sachsenspiegel, emblem books, portraits, and festival books (direct quotation).[^1_27]

- This page does not host images itself but acts as a structured gateway to multiple image collections relevant for legal history and iconography.
- It is particularly useful to discover specialized, sometimes smaller, collections that would not appear easily in general web searches (my interpretation).


### 3.4. Legal iconography resources at Yale Law Library

The Lillian Goldman Law Library (Yale) page on “Legal iconography resources” reports:

- A “Justitia gallery” in Yale’s rare book Flickr site, containing multiple images of Justitia / Lady Justice from early modern printed sources.
- A “Representing Justice” gallery connected to Judith Resnik and Dennis Curtis’s book *Representing Justice*, which includes over 220 illustrations of courts and justice iconography.[^1_24]
- The same page points to online resources (including NCRD and Rechtshistorie’s collections) as core digital tools for legal iconography research.[^1_24]

(Interpretation, my formulation based on this source.)

## 4. Brazilian and post‑colonial visual portals relevant to your corpus

For a project centered on Brazil and post‑colonial contexts, you will likely need to combine European iconographic infrastructures with Brazilian and Latin American digital projects.

### 4.1. Brasiliana Iconográfica

The portal *Brasiliana Iconográfica* is a joint initiative of the Biblioteca Nacional do Brasil, Instituto Moreira Salles, Pinacoteca de São Paulo, and Itaú Cultural, among others.[^1_28][^1_29][^1_30]

- It aims to “bring together in a single web portal iconographic sources – drawings, watercolours, paintings, prints and printed materials – dispersed across public and private collections in Brazil and abroad” (direct quotation, my translation).[^1_28]
- The corpus covers images related to Brazil from the 16th century to the early 20th century and provides high‑resolution images plus technical data and interpretive essays.[^1_30][^1_28]

(Translation: *“The Brasiliana Iconográfica project proposes to bring together in a single web portal iconographic sources – drawings, watercolours, paintings, prints and printed materials – dispersed across public and private collections in Brazil and abroad, making them accessible to a broad international public via virtual consultation.”* — my translation of the Portuguese text in the project description.)[^1_28]

For your topic:

- This is a key place to look for allegories of the Nation, Republic, Law, and “Brazil” as a feminine figure in 19th–20th‑century visual culture (my interpretation).


### 4.2. Brazilian museum digital collections

Several major Brazilian art museums now offer online access to significant parts of their collections:

- **Pinacoteca de São Paulo**: its digital collection comprises more than 10,000 items, including paintings, sculptures, drawings, photographs, video, prints, performance and installations, reflecting Brazilian art since 1905.[^1_31][^1_32][^1_33]
- **MASP (Museu de Arte de São Paulo)**: individual work records (e.g. No Martins’s *Senhora Injustiça*, 2017) show how contemporary Brazilian artists explicitly engage with the allegory of Justice and its racialized, political dimensions.[^1_34]

(Interpretation, my formulation based on these sources.)

These collections generally do not yet have an iconography‑centric classification like Iconclass, but they provide essential material. For your corpus, the strategy would be:

- Retrieve relevant works via textual descriptors (e.g. “Justiça,” “República,” “alegoria,” “nação”).
- Re‑index those works in your own database using Iconclass and/or Getty IA concepts, thus “importing” Brazilian and post‑colonial material into an iconographically coherent corpus.

## 5. How to use these resources methodologically

Below is a concrete workflow, focusing on your topic (female allegories in legal culture, Brazil/Europe/USA).

### 5.1. Choose and stabilize your iconographic vocabulary

1. Use the Iconclass Browser to define the core set of codes for:
    - Justice (Justitia, Themis, related virtues).
    - Nation/personification (Marianne, República, Columbia, Germania, Britannia, etc.).
    - Attributes (scales, sword, blindfold, fasces, law tablets, crown, lion, etc.).[^1_2][^1_1][^1_4]
2. Where Iconclass is weak on specific national personifications, supplement with Getty Iconography Authority entries and AAT terms for allegories and conceptual subjects.[^1_6][^1_5]

(Interpretation, my formulation.)

### 5.2. Build a “European core” of legal allegories

- Search **Arkyves**, **Bildindex**, **Warburg Iconographic Database**, and **RKDimages** systematically by your Iconclass codes and by keywords (“Justitia,” “Gerechtigkeit,” “Justice,” “Justice allégorique,” “allegory of justice,” etc.).[^1_9][^1_8][^1_11][^1_3]
- Extract metadata (artist, date, place, type of object, current location) and, where possible, any existing Iconclass or subject terms.[^1_13][^1_3]
- Link each image in your own corpus to the corresponding Iconclass and Getty IA concepts (my interpretation).


### 5.3. Add legal‑specific corpora

- From the **Karl Frölich collection**, select images that show the material staging of justice (pillories, court buildings, prisons, coats of arms, civic statues), and index them with Iconclass and/or your own controlled terms for “legal monuments,” “judicial spaces,” etc..[^1_23][^1_21][^1_20]
- Use **NCRD** (if accessible) and the gateways provided by **Rechtshistorie** and **Yale’s legal iconography page** to discover further image databases explicitly labelled as legal iconography.[^1_25][^1_27][^1_24]

(Interpretation, my formulation.)

### 5.4. Integrate Brazilian and post‑colonial material

- In **Brasiliana Iconográfica**, **Pinacoteca**, **MASP**, and other Brazilian portals, identify works that represent Justice, Law, Nation or the Republic as female figures (including contemporary critical re‑appropriations such as No Martins’s *Senhora Injustiça*).[^1_32][^1_29][^1_31][^1_34][^1_30][^1_28]
- Treat these as case studies for how European iconographic models are translated, racialized, or contested in Brazilian visual culture (my interpretation).
- Code them with the same Iconclass and Getty IA schemas you use for European and US material, so that you can compare, for example, “Justice with scales and sword, blindfolded” across Europe and Brazil, and then trace when and where those attributes are dropped, inverted, or re‑signified.

## 6. Minimal “toolbox” summary

If the goal is to approximate the functionality of Iconclass while constructing your own corpus, the following resources are structurally closest and most immediately useful:

    - Getty Iconography Authority (IA) + AAT/CONA for subject control.[^1_5][^1_6]

    - Brasiliana Iconográfica.[^1_29][^1_30][^1_28]
    - Digital collections of Pinacoteca, MASP, and other Brazilian museums for contemporary and national allegories.[^1_33][^1_31][^1_32][^1_34]

## 1. Iconographic vocabularies and authorities (beyond Iconclass)

### 1.1. Iconclass itself (as vocabulary and as access point)

Even though you already know Iconclass, it is worth underlining two aspects that matter for corpus construction:

- Iconclass is “a classification system designed for art and iconography” and is described as “the most widely accepted scientific tool for the description and retrieval of subjects represented in images” (direct quotation from an ICOM CIDOC working group page).[1]
- It currently includes c. 28,000 hierarchically ordered definitions of subjects and about 14,000 keywords, divided into ten main divisions (0–9) from abstract art to classical mythology and ancient history.[2][1]

The official Iconclass website lists multiple external databases that can be searched via Iconclass codes (see section 2). For building a corpus, this means:[3]

- You can use the Iconclass Browser to stabilize your subject terms (e.g. 11MM44 for “Justice, Justitia, as one of the four cardinal virtues” as used in a Tübingen “Allegorie der Gerechtigkeit” print).[4]
- The “Iconclass Artificial Intelligence Test Set” offers 87,749 images already indexed with Iconclass concepts, downloadable as a combined image–metadata dataset. This is effectively a ready‑made, structured iconographic corpus that you can re‑filter around justice, law, sovereignty, nation, etc.[3]

(Interpretation, my formulation based on these sources.)

### 1.2. Getty vocabularies and the Getty Iconography Authority (IA)

The Getty Research Institute maintains a family of controlled vocabularies (AAT, TGN, ULAN, CONA) and, more recently, the Getty Iconography Authority (IA).[5][6]

- The IA “includes the proper names of subjects not covered by other Getty Vocabularies,” with scope covering “named historical events, religious or mythological iconography, religious and fictional characters and places, themes from literature” (direct quotation).[6][5]

## Busca no Corpus: Lacunas Resolvidas

### Alemanha

A pesquisa revelou uma **descoberta metodológica importante**: o *Münzgesetz* de 1871 determinou que moedas do Kaiserreich apresentassem exclusivamente o retrato do soberano (*Landesherr*) no anverso e o *Reichsadler* no reverso — **sem alegorias femininas** na moeda corrente. Isso contrasta diretamente com a França (Marianne/Semeuse desde 1897) e o Reino Unido (Britannia contínua desde o séc. XVII). A Germania como alegoria aparece em **monumentos**, não em moedas: o **Niederwalddenkmal** (1877–1883), em Rüdesheim am Rhein, apresenta Germania colossal de 12,5 m com coroa imperial na mão direita e espada abaixada na esquerda, olhando para o novo Estado. Um item do IKMK (Münzkabinett Berlin) foi incluído como **controle negativo** — moeda Kaiserreich 1875 sem alegoria feminina.[^11_1][^11_2][^11_3][^11_4]

### Bélgica

A maior descoberta foi o artigo de **Stefan Huygebaert** (2015), um estudo iconológico completo do "culto constitucional" belga pós-1848, que aplica Panofsky, Edelman e Hobsbawm à iconografia constitucional da Bélgica. A litografia *Respect à la Constitution* (Meganck/Stroobant, 1848, KBR S.III 106186) mostra **Justiça sem venda nos olhos** encarando a personificação ambígua de Belgica/Constituição — desvio do tipo que ativa o indicador de agência visual. A **Colonne du Congrès** (1850–1859) tem quatro figuras femininas na base personificando as liberdades constitucionais. O **Petit album allégorie-patriottique** (KBR ms. II 7557) contém 74 aquarelas originais sobre a Revolução Belga, digitalizado na plataforma Belgica. A KBR possui mais de 220.000 objetos numismáticos.[^11_5][^11_6][^11_7][^11_8]

## Validação e Consistência

O relatório de validação v2 confirma que todos os 18 item_ids no sampling_log são consistentes com as 20 claims da evidence_matrix e os 5 itens codificados no coding_sheet. Todas as URLs novas foram testadas e estão acessíveis.

## Descoberta Central para a Tese

O achado mais significativo desta rodada é o **contraste Alemanha × França × Reino Unido** na circulação monetária: enquanto a França e o Reino Unido colocam alegorias femininas (Marianne, Britannia) em moedas de uso cotidiano massivo, a Alemanha imperial **masculiniza** a moeda (retrato do Kaiser) e **feminiza** apenas o monumento público (Germania colossal). Isto demonstra que o "contrato visual" estado–alegoria feminina não é universal e que o regime iconocrático opera por caminhos distintos conforme a cultura constitucional de cada Estado — argumento diretamente relevante para os indicadores IC (intensidade iconocrática) e II (integração institucional) do codebook.[^11_2][^11_1]

# preciso de mais informacoes sobre a verificacao cruzada e amplia-la

Aqui está o pacote completo de verificação cruzada ampliada, com protocolo e cinco arquivos operacionais.

## Protocolo de Verificação Cruzada v2.0

O documento central estabelece cinco camadas de verificação que operam simultaneamente: (1) rastreabilidade fonte→claim, (2) vinculação claim→indicador, (3) sustentação indicador→item do corpus, (4) proveniência item→sampling log, e (5) triangulação cruzada entre tipos de fonte independentes . Cada camada tem regras de integridade invioláveis — por exemplo, nenhum `item_id` no coding sheet pode estar ausente do sampling log, e nenhuma claim de tier A pode ter campo de localizador vazio.[^12_1][^12_2]

### Confiabilidade Intercodificador

O protocolo adota o **α de Krippendorff** (nível ordinal, para escalas 0–3) como métrica primária de concordância, com limiar aceitável de α ≥ 0.667 e desejável de α ≥ 0.800, conforme o padrão estabelecido por Krippendorff para análise de conteúdo. O design prevê uma amostra piloto de 30 itens estratificados por país e suporte, codificados independentemente por dois codificadores, com um intervalo intra-codificador de ≥14 dias para teste de estabilidade temporal . Discordâncias superiores a 1 ponto na escala são registradas no log de discordância e desencadeiam revisão do codebook.[^12_3][^12_4]

### Triangulação por Tipo de Fonte

A triangulação segue a tipologia clássica de Denzin (triangulação de dados, metodológica e teórica) e exige composições mínimas conforme o tipo de claim . Uma claim factual simples precisa de apenas 1 fonte catalográfica, mas uma claim interpretativa nuclear — como "o regime iconocrático produz feminilidade estatal" — exige no mínimo 3 tipos de fonte: visual primária + literatura teórica + literatura empírica + confirmação em ≥2 países . O registro de triangulação já inclui 8 claims preenchidas com fontes reais, das quais 5 atingiram status T3+ (triangulação plena) e 1 permanece em T1, identificada como lacuna prioritária .

### Verificação Comparativa Entre Países

A matriz de verificação entre países testa cada um dos 5 mecanismos centrais (contrato sexual visual, regime iconocrático, feminilidade estatal, purificação, reativação em crise) em pelo menos 2 países de teste e 1 país de controle negativo . O achado mais significativo até agora é o **contraste Alemanha × França**: o *Münzgesetz* de 1871 exclui alegorias femininas da moeda alemã (controle negativo confirmado), enquanto a França institucionaliza Marianne em moeda de circulação massiva. Isso demonstra que o contrato sexual visual opera por caminhos distintos conforme a cultura constitucional, fortalecendo a especificidade do argumento.[^12_5][^12_6]

### Verificação Texto-Imagem (Panofsky)

Cada item do corpus é submetido a uma checklist de consistência entre os três níveis de Panofsky: a interpretação iconológica (nível 3) nunca pode contradizer a descrição pré-iconográfica (nível 1), e cada salto interpretativo deve ser explicitamente justificado no campo `interpretation_flag` do coding sheet . Essa regra previne o risco de *over-interpretation* — um dos 11 riscos identificados no plano de trabalho.[^12_7]

## Log de Discordância

O template inclui um exemplo piloto preenchido (indicador PR no item FR-COIN-001), onde a diferença entre Codificador A (pontuação 3) e Codificador B (pontuação 2) levou a uma revisão da definição operacional de "purificação máxima" no codebook .

## Rastreador de Lacunas

O gap tracker identifica 7 lacunas ativas com prazos e planos de resolução. As 3 lacunas de prioridade alta são: (1) falta de referência empírica precisa para o mecanismo de purificação (Douglas, 1966 — excerto com página ainda não extraído), (2) piloto de confiabilidade intercodificador ainda não realizado (prazo: 14/04/2026), e (3) codificação piloto da Justiça de Ceschiatti (STF) para testar se o modernismo altera o grau de purificação .
