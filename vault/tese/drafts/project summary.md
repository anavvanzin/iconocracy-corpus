# Tropical Iconoclasm, Legal Literacy and the Crisis of the Economy of Images*\**[^1] 

# *Ana Vitória Vanzin Mendes\*\*[^2]*

*Abstract:* Legal literacy, as framed in this project, designates the capacity to read and interpret normative texts together with a historically acquired competence to recognise which images anchor authority and how they orient collective habits of seeing. Drawing on Aby Warburg’s notion of the Pathosformel, the research approaches recurrent female allegories—Justice, Republic, Nation—as charged visual formulas in which affects, political programmes and juridical expectations condense and circulate across different historical conjunctures. In dialogue with Marie-José Mondzain’s reflections on the political use of images, these allegories are understood as operative devices within the Brazilian legal imaginary, organising how legality is seen, trusted and navigated in public space. By tracing how the modern constitutional state depends on such »regimes of visibility« for the stabilisation of its legitimacy, the project situates Brazilian visual-legal culture within broader debates on the mediation of normativity across textual, spatial and iconographic media.  
*Keywords:* Iconoclasm, Brazil, Legal Literacy, Brazilian Republic, Visual Literacy.

1. ## *Background*

Legal historiography has, by convention, privileged the textual over the visual, directing its analytical rigour toward codes, statutes, and jurisprudence while treating the material and iconographic manifestations of law as marginal decoration rather than as sites of juridical meaning. Such a hierarchy, however, obscures the fact that law has always operated as a multisensory and multimodal enterprise, communicated through architecture, ritual, emblems, and allegorical figures that render normativity perceptible and believable. Recasting legal history as a continuous process of »translation of knowledge of normativity« across disparate media, in Thomas Duve’s sense, makes it possible to see legal literacy not merely as the capacity to decode written norms, historically restricted to a narrow clerisy, but as an infrastructural and visual competence: the historically learned ability to recognise, trust, and navigate legality through images, spatial arrangements, and material cues. In this light, the legitimacy of the modern constitutional state rests on a specific »regime of visibility«, in which, as Paolo Grossi suggests, the history of European law appears as a history of establishing order through forms of validation and appearance, where the *mise-en-scène* of justice—its architecture, ceremonies, and allegorical personifications—is as constitutive of legality as the verdict itself.  
This investigation brings into dialogue José Murilo de Carvalho’s account of the fragility of the Brazilian republican imaginary, Aby Warburg’s theory of migrating emotional formulas (Pathosformeln), and Valérie Hayaert’s analysis of the allegory of Justice to propose a distinct understanding of crisis as a failure of legal literacy. Legal literacy is defined here not as mere textual competence, but as a specifically visual capacity to distinguish between the icon—an image that mediates authority in a relational, open way—and the idol—an image reduced to an object of blind submission or annihilation. The storming of the Praça dos Três Poderes in Brasília on 8 January 2023, and in particular the assault on Alfredo Ceschiatti’s A Justiça (1961), marked a rupture in the visual continuity of the Brazilian republic and a violent renegotiation of its symbolic infrastructure. The graffiti “Perdeu, mané” (“You lost, fool”) across the statue’s chest functioned as an act of counter‑literacy that re-coded the allegorical figure from a universalising emblem of justice into a partisan actor within a contingent struggle, exposing the vulnerability of the state’s regime of images to appropriation and desecration. This episode thus becomes the fulcrum for a broader inquiry into how legality and legitimacy are staged, maintained, and destroyed through images, and how the republican project depends on cultivating the perceptual skills required to read these visual negotiations of authority.  
The architectural and theoretical specifications for such an analysis are established herein. It is posited that legal literacy must be redefined as an »infrastructural and visual competence«—the historically situated capacity to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material cues, spatial arrangements, and iconographic systems (Goodrich 2013; Stern, Del Mar, and Meyler 2020). Modern legal orders, despite their claims to be impersonal, code-based, and bureaucratic, have historically depended on dense iconographic networks to make their authority legible. From the French Revolution’s reinvention of sovereignty to the high modernist construction of Brasília, the abstract principles of the state have been consistently embodied in the female form. The central paradox to be explored is the »visual sexual contract« (Pateman 1988). Precisely during the historical periods when women were most systematically excluded from political agency, civil capacity, and the legal profession, the female body proliferated as the omnipresent official face of the Law and the Nation. Figures such as *Marianne*, *Britannia*, *Germania*, and Ceschiatti’s *A Justiça* functioned as conduits for legal knowledge, teaching the public »what law looks like« while simultaneously masking the gendered exclusions of the actual legal order. The reconstruction of this background knowledge is sought, tracing the genealogy of the female allegory from the 18th-century revolutionary Atlantic to the vandalized modernist plazas of 21st-century Brazil.  
The discipline of legal historiography has, by convention, accorded primacy to the textual modality over the visual, concentrating its analytical rigor upon the exegesis of codes, statutes, and jurisprudence, whilst relegating the material and iconographic manifestations of the law to the subordinate status of mere illustration. Notwithstanding this established tradition, a comprehensive apprehension of juridical phenomena necessitates a departure from strictly textual analysis to conceive of legal history as a continuous process of the »translation of knowledge of normativity« across disparate media (Duve 2022). Within this analytical procedure, legal literacy is understood as a form of infrastructural competence: the historically constituted faculty to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through images, spatial arrangements, and material cues. The legitimacy of the modern constitutional state is predicated upon a specific »regime of visibility«. As observed by Paolo Grossi, the history of European law may be understood as a history of establishing order through specific forms of validation and appearance (Grossi 2010), where the *mise-en-scène* of justice is as critical as the verdict itself.  
It is argued that the transition from the *Ancien Régime* to the modern nation-state—spanning the long period from 1789 to 1945—engendered a fundamental rupture in the metaphysics of sovereignty. Where political authority was once incarnated in the physical body of the monarch, the revolutions of the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries necessitated a new vessel for the abstraction of »the people«. The desacralization of the King's body created a terrifying representational vacuum that required immediate filling to ensure the continuity of obedience. Modern legal orders, while presenting themselves as impersonal and code-based, simultaneously relied upon dense iconographic systems to render sovereignty and adjudication publicly legible. Paradoxically, as the emerging republics of Europe and the Americas systematically excluded women from the rights of active citizenship, the female form was appropriated as the primary visual interface of the state (Agulhon 1981; Landes 2001). From the barricades of Paris to the plazas of Rio de Janeiro, the abstraction of the State was rendered visible through the allegorical female body—*Marianne*, *Germania*, *Britannia*, and the *Alegoria da República*—condensing constitutional claims into stable attributes and postures. This was not merely decorative but foundational: the female allegory provided a body that could be loved and died for, without threatening the patriarchal monopoly on actual political power held by men.  
This constitutive contradiction is identified as the »Visual Sexual Contract« (Pateman 1988). The ubiquity of the female allegory functioned as a technology of political legitimacy that stabilized the fraternal social contract while offering a symbolic compensation for political exclusion. In the specific context of Brazil, this phenomenon acquires distinct dimensions. Following the proclamation of the Republic in 1889, a »symbolic deficit« emerged, necessitating the rapid fabrication of a republican imaginary to replace the imperial iconography (Carvalho 1990). The resulting »Tropical Iconoclasm« juxtaposes the positivist »Religion of Humanity«—which elevated the female allegory to the status of a deity—with a juridical reality that, under the Civil Code of 1916, maintained married women in a state of relative incapacity. The allegory thus functioned less as a »Mother« and more as a »Step-Mother«—a distant, Europeanized figure promising justice but delivering exclusion to the marginalized populations described by Carvalho as »bestialized« spectators of the regime. This disconnect created a »schizophrenic« visual order where the populace was trained to revere an image of Justice that systematically concealed the reality of their own disenfranchisement.  
The insurrectionary events of January 8, 2023, in Brasília constituted a violent rupture in this established visual order. When a radicalized mob breached the *Praça dos Três Poderes* and defaced the symbols of the Supreme Federal Court (STF), a violent renegotiation of the state’s visual infrastructure was enacted. The graffiti scrawled across the statue’s chest is interpreted not merely as an act of vandalism, but as a demonstration of »counter-literacy« (Mendes 2024). This brutal reading stripped the allegorical figure of her universal pretensions, reducing her to a partisan combatant in a localized political struggle. By writing a specific, vulgar phrase attributed to a sitting justice onto the timeless body of the allegory, the rioters collapsed the distinction between the office and the person, destroying the transcendence essential for legal authority. This event serves as the fulcrum for the research, necessitating an investigation into how the »administrative economy of the visible« (*oikonomia*)—which manages the relationship between invisible authority and its visible manifestations (Mondzain 2005)—is currently under siege by a rising »iconocracy« of the digital mob (Baudrillard 1994), which demands the immediate, unmediated presence of power.

## Research Questions & Hypothesis

The investigation is structured around three principal lines of inquiry designed to bridge the lacuna between high legal theory and the material history of the Global South. The first line of inquiry interrogates the constitution of the Visual Sexual Contract within the Brazilian Republic. It asks in what manner the »administrative economy« (*oikonomia*) of the state constructs legal authority visually, and specifically, by what means the female allegory (Themis/Marianne) was utilized to obfuscate the exclusion of women and racial minorities from the »fraternal social contract« (Pateman 1988). It further examines how the circulation of these images on material supports—such as currency, stamps, and public statuary—functioned to train the »legal literacy« of the population, creating a habitus of obedience to an abstract state personified by a figure denied civil capacity. This inquiry seeks to understand if the proliferation of the female form served as a compensatory mechanism, a »noble lie« in the Platonic sense, that allowed the state to claim universal representation while practicing particularistic exclusion.  
The second line of inquiry focuses on the nature of the »Negative Legal Literacy« demonstrated during the insurrection of January 8\. It questions how, if the rioters were indeed »illiterate« regarding the textual nuances of constitutional law, they nonetheless possessed the »iconological competence« requisite to target specific artifacts with such liturgical precision. The research seeks to determine whether the destruction of the *Sphragis* (Coat of Arms), the *Cathedra* (Justices' chairs), and the *Eikon* (Statue of Justice) reveals a sophisticated, albeit pathological, understanding of the »loci« of sovereignty. Specifically, it investigates the »magical literacy« implied by the theft of the Coat of Arms, wherein the mob appeared to believe that possessing the physical seal equated to »decertifying« the court’s authority. This suggests a reversion to a pre-modern understanding of the image, where the symbol *is* the power it represents, rather than a mere signifier. The selective nature of the violence—ignoring the cafeteria to destroy the Plenary—indicates a precise understanding of the spatial grammar of power.  
Finally, the third line of inquiry employs the concept of »Tropical Iconoclasm« to elucidate the collision between the analog authority of the State and the digital »iconocracy« of the mob. It questions whether the destruction of the *eikon* signifies a demand for the »idol«—the unmediated leader—and whether the vandalism represents a rejection of the »interval« of judgment required by the *oikonomia* (Mondzain 2005\) in favor of the immediate, totalizing visibility of the authoritarian image. In the digital age, the patience required for legal mediation is replaced by the demand for instant gratification and total transparency. The smashing of the glass façades of the STF can be read as a physical manifestation of this demand to expose the »black box« of the judiciary. This inquiry extends to the cultural sphere, examining the slashing of Di Cavalcanti's mural *As Mulatas* as a rejection of the modernist project of a mixed-race civilization in favor of a reactionary purity, revealing the racial dimensions of the insurrectionist aesthetic.  
It is hypothesized that the violence of January 8 constituted not merely a security failure but an epistemological clash between two opposing regimes of visibility. The modern State relies upon the »kenosis« (emptying) of the image to create an »interval« for judgment; the image of Justice is blindfolded precisely to mark this separation between the visible and the invisible (Mondzain 2005). The mob, habituated by the simulacra of social media (Baudrillard 1994), is understood to have rejected this interval as »opacity«, demanding instead the »total visibility« of the »idol«. The vandalism of *A Justiça*—specifically the inscription of »Perdeu, mané« upon her form—is interpreted as a ritual attempt to collapse the »Two Bodies of the Law« (Kantorowicz 1957). By inscribing a partisan quote from a specific magistrate onto the universal body of the allegory, the rioters forced the »Body Natural« of the judge to merge with the »Body Politic« of the state, thereby destroying the latter's transcendence. Thus, the crisis of Brazilian democracy is fundamentally diagnosed as a crisis of its *oikonomia*, where the »magical literacy« of the rioters successfully identified and dismantled the sacramental infrastructure of the Republic.

## Methodology

A transdisciplinary methodology combining Legal Iconology, Forensic Architecture, and Political Theology is employed. The research moves beyond the exegesis of texts to the forensic examination of the »scene of the crime« of representation. The analysis concentrates upon a specific corpus of legal artifacts identified as »nodes« of the republican *oikonomia*. These objects are treated not as passive decorations but as active agents in the translation of normativity. Primarily, the research examines monetary iconography, analyzing coins and banknotes from the First Republic (1889) to the present (Real) to trace the evolution of the female effigy and the »Visual Sexual Contract« in mass-circulated media. This analysis reveals how the state inserts its image into the daily economic transactions of its citizens, naturalizing its authority through the haptic experience of currency. Secondarily, it addresses monumental programs, specifically the statuary of the *Praça dos Três Poderes* and Alfredo Ceschiatti’s *A Justiça* (1961), analyzing its spatial relationship to the *Palácio do Planalto* and the *Congresso Nacional*. The positioning of these monuments creates a liturgical space that directs the citizen's gaze and body, enforcing a choreography of respect and submission. Furthermore, the inquiry extends to legal paratexts, examining the visual framing of authority through the *Sphragis* (Coat of Arms/Seal of the Republic) found on documents and the Zalszupin furniture (*Cathedra*) destroyed in the plenary.  
Crucially, the methodology integrates forensic data from the *Lacorpi* project (Laboratório de Conservação e Restauração de Pintura e Escultura). This involves the utilization of technical reports detailing the »tintas, vernizes e facadas« (paints, varnishes, and stabs) found on the vandalized artifacts (Pena, Ferreira, and Bordini 2025). This forensic evidence provides the material basis for understanding the physical violence enacted against the allegorical body. For instance, the specific targeting of the Zalszupin chairs is analyzed as an act of »uprooting the hierarchy«, a ritual of leveling the vertical space of judgment. By physically tearing the seats of judgment from the floor, the mob attempted to democratize the space through destruction, asserting a horizontal power against the vertical authority of the court. Similarly, the »Perdeu, mané« graffiti is subjected to a »forensic hermeneutics«, interpreted as a profane captioning that repurposes the *eikon* into a ventriloquist's dummy for political rage. The choice of red lipstick or paint to mark the female body of the statue also invokes a gendered violence, reenacting the patriarchal domination inherent in the »Visual Sexual Contract«.  
The analytical procedures follow the Warburgian method, treating the vandalized images as *Pathosformeln* (formulas of pathos). The »survival« (*Nachleben*) of the classical Themis into the modernist Brazilian context will be traced, and the manner in which the »energy« of the symbol was inverted by the mob will be analyzed (Warburg 1999; Becker 2013). This approach allows us to see the riot not as a singular event, but as the latest eruption of long-dormant iconographic tensions. This is complemented by an »Anatomy of Allegory«, using the framework of Valérie Hayaert and Marina Warner to deconstruct the »Visual Sexual Contract«. This entails a comparative analysis of the idealized female allegory in public art versus the status of women in the Brazilian Civil Code (1916 and 2002), highlighting the dissonance between the »Mother-Republic« and the »minor-woman« (Hayaert forthcoming; Warner 1985). Finally, the analysis of the *Lacorpi* restoration efforts—specifically the debate between erasure and preservation of scars—is framed as a theological-political operation to reconstitute the broken *oikonomia*, transforming the »Temple« of the law into a »Museum of Survival«. The decision to keep certain scars acknowledges the vulnerability of the legal order, moving away from the myth of eternal, untouchable authority.  
Regarding potential problems, the subjective nature of visual interpretation and the risk of over-reading specific acts of vandalism pose significant challenges. To mitigate this, iconological analysis is rigorously grounded in the empirical data of legal history—such as statutes and parliamentary debates regarding symbols—and the hard forensic reports of the *Lacorpi* project (Pena, Ferreira, and Bordini 2025), ensuring that visual readings are corroborated by material and textual evidence. Additionally, the theoretical density of »iconocracy« (Mondzain) when applied to a contemporary secular event in the Global South requires careful handling. This is addressed through the rigorous adaptation of theological categories (*eikon*, *idol*, *oikonomia*) to the secular jurisprudence of the Brazilian STF, treating the Court as the »economist« of the republican image and validating the theoretical transfer through the work of constitutional scholars like Paulo Grossi (2010) and Thomas Duve (2022). This ensures that the analysis remains firmly rooted in legal history while benefiting from the explanatory power of political theology.
