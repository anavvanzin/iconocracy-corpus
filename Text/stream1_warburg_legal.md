## 1. State of the Field

The application of Aby Warburg's methodology to legal, juridical, and state iconography remains a largely unrealized project. The field of "law and the visual" — consolidated in the late 1990s through foundational volumes such as Douzinas and Nead's *Law and the Image* (University of Chicago Press, 1999) and advanced by Goodrich's *Legal Emblems and the Art of Law* (Cambridge University Press, 2013) — has developed in productive parallel with Warburgian *Bildwissenschaft*, but the two bodies of scholarship seldom speak directly to each other. Legal iconographers such as Peter Goodrich, Costas Douzinas, Judith Resnik, and Valérie Hayaert operate within traditions of legal rhetoric, emblematics, and institutional history that share Warburg's concern with the affective power of images but almost never invoke his conceptual apparatus by name: *Pathosformel*, *Nachleben*, *Denkraum*, *Bilderfahrzeug* or the "iconology of the interval." On the Warburgian side, the reception of the *Mnemosyne Atlas* by figures such as Giorgio Agamben, Georges Didi-Huberman, Horst Bredekamp, and Philippe-Alain Michaud has generated a rich political iconology that touches the edges of juridical imagery — particularly in studies of sovereignty (Bredekamp on Hobbes's *Leviathan*), of the female national allegory (Colleen Becker on Germania), and of the "politics of images" (Didi-Huberman) — without producing a sustained Warburgian reading of Justitia, Libertas, Marianne, Britannia, or Columbia. Igor Stramignoni's 2024 article "Nachleben der Antike, Time, and Restitution: Notes for a Nocturnal Jurisprudence of the Image" (*Law and Critique*) is the most explicit and recent attempt to bridge the gap, situating Warburg's *Pathosformeln* directly within a theory of juridical image-experience. Hayaert's *Lady Justice: An Anatomy of Allegory* (Edinburgh University Press, 2020) deploys gestural, affective, and mnemonic analysis close in spirit to Warburg's methods — though without citing the *Atlas* — offering the richest existing resource for a thesis aiming at a fully Warburgian legal iconology. The gap between the two scholarly traditions constitutes a highly productive lacuna for doctoral research.

## 2. Core Direct Applications (Warburg → Law/State Iconography)

### 2.1 Igor Stramignoni — "Nocturnal Jurisprudence of the Image"

**Full citation:** Igor Stramignoni, "Nachleben der Antike, Time, and Restitution: Notes for a Nocturnal Jurisprudence of the Image," *Law and Critique* 35, no. 2 (March 2024): 445–482. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/s10978-023-09371-4

**Author affiliation:** LSE Law School. Profile: https://lse-uk.academia.edu/UmbertoIgorStramignoni

**What the article argues:** Stramignoni takes Warburg's 1905 Hamburg lecture "Dürer and Italian Antiquity" as a founding text for a "nocturnal jurisprudence of the image," arguing that *Pathosformeln* embedded in the allegory of *Ivstitia* — the female figure with scales, sword, and blindfold — carry dormant affective energies from antiquity (Livia's coins, Bamberg bas-relief c. 1250, the sixteenth-century blindfold) that were never neutralized by modernization. Drawing on Warburg's concept of images as "crystals of historical memory" (via Agamben), Didi-Huberman's call for the "restitution" of images to the public sphere, and Michel Serres's "thousand nocturnal flashes," Stramignoni argues that a juridical encounter with these *Pathosformeln* requires recognizing their non-representational *presence* — their capacity to reactivate sacrificial and violent energies encoded in antiquity. The essay explicitly applies *Nachleben* to juridical allegory, and is the closest existing convergence of Warburg and legal iconography.

**Direct quotation (from abstract/PhilPapers):** "In this essay, I take my cue from Aby Warburg and the Pathosformeln that, he suggested, can be glimpsed through certain material objects." ([PhilPapers](https://philpapers.org/rec/STRNDA-4))

**Confidence:** High (peer-reviewed journal, article indexed at PhilPapers and Springer; DOI verified).

### 2.2 Colleen Becker — "Pathosformel as Methodological Paradigm" (Germania)

**Full citation:** Colleen Becker, "Aby Warburg's Pathosformel as Methodological Paradigm," *Journal of Art Historiography* 9 (December 2013): 1–23. URL: https://arthistoriography.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/becker.pdf; DOAJ: https://doaj.org/article/58b051219d61444cb8171e5ebcc44df4

**What the article argues:** Becker demonstrates the cross-disciplinary applicability of *Pathosformel* by using the national personification *Germania* as a case study — one of the paradigmatic examples of female civic allegory. Germania, modeled after Athena Parthenos, shares her morphological heritage with Britannia, Polonia, Marianne, and Italia Turrita. Becker shows that Germania functions as a *Pathosformel* precisely because she encodes "emotionally charged figurations of antique origin that appear as manifestations of communal crisis" (p. 16). Crucially, Becker demonstrates that Warburg himself intended to include civic and political personifications in the *Bilderatlas*: the letter of 16 May 1927 to Fritz Saxl confirms Warburg planned panels showing "specimens of revival of Antiquity serving a practical political purpose," including postage stamps featuring Semeuse (linked to Marianne) and others (p. 23). *Tafel 77* of the *Atlas* includes Victoria, coinage, and other state imagery.

**Direct quotation:** "Germania gave physical form to a collective crisis surrounding the promise and prospect of assimilation within a newly minted German state... she was an allegory that signaled socio-political conflicts and incongruities, both internal and external, rather than a singular and static vision of national unity" (p. 10, 19).

**Confidence:** High (open-access, peer-reviewed journal; article downloaded and read).

### 2.3 Valérie Hayaert — *Lady Justice: An Anatomy of Allegory*

**Full citation:** Valérie Hayaert, *Lady Justice: An Anatomy of Allegory*. Edinburgh: Edinburgh University Press, 2020. URL: https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-lady-justice.html

**What the book argues:** Hayaert's monograph is the most sustained iconographic analysis of Justitia published in the twenty-first century. It analyzes thousands of images from 1400–1600 across six countries, decomposing the allegorical body of Lady Justice organ by organ, limb by limb — an approach methodologically convergent with Warburg's gestural analysis of the Nymph, Fortuna, and the maenad. Hayaert identifies Justitia as "the seat of law's conscience, an archetype of the judge's *daimon*, and an affective, numinous address" — language resonant with *Pathosformel*. While Hayaert does not explicitly deploy Warburgian terminology, her analytical procedure — tracing gestural *Pathosformeln* across the allegorical body of Justice, analyzing scales-sword-gaze as a triangular physiological scheme — constitutes an implicit Warburgian iconology of legal allegory. Hayaert co-edited (with Goodrich) *Genealogies of Legal Vision* (Routledge, 2015), situating her work in the mainstream of visual jurisprudence.

**Direct quotation (from publisher):** "In Justice's gestures, postures, choreography, her contradictions and enigmas, we find both 'the monstrosity of human judgement' and a longing to repair law's estrangement from justice." (Edinburgh University Press page)

**Confidence:** High (publisher page read; book reviewed in secondary literature).

### 2.4 Judith Resnik and Dennis Curtis — *Representing Justice*

**Full citation:** Judith Resnik and Dennis Curtis, *Representing Justice: Invention, Controversy, and Rights in City-States and Democratic Courtrooms*. New Haven: Yale University Press, 2011, pp. 668, ISBN 9780300110968. URL: https://documents.law.yale.edu/representing-justice

**What the book argues:** The most comprehensive English-language survey of Justitia's iconographic history in relation to legal institutions. Resnik and Curtis trace the female allegory of Justice from Renaissance Italian city-states through the architecture and statuary of democratic courthouses worldwide, with over 220 images. Crucially, they theorize the "rites-to-rights" transformation: showing how civic iconography of Justice functioned as legitimating apparatus for state power, surviving and mutating across centuries. They ask whether the icon of Justice — a white woman with scales, sword, and blindfold — is adequate to democratic representation, noting only one depiction of Justice as a woman of color in plain view in any federal courthouse (p. 121). The book's methodology — tracing the *longue durée* survival of an allegorical figure across radically changed contexts — is structurally Warburgian without invoking the concept of *Nachleben*. A Warburgian reading would add interpretive depth to Resnik and Curtis's institutional analysis by theorizing the affective energies (*Pathosformeln*) encoded in the blindfold, the sword, and the scales as surviving antiquity.

**Direct quotation:** "Justice survived and thrived as a legitimizing aspect of state power... she became a valuable marketing tool, so to speak." (book review, *Journal of Legal Education*: https://jle.aals.org/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1047&context=home)

**Confidence:** High (book widely reviewed; Yale Law Library site confirmed; author affiliations verified).

## 3. Indirect / Adjacent Applications (Political Iconography, Bildwissenschaft with Legal Resonance)

### 3.1 Horst Bredekamp — *Leviathan: Body Politic as Visual Strategy in the Work of Thomas Hobbes*

**Full citation:** Horst Bredekamp, *Leviathan: Body Politic as Visual Strategy in the Work of Thomas Hobbes*. Translated, edited and adapted by Elizabeth Clegg. Berlin/Boston: De Gruyter, 2020 (revised from German 1st ed. *Thomas Hobbes Visuelle Strategien*, Berlin: Akademie Verlag, 1999; 3rd German ed. 2006). Scribd preview: https://www.scribd.com/document/671448690/

**What the work argues:** Bredekamp is the closest heir to Warburg in contemporary German *Bildwissenschaft*. His study of Hobbes's *Leviathan* frontispiece (1651) established what he calls *political iconography* — the analysis of sovereign images as active political agents, not mere illustrations. The frontispiece, depicting the body politic as a composite artificial man formed by the consent of citizens, constitutes a visual *Pathosformel* of sovereignty: it crystallizes the antique traditions of the *body politic* (corpus mysticum, Psalms, Aesop), the automaton, and the Roman triumph. Bredekamp does not cite Warburg directly in this work but operates in the Warburgian tradition of tracing the Nachleben of classical forms in state imagery. His broader *Bildakt* theory (the image as performing action on beholders) provides the closest available theoretical bridge between Warburg's *Pathosformel* and the juridical force of legal iconography. As one review notes: "Bredekamp and Ginzburg speak with regard to the frontispiece of *political iconography*." ([Cartographies of the Absolute](https://cartographiesoftheabsolute.wordpress.com/2015/11/16/images-of-state-and-stasis/))

**Confidence:** High (book read via Scribd extract and multiple secondary reviews; DOI/OCLC confirmed).

### 3.2 Georges Didi-Huberman — *The Surviving Image* and *Atlas*

**Full citations:**  
(a) Georges Didi-Huberman, *The Surviving Image: Phantoms of Time and Time of Phantoms: Aby Warburg's History of Art*. Trans. Harvey Mendelsohn. University Park: Penn State University Press, 2016 (French orig. *L'Image survivante*, Paris: Minuit, 2002). URL: https://www.psupress.org/books/titles/978-0-271-07208-1.html  
(b) Georges Didi-Huberman, *Atlas ou le gai savoir inquiet: L'œil de l'histoire, 3*. Paris: Minuit, 2011 (exhibition catalog and theoretical essay for "Atlas" exhibition, 2010–2011, Karlsruhe/Hamburg/Madrid).

**What the works argue:** *The Surviving Image* is the most authoritative philosophical reconstruction of Warburg's method. Didi-Huberman argues that Warburg constructed a "phantasmal model" of history based on "hauntings, 'survivals,' residues, and the persistent return of forms" — temporal strata rather than linear chronology. For Didi-Huberman, Warburg's images are "not representations but presences," carriers of affective energy that survive intact across epochs. This theoretical framework has direct juridical applications: the *Pathosformel* of Justitia's scales and sword can be read as exactly this kind of "hauntological" presence — ancient sacrificial and decisional violence surviving in the most rationalized icon of modern law. *Atlas* extends this to a political philosophy of images: Didi-Huberman reads the *Bilderatlas* as a political device for exposing the hidden forces of domination encoded in cultural memory. He situates Warburg alongside Walter Benjamin, proposing that the "dialectical image" is inseparable from a politics of knowledge. While never directly addressing Justitia or Marianne, this framework is the most immediately mobilizable for an "Iconocracia" project.

**Direct quotation:** "For the natural model of cycles of 'life and death' and 'greatness and decline,' Warburg substituted a resolutely non-natural, symbolic model, a cultural model of history in which temporal periods are no longer fashioned according to biomorphic stages, but, instead, are expressed by strata, hybrid blocks, rhizomes, specific complexities, by returns that are often unexpected and goals that are always thwarted." (*The Surviving Image*, Penn State UP page)

**Confidence:** High (publisher pages read; book widely reviewed in peer-reviewed journals).

### 3.3 Peter Goodrich — *Legal Emblems and the Art of Law* and *Genealogies of Legal Vision*

**Full citations:**  
(a) Peter Goodrich, *Legal Emblems and the Art of Law: Obiter Depicta as the Vision of Governance*. New York: Cambridge University Press, 2013. ISBN 978-1-107-03599-7. URL: https://aidel.it/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/2013_Goodrich-_Legal_Emblems_and_the_Art_of_Law2.pdf  
(b) Peter Goodrich and Valérie Hayaert, eds., *Genealogies of Legal Vision*. London: Routledge, 2015. ISBN 9780415749060. URL: https://www.taylorfrancis.com/books/edit/10.4324/9781315774268/

**What the works argue:** Goodrich argues that the emblem book, invented by the humanist lawyer Andrea Alciato in 1531, constitutes the founding genre of "visual jurisprudence" — a genre dominated in authorship and content by lawyers who understood that "the image... is itself a form of legislation." His concept of *visiocracy* — the governance of vision — is structurally analogous to Warburg's political iconology: both theorize images as normative forces conditioning subjects' cognitive and affective orientations. Goodrich explicitly traces the survival of classical visual forms in legal emblems (the figure of Justitia with scales, Justice as Janus-faced deity, etc.) and shows how these *Nachleben* structures continue to organize contemporary legal subjectivity. He does not cite Warburg, but describes the unconscious archive of legal images as a "prior picture of a judge and court" stored in the "image archive that Freud calls the unconscious" — a formulation structurally identical to Warburg's "Mnemosynic" cultural memory. *Genealogies of Legal Vision* (co-edited with Hayaert) explicitly addresses "the genealogy of law's visual structures" and includes Resnik and Curtis as discussion partners.

**Direct quotation:** "These prior images, these imaginary representations of legality, are modern day and subsisting emblems directing vision and establishing sites of recognition that correspond remarkably closely to the legal tradition of figures of justice." (*Legal Emblems*, p. xxi — frontmatter PDF)

**Confidence:** High (frontmatter PDF and Scribd preview directly read; publisher confirmed).

### 3.4 Costas Douzinas — "Prosopon and Antiprosopon: Prolegomena for a Legal Iconology" and "The Legality of the Image"

**Full citations:**  
(a) Costas Douzinas, "Prosopon and Antiprosopon: Prolegomena for a Legal Iconology," in Costas Douzinas and Lynda Nead, eds., *Law and the Image: The Authority of Art and the Aesthetics of Law*, pp. 36–67. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1999. ISBN 9780226569543. URL: https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/L/bo3622830.html  
(b) Costas Douzinas, "The Legality of the Image," *The Modern Law Review* 63, no. 6 (2000): 813–830. URL: https://www.scribd.com/document/521499317/

**What the works argue:** Douzinas explicitly calls for a "legal iconology" — a systematic study of the image-law relationship — and argues that intimate links exist between political power, law, and images that both iconophilic and iconoclastic traditions have shaped. His concept of *antiprosopon* (the gaze-returned-from-the-image) echoes Warburg's theory of the *Pathosformel* as a force that acts on the viewer rather than merely representing a subject. Douzinas traces the history of legal governance of images from Byzantine theological disputes through Reformation iconoclasm to contemporary visual jurisprudence. The essay in *Law and the Image* positions Douzinas alongside Martin Jay, Peter Goodrich, and Georges Didi-Huberman — who is a contributor to the same volume — creating a direct bridge between law/image studies and Warburgian iconology. Douzinas cites Pierre Legendre's *Dieu au miroir* (Paris: Fayard, 1994) in connection with the governance of images (footnote 11), and invokes W.J.T. Mitchell's *Iconology* — signaling awareness of the image-theory canon adjacent to Warburg.

**Direct quotation:** "The law understands the importance of the governance of images for the maintenance of the social bond and helps organise a regime of permitted images and forbidden idols which amounts to a complex legal administration of aesthetics and a related aesthetic organisation of law." (*The Legality of the Image*, Scribd)

**Confidence:** High (article read via Scribd; volume confirmed at University of Chicago Press).

### 3.5 Cornelia Vismann — *Files: Law and Media Technology* and *Bildregime des Rechts*

**Full citations:**  
(a) Cornelia Vismann, *Files: Law and Media Technology*. Trans. Geoffrey Winthrop-Young. Stanford: Stanford University Press, 2008 (German orig. *Akten*, Frankfurt: Fischer, 2000). Google Books: https://books.google.com/books/about/Files.html?id=0lAOAQAAMAAJ  
(b) Cornelia Vismann and Thomas Weitin, eds., *Bildregime des Rechts* [Visual Regimes of Law]. Stuttgart: Clett-Kotta, 2008. [Reference confirmed from Scribd document: https://www.scribd.com/document/916548874/Vismann-Image-and-Law-a-Troubled-Relationship-55]  
(c) Cornelia Vismann, "Image and Law — a Troubled Relationship," in *Bildregime des Rechts*, pp. 55ff. (included in the Scribd document above).

**What the works argue:** Vismann's media-theoretical approach to law analyzes legal artifacts — files, tables, courtrooms — as *cultural techniques* that precede and condition legal subjects. While *Files* does not engage Warburgian iconology directly, her posthumous volume *Medien der Rechtsprechung* (Frankfurt: Fischer, 2011) develops a theory of the juridical theater and the agonal legal spectacle that converges with Warburg's interest in the Dionysian triumphal procession and the performative power of legal ceremony. *Bildregime des Rechts* (co-edited with Weitin) directly addresses the "visual regimes of law," covering topics from courtroom architecture and judicial ceremonialism to legal portraiture — the closest German legal scholarship comes to a systematic Bildwissenschaft of law. Vismann's essay "Image and Law — a Troubled Relationship" is the key text.

**Confidence:** Medium for *Bildregime des Rechts* (cited in secondary literature, Scribd entry confirmed, full text not verified); High for *Files* (widely cited, Google Books confirmed).

### 3.6 Pierre Legendre — Legal Anthropology and the Dogmatic Image

**Full citation:** Pierre Legendre, *Dieu au miroir: Étude sur l'institution des images*. Paris: Fayard, 1994; and the series *Leçons* (9 vols., Fayard, 1983–1999). The essay *Leçons I: La 901e conclusion — Étude sur le théâtre de la Raison* is the most relevant volume.

**What the works argue:** Legendre developed an "anthropologie dogmatique" arguing that law is a fundamentally theatrical and image-mediated institution: the *architecture dogmatique* of Western societies depends on representational fictions and spectacular mise-en-scène. His concept of the *institution des images* (the institutional role of images in constituting subjects through identification with normative representations) is structurally homologous with Warburg's account of *Pathosformeln* as mnemic forces that shape collective identity. *Dieu au miroir* analyzes the image of the divine in relation to legal authority — approaching the theological-juridical roots of sovereignty iconography that Warburg traced in the medieval and early modern traditions. Douzinas explicitly cites Legendre alongside Mitchell on the governance of images. Legendre died in 2023 at age 92.

**Confidence:** Medium (cited authoritatively in Douzinas, Erudit.org review, and secondary literature; primary texts not directly read in this research).

### 3.7 Desmond Manderson — *Danse Macabre: Temporalities of Law in the Visual Arts*

**Full citation:** Desmond Manderson, *Danse Macabre: Temporalities of Law in the Visual Arts*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2019. ISBN 9781107158665. URL: https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/danse-macabre/BD8A5C8C7E4549556FAE23FAEBFDED3C

**What the book argues:** Manderson performs close readings of seven major artworks in relation to law and temporality — including Bruegel's *Justicia*, Reynolds's *Justice*, Klimt's *Jurisprudence* panels, and Cauduro's murals in the Mexican Supreme Court. His temporal analysis of Klimt's lost *Jurisprudence* (Chapter 5) directly addresses the allegorical body of Justitia in its suspended/violated form, reading it through Agamben's "state of exception" and Warburg's idea of *surviving images* (though Warburg is not named). The book's general claim — that "images are the sites of law's theory, praxis and critique" — aligns with a Warburgian approach that treats images as condensed cultural energies. A reviewer notes that Manderson's method involves "a close and extended reading of his chosen artworks... through the innovative methodologies he uses to unpack them," including psychoanalytical, hermeneutic, and phenomenological approaches to art history.

**Direct quotation:** "The visual and spectacular are indispensable elements of how we come to know and are known by politics, law, and regulation" (p. 16, cited in Brunon-Ernst review: https://journals.openedition.org/etudes-benthamiennes/5333)

**Confidence:** High (Cambridge Core entry confirmed; multiple peer-reviewed reviews read).

## 4. Gender-Aware Warburgian Work on Civic/Legal Allegory

### 4.1 Colleen Becker on Germania as Pathosformel (see §2.2 above)

Becker's article explicitly connects the Warburgian *Pathosformel* to the female national personification across Western nation-states: "The Athena Parthenos provided a clear antecedent to eighteenth- and nineteenth-century icons like Britannia, Polonia, Marianne, Italia Turrita, and Germania. They are all crowned with symbolic headdress, are frequently armed or carrying shields, and they were costumed in Greco-Roman-inspired togas" (p. 13). The shared matrix of the "virgin warrior" goddess — distinct from earth-mother deities — unites the legal-civic allegories of Justitia with the national personifications; both derive their formal coherence from the same classical Pathosformel of Athena/Victory.

### 4.2 Sigrid Schade and Silke Wenk — Gender and Bildwissenschaft

**Full citation (joint publication):** Sigrid Schade and Silke Wenk, "Inszenierungen des Sehens: Kunst, Geschichte und Geschlechterdifferenz," in *Genus: Zur Geschlechterdifferenz in den Kulturwissenschaften*, eds. Hadumod Bussmann and Renate Hof. Stuttgart: Kröner, 2005, pp. 144–184.

**Relevant works:**  
Sigrid Schade, "Bildwissenschaft — a new 'discipline' and the absence of women," in *Inscriptions/Transgressions: Kunstgeschichte und Gender Studies*, eds. Imesch et al. Emsdetten/Berlin, 2008, pp. 31–51. PDF: https://blog.zhdk.ch/sigridschade/files/2013/07/Billwissenschaft_and_the_absence_of_women_27_7_09_000.pdf

**What the works argue:** Schade and Wenk provide the critical feminist intervention in German *Bildwissenschaft*, arguing that the male-dominated "iconic turn" debate (Boehm, Bredekamp, Belting) systematically excluded women scholars and the gender-analytical contributions of feminist art history. Schade's essay specifically critiques the way *Bildwissenschaft* reintroduces essentialist categories of the body and image without engaging twenty-five years of feminist work on gendered representation. For the "Iconocracia" project, this critique is indispensable: any Warburgian reading of the female civic allegory (Justitia, Marianne, Columbia) must account for the fact that the universal female body is deployed to represent abstract virtues (Justice, Liberty, Republic) in ways that erase actual women from legal and political subjecthood. Schade's critical framework — that the spectacular female body in civic imagery performs a normative function analogous to the *Pathosformel* — provides the feminist inflection missing from both Warburg and most legal iconographers.

**Confidence:** High (Schade PDF directly read; Schade/Wenk article confirmed in bibliography).

### 4.4 Maurice Agulhon and the Iconography of Marianne

**Reference:** Maurice Agulhon's multi-volume studies of Marianne as French republican symbol (including *Marianne au combat: L'imagerie et la symbolique républicaines de 1789 à 1880*, Paris: Flammarion, 1979; *Marianne au pouvoir*, Paris: Flammarion, 1989) constitute the founding scholarly treatment of this female civic allegory. Agulhon's approach — tracing the morphological survival of Libertas/Republic/Nation through successive political crises of French history — parallels Warburg's *Nachleben* method without the psycho-historical depth. A Warburgian reading of Marianne would add: (a) the analysis of her *Pathosformel* as antique survival; (b) the affective charge of the Phrygian cap, the tricolor, and the bare breast (as in Delacroix's *Liberty Leading the People*); (c) the interval between the serenely classical and the erotically charged-Dionysian poles Warburg identified in the Nymph.

**Confidence:** Medium (Agulhon works cited in secondary literature; Wikipedia/EHNE entries on Marianne confirm).

## 5. Gaps — Where Warburg Has NOT Been Systematically Applied to Legal Iconography

The following constitutes an argued gap supporting the doctoral project "Iconocracia":

**5.1 No systematic Warburgian reading of Justitia.** Despite Hayaert's implicit proximity to Warburgian method, neither she nor any legal iconographer has explicitly deployed *Pathosformel*, *Nachleben*, or *Denkraum* to analyze the iconographic survival of Justitia from Roman coins (*Iustitia Augusta*, minted under Vespasian) through medieval Christian virtue-theory to early modern emblem books and contemporary courthouse statuary. The specific Warburgian questions — What affective energies are crystalized in the blindfold? What does the sword's *Nachleben* carry forward from sacrificial antiquity? What is the *Zwischenraum* (interval) between the balanced scales and the drawn sword? — have never been asked of the Justitia complex.

**5.2 No Warburgian atlas of female civic allegory.** The *Bilderatlas Mnemosyne* itself does not include a systematic panel on Justitia, Marianne, or Liberty, though Warburg's correspondence confirms he planned to incorporate postage stamps and civic imagery as "specimens of revival of Antiquity serving a practical political purpose" (letter to Saxl, 16 May 1927, cited in Becker, p. 23). The unfinished panels 77–79 gesture toward political imagery without completing it. A doctoral thesis could propose — and at least virtually construct — the "missing panel" of a Warburgian legal atlas: an iconographic montage tracing Justitia, Libertas, Marianne, Columbia, Britannia, and Germania as a single *Pathosformel* family with antique roots in Themis, Dike, Justitia Augusta, and Athena Parthenos.

**5.3 The visual jurisprudence tradition has not engaged Warburg.** Goodrich's *visiocracy*, Douzinas's *legal iconology*, Resnik and Curtis's institutional iconography, Manderson's *aesthetic jurisprudence*, and Sherwin's *visual jurisprudence* all operate without invoking Warburg's apparatus. This gap is all the more remarkable given that Goodrich, in *Legal Emblems*, describes the "image archive that Freud calls the unconscious" (p. xxi) — an exact functional equivalent of the Warburgian *Engramm* — without naming Warburg. The methodological infrastructure is already present in visual jurisprudence; the Warburgian conceptual vocabulary is missing.

**5.4 The post-colonial and non-Western dimensions.** Warburg's own methodological openness — evidenced by his 1896 study of Hopi snake-dance rituals as evidence for the *Pathosformel* of cosmic anxiety — points toward applications of the *Nachleben* concept to non-Western legal allegory (the Brazilian civic/republican allegorical tradition, Tiradentes iconography, the *República* female allegory in Brazilian banknotes and currency) that remain entirely undeveloped.

**5.5 The interval-as-juridical-concept.** Warburg's "iconology of the interval" — the *Zwischenraum* between the Dionysian and Apollonian poles — has been read as an aesthetic and psychological concept (by Agamben, Didi-Huberman, Michaud) but never as a specifically juridical concept. Yet the interval between law and violence, between the scales of judgment and the sword of execution, between the blindfold (impartiality) and the gaze (surveillance), is structurally analogous to Warburg's *Denkraum*: the breathing space between magical compulsion and rational thought where civilization manages its relation to foundational violence.

## 6. Key Concepts to Mobilize

### 6.1 Pathosformel (Formula of Pathos / Emotional Formula)

**Source-anchored definition:** "The Pathosformel [is] an emotionally charged visual trope" (Becker, 2013, p. 1, citing Warburg). Agamben defined it as "an indissoluble intertwining of an emotional charge and an iconographic formula in which it is impossible to distinguish between form and content" (*Potentialities*, cited in Becker, p. 10). Warburg introduced the term in his 1905 Hamburg lecture "Dürer and Italian Antiquity," applying it to the gestural formula of defeated Orpheus: the pose encodes "the true voice of antiquity... the dark mystery play of Dionysian legend" (Warburg 1999, p. 554, cited in Stramignoni 2024).

**Application to legal iconography:** Justitia's sword, scales, and blindfold are Pathosformeln encoding foundational juridical affects — decision, measurement, and impartiality — whose affective charge derives from sacrificial antiquity and survives in every courthouse statue. The Phrygian cap of Marianne/Libertas is a Pathosformel encoding the bodily sensation of released constraint; the laurel crown of Columbia encodes the Roman imperial virtue-transfer. Each attribute carries a temporal stratum whose affects are reactivated every time the image appears.

---

### 6.2 Nachleben der Antike (Afterlife / Survival of Antiquity)

**Source-anchored definition:** Warburg's concept of *Nachleben* ("afterlife" or "survival") describes the persistence of antique expressive forms — not as mere stylistic quotation but as living psychic energies that "survive" in later images as latent but reactivatable forces. As Didi-Huberman formulates it: a "phantasmal model of history" expressed by "hauntings, 'survivals,' residues, and the persistent return of forms... notions that are unthought, and... unconscious aspects of time" (*The Surviving Image*, Penn State UP).

**Application to legal iconography:** The *Nachleben* concept allows a doctoral thesis to trace the survival of Themis/Dike (Greek divine justice) → *Justitia Augusta* (Roman imperial legitimation) → medieval Iustitia as cardinal virtue → early modern emblem-book Justitia → courthouse statuary, not as a linear history of influences but as a field of affective energies whose subsequent reappearances each reveal different aspects of the founding political-juridical compact.

---

### 6.3 Zwischenraum / Denkraum (Interval Space / Thinking Space)

**Source-anchored definition:** Warburg's term *Denkraum* (literally "space for thinking," sometimes translated "thinking space" or "room for reflection") designates the psychological and cultural interval that civilization maintains between itself and magical/compulsive responses to images. In the *Mnemosyne Atlas* introduction, Warburg describes the "founding act of human civilization" as "the conscious creation of distance between oneself and the external world" (Warburg, "The Absorption of the Expressive Values of the Past," trans. Rampley, *Art in Translation* 1:2 [2009]: 277).  *Zwischenraum* (interval/in-between space) refers specifically to the gap between images in montage — the productive space of meaning generated by juxtaposition.

**Application to legal iconography:** The *Denkraum* concept is directly applicable to the juridical institution as a space designed to maintain controlled distance from violence — the courtroom as a ritual architecture of the *Zwischenraum* between accusation and punishment, between the scales of judgment and the sword of execution. Vismann's analysis of the courtroom table as the fundamental cultural technique of jurisdiction can be read as a material instantiation of Warburg's *Denkraum*: the table interposes structured distance.

---

### 6.4 Bilderfahrzeug (Image-Vehicle / Image-Carrier)

**Source-anchored definition:** Warburg's rare but theoretically important term *Bilderfahrzeug* ("image-vehicle" or "vehicle of images") refers to the physical or medial support that carries and transports images across time and space — coins, stamps, broadsides, postcards, newspaper photographs. The concept anticipates later theories of media as vehicles of cultural memory. Bilderfahrzeug is the material infrastructure of *Nachleben*.

**Application to legal iconography:** For a thesis on the "iconocracia" of the state, the *Bilderfahrzeug* concept is essential for analyzing how Justitia, Marianne, and Libertas travel across their specific vehicles (coins → judicial seals → courthouse murals → banknotes → protest imagery) and how each vehicle modulates the affective charge of the Pathosformel. Warburg's own interest in postage stamps — "a Semeuse and Barbados stamps" as "specimens of revival of Antiquity serving a practical political purpose" (letter to Saxl, 16 May 1927, cited in Becker p. 23) — explicitly included civic/state images.
