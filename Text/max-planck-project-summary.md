# Project Summary

## Title and Abstract

**Title (working):**  
*Brazil and the Vandalized Justice: Legal Literacy, Visual Sexual Contract, and Iconoclasm from the Atlantic Revolutions to Brasília*

**Abstract**

This project investigates how modern legal orders have taught populations to recognise, trust, and contest law not only through texts, but through images, monuments, and spatial arrangements. It argues that, from the late eighteenth century to the present, legal legitimacy has depended on a dense visual infrastructure in which feminised allegories of the Nation and Justice became the most visible "face" of the state. Precisely in periods when women were systematically excluded from voting, judging, and legislating, figures such as Marianne, Britannia, Germania, Helvetia, the Brazilian República and Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* condensed constitutional claims into stable attributes and postures, producing a form of background knowledge that made law operable for largely non‑literate populations.

The empirical core of the project is the 8 January 2023 attack on Brazil's Praça dos Três Poderes in Brasília, when supporters of former president Jair Bolsonaro invaded the seats of the executive, legislative, and judiciary powers. The rioters vandalised *A Justiça*—a 3.3‑metre granite sculpture representing a blindfolded seated woman that guards the entrance of the Supreme Federal Court—by writing "Perdeu, mané" ("You lost, fool") in lipstick across her chest, quoting a partisan remark by Justice Luís Roberto Barroso. In November 2024, an attacker returned to the same statue with gasoline and a letter suggesting that TNT would have been the proper means to deal with the "damned" effigy. Taken together with the destruction of Di Cavalcanti's *As Mulatas* and Marianne Peretti's *Araguaia* stained glass, these acts constitute a rare, well‑documented case of counter‑literacy: actors who read the feminised icon of Justice as the judiciary's visible body and seek to unmask and annihilate its universalist pretensions through targeted iconoclasm.

The project introduces the concepts of **legal literacy as infrastructural and visual competence** and of the **visual sexual contract** to analyse this history. Building on Marie‑José Mondzain's notion of "iconocracy", Carole Pateman's sexual contract, and Paolo Grossi's metaphor of the "scythe of equality", it redefines legal literacy beyond textual competence to encompass the historically situated capacity to navigate legality through material cues, visual symbols, and built environments. By treating images as primary legal sources, the research reconstructs the visual pedagogy through which most subjects—peasants, enslaved people, workers, and non‑professionals—actually encountered state authority. It then examines how this visual order is challenged when populist movements appropriate, vandalise, or destroy the very icons that once taught "what law looks like", as in the January 8 events.

Hosted at the Max Planck Institute, the project will result in a monograph and a series of articles that reframe current debates on democratic crisis and judicial legitimacy through the lens of legal iconography and gender, bringing Latin American material into conversation with European legal history. It also aims to produce an annotated corpus of legal and political allegories spanning Europe and Brazil, providing a conceptual toolkit—"visual sexual contract", "background knowledge", and "counter‑literacy"—for scholars concerned with how law becomes visible, lovable, and, in moments of crisis, destructible.

## 1. Background and State of the Art

The starting point of this project is a classic problem in the history of political representation: what happens when the sovereign's body disappears. The Atlantic revolutions of the late eighteenth century destroyed the natural body of the monarch, epitomised by the decapitation of Louis XVI, but did not abolish the need for authority to appear in a recognisable human form. Under the Old Regime, as Ernst Kantorowicz argued, the king possessed a "body natural" (mortal and fallible) and a "body politic" (immortal, infallible), and his physical presence, seal, and image acted as direct carriers of the law. When sovereignty was redefined in terms of "the people", "the nation", "the constitution", or "the law", these new abstractions lacked corporeal form. One cannot bow to the Civil Code or shake hands with the General Will. Yet the perceptual and affective need for a body to love, fear, or obey persisted.

Marie‑José Mondzain's theory of **iconocracy** offers a long‑range conceptual frame for this predicament. Analysing the Byzantine iconoclastic controversies, she shows how Christian theology resolved the prohibition of idolatry by positing a dual doctrine of the image: the icon is visible, yet refers to an invisible truth. This theological "economy" of images, she argues, provided a template for later regimes of representation in which power is mediated, legitimised, and contested through images that simultaneously reveal and conceal. Modern states inherit this logic when they rely on icons—coins, portraits, statues, architecture—to make absent principles perceptible, stabilising an "economy of appearances" that underwrites obedience. The project uses this lens to analyse how female allegories operate as conduits that make legal abstractions perceptible and lovable, while masking the concrete institutional practices that give them force.

In the legal domain, Paolo Grossi has highlighted the violence of the post‑revolutionary "scythe of equality" (*falce dell'uguaglianza*), which cut through medieval corporate bodies and local jurisdictions to produce a "socially naked" legal subject, an abstract, de‑historicised individual. This new juridical landscape, codified in Napoleonic style civil codes, required what Grossi calls a "mythological machine" to naturalise an artificial, highly centralised order. Visual allegories formed a crucial part of this machine: they offered a human face for an impersonal legal grid and helped transform the subject from a viewer of royal spectacles into a reader of republican signs.

The literature on female allegories has documented how, in this context, the state increasingly appeared as a woman. Maurice Agulhon, Marina Warner, and Joan Landes have traced how Marianne became the embodiment of the French Republic, oscillating between a radical, bare‑breasted virago leading the people and a conservative, fully draped seated figure holding attributes of law and order. Similar processes can be observed in Britannia, Germania, and Helvetia, which personify empire, national unity, or republican stability while erasing the biographies of actual women and the conflicts of concrete political actors. Yet this scholarship tends to remain within art history or national political symbolism, without fully engaging the legal dimensions of these images: how they train perceptions of legality and structure everyday encounters with courts, constitutions, and rights.

At the same time, debates on **legal literacy** have largely focused on textual skills. Legal literacy is usually defined as the ability to read and understand statutes, identify rights, and navigate procedural complexities. While this focus has been crucial for access‑to‑justice agendas, it leaves out the majority of historical subjects who encountered law not in codes or treatises, but in coins, seals, courthouse façades, and statues. The project identifies a significant gap here: there is no systematic account of legal literacy as a visual and infrastructural competence, that is, as the tacit capacity to recognise a building as a court, a figure as Justice, or a symbol as the state's presence.

This is where the project intervenes. It brings together legal history, feminist political theory, and visual culture studies to argue that modern legal orders are inconceivable without a visual pedagogy, and that this pedagogy has been overwhelmingly gendered. It also suggests that contemporary crises of democratic legitimacy may be better understood if one looks not only at texts and doctrines, but at the images that have long carried law's promise of universality—and that are now being violently contested.

## 2. Research Questions and Objectives

The project is guided by four interrelated research questions.

First, **how was a stable visual grammar of legal authority constructed between 1789 and 1945, and with what exclusions**. The project asks how female allegories came to condense complex constitutional arrangements into a small set of attributes—blindfold, scales, sword, Phrygian cap, laurel wreath—that could be instantly read by largely non‑literate populations across Europe and the Atlantic. It investigates what social and gendered hierarchies were concealed or naturalised in the process.

Second, **how was this grammar appropriated, racialised, and modernised in Brazil**. Brazil's transition from Empire to Republic offers a particularly revealing case of visual continuity and transformation. The 1889 coup replaced the imperial coat of arms with a positivist celestial globe and motto, yet retained the imperial green and yellow associated with the Braganza–Habsburg dynasty, producing a sense of visual seamlessness in which the nation appeared the same to its subjects' eyes even as sovereignty shifted from emperor to republic. Simultaneously, Brazilian elites imported the French iconographic kit of the Republic, including a white, classicising female effigy on coins and monuments, projecting an image of European "civilisation" in a majority Black and mixed‑race society. The project examines this hybrid repertoire as a **visual racial contract**, in which the allegorical woman is both gendered and whitened.

Third, **what forms of counter‑literacy emerge when populist movements appropriate, vandalise, or destroy state icons**, and what do these practices reveal about the fragility of legal legitimacy. The January 8 attacks in Brasília are a focal point here. The rioters wrapped themselves in the national flag, wore yellow and green football jerseys, and claimed to act in the name of the "true" people and the constitution, all while attacking the institutions that formally embody democratic legality. Their acts against *A Justiça* and other symbolic objects show a sophisticated grasp of which images anchor authority in the public eye. The project asks how such actors read and rewrite state iconography, and how their practices reconfigure the visual contract between citizens and law.

Fourth, **how do legal institutions respond to assaults on images, and what do these responses tell us about the ontological status of images in modern legal orders**. The Supreme Federal Court treated the lipstick inscription on *A Justiça* as part of a broader attack on the democratic rule of law, and imposed a 14‑year sentence on the woman identified as the author. Restoration teams swiftly removed the graffiti to avoid permanently fixing partisan meaning onto a supposedly universal symbol. The project investigates how courts and state agencies legally construct images as extensions of the state's body, and how rituals of repair and re‑consecration seek to restore visual authority.

In light of these questions, the main objectives of the project are: (1) to reconstruct the transatlantic genealogy of the feminised allegorical body as the body of the state; (2) to formulate and empirically ground the concept of legal literacy as infrastructural and visual competence; (3) to develop the theoretical framework of the visual sexual contract and its mechanisms of substitution, sacralisation, and disciplining; and (4) to analyse the January 8 attacks as a paradigmatic case of iconoclasm in a contemporary constitutional democracy.

## 3. Theoretical Framework

The project's theoretical framework connects three major strands of thought: iconocracy and the economy of images; feminist critiques of the social contract; and legal historiography on the abstract individual.

Building on Mondzain, the project understands **iconocracy** as a regime in which power is mediated, legitimised, and contested through images that simultaneously reveal and conceal. The Byzantine solution to idolatry—allowing images as long as they pointed beyond themselves—creates a pattern in which icons embody an absent authority while always referring to a transcendent source. Modern legal states reproduce this structure when they place statues of Justice on courthouse pediments or mint coins with the face of the Republic: the visible form is meant to stand for an invisible principle, such as the rule of law or the nation. The project uses this lens to analyse how female allegories operate as conduits that make legal abstractions perceptible and lovable, while masking the concrete institutional practices that give them force.

Carole Pateman's theory of the **sexual contract** offers a second crucial pillar. Pateman argues that classical social contract theory, from Hobbes to Rousseau, obscures the fact that civil freedom among men is predicated on a prior contract that institutionalises men's political right over women. The "individual" who contracts into the state is implicitly male, already a master in the private sphere. Extending this argument to the visual field, the project introduces the concept of the **visual sexual contract**: a regime in which the omnipresence of the female body as the state's symbol functions as a substitute for women's political inclusion. The female allegory is hyper‑visible precisely because women are structurally invisible in formal politics. As Marina Warner notes, a male statue tends to invoke specific individuals, with biographies and factions; a female figure, historically excluded from ruling, can be perceived as an empty vessel onto which ideals such as Liberty or Justice are projected.

The project identifies three main mechanisms through which this contract operates. **Substitution**: allegorical women occupy the symbolic space where actual women are absent; Marianne stands for the Republic decades before French women gain the vote. **Sacralisation**: the feminine figure is elevated to a plane of purity above the "dirty" realm of political bargaining; Justice is seated, blindfolded, and detached from the conflicts and compromises that define adjudication. **De‑sexualisation and disciplining**: to serve as state symbols, female bodies are shorn of unruly or erotic traits associated with witches, prostitutes, or revolutionary viragos, and recast as draped, monumental, maternal figures. These mechanisms transform the allegorical woman into a manageable, reverent image that both demands loyalty and conceals male fraternal power.

Paolo Grossi's metaphor of the **"scythe of equality"** provides the third strand. Grossi shows how revolutionary law flattened a complex landscape of statuses, privileges, and corporate bodies into a terrain of formally equal individuals. This abstraction required not only a textual code, but also a visual correlate: a figure that could stand for "everyone and no one". The project reads female allegories as this abstract body, the visual counterpart to the coded legal subject. A statue of a concrete male hero divides the population; some revere him, others resent him. By contrast, a classicised female figure without biography appears neutral and eternal. Jean Baudrillard's concept of simulacra further suggests that, as liberal capitalism's contradictions deepen, such images can become more real than the institutions they represent, masking not simply reality, but the absence of the reality they claim to embody.

By articulating iconocracy, sexual contract, and abstract legality, the project offers a theoretical vocabulary for understanding how gendered images support the authority of modern legal orders—and why attacks on these images resonate so powerfully in moments of crisis.

## 4. Case Studies and Methodology

The project's empirical work is organised around four main case studies, which together span from the late eighteenth century to the present and connect European and Brazilian contexts.

The first case study reconstructs the **Atlantic genealogy of the allegorical body** between 1789 and 1945. It examines how revolutionary France, imperial Britain, unified Germany, and republican Switzerland developed and circulated feminised figures of the nation and of justice. In France, Marianne evolves from the militant Liberty of Delacroix's *Liberty Leading the People*, bare‑breasted and leading a barricade, to the seated, draped figure that appears on official seals and courthouse decorations. This shift parallels the pacification and domestication of revolutionary energies in the legal order. In Britain, Britannia coexists with a living monarch, embodying the maritime empire rather than the person of the sovereign; in Germany, Germania moves from romantic cultural unity to a martial guardian of the empire after 1871; in Switzerland, Helvetia serves as a unifying "mother" on coins and stamps amid linguistic and religious fragmentation. The project will analyse coins, stamps, public monuments, judicial architecture, and official prints, drawing on museum collections and national archives, to map the shared attributes and national variations of this visual grammar.

The second case study follows the **Brazilian reception and transformation** of this repertoire. When Brazil proclaimed the Republic in 1889, the new regime faced the challenge of erasing the emperor's image and installing a republican imaginary in a largely illiterate, post‑slavery society. Republican leaders, influenced by French positivism, adopted the Effigy of the Republic—a white, Greco‑Roman woman with Phrygian cap and laurel wreath—as the official face of the state on coins, medals, and monuments. At the same time, they retained imperial colours and certain dynastic references, producing what can be described as visual seamlessness between empire and republic. The project examines this hybrid grammar—"dynastic colours plus republican allegory"—as a way of reconciling a formally new constitutional order with a deeply conservative social structure, and as a key site where a **visual racial contract** operates. The allegorical body is not only female but also coded as white and European in a country whose population was and is largely Afro‑descendant and mixed.

The third case study turns to **Brasília and the modernist goddess**. Inaugurated in 1960 as a high‑modernist capital, Brasília was conceived as a total work of art in which architecture would express the rationality, transparency, and progress of the state. Oscar Niemeyer's glass façades suggest that power is visible and accessible, yet, as the project argues, this transparency often functions as a simulacrum: citizens can see the workings of power but cannot touch them. Alfredo Ceschiatti's *A Justiça*, carved from a monolithic block of Petrópolis granite and placed in front of the Supreme Federal Court, is a crucial node in this visual regime. The sculpture represents a blindfolded seated woman holding a sword across her lap, stripped of ornament and of the traditional scales. A comparative examination of iconographic attributes (eyes, posture, right hand, left hand, dress) highlights the symbolic implications of this choice: while the blindfold canonises impartiality and the sword signals latent coercive force, the absence of scales removes the "weighing" function that traditionally represents judgment and balance. The project explores how this minimalistic, abstracted Justice functions as a threshold figure between the profane plaza and the sacred interior of the Court, and how its specific design may have shaped the ways in which it became a target.

The fourth case study focuses on the **January 8 attacks and subsequent iconoclasm**. On that day, Bolsonarist crowds stormed the Praça dos Três Poderes, vandalising the chambers of Congress, the presidential Planalto Palace, and the Supreme Federal Court. Among numerous acts of destruction, the lipstick inscription "Perdeu, mané" on *A Justiça* stands out for its precise targeting of the judiciary's most iconic public body. The slogan itself had circulated as a confrontational retort in disputes over electoral legitimacy before migrating from speech to stone. The project traces this trajectory and analyses how the act transforms the statue from a silent universal symbol into a partisan mouthpiece, collapsing the distinction between the law's "body politic" and the fallible "body natural" of specific judges. The later attempted arson with gasoline and threats of TNT intensify this logic by framing the earlier gesture as insufficient and calling for explosive erasure. Lipstick and TNT, the project suggests, belong to different registers of violence but address the same problem: how to attack a dispersed, procedural authority that does not speak through a single sovereign body.

For this case study, sources will include Supreme Federal Court decisions, such as the judgment sentencing Débora Rodrigues dos Santos to 14 years in prison for her role in the graffiti, Federal Police reports on the planned "Operation 142" and its failure to secure military backing, media coverage, and photographic and video records of the attacks. The project will treat these materials not only as evidence of criminal acts, but as documentation of visual practices and counter‑pedagogies that seek to re‑educate the public eye by substituting contempt for recognition.

Methodologically, the project combines iconographic and iconological analysis, legal and political discourse analysis, and comparative historical research. It adapts classical art‑historical tools to a materialist and gender‑sensitive reading of legal images, and reads legal texts—statutes, judicial opinions, speeches—not only for their explicit content but for their implicit assumptions about the status of images and their role in legal order. Comparative work will connect European and Brazilian trajectories, highlighting both shared patterns and specific colonial and racial dynamics.

## 5. Expected Outcomes and Contribution

The project is expected to produce several concrete outcomes. The central scholarly product will be a monograph in English that develops the concepts of legal literacy as infrastructural and visual competence and of the visual sexual contract, grounded in the four case studies outlined above. In addition, the project will generate a series of peer‑reviewed articles targeted at journals in legal history, feminist theory, and visual culture—for instance, an article focusing on *A Justiça* as a modernist reconfiguration of Justice, and another on the January 8 events as a paradigmatic case of legal iconoclasm and counter‑literacy.

Beyond textual outputs, the project aims to compile an annotated digital corpus of legal and political allegories from Europe and Brazil, spanning coins, stamps, statues, and buildings from 1789 to the present. This corpus, hosted online if possible, would make accessible to other researchers and students the visual material that legal historians often treat as peripheral, thereby encouraging further work on legal iconography and political theology in a comparative key.

Conceptually, the project contributes four main innovations. First, it consolidates the notion of **legal literacy visual/infrastructural**, shifting attention from reading texts to reading spaces and symbols. Second, it fully articulates the **visual sexual contract**, showing how visual hyper‑visibility of allegorical women operates as a substitute and cover for political exclusion in both European and Brazilian contexts. Third, it refines the idea of **background knowledge** of law: the tacit recognition that tells a citizen that a building with classical columns and a blindfolded woman is a place of judgment rather than commerce, and that gives credibility to legal proceedings even for those who cannot read. Fourth, it theorises **counter‑literacy** as an insurgent mode of reading and rewriting state iconography, capable of destabilising established visual contracts and revealing the contingency of legal legitimacy.

The broader relevance of the project lies in its capacity to reframe contemporary concerns about democratic crisis, polarisation, and attacks on courts. While much of the current debate focuses on constitutional doctrine, electoral rules, or social media, this project shows that a crucial front of conflict is visual. Statues are toppled, flags are re‑signified, buildings are stormed, and images are weaponised to claim or deny legitimacy. By situating these phenomena in a long history of iconocracy and sexual contract, the project offers tools to think critically about the design of legal spaces and symbols, the politics of heritage protection, and the pedagogies through which citizens are taught to recognise law.

## 6. Work Plan and Feasibility

The project is designed as a three‑year research endeavour, structured in overlapping phases.

In **Year 1**, the focus will be on consolidating the theoretical framework and mapping the European visual repertoire. This involves an intensive review of literature on iconocracy, political theology, social and sexual contract theory, and legal history, as well as the collection of visual sources related to Marianne, Britannia, Germania, and Helvetia from digital archives and museum collections. The aim is to establish a robust comparative matrix of attributes, postures, and contexts of use for these allegories, and to link them to specific legal transformations (codification, judicial reforms, constitutional changes).

In **Year 2**, the attention shifts to Brazil. Archival work in Brazilian collections—both physical and digital—will focus on the late imperial and early republican periods, tracing how the Effigy of the Republic and related symbols were deployed in coins, seals, schoolbooks, and courthouse architecture. Research on Brasília will involve analysis of architectural plans, photographic archives, and critical literature on Niemeyer and Ceschiatti, to situate *A Justiça* within broader modernist strategies of transparency and abstraction. During this year, draft chapters on the Brazilian reception and the Brasília case study will be written and presented in seminars for feedback.

In **Year 3**, the project will concentrate on the January 8 attacks and their legal and visual aftermath. This includes systematic analysis of Supreme Federal Court decisions, Federal Police reports, media archives, and audiovisual material documenting the attacks and the process of restoration. The aim is to produce a detailed reconstruction of how different actors—rioters, judges, politicians, restorers, journalists—have read and re‑inscribed the images at stake. The final period will be dedicated to integrating the findings from all case studies into the monograph and to submitting articles to journals.

The Max Planck Institute offers an ideal setting for this project. Its strong tradition in legal history and legal theory, combined with interdisciplinary engagement with political science, sociology, and philosophy, provides a fertile environment for developing the project's conceptual and comparative ambitions. Access to European libraries and archives will facilitate the reconstruction of the early visual repertoire, while the Institute's seminar culture will offer opportunities to test the project's key concepts—legal literacy, visual sexual contract, counter‑literacy—before diverse expert audiences. The feasibility of the project is enhanced by the availability of many visual sources in digitised form, by the clear delimitation of case studies, and by the applicant's existing work on the vandalisation of *A Justiça* and the visual continuity of Brazilian law.

---

## Conclusion

This project offers a novel approach to understanding legal legitimacy through the lens of visual culture and feminist political theory. By reconstructing the genealogy of the female allegory from the Atlantic revolutions to Brasília, and by analysing the January 8 attacks as a crisis of this visual infrastructure, the project reframes contemporary debates on democratic legitimacy and judicial authority. It shows that images are not peripheral to legal order but central to its functioning, and that in moments of crisis, they become sites of intense political struggle. The concepts of legal literacy as visual competence and the visual sexual contract promise to be productive tools for scholars across legal history, gender studies, and visual culture studies, while the project's attention to Brazil's specific trajectories offers an important correction to predominantly European-focused accounts of legal modernity.
