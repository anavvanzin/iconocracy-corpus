## Executive Assessment

**Recommendation, stated directly:** The "Iconocracia" thesis is best served by Warburgian cartography — specifically, the hybrid of Nachleben analysis, Foucauldian genealogical discontinuity, Bourdieusian field mapping, and feminist cartography outlined in Stream 5. An atlas-topological arrangement of the image corpus is not a methodological consolation prize: it is the method most adequate to the thesis's triple object (diachronic visual survival, state-juridical power, female embodiment of legal abstraction).

The five research streams converge on a single structural finding that supports this recommendation. Stream 1 establishes that the application of Warburg's methodology to legal iconography remains a productive lacuna: [Stramignoni's 2024 article](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10978-023-09371-4) in *Law and Critique* is currently the only explicit convergence of Warburgian Pathosformel analysis and a theory of juridical image-experience, and [Hayaert's *Lady Justice*](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-lady-justice.html) (Edinburgh UP, 2020) deploys an implicitly Warburgian gestural method without naming the apparatus. The entire tradition of visual jurisprudence — Goodrich, Douzinas, Resnik and Curtis, Manderson — operates without invoking Nachleben, Denkraum, or Bilderfahrzeug. The thesis fills this gap.

Stream 2 establishes that no existing digital project combines dedicated focus on female allegorical figures in legal/juridical contexts with pan-Atlantic scope, plate-based organisation, and feminist or decolonial framework. The Dutch [NCRD](https://geheugen.delpher.nl) — the only dedicated legal iconography database — closed in December 2021. The closest structural model for what a thesis-atlas would need to be is the [Erdteilallegorien database at Vienna](https://erdteilallegorien.univie.ac.at): GIS-mapped, Iconclass-indexed, multi-interface, open-access. Brazilian resources ([BNDigital](https://bndigital.bn.gov.br), [Brasiliana Iconográfica](https://www.brasilianaiconografica.art.br), [IMS Acervo Digital](https://acervos.ims.com.br)) contain rich material but lack legal-thematic organisation.

Stream 3 mounts a cumulative, multi-author case against scalar analysis of female civic imagery. Drucker's capta/data distinction, Merry's law-adjacent seductions-of-quantification argument, Haraway's situated knowledges, Warner and Wenk on the constitutive paradox of female allegory, and Didi-Huberman's anachronism argument together demonstrate that an ordinal Legal-Political Allegory Index (LPAI) cannot hold the temporal, political, and gendered overdetermination that is the thesis's proper subject.

Stream 4 establishes the empirical historical ground: the militarization of female civic allegory intensifies under republican rupture and external military threat (France 1789–1870–1914, Germany 1848–1871, Brazil 1889–1910), but is *displaced rather than intensified* under personalist authoritarianism (Estado Novo, Peronism). This finding runs counter to intuition and provides one of the thesis's strongest original contributions.

Stream 5 proposes a three-axis synthesis — (a) diachronic archaeology of the legal dispositif (Warburg × Foucault via Didi-Huberman), (b) mapping the field of juridical vision (Bourdieu × Artl@s × feminist cartography), (c) the thesis-as-atlas curatorial model (Pollock's Virtual Feminist Museum) — and identifies the concept of the "juridical imaginary" (after Castoriadis and Legendre) as the field to be mapped.

## 1. The Question Reframed

The methodological question the "Iconocracia" thesis must answer is not "how do I measure female legal allegory?" but "what kind of knowledge-object is female legal allegory?" The distinction is consequential. A scored Legal-Political Allegory Index (LPAI) treats images as data-points on a pre-set scale; an atlas-topological arrangement treats them as carriers of affective, political, and temporal force whose significance emerges from proximity and interval — the Zwischenraum between images — rather than from a fixed position on a numerical axis.

This is a live methodological question in 2026 for three reasons. First, the "quantitative turn" in digital humanities — epitomised by Manovich's cultural analytics and operationalised in tools for computational image analysis — has made the scored approach technically feasible at corpus scale. Arnold and Tilton's [*Distant Viewing*](https://direct.mit.edu/books/oa-monograph/5674/Distant-ViewingComputational-Exploration-of) (MIT Press, 2023) provides a rigorous framework for extracting semantic metadata from large visual corpora, including the formal attributes of allegorical figures (blindfold, scales, sword, body posture). A doctoral thesis could deploy these tools.

Second, the feminist and postcolonial critique of indices has matured sufficiently to constitute a disciplinary standard. [Drucker's capta/data argument](https://dhq-static.digitalhumanities.org/pdf/000091.pdf) (DHQ 2011), [Merry's *Seductions of Quantification*](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/S/bo22726282.html) (Chicago UP, 2016), [D'Ignazio and Klein's *Data Feminism*](https://data-feminism.mitpress.mit.edu) (MIT Press, 2020), and [Posner's challenge](https://dhdebates.gc.cuny.edu/read/untitled/section/a22aca14-0eb0-4cc6-a622-6fee9428a357) to the data models of DH are not minority positions but have become canonical within digital humanities itself. A thesis that adopts a scored index without engaging this critique will not pass methodological scrutiny.

Third, the tradition of the Warburgian atlas has been significantly renewed and made practically accessible through the [Engramma digital critical edition](https://www.engramma.it/eOS/core/frontend/eos_atlas_index.php?id_articolo=5746&lang=eng) of the *Mnemosyne Atlas*, the [HKW Berlin 2020 reconstruction](https://archiv.hkw.de/en/programm/projekte/2020/aby_warburg/partner_aby_warburg/start.php), Didi-Huberman's *Atlas, ou le gai savoir inquiet* (Minuit, 2011; English trans. Chicago UP, 2018), and Pollock's Virtual Feminist Museum as a practical feminist application. The atlas is no longer an inspiring metaphor; it is a deployable methodological infrastructure.

What is at stake for the thesis is not merely which method to choose but what claim the thesis makes about the kind of knowledge that iconographic history produces. An LPAI claims to measure; an atlas claims to think. For a thesis named "Iconocracia" — the governance of images, the rule of the image — the method must itself be an argument about how images rule.

## 2. Warburg in Legal Iconography — a Productive Non-Encounter

### The State of the Field

The application of Warburg's methodology to legal, juridical, and state iconography remains a largely unrealized project. The field of "law and the visual" — consolidated through [Douzinas and Nead's *Law and the Image*](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/L/bo3622830.html) (Chicago UP, 1999) and [Goodrich's *Legal Emblems and the Art of Law*](https://aidel.it/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/2013_Goodrich-_Legal_Emblems_and_the_Art_of_Law2.pdf) (Cambridge UP, 2013) — has developed in productive parallel with Warburgian Bildwissenschaft, but the two bodies of scholarship seldom address each other directly. Legal iconographers share Warburg's concern with the affective power of images but almost never invoke his conceptual apparatus by name: Pathosformel, Nachleben, Denkraum, Bilderfahrzeug. This gap is all the more remarkable given that Goodrich, in *Legal Emblems*, describes the legal "image archive that Freud calls the unconscious" (p. xxi) — a formulation structurally identical to the Warburgian Engramm — without naming Warburg.

### The Four Direct Applications

**Igor Stramignoni — "Nocturnal Jurisprudence"** [High]

The closest existing convergence. Stramignoni's "Nachleben der Antike, Time, and Restitution: Notes for a Nocturnal Jurisprudence of the Image" ([*Law and Critique* 35:2, March 2024, pp. 445–482](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10978-023-09371-4)) takes Warburg's 1905 Hamburg lecture "Dürer and Italian Antiquity" as a founding text for a "nocturnal jurisprudence of the image." He argues that Pathosformeln embedded in the allegory of Ivstitia — the female figure with scales, sword, and blindfold — carry dormant affective energies from antiquity that were never neutralized by modernization. Drawing on Warburg's concept of images as "crystals of historical memory" (via Agamben), Didi-Huberman's call for the "restitution" of images, and Michel Serres's "thousand nocturnal flashes," Stramignoni proposes that a juridical encounter with these Pathosformeln requires recognizing their non-representational presence. His own formulation: «In this essay, I take my cue from Aby Warburg and the Pathosformeln that, he suggested, can be glimpsed through certain material objects.» ([PhilPapers](https://philpapers.org/rec/STRNDA-4))

**Colleen Becker — Germania as Pathosformel** [High]

Becker's "[Aby Warburg's Pathosformel as Methodological Paradigm](https://arthistoriography.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/becker.pdf)" (*Journal of Art Historiography* 9, December 2013) demonstrates the cross-disciplinary applicability of Pathosformel using *Germania* as a case study — a figure sharing her morphological heritage with Britannia, Marianne, and Columbia. Becker's key finding: «Germania gave physical form to a collective crisis surrounding the promise and prospect of assimilation within a newly minted German state... she was an allegory that signaled socio-political conflicts and incongruities, both internal and external, rather than a singular and static vision of national unity» (pp. 10, 19). Critically, Becker documents that Warburg himself planned to include civic imagery in the *Bilderatlas*: a letter of 16 May 1927 to Fritz Saxl confirms he intended panels showing "specimens of revival of Antiquity serving a practical political purpose," including postage stamps featuring Semeuse (linked to Marianne) and others (p. 23). Tafel 77 of the *Atlas* includes Victoria, coinage, and other state imagery — Warburg's own intended direction of travel.

**Valérie Hayaert — *Lady Justice: An Anatomy of Allegory*** [High]

Hayaert's monograph ([Edinburgh UP, 2020](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-lady-justice.html)) is the most sustained iconographic analysis of Justitia published in the twenty-first century. Analyzing thousands of images from 1400–1600 across six countries, she decomposes the allegorical body of Lady Justice organ by organ — an approach methodologically convergent with Warburg's gestural analysis of the Nymph and the maenad. The publisher summarizes: «In Justice's gestures, postures, choreography, her contradictions and enigmas, we find both 'the monstrosity of human judgement' and a longing to repair law's estrangement from justice.» While Hayaert does not deploy Warburgian terminology, her procedure — tracing gestural Pathosformeln across the allegorical body, analyzing scales-sword-gaze as a triangular physiological scheme — constitutes an implicit Warburgian iconology of legal allegory. The absence of the explicit Warburgian vocabulary is itself a finding: the thesis can name what Hayaert performs without labelling it.

**Judith Resnik and Dennis Curtis — *Representing Justice*** [High]

The most comprehensive English-language survey of Justitia's iconographic history in relation to legal institutions ([Yale UP, 2011](https://documents.law.yale.edu/representing-justice); 668 pp., 220+ images). Resnik and Curtis trace the female allegory of Justice from Renaissance Italian city-states through the architecture and statuary of democratic courthouses worldwide, theorizing the "rites-to-rights" transformation: how civic iconography survived and mutated across centuries as a legitimating apparatus of state power. Their most important finding for the thesis: of an exhaustive worldwide catalog, only ONE courthouse depicts Justice as a woman of color in plain view (St. Croix, Virgin Islands, 1993). Their method — tracing the *longue durée* survival of an allegorical figure across radically changed contexts — is structurally Warburgian without invoking Nachleben. A Warburgian reading would theorize the affective energies crystallized in the blindfold, sword, and scales as surviving antiquity — adding interpretive depth to Resnik and Curtis's institutional analysis.

### The Indirect Circle

The Warburgian tradition itself has generated a rich political iconology touching the edges of juridical imagery without producing a sustained reading of Justitia or Marianne. [Bredekamp's *Leviathan*](https://www.scribd.com/document/671448690/) (De Gruyter, 2020) established political iconography as a discipline through the Hobbes frontispiece, demonstrating that sovereign images act as political agents, not illustrations. [Didi-Huberman's *The Surviving Image*](https://www.psupress.org/books/titles/978-0-271-07208-1.html) (Penn State UP, 2017) reconstructs Warburg's "phantasmal model" of history — «strata, hybrid blocks, rhizomes, specific complexities, by returns that are often unexpected and goals that are always thwarted» (p. 12) — and his *Atlas, ou le gai savoir inquiet* (Minuit, 2011) extends this to a political philosophy of images. [Goodrich's *Legal Emblems*](https://aidel.it/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/2013_Goodrich-_Legal_Emblems_and_the_Art_of_Law2.pdf) (Cambridge UP, 2013) develops visiocracy — the governance of vision — as a structural analogue to Warburg's political iconology, demonstrating how emblem books constitute a normative force: «These prior images, these imaginary representations of legality... correspond remarkably closely to the legal tradition of figures of justice» (p. xxi). [Douzinas's "Prosopon and Antiprosopon"](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/L/bo3622830.html) in *Law and the Image* (Chicago UP, 1999) calls explicitly for a "legal iconology" and argues that «the law understands the importance of the governance of images for the maintenance of the social bond and helps organise a regime of permitted images and forbidden idols.» [Manderson's *Danse Macabre*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/danse-macabre/BD8A5C8C7E4549556FAE23FAEBFDED3C) (Cambridge UP, 2019) performs close readings of Bruegel's *Justicia*, Klimt's *Jurisprudence*, and Cauduro's Mexican courthouse murals — reaching a Warburgian interpretation of images as law's theory without naming Warburg: «The visual and spectacular are indispensable elements of how we come to know and are known by politics, law, and regulation» (p. 16). Vismann's *Bildregime des Rechts* (Stuttgart: Clett-Kotta, 2008) [Medium — Scribd reference; library check needed] is the closest existing German-language approach to a systematic Bildwissenschaft of law. Legendre's institutional anthropology of dogmatic imagery (*Dieu au miroir*, Fayard, 1994) [Medium — library check needed] and Schade's feminist critique of Bildwissenschaft's [exclusion of women](https://blog.zhdk.ch/sigridschade/files/2013/07/Billwissenschaft_and_the_absence_of_women_27_7_09_000.pdf) (2008) complete the indirect circle.

### The Gap Stated

Five lacunae constitute the thesis's original contribution: (1) no systematic Warburgian reading of Justitia by name; (2) no Warburgian atlas of female civic allegory, despite Warburg's own stated intention to include this material; (3) the visual jurisprudence tradition has not engaged Warburg; (4) the post-colonial and non-Western dimensions of Nachleben in legal allegory are entirely undeveloped; (5) the interval between law and violence — the Zwischenraum between the scales and the sword — has never been read as the specifically juridical form of Warburg's Denkraum.

## 4. The Case Against the Score — Feminist and Postcolonial Critiques

The following argument is cumulative: each authority adds a distinct layer to the case against scalar analysis of female civic imagery. The strongest version of the counter-argument — in favour of a reframed LPAI as capta — is given at the end of this section.

### Merry — The Law-Adjacent Backbone [High]

Sally Engle Merry's [*The Seductions of Quantification*](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/S/bo22726282.html) (Chicago UP, 2016) is the single most proximate analogue to the LPAI question: both score legal phenomena (rights compliance; allegorical legal coding) against a comparative grid. Merry argues that indicators are not revelations of truth but productions of it:

> «Counting things requires making them comparable, which means that they are inevitably stripped of their context, history, and meaning.» (Ch. 1, p. 4)

> «Rather than revealing truth, indicators create it. However, the result is not simply a fiction but a particular way of dividing up and making known one reality among many possibilities.» (Ch. 1, p. 5)

Her analysis predicts that the LPAI will (a) embed a specific theory of what "legal allegory" means and (b) systematically marginalise images that resist those categories — precisely those images most likely to be decolonially or politically interesting.

### Haraway — The God Trick [High]

Donna Haraway's "[Situated Knowledges](https://philpapers.org/archive/HARSKT.p)" (*Feminist Studies* 14:3, 1988) diagnoses the pretension of any index to see all images from no particular position:

> «Vision in this technological feast becomes unregulated gluttony; all seems not just mythically about the god trick of seeing everything from nowhere, but to have put the myth into ordinary practice.» (p. 582)

> «Feminist objectivity is about limited location and situated knowledge, not about transcendence and splitting of subject and object. It allows us to become answerable for what we learn how to see.» (p. 583)

An atlas-topology, which foregrounds the researcher's acts of selection and juxtaposition, is structurally more consistent with situated-knowledges methodology than an ordinal score.

### Drucker — Capta, Not Data [High]

Johanna Drucker's "[Humanities Approaches to Graphical Display](https://dhq-static.digitalhumanities.org/pdf/000091.pdf)" (DHQ 5:1, 2011) provides the most precise technical critique of what a scored index does epistemologically:

> «Such graphical tools are a kind of intellectual Trojan horse, a vehicle through which assumptions about what constitutes information swarm with potent force. These assumptions are cloaked in a rhetoric taken wholesale from the techniques of the empirical sciences that conceals their epistemological biases under a guise of familiarity.» (p. 2)

> «Data are capta, taken not given, constructed as an interpretation of the phenomenal world, not inherent in it.» (p. 10)

A doctoral thesis built on scored data presents capta as if it were data. An atlas makes the researcher's constructive choices legible.

### Espeland and Sauder — Reactivity [High]

Espeland and Sauder's "Rankings and Reactivity" (*American Journal of Sociology* 113:1, 2007, [DOI: 10.1086/517897](https://doi.org/10.1086/517897)) establish that rankings do not passively measure pre-existing reality: they create the social world they claim to describe. Applied to the LPAI: an index creates pressure toward images that "score well" on its terms, marginalising precisely the images most unexpected or most challenging to the framework.

### Azoulay, Mirzoeff, Campt — The Archive and its Exclusions

Ariella Azoulay's [*Potential History: Unlearning Imperialism*](https://www.versobooks.com/products/2103-potential-history) (Verso, 2019) argues that the camera's shutter is «a synecdoche for the operation of the imperial enterprise altogether» (p. 2) — it decides in advance what the image is, bracketing what it *could be*. The LPAI performs an analogous closure. Nicholas Mirzoeff's [*The Right to Look*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/the-right-to-look) (Duke UP, 2011) shows that classification systems are instruments of visuality — the three techniques of power (classification, separation, aestheticisation) make empire appear natural and self-evident. Tina Campt's [*Listening to Images*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/listening-to-images) (Duke UP, 2017) argues against extractive visual methods that «classify types rather than identify individuals», calling instead for attention to images' «quiet frequencies» — the affective and haptic registers that exceed classification. [High for all three; specific page numbers for Azoulay and Campt partially verified from secondary sources.]

### Warner / Wenk — The Constitutive Paradox [High]

Marina Warner's [*Monuments and Maidens*](https://www.ucpress.edu/books/monuments-and-maidens/paper) (Weidenfeld & Nicolson, 1985) identifies the foundational paradox: «The female form tends to be perceived as generic and universal, with symbolic overtones; the male as individual, even when it is being used to express a generalized idea» (p. 38; cited in Pejić, [MoMA essay](https://moma.org)). Warner's general paradox — figures of women are chosen to personify an ideal while most women are excluded from pursuing that ideal practically — means that the "legal-political allegorical content" of a female image cannot be scored without simultaneously tracking the exclusion that content performs. No ordinal scale can hold both the allegorical claim and its constitutive contradiction. Silke Wenk's *Versteinerte Weiblichkeit* (Böhlau, 1996) sharpens this: female allegories are "female" precisely because «only the images of women — who stay outside (economic and state) competition — were appropriate to embody the imagined community's interests» (cited in Pejić, [MoMA 2001](https://moma.org/interactives/exhibitions/2011/sanjaivekovic/essays)). The "legal-political" dimension of female allegory is a structural and constitutive property, not a measurable one.

### Didi-Huberman, Bal — Anachronism and Preposterous History [High]

Didi-Huberman's argument in *Devant le temps* (Minuit, 2000; English excerpts in *Compelling Visuality*, Minnesota UP, 2003) is foundational: images are «temporally impure, complex, overdetermined... polychronistic, heterochronistic, or anachronistic objects.» An ordinal index assigns images to a single conceptual axis; Didi-Huberman shows that images exceed any single classification — «in a first approximation, anachronism would be the temporal way of expressing the exuberance, complexity, and overdetermination of images.» [PAGE UNVERIFIED — pages confirmed in Scribd scan of English translation but anthology reprinting changes pagination.] Mieke Bal's concept of "preposterous history" (works that appear chronologically first operate as aftereffects of later works, *Quoting Caravaggio*, Chicago UP, 1999) is the directly feminist version of the anachronism argument: it refuses the patrilineal genealogy of influence and allows the researcher to read legal allegories "forward from their effects" as much as "backward from their origins."

### The 5-vs-5 Synthesis

**Five arguments against the LPAI:**

1. **Commensuration erases context** (Merry; Espeland/Stevens): scores strip images of history, meaning, and the specificity that makes them analytically interesting.
2. **Scores perform the god trick** (Haraway; Drucker): they claim a view from nowhere while concealing the researcher's interpretive commitments behind numerical legibility.
3. **Indices discipline toward conformity** (Espeland/Sauder; Porter): they create reactivity — images that score well are valued; unexpected images are marginalised.
4. **Classification reproduces colonial and imperial orderings** (Mirzoeff; Azoulay; Risam): the categories for "legal," "political," and "allegorical" are derived from Euro-centric canon; scoring a corpus including non-Western images against this canon ranks them as "low" by design.
5. **Female allegory is constitutively overdetermined** (Warner; Wenk; Bal; Didi-Huberman): any single scale produces systematic misreading because the image is simultaneously a legal allegory, a colonial fantasy, a gender inversion, and a temporal survival.

**Five conditions under which a score might still be defensible, if reframed as capta:**

1. If the researcher explicitly documents every interpretive choice embedded in the scoring schema and presents the schema itself as an argument (following Drucker's demand that capta replace data).
2. If the score is used only for corpus discovery (identifying candidate images from a large database for close reading) rather than as the final analytical statement.
3. If multiple competing scores are used simultaneously (one Warburgian axis, one Bourdieusian axis, one feminist cartographic axis) so that the different orderings generate productive friction rather than a false hierarchy.
4. If each score is presented with an explicit confidence tier and the unscoreable residue is preserved as a named analytical category (following Drucker's concept of uncertainty visualisation).
5. If the LPAI is explicitly positioned as a feminist counter-canon move — documenting absences (how many Brazilian courthouse statues depict Justice as a Black woman?) rather than ranking presence. In this reframing, the index becomes a tool for documenting Resnik and Curtis's key finding (only ONE representation of Justice as a woman of color in a worldwide survey) at scale.

## 5. Militarization of Female Civic Allegory — Historical Ground

The militarization of female civic allegory is not a continuous or universal process: it is historically specific, intensifying under particular conditions and being displaced or domesticated under others. The structural finding — which constitutes one of the thesis's strongest empirical contributions — is that militarization intensifies under republican rupture, external military threat, and imperial expansion, but is *displaced* under personalist authoritarianism (Estado Novo, Peronism). This runs counter to the intuition that authoritarian regimes are more prone to militarize national symbols.

### The Theoretical Framework

Warner's foundational paradox (*Monuments and Maidens*, 1985): «When the figure is female and anonymous, when she bears no proper name but stands for something, she can transcend the particular, and achieve the condition of the universal» (p. 66). This universalizing function explains why the female allegory may carry a sword as token of status while being prohibited from the role of soldier: she is the territory for which men fight, not a combatant. Wenk identifies the specifically modern form of this logic: female allegories are constitutively bound to the nation-state — they appear «together with the constitution of the (nation) states» — and carry a «state-building function.» Mosse's *Fallen Soldiers* (Oxford UP, 1990, p. 7) provides the structural logic: the "Myth of the War Experience" required the feminization of the homeland as a passive, penetrable body defended by male sacrifice. Monumental female allegories on war memorials function as the territory *for which* men die. Militarization of the female allegory is therefore the exception — the moment when the rule is visibly strained.

**The structural finding** [interpretation / our synthesis]: The data confirm that republican regimes under external military pressure or in the process of institutional consolidation produce the most explicitly militarized female allegories. The armed Marianne of 1792 and 1914, the sword-bearing *A República* of 1889–1910, and the armored Germania of 1848 and 1871 all belong to moments of republican foundation under threat. By contrast, both Estado Novo and Peronism — personalist authoritarian regimes that sought to liquidate the Republican tradition rather than defend it — displaced the armed female allegory in favour of the masculine leader-cult combined with domesticated, maternal femininity. The militarized female republic carries revolutionary and democratic connotations that are incompatible with personalist authoritarianism. For the thesis, this means that the militarization-displacement pattern is itself a visual-political argument: it maps the ideological tension between republican and personalist forms of state power across national visual cultures.

[Carvalho's *A Formação das Almas*](https://www.companhiadasletras.com.br) (Companhia das Letras, 1990) provides the definitive Brazilian evidence. His key finding: «A batalha pela alegoria feminina terminou em derrota republicana. Mais ainda, em derrota do cívico perante o religioso» (p. 94). [Translation:] "The battle for the female allegory ended in Republican defeat. Furthermore, in the defeat of the civic before the religious." The Brazilian Republic's female allegory was uniquely vulnerable: it had to compete against Princess Isabel (the "Redemptrix" associated with the Monarchy and the Lei Áurea), against the Catholic Virgin (in a heavily Catholic society), and against the ironic-satirical press treatment of the Republic as a prostituted or debased female figure. This produced an ironic militarization — the Republic armed but not triumphant — that is a distinctly Brazilian variant requiring a specifically Brazilian chapter in the thesis atlas.

## 6. A Methodological Synthesis: Warburgian Cartography of the Juridical Imaginary

### Didi-Huberman as Hinge

Georges Didi-Huberman is the indispensable mediator between Warburg and the theoretical traditions the "Iconocracia" thesis must also mobilize. His argument in [*The Surviving Image*](https://www.psupress.org/books/titles/978-0-271-07208-1.html) (Penn State UP, 2017 trans.; orig. *L'image survivante*, Minuit, 2002) is that Warburg's history of images operates not through linear influence but through temporal strata: periods are «expressed by strata, hybrid blocks, rhizomes, specific complexities, by returns that are often unexpected and goals that are always thwarted» (p. 12). This is structurally parallel to Foucault's dispositif as a heterogeneous ensemble linking discourses, institutions, and material practices across discontinuous moments. The crucial connection: both Warburg's Nachleben and Foucault's genealogy work against teleology — against the idea that images (or power-formations) simply "progress." Their convergence licenses a methodology that simultaneously asks how did the Justitia image survive across legal orders? (Warburgian) and at what moments did the legal-political configuration of power require a new image of Justice? (Foucauldian).

In [*Atlas, ou le gai savoir inquiet*](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/A/bo25402951.html) (Minuit, 2011; Chicago UP, 2018), Didi-Huberman extends this to read the atlas-form itself — in Warburg, in Richter, in Feldmann — as an epistemological device: knowledge produced by montage rather than argument. Montage — the collision of heterogeneous temporalities — is the method shared by Warburg, Benjamin, Brecht, and Foucault's archaeological juxtapositions. His fourth volume in the *L'œil de l'histoire* series, *Peuples exposés, peuples figurants* (Minuit, 2012), connects Warburg's concern with figures and gestures directly to political visibility: the female body-as-Justice is precisely the figural reduction Didi-Huberman critiques — woman reduced to allegorical type, deprived of singularity, even as she is exposed as the image of legal power. This is the thesis's central political claim, and Didi-Huberman provides its theoretical warrant.

### Pollock's Virtual Feminist Museum as Feminist-Atlas Precedent

[Griselda Pollock](https://arthistoriography.files.wordpress.com/2012/12/pollock.pdf) is the scholar who has most explicitly adapted Warburg's *Mnemosyne Atlas* as a feminist methodological tool. Her Virtual Feminist Museum (VFM), developed across [*Encounters in the Virtual Feminist Museum*](https://books.google.com/books/about/Encounters_in_the_Virtual_Feminist_Museu.html?id=uKHpAAAAMAAJ) (Routledge, 2007) and subsequent lectures, invokes the atlas-form as a counter-canon strategy. She draws on «the models of both Aby Warburg's Mnemosyne Atlas and Freud's private museum of antiquities as well as [Bracha] Ettinger's concept of subjectivity as encounter to propose a differencing journey through time, space and archive.» The VFM is explicitly contrasted with Alfred Barr's modernist grid (the MoMA model): while Barr fixes art into period, style, oeuvre, and master — a patrilineal genealogy — Warburg's panels allow images to be placed «side by side in exhibitionary formats [to] track the movement of thought and passion and the oscillations within culture.»

Pollock's explicit statement of her Warburgian method: «Clearly inspired by Aby Warburg's legacy and his unfinished and perplexing project in his Mnemosyne BilderAtlas, the Virtual Feminist Museum [...] uses the most interesting aspect of the museum: the encounter between and with artworks arranged according to a scheme that is not identical with their making» (*Journal of Art Historiography*, 2012). For the "Iconocracia" thesis, Pollock's VFM provides a direct precedent for treating female legal allegories as *formulae of pathos* placed in a feminist-atlasal arrangement that disrupts the patrilineal canon of state iconography.

### Bourdieu / Artl@s / Feminist Cartographies

Bourdieu's champ juridique ("The Force of Law," *Hastings Law Journal* 38, 1987) posits law as a field of struggle over the monopoly of legitimate symbolic violence. Female legal allegories participate in this struggle: they are commissioned, displayed, and reproduced by agents within the legal field (state architects, judicial academies, constitutional tribunals) to consolidate and legitimate state authority. The Bourdieusian field analysis asks: who commissions these images? What is their position in the field of power? How does the habitus of legal image-makers reproduce or transform the received formulae of Justitia?

The [Artl@s project](https://artlas.huma-num.fr/en/about/) (ENS Paris, founded by Joyeux-Prunel, 2009) applies Bourdieu's field theory spatially through cartographic visualization: «a perspective nourished by critical and sociological theory that aims to highlight not only the link between a work of art and its space, but also its underlying political, social, aesthetic or economic issues.» Artl@s makes Bourdieu's field visible as a map — the direct technical precedent for a cartographic mapping of the juridical imaginary.

Haraway's situated knowledges, Braidotti's nomadic cartography («a theoretically-based and politically-informed reading of the present which fulfils the function of providing both analytic and exegetical tools for critical thought and also creative theoretical alternatives» — [*Comparative Critical Studies* 11:2–3, 2014](https://rosibraidotti.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Braidotti-Rosi-Writing-as-a-Nomadic-Subject.pdf)), and Rolnik's *cartografia sentimental* ([Sulina/UFRGS, 2014](https://www.scribd.com/document/420971831/343746847-ROLNIK-Suely-Cartografia-Sentimental-pdf-pdf)) add the political-ethical and Brazilian-situated dimensions that Artl@s, as a positivist-Bourdieusian project, cannot itself supply.

### The Three-Axis Framework

The following synthesis represents the author's original interpretive proposal, elaborated from Stream 5, explicitly labelled as such.

**[interpretation / author's synthesis — three-axis framework]**

**Axis (a): Diachronic Archaeology of the Legal Dispositif — Warburg × Foucault**

The Justitia allegory is treated as a dispositif-image: it participates in Foucault's dispositif of state sovereignty but its historical duration exceeds any particular dispositif. The allegory persists across discontinuous legal orders (Roman law, medieval canon law, Enlightenment natural law, modern constitutional states) because it operates not at the level of meaning but at the level of force: it exerts affective-political pressure on legal subjects independently of semantic content. The methodological operation is to identify moments of iconographic rupture (Foucauldian discontinuity) AND trace survivals across those ruptures (Warburgian Nachleben) — producing a differential archaeology of the juridical imaginary.

**Axis (b): Mapping the Field of Juridical Vision — Bourdieu × Artl@s × Feminist Cartography**

Bourdieu's champ juridique + Haraway's situated knowledges generate a question neither poses alone: from where is the Justitia image seen, and by whom? A field analysis maps positions of agents who commission, display, and reproduce the female allegory. A feminist cartography makes the asymmetry of this scopic regime visible: the female body is constituted as object of the legal gaze even as it is used to represent the instrument of that gaze. The technical model is Artl@s; the feminist-ethical inflection is Rolnik's affective cartography and Braidotti's nomadic mapping.

**Axis (c): The Thesis-as-Atlas, Curatorial Model — Pollock's VFM**

The doctoral thesis is not just an argument; it is an atlas — a series of panels juxtaposing Justitia images across time, place, and commission context. Each panel enacts Pollock's "encounter" between images, revealing the Pathosformel beneath the surface variation. The atlas is organized not chronologically but thematically: by formula (scales, blindfold, sword), by commission context (ecclesiastical, royal, republican, corporate), and by gender trouble (bare-breasted/draped, armed/passive, seeing/blind). The thesis-atlas has three simultaneous dimensions: diachronic (Warburg), relational (Bourdieu/Foucault), and feminist-cartographic (Haraway/Pollock/Rolnik).

**The concept of the "juridical imaginary"** — after Cornelius Castoriadis's social imaginary and Pierre Legendre's juridical-theological imaginary — designates the ensemble of visual, textual, and affective forms through which the legal order represents itself to itself and its subjects. The female legal allegory condenses in a single image the masculinist symbolic order's need to represent justice as simultaneously powerful (armed) and impartial (blind), feminine (nurturing, pure) and sovereign (sword-bearing). The Warburgian cartographic approach makes this condensation visible by tracing its historical depth, relational field positions, and feminist blind-spots simultaneously.

## 7. Assessment and Recommendation

**Ranked comparison of five candidate methods:**

| Rank | Method | Strength | Weakness | Fit for Thesis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Warburgian cartography hybrid** (Warburg × Foucault × Bourdieu × feminist cartography) | Accounts for temporal survival, power-field positioning, feminist critique, and Brazilian particularity simultaneously | Demands methodological transparency and explicit authorial positioning | Highest |
| 2 | **Atlas-topological arrangement** (Pollock VFM model, standalone) | Makes researcher's analytical moves visible; generates knowledge by adjacency; consistent with situated-knowledges | Requires significant curatorial labor; must be defended against charge of impressionism | Very High |
| 3 | **Panofskian iconology** | Systematic, widely understood, established legal iconographic tradition (Goodrich, Hayaert) | Teleological, vertical, pretends to decode images step-by-step; Bal's critique is decisive against it as primary method | Medium — use only as first descriptive layer |
| 4 | **Distant viewing** (Arnold/Tilton) | Enables corpus-scale discovery; identifies formal patterns across hundreds of images | Epistemic mismatch with humanistic inquiry (Drucker); Pathosformel resists quantification | Low as primary method — High as corpus discovery tool |
| 5 | **Scored LPAI** (reframed as capta) | Quantifies absences (Resnik & Curtis finding); generates cross-national comparability | Performs Merry's seductions; conceals interpretive choices; Warner's paradox cannot be scored | Low as primary analysis — acceptable as supplementary documentation tool |

**Best fit recommendation:**

The **Warburgian cartography hybrid** (Axis a + b + c as outlined in Section 6) is recommended as the primary methodological framework, for two reasons. First, it is the only framework that accounts for all three dimensions of the thesis's object simultaneously: the temporal survival of allegorical forms (Nachleben), the institutional positioning of those forms within juridical fields of power (Bourdieu/Foucault), and the gendered exclusion performed by female allegory (feminist cartographies). Second, it is methodologically defensible: Didi-Huberman's extensive theoretical elaboration, Pollock's practical implementation, and the Engramma and Erdteilallegorien digital projects provide established precedents. The thesis does not need to invent the methodology; it needs to apply it to a corpus for which it has never been used.

**What to retain from the existing LPAI:**

If the researcher keeps the LPAI, it should be reframed explicitly as capta and deployed only for (a) corpus discovery — identifying candidate images from BNDigital, Hemeroteca, and Brasiliana Iconográfica for close reading — and (b) documenting absence: how many Brazilian courthouse statues depict Justice as a Black woman? How many as Indigenous? The index becomes a tool for systematically registering what the iconographic tradition *excludes*, rather than what it includes. This is Resnik and Curtis's method — and it is powerful precisely because it transforms the score into a political argument rather than a neutral measurement.

**Explicit caveats and risks:**

- The Warburgian cartographic method requires the researcher to make every juxtaposition decision explicit and defensible; this is more demanding, not less, than a scored index. The thesis must include a methodological chapter that names the choices.
- The feminist cartographic axes (Rolnik, Braidotti) risk being decorative additions rather than analytical tools unless they generate specific interpretive claims about specific images.
- The Brazilian corpus is underrepresented in all existing scholarly literature on this subject (Carvalho, Schwarcz, and Lima Júnior are exceptions). The thesis must supplement library-based iconographic analysis with archival research in BNDigital, IMS, and Arquivo Nacional.
- Stream 1's key supporting source — Stramignoni 2024 — is a single article in a specialist journal. If the thesis positions itself as building on Stramignoni, it must also explain the gap between his "nocturnal jurisprudence" and the feminist-cartographic dimension the thesis adds.

### Empirical Gaps the Thesis Can Fill

**Gap 1 — No Warburgian reading of Justitia by name.** The thesis can provide the first sustained application of Pathosformel, Nachleben, and Denkraum to the iconographic complex of Brazilian female legal allegory (Justitia, *A República*, *A Pátria*). **Gap 2 — No pan-Ibero-American comparative study.** All existing legal iconographic databases are North Atlantic. The thesis can be the first to systematically document the Brazilian/Ibero-American tradition through the BNDigital Hemeroteca, Brasiliana Iconográfica, and IMS archives.
