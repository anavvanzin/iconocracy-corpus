# *From Allegory to Simulacrum*: Legal Literacy and Visual Regimes of the Law in Brazil

## Ana Vitória Vanzin Mendes

---

## I. Introduction: The Scene of Illegibility

On January 8, 2023, rioters entered Brazil's Supreme Federal Court and marked Alfredo Ceschiatti's 1961 sculpture *A Justiça* with red lipstick, scrawling "Perdeu, Mané" ("You lost, sucker"). Simultaneously, another rioter seized a replica of the 1988 Constitution, draped it in the Brazilian flag, and held it aloft as a sacred relic. These acts were not mere vandalism; they were contests over the regime of visibility through which law becomes publicly legible. They reveal a rupture in the visual-legal order undergirding state authority.

What constitutes legal knowledge? This project answers by rejecting the premise that law is primarily textual. Modern constitutional authority operates through a *regime of visibility*—an economy of images that secures legitimacy while remaining structurally vulnerable to counter-literacy: practices that simultaneously re-read and re-authorize legal symbols as claims to alternative institutional authority.

The insurrection crystallizes a paradox: citizens fought not against law itself, but over the images that make law intelligible. By treating January 8 as a "literacy event" where competing interpretations of legal symbols violently clashed, this project develops **Visual Legal History** as a methodology for understanding how normativity is translated, contested, and potentially undone.

**Research Questions:**

1. How does the *regime of visibility* function within the Brazilian legal imaginary?
2. What explains the structural instability of post-colonial legal iconographies?
3. How does the female body operate as a privileged interface of state legitimacy amid women's political exclusion?
4. How does digital religious discourse create rival systems of legal knowledge that compete with institutional law?
5. What does the ritualistic appropriation of legal artifacts reveal about the fragility of modern constitutional orders?

## II. Four Conceptual Interventions

### Tropical Iconocracy

Existing scholarship on legal iconography treats imported symbols as stable or gradually acculturated. This project theorizes instead *post-colonial instability*: the permanent tension between the universalist promise of imported legal images and their contested realization in societies marked by deep historical rupture.

Brazil's visual-legal order inherited European allegorical grammar (female personifications of the nation, justice, order) at the moment of the Republic's proclamation in 1889. Yet this order was *born fragmented*. Unlike the French or American republics, forged in insurrections that produced powerful *lieux de mémoire*—the Bastille, Valley Forge—Brazil's Republic emerged from a military coup negotiated among narrow elites, producing no founding myths capable of binding masses to the regime (Carvalho 1990). The early republican allegory of the nation, derived from Comte's positivist vision of a *femme-mère-vierge*, was quickly resignified in popular caricatures as a prostitute, an object of ridicule "even by those who had initially supported the new regime" (Carvalho 1990, 88).

This nineteenth-century failure of symbolic translation foreshadows January 8. The lipstick-marked Justice statue echoes earlier degradations of the female allegory. Tropical Iconocracy names this recurrent phenomenon: in post-colonial contexts, imported legal iconographies remain permanently open to violent counter-reading. The fragility is structural, not accidental.

### The Visual Sexual Contract

Carole Pateman theorized that the modern social contract is preceded by a *sexual contract* establishing men's political rights over women. This project extends her insight into the visual-legal sphere: the female allegorical body functions as a medium for legal knowledge while simultaneously masking gendered exclusions—what I term the **Visual Sexual Contract**.

The proliferation of the female body as the primary interface of the state—Justice, the Republic, the Nation—occurs precisely when actual women are systematically excluded from political agency and legal capacity. The female allegory functions as Marina Warner's (1985) "negative imprint of domination": an idealized, disembodied form substituting for the presence of real women in the public sphere.

For subaltern actors, particularly women, legal literacy becomes paradoxical: how does one "read" the law when its primary visual interface is a feminized body that represents both abstract authority and one's own structural exclusion?

The vandalism of *A Justiça* by Débora Rodrigues crystallizes this paradox. A female iconoclast defaced a female allegory—yet not to dismantle patriarchal representation. Rather, she re-appropriated the object itself, marking it with quintessentially feminine material (lipstick) as an act of partisan desecration within a discourse of "spiritual warfare." The contradiction is profound: Rodrigues did not reject the allegory's authority; she sought to capture it.

### The Theologico-Political Simulacrum

Existing legal historiography has not adequately theorized how digital media and religious discourse combine to produce new regimes of legal (il)literacy. The concept of the **Theologico-Political Simulacrum** addresses this gap by analyzing how "spiritual warfare" rhetoric creates a *rival normative order* with its own sources of legitimacy.

Within Bolsonarista networks circulating on WhatsApp and Telegram, the Brazilian state and its legal institutions are depicted not as political opponents but as *demonic entities*. The Supreme Federal Court is systematically coded as satanic; Bolsonaro is framed as a messianic figure chosen by God (The Revealer 2023). This process does not merely spread disinformation; it generates an alternative system of legal authorization (divine will, prophetic declaration) that competes directly with institutional law.

Drawing on Carl Schmitt's political theology and Jean Baudrillard's theory of simulation, this framework illuminates how a segment of the Brazilian populace inhabits a dualistic, Manichaean worldview where political opponents are coded as demonic and state institutions as profane. The January 8 insurrection must be understood as an eruption of this alternative legal order into physical space.

### Fetishized Legal Literacy

The theft of a replica of the 1988 Constitution by Marcelo Fernandes Lima constitutes a paradigmatic case of what this project terms **Fetishized Legal Literacy**: a form of material counter-literacy where legal objects are ritually appropriated and invested with extra-legal (theological, charismatic) meaning, displacing institutional legal knowledge.

Lima held the replica aloft not as a rejection of law, but as a claim to a *higher*, divinely sanctioned legality. The replica—a copy devoid of juridical force—was transmuted into a talismanic artifact, a sacred relic, while the institution it represented (the Supreme Federal Court) was simultaneously desecrated. This act of counter-translation detaches the legal object from its institutional context and reinvests it with pneumatic efficacy that supersedes positive law.

The paradox is acute: the material copy becomes more legible, more authoritative, than the institution. Legal literacy inverts: fetish supersedes institution.

## III. Brasília and the Modernist Shift

The construction of Brasília in the late 1950s marked a fundamental transformation in Brazil's visual-legal order. Designed by Lúcio Costa and Oscar Niemeyer, the new capital embodied a shift from narrative, European-style allegory to modernist abstraction.

At the heart of Brasília stands *A Praça dos Três Poderes* (Three Powers Plaza), where the judiciary's primary symbol is not a narrative painting but Alfredo Ceschiatti's 1961 *A Justiça*: a seated, blindfolded female figure carved from granite, embodying abstract concepts of impartiality through pure form and mass. This shift from readable allegory to minimalist abstraction fundamentally altered the demands on legal literacy. Citizens are no longer asked to decode a narrative; they are asked to intuitively grasp authority conveyed by geometric abstraction and spatial arrangement—a mode of communication more vulnerable to counter-interpretation.

January 8 targeted precisely this modernist Justice. The lipstick mark violated the sculpture's austere formalism, reinscribing particularistic meaning (an insult to Justice Minister Alexandre de Moraes) onto abstract legal authority. The act exposed abstraction's fragility: it cannot resist partisan re-reading.

## IV. January 8, 2023: A Literacy Event

The insurrection of January 8 functions as a culminating literacy event revealing the rupture in Brazil's visual-legal order. Two emblematic episodes suffice.

**The Lipstick Defacement**

Débora Rodrigues marked *A Justiça* with red lipstick, scrawling "Perdeu, Mané"—a taunt directed at Supreme Court Justice Alexandre de Moraes. This act crystallizes the Visual Sexual Contract's internal contradictions: a female iconoclast defaced a female allegory not to dismantle gender representation but to hijack it. Within the theological framework animating many rioters, the act functioned to desecrate the "universal mother" of abstract law, reducing her to an object of partisan humiliation. The use of lipstick—quintessentially feminine material—to profane a monumental female body exposed the theological fragility inherent in female allegory itself.

**The Constitution as Relic**

Marcelo Fernandes Lima seized a replica of the 1988 Constitution, draped it in the national flag, and held it aloft as a sacred object. The act was paradigmatic of Fetishized Legal Literacy: the juridically inert copy was transformed into a talisman, invested with pneumatic efficacy (divine authorization) that superseded institutional law. The rioter did not reject legality; he claimed access to a *higher* legality, one sanctioned by divine will rather than institutional procedure.

These episodes reveal that January 8 was not a rejection of law but a violent contest over *how law becomes legible*—over the regime of visibility itself.

## V. Comparative Perspective: Post-Colonial Fragility

The fragility of imported legal iconographies is not unique to Brazil. Three comparative cases illuminate the broader phenomenon:

**#RhodesMustFall (South Africa, 2015):** Student activists targeted colonial-era monuments as acts of "violent interpretation" (de Villiers-Human 2022), rejecting the legitimacy of inherited symbols while claiming counter-authority.

**January 6 (United States, 2021):** The storming of the Capitol combined religious revivalism, conspiratorial symbols (QAnon), and attacks on institutional architecture—a parallel theologico-political spectacle where rival iconographies (Christian nationalism) contest the visual order of secular state authority.

***Bharat Mata* (India):** Ongoing tensions between indigenous Hindu nationalism and colonial-inherited legal iconography reveal persistent contestation over which visual regime legitimates state authority in post-colonial contexts.

These cases suggest that Tropical Iconocracy names a broader post-colonial phenomenon: the structural vulnerability of imported legal symbolism to violent counter-reading in contexts where foundational legitimacy remains contested.

## VI. Methodology: Visual Legal History

This project advances **Visual Legal History** as a methodological contribution to legal scholarship. Rather than treating images as illustrations of law, this approach reads visual and material sources as primary evidence—as *deontic images* (Siniscalchi 2021) and *legal emblems* (Goodrich 2013) that perform normative work.

Acts of iconoclasm are interpreted not as simple vandalism but as forms of "violent interpretation" that reveal the power invested in legal symbols themselves. The vandalised artifacts of January 8 become primary sources for tracing how law becomes publicly legible and how that legibility fractures.

The project's corpus includes:

- Official iconography (allegorical paintings, sculptures, architectural programs, currency)
- Material legal artifacts (judicial robes, constitutional replicas, courtroom furniture)
- Ephemera and media (political cartoons, newspaper photographs, digital memes)
- Digital archives (images and videos circulated on WhatsApp, Telegram, Twitter/X)

This methodology extends the Max Planck Institute's global legal history tradition to non-textual sources, treating allegory, architecture, and circulated images as evidence for how normativity is translated across media and how that translation becomes contested.

---

## VII. Scholarly Gaps and Significance

**European Dominance in Legal Iconography:** Existing scholarship (Resnik & Curtis 2011; Goodrich 2013) focuses predominantly on European and North American contexts. This project addresses the gap by centering post-colonial Brazil as a site where imported legal iconographies are structurally unstable.

**Neglect of Non-Textual Legal Knowledge:** Legal history remains text-bound. By treating images, objects, and rituals as constitutive of legal knowledge, this project challenges disciplinary assumptions about what counts as legal evidence.

**Gender Analysis Absent from Legal History:** While feminist theory has critiqued the sexual contract (Pateman 1988) and visual culture studies has analyzed female allegory (Warner 1985), legal history has not systematically theorized the gendered infrastructure of legal authority itself.

**Digital Legal History Nascent:** The intersection of digital media, religious discourse, and legal authority remains under-theorized in legal scholarship. This project pioneers analysis of how digital platforms generate alternative regimes of legal (il)literacy.

---

## VIII. Conclusion: Reading the Rubble

The insurrection of January 8, 2023, was not a failure of legal literacy but a spectacular revelation of its fragility. By treating the scene as a literacy event, this project reads state authority through the ruptures in its own visual order.

When citizens mark a Justice statue with lipstick and elevate a constitutional replica to sacred relic, they expose a fundamental paradox: modern law depends on images it cannot fully control. The regime of visibility that legitimates state authority is simultaneously the site of its radical contestation.

This project asks: how does legal authority re-legitimate itself after such iconoclastic violence? What visual regimes might follow? And what does the Brazilian case teach us about the fragility of post-colonial constitutional orders?

By advancing Visual Legal History as methodology and theorizing four concepts—Tropical Iconocracy, the Visual Sexual Contract, the Theologico-Political Simulacrum, and Fetishized Legal Literacy—this project reads legal history through the rubble of its own symbols.
