## **I. RESEARCH QUESTION AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK**

### **A. Central Question**

**How did modern legal states construct legitimacy through visual iconography when the vast majority of their populations could not read legal texts?** 

This project investigates a fundamental paradox in the historical construction of legal authority: while law claims to operate through written codes, statutes, and doctrines accessible primarily to literate elites, most historical populations encountered the state's authority through **visual and material infrastructures**—coins, stamps, courthouse façades, and monumental sculptures. This dissertation redefines **legal literacy** beyond textual competence to encompass **visual/symbolic literacy**: the historically situated capacity to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material cues, spatial arrangements, and iconographic systems.

### **B. The Visual Sexual Contract: Core Theoretical Innovation**

The project's central theoretical innovation is the concept of the **Visual Sexual Contract**, which extends Carole Pateman's foundational analysis of the sexual contract to the domain of visual culture and legal iconography. This framework explains a striking historical pattern: **precisely when women were most systematically excluded from political agency, civil capacity, and the legal profession (18th–early 20th centuries), the female body proliferated as the omnipresent official face of Law and the Nation.**

The Visual Sexual Contract operates through three distinct mechanisms:

1. **Substitution**: The allegorical woman occupies the symbolic space where real women are absent. Marianne represents the French Republic, but French women cannot vote until 1944. The image serves as a "phantom" presence, allowing the state to claim it represents "humanity" while only empowering men.

2. **Sacralization**: The feminine figure is elevated to a plane of moral purity precisely because she is removed from the "grubby" reality of political bargaining. Justice is pure because she does not participate in the dirty work of politics. She sits above it, frozen in stone.

3. **De-sexualization and Disciplining**: To function as a state symbol, the female body must be disciplined. It is draped in heavy classical robes, maternalized, or monumentalized to strip it of erotic or threatening agency. The "orderly" body replaces the "disorderly" body of the witch or revolutionary virago.

This arrangement represents a form of **allegorical hypervisibility** that functions as a substitute for political inclusion. Women are everywhere as symbols, nowhere as citizens. The allegory teaches populations "what law looks like" while simultaneously masking the gendered exclusions of the actual legal order.

### **C. The January 8, 2023 Crisis as Diagnostic Moment**

The empirical fulcrum for this investigation is the **January 8, 2023, attack on Brazil's Supreme Federal Court** (Supremo Tribunal Federal, STF), when supporters of former President Jair Bolsonaro stormed the Praça dos Três Poderes in Brasília. Rioters defaced Alfredo Ceschiatti's monumental sculpture *A Justiça* (1961)—a 3.3-meter carved granite figure dominating the court's entrance—by scrawling **"Perdeu, mané"** (You lost, fool) across its chest in red lipstick.

This was not collateral vandalism. The attack was carefully targeted. The phrase itself was a direct quotation from Justice Luís Roberto Barroso, spoken dismissively to a Bolsonaro supporter in New York in November 2022: "Perdeu, mané, não amola" (You lost, fool, don't bother me).

By inscribing Barroso's partisan, contemptuous words onto the body of the abstract, universal Goddess of Justice, the rioters performed an act of **iconoclastic unmasking**—a violent revelation that the statue's universal pretensions were a mask concealing partisan judicial politics. They demonstrated sophisticated **counter-literacy**: the ability to read and rewrite state iconography against the grain, to collapse the careful distinction between the Law's immortal "body politic" (transcendent Justice, blind and impartial) and its mortal "body natural" (fallible judges with biases and contempt).

This event forces legal historians to confront troubling questions:

- **On what material, affective, and visual foundations rests the legitimacy of modern legal orders?**
- **If that legitimacy can be destroyed by forcing a stone statue to speak a partisan judge's voice, how stable are the legal systems built upon such symbolic infrastructure?**
- **What does the attempted rupture of visual continuity reveal about the fragility of democratic legitimacy in the 21st century?**

## **II. STATE OF THE FIELD AND RESEARCH GAP**

### **A. Legal History and Visual Culture**

This project stands at the intersection of three established but insufficiently integrated scholarly fields:

**1. Legal History and Constitutional Theory**

Traditional legal history focuses on the evolution of doctrines, institutions, and black-letter law. Recent work by scholars such as Peter Goodrich (*Legal Emblems and the Art of Law*, 2013), Judith Resnik and Dennis Curtis (*Representing Justice*, 2011), and Valérie Hayaert (*Lady Justice: An Anatomy of Allegory*, 2023) has begun to take legal iconography seriously. However, these studies treat images primarily as **illustrations** of legal concepts rather than as **constitutive** of legal authority itself.

**Research Gap**: No systematic historical analysis exists that treats visual iconography as **primary legal infrastructure**—as the background knowledge that made law legible and enforceable for non-literate populations.

**2. Visual Culture and Political Iconography**

Art historians and visual culture scholars have extensively documented the proliferation of female national allegories. Maurice Agulhon's foundational work on Marianne (*Marianne au combat*, 1979; *Marianne au pouvoir*, 1989), Marina Warner's *Monuments and Maidens* (1985), and Joan Landes's *Visualizing the Nation* (2001) provide rich empirical documentation of how female figures came to personify abstract republican virtues.

**Research Gap**: These studies focus primarily on **national identity** and political symbolism, largely neglecting the specific domain of **legal legitimacy**. They do not systematically analyze how female allegories functioned as **legal pedagogy**—teaching populations how to recognize, trust, and obey the law.

**3. Feminist Political Theory and the Sexual Contract**

Carole Pateman's *The Sexual Contract* (1988) demonstrated that the social contract rests on a prior fraternal settlement organizing women's subordination. Subsequent feminist scholarship has extended this analysis across multiple domains of political theory. Silvia Federici's *Caliban and the Witch* (2004) documented how the transition to capitalism required the violent subjugation of women's bodies and knowledge.

**Research Gap**: Feminist political theory has not systematically extended its analysis to the **visual domain**. The question of *why* the female body proliferates as the symbol of states that exclude women has not received sustained theoretical attention linking visual culture to the sexual contract.

### **B. This Project's Contribution**

This dissertation fills these gaps by:

1. **Treating images as primary legal sources** constituting the "background knowledge" through which most populations—illiterate, subaltern, excluded from the legal profession—actually encountered the state's authority.

2. **Extending the sexual contract thesis to visual culture**, demonstrating that allegorical hypervisibility is not incidental but **structurally necessary** to modern legal orders built on women's exclusion.

3. **Developing a rigorous methodology** (Iconographic Reconstruction for Legal History) that legal historians can apply to analyze how legitimacy is visually staged, maintained, and destroyed.

4. **Providing the first systematic comparative analysis** of female legal allegories across jurisdictions (France, Britain, Germany, Switzerland, Brazil, USA) and media (coins, stamps, statues, architecture) from 1789 to 2023.

5. **Linking historical analysis to contemporary crisis**, demonstrating that the January 8 attacks represent not an aberration but the logical endpoint of tensions embedded in visual legal legitimacy since the Enlightenment.

## **III. TEMPORAL AND GEOGRAPHICAL SCOPE**

### **A. Periodization: 1789–2023**

The project spans from the **French Revolution** (1789)—which created the modern problem of visualizing the abstract republican state—to the **January 8, 2023, Brasília attacks**—which represent a crisis in that visual regime.

This periodization is divided into three phases:

**Phase 1: Consolidation (1789–1870)**  
The invention and stabilization of female republican allegory during the revolutionary and post-revolutionary period. Key developments:
- The decapitation of Louis XVI and the representational vacuum
- The creation of Marianne, Libertas, Columbia, Britannia
- The proliferation of female figures on coins, stamps, and state seals
- The codification of iconographic attributes (blindfold, scales, sword, Phrygian cap, laurel wreath)

**Phase 2: Global Dissemination (1870–1945)**  
The transatlantic and transnational circulation of female legal/national allegories. Key developments:
- The spread of the iconographic model to Latin America, including Brazil (1889 Republic)
- The standardization of Justice iconography in courthouse architecture
- The "Lag Analysis" period: measuring the gap between allegorical consolidation and women's suffrage
- The emergence of contestation (suffragette defacement of coins, early feminist critique)

**Phase 3: Modernist Abstraction and Contemporary Crisis (1945–2023)**  
The transformation of allegory in the postwar period and its crisis in the 21st century. Key developments:
- The construction of Brasília (1956–1960) and Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* (1961)
- The partial obsolescence of allegory following women's suffrage
- The "living allegory" controversies (2013 Marianne/Inna Shevchenko stamp)
- The January 8, 2023, iconoclastic attack and attempted rupture

### **B. Geographical Scope: Comparative Atlantic Focus**

The core geographical focus is the **Atlantic world**—France, Britain, Germany, Switzerland, United States, and Brazil—with selective incorporation of other jurisdictions where relevant.

**France** serves as the **genealogical origin**: the French Revolution invented modern republican allegory, and Marianne became the template emulated globally.

**Brazil** serves as the **empirical endpoint**: the January 8 attacks on Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* provide the contemporary crisis that structures the entire investigation.

The project employs a **circulating reference model**: tracing how iconographic motifs (attributes, poses, styles) circulated through prints, coins, diplomatic gifts, and international exhibitions, creating a transnational visual literacy that transcended linguistic barriers.

## **IV. METHODOLOGICAL FRAMEWORK**

### **A. Iconographic Reconstruction Protocol for Legal History**

This project develops a rigorous seven-step protocol entitled **Iconographic Reconstruction for Legal History**, which treats images as legal texts requiring the same analytical rigor as statutes or case law.

#### **Step 1: Iconographic Description**

**Objective**: Systematic cataloging of visual attributes, noting both presences and absences.

**Procedure**:
- Document all symbolic attributes (scales, sword, blindfold, cap, laurel wreath, torch, tablet, etc.)
- Record posture (seated vs. standing, active vs. passive)
- Note dress (classical drapery, contemporary clothing, armor, nudity)
- Identify **absences** as analytically significant (e.g., Ceschiatti's missing scales)
- Measure dimensions and materials (marble, granite, bronze, wood)

**Theoretical Rationale**: What is included—and crucially, what is excluded—reveals historical priorities. The absence of scales from Ceschiatti's Justice is not neutral; it restructures the statue's meaning.

#### **Step 2: Pedagogy of Encounter**

**Objective**: Analyzing the social and spatial conditions under which populations encountered the image.

**Procedure**:
- Determine the image's location (public square, courthouse pediment, coin in pocket, stamp on letter)
- Identify the intended audience (elites, general public, illiterate workers, women, children)
- Assess frequency of encounter (daily, occasional, once in a lifetime)
- Analyze scale relationships (monumental vs. miniature, viewed from below vs. held in hand)
- Document accompanying texts (inscriptions, legends, captions)

**Theoretical Rationale**: The same iconographic motif functions differently depending on how it is encountered. A Justice statue viewed from below in a courthouse generates awe and submission. A Justice on a coin held in the hand generates intimacy and trust. Understanding **where and how** the image appears is essential to understanding its pedagogy.

**Key Categories**:
- **Monumental Verticality**: Statues, courthouse pediments, public squares (soliciting awe, fear, submission)
- **Intimate Ubiquity**: Coins, banknotes (generating familiarity, trust, everyday presence of the state)
- **Global Communication**: Stamps (projecting national identity internationally)
- **Authenticating Paratexts**: Seals, letterheads, code frontispieces (marking documents as legally valid)

#### **Step 3: Intervisual Genealogy**

**Objective**: Tracing the circulation and mutation of iconographic motifs across time, space, and media.

**Procedure**:
- Identify the visual "ancestors" of a given image (e.g., Does this Brazilian Justice cite French Marianne? Does she reference Roman Justitia or Egyptian Ma'at?)
- Map circulation routes (through prints, engravings, international exhibitions, diplomatic gifts)
- Document transformations as motifs move across borders (What changes? What persists?)
- Analyze "visual borrowing" and "iconographic colonialism" (How do peripheral nations import center iconography?)

**Theoretical Rationale**: Images are never created ex nihilo. They cite, remix, and transform prior visual forms. Tracing these genealogies reveals the **transnational visual literacy** that made law recognizable across linguistic and cultural boundaries.

**Methodological Inspiration**: This step draws on Aby Warburg's concept of **Pathosformel** (pathos formula)—the migration of emotionally charged visual forms across centuries and cultures. Warburg demonstrated that Renaissance artists "quoted" ancient Greco-Roman poses and gestures, creating a visual memory that persisted beneath conscious awareness. This project applies similar logic to legal iconography.

#### **Step 4: Gendered Operation**

**Objective**: Analyzing how the femininity of the allegorical figure functions within the legal/political order.

**Procedure**:
- Determine the affective register: Does the figure solicit maternal love? Erotic desire? Filial obedience? Fear?
- Assess how the female body is "disciplined": Is she nude or clothed? Eroticized or de-sexualized? Active or passive?
- Identify whether she represents virtues (Justice, Liberty, Equality) or territorial entities (the Nation, the Republic, the Empire)
- Analyze the relationship between the allegorical female body and real women's legal status in the jurisdiction
- Apply **Lag Analysis**: Measure the temporal gap between allegorical consolidation and women's suffrage

**Theoretical Rationale**: The femininity of legal allegories is not decorative. It performs specific ideological work: generating affective bonds, masking exclusions, and substituting symbolic presence for political agency. Understanding **how** femininity functions is central to understanding the Visual Sexual Contract.

**Key Questions**:
- Why is Justice always female? (Grammatical accident? Political strategy? Theological inheritance?)
- What is the relationship between the "ideal" woman of the allegory and the "real" women excluded from the courtroom?
- How does allegorical femininity change after women's suffrage? (Does the allegory become obsolete? Contested? Transformed?)

#### **Step 5: Contestation Analysis**

**Objective**: Documenting and analyzing how images have been challenged, mocked, satirized, vandalized, or destroyed.

**Procedure**:
- Collect evidence of iconoclasm (physical vandalism, graffiti, statue toppling)
- Identify satirical reinterpretations (cartoons, memes, parodies)
- Document "counter-iconography" (alternative images created by excluded groups)
- Analyze state responses to iconoclasm (prosecution, restoration, memorialization)
- Theorize what contestation reveals about the fragility of visual legitimacy

**Theoretical Rationale**: Contestation is a form of critical "reading" of state iconography. When suffragettes defaced pennies bearing Britannia's image, they demonstrated sophisticated visual literacy—recognizing that the state's claim to represent "the people" excluded them. When January 8 rioters inscribed partisan words on Justice's chest, they collapsed the distinction between abstract universality and concrete partisanship. Contestation reveals the **counter-literacy** of subordinated populations.

**Historical Examples to Analyze**:
- Suffragette defacement of British coins (1913–1914)
- Indigenous attacks on Brazilian *bandeirante* monuments (2013, 2021)
- Borba Gato statue burning (2021)
- 2013 Marianne stamp controversy (Inna Shevchenko, FEMEN activist)
- January 8, 2023, Brasília attacks

#### **Step 6: Affective Registers and Emotional Mobilization**

**Objective**: Analyzing the emotions images are designed to generate in different audiences.

**Procedure**:
- Identify the intended emotional response (awe, love, fear, trust, submission, pride)
- Assess how different populations may have experienced the same image differently
- Document textual evidence of emotional responses (memoirs, letters, judicial decisions)
- Theorize the relationship between affect and legitimacy

**Theoretical Rationale**: Legal legitimacy is not merely cognitive (believing law is valid) but **affective** (feeling loyalty, trust, or fear toward legal institutions). Images are technologies for generating these emotions. The blindfolded Justice generates trust in impartiality; the armed Justice generates fear of punishment; the maternal Republic generates filial loyalty.

**Key Questions**:
- What emotions stabilize legal legitimacy? (Trust? Fear? Love? Awe?)
- How do emotions differ by population? (Elites vs. workers? Men vs. women? Citizens vs. non-citizens?)
- When do images lose their affective power? (January 8 suggests: when counter-literacy proliferates)

#### **Step 7: Material Vulnerability and the Conditions of Destruction**

**Objective**: Understanding under what conditions images can be successfully contested or destroyed.

**Procedure**:
- Assess material properties (Can the statue be easily defaced? Is the coin too small to vandalize effectively?)
- Analyze spatial positioning (Is the image accessible? Protected by barriers or guards?)
- Evaluate cultural/legal protections (Is it heritage? Sacrosanct? Permitted to mock?)
- Theorize the relationship between material form and ideological resilience

**Theoretical Rationale**: Not all images are equally vulnerable. Coins circulate intimately but are too small to effectively vandalize (though suffragettes managed). Statues are monumental but accessible to vandalism if unguarded. Digital images can be infinitely reproduced but also infinitely altered. Understanding material vulnerability helps explain why certain images become targets during political crises.

**Case Study: Ceschiatti's Modernist Abstraction as Vulnerability**

Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* exemplifies how modernist abstraction created iconographic vulnerability. By removing the scales and simplifying the figure, Ceschiatti intended to express timeless, universal Justice. However, this abstraction created a "blanker screen" onto which multiple interpretations could be projected. Unlike neoclassical Justice figures with clearly legible attributes, Ceschiatti's statue was enigmatic—and therefore vulnerable to partisan reinterpretation.

### **B. Corpus and Archival Strategy**

The project will curate a **comparative corpus of visual legal artifacts** from 1789 to 2023, focusing on four "supports" with distinct circulation logics:

#### **1. Monetary Iconography (Coins and Banknotes)**

**Why Monetary Iconography Matters**: Money represents the "intimate ubiquity" of the state. Every market transaction involves touching the allegory. For illiterate populations, coins were often the **only** regular encounter with state symbolism.

**Research Sites**:
- Bibliothèque nationale de France (Cabinet des Médailles)
- British Museum (Department of Coins and Medals)
- Münzkabinett, Staatliche Museen zu Berlin
- Museu Histórico Nacional, Rio de Janeiro
- American Numismatic Society, New York

**Analytical Focus**:
- When do female allegories first appear on national currencies?
- How do monetary images change during regime transitions? (Empire to Republic, Monarchy to Democracy)
- What is the relationship between the face on the coin and the "face" of sovereignty?

#### **2. Postal and Philatelic Iconography**

**Why Stamps Matter**: Stamps are "global communicators" of national identity. They project the nation's self-image internationally while simultaneously educating domestic populations about national virtues.

**Research Sites**:
- Archives nationales, Paris (postal records)
- British Postal Museum
- Arquivo Nacional, Brasília
- Smithsonian National Postal Museum, Washington D.C.

**Analytical Focus**:
- How do Justice/Republic figures circulate globally through stamps?
- What iconographic choices do newly independent nations make? (Do they innovate or imitate European models?)
- The 2013 Marianne/Inna Shevchenko controversy: What happens when the "model" behind the allegory is revealed?

#### **3. Monumental Programs (Statues and Courthouse Architecture)**

**Why Monuments Matter**: Monuments operate through "monumental verticality"—dominating public space, soliciting awe from below, marking the boundary between profane and sacred space.

**Research Sites**:
- On-site documentation at:
  - Palais de Justice, Paris
  - Old Bailey, London
  - Reichsgericht, Leipzig / Bundesgerichtshof, Karlsruhe
  - Supremo Tribunal Federal, Brasília
  - United States Supreme Court, Washington D.C.
- Photographic archives (Alinari, Library of Congress, Arquivo Nacional)

**Analytical Focus**:
- How does the physical placement of Justice statues structure citizens' relationship to the court?
- What changes in the 20th century? (Modernist abstraction vs. neoclassical ornamentation)
- The January 8 attacks: What does physical vandalism reveal about the statue's symbolic function?

#### **4. Legal Paratexts (Seals, Letterheads, Code Frontispieces)**

**Why Paratexts Matter**: These are the "authenticating images" that give documents legal force. A seal or signature transforms paper into law.

**Research Sites**:
- National Archives (France, UK, Germany, Brazil, USA)
- Legal manuscript collections
- Published code editions (frontispieces often feature Justice allegories)

**Analytical Focus**:
- How do authenticating images create the **performative effect** of legality?
- What is the relationship between the handwritten signature (authenticating through individual identity) and the allegorical seal (authenticating through abstract sovereignty)?
- How have digital authentication technologies transformed this visual regime?

### **C. Methodological Integration: Warburg's Pathosformel and Legal Iconography**

This project draws methodological inspiration from **Aby Warburg's concept of Pathosformel** (pathos formula)—the idea that emotionally charged visual forms migrate across centuries and cultures, carrying affective intensities that persist beneath conscious interpretation.

Warburg demonstrated that Renaissance artists unconsciously "quoted" Greco-Roman gestures and poses, particularly in moments of heightened emotion (ecstasy, suffering, violence). These visual forms functioned as a kind of **cultural memory**, transmitting affects across time.

**Application to Legal Iconography**: Female allegories of Justice function as **legal Pathosformeln**—emotionally charged forms that circulate globally, teaching populations **what law feels like** before they understand what law says. The blindfold, scales, and sword are not merely decorative; they are affective technologies generating trust, fear, and submission.

**Key Methodological Transfer**:
- Just as Warburg traced the migration of poses across artworks, this project traces the migration of legal attributes across jurisdictions and media
- Just as Warburg analyzed the "survival" (*Nachleben*) of ancient forms in modernity, this project analyzes the survival of Enlightenment allegory in 21st-century iconoclasm
- Just as Warburg emphasized the **affective intensity** of images, this project analyzes how legal images mobilize emotions essential to legitimacy

**Difference from Warburg**: While Warburg focused primarily on elite art (paintings, frescoes, manuscripts), this project focuses on **mass-distributed images** (coins, stamps, public statues)—the visual infrastructure of everyday legal experience.

## **V. CORE CASE STUDIES**

### **A. The French Revolution and the Invention of Marianne (1789–1799)**

**Research Question**: How did the French Revolution solve the problem of visualizing the abstract Republic?

**Key Developments**:
- The decapitation of Louis XVI created a "representational vacuum": the state's body (the King) was destroyed
- Early revolutionary imagery featured **male** figures (Hercules representing popular sovereignty)
- The gradual feminization of Liberty and the Republic (La République, Marianne)
- The split between "radical Marianne" (bare-breasted, Phrygian cap, active/violent) and "conservative Marianne" (seated, draped, holding legal tablets)

**Archival Research**:
- Revolutionary prints and pamphlets (Bibliothèque nationale de France)
- Early republican coins and assignats (Cabinet des Médailles)
- Iconographic debates in the Convention (Archives nationales)

**Analytical Focus**:
- Why did revolutionaries choose a female figure to represent a Republic that excluded women?
- How was the "dangerous" revolutionary virago domesticated into the orderly state symbol?
- What was the relationship between Marianne and real revolutionary women (Olympe de Gouges, Théroigne de Méricourt)?

**Lag Analysis**: 
- Marianne consolidated: ~1792
- Women's suffrage: 1944
- **Lag: 152 years**

### **B. Britannia and the Paradox of Female Monarchy (1707–1901)**

**Research Question**: How did Britain reconcile a living female monarch (Victoria) with a symbolic female nation (Britannia)?

**Key Developments**:
- Britannia as imperial symbol (trident, shield, maritime dominance)
- The coexistence of two female sovereigns: Victoria (political) and Britannia (symbolic)
- Suffragette attacks on Britannia iconography (defacing pennies, 1913–1914)

**Archival Research**:
- Royal Mint archives
- British Museum (numismatic collection)
- Suffragette pamphlets and trial records (Women's Library, LSE)

**Analytical Focus**:
- What is the difference between a "real" female sovereign and an allegorical one?
- Why did having a female monarch **not** translate into women's suffrage until 1918/1928?
- How did suffragettes weaponize Britannia against the state that excluded them?

**Lag Analysis**: 
- Britannia on currency: ~1707
- Women's suffrage (partial): 1918; (full): 1928
- **Lag: 211–221 years**

### **C. Germania: From Romantic Unity to Imperial Militarization (1848–1945)**

**Research Question**: How did Germania transform from a symbol of cultural unity into a symbol of martial imperial power?

**Key Developments**:
- Pre-unification Germania: romantic, cultural, Romantic nationalism
- Post-1871 Germania: militarized, armored, defending the Rhine
- The Niederwalddenkmal (1883): massive monument to imperial Germania

**Archival Research**:
- Germanisches Nationalmuseum, Nuremberg
- Bildarchiv Preußischer Kulturbesitz, Berlin
- Bundesarchiv (imperial coins, stamps, posters)

**Analytical Focus**:
- How does national allegory shift during regime transformation? (Confederation → Empire → Republic → Reich → Republic)
- The militarization of the female body: armored Germania as "valkyrie"
- The disappearance of Germania after 1945: Why did West Germany abandon the national allegory?

**Lag Analysis**: 
- Germania iconography: ~1848
- Women's suffrage: 1918
- **Lag: 70 years**

### **D. The Brazilian Republic and Racial Whitening (1889–1961)**

**Research Question**: How did Brazil's republican allegory mask the racial composition of a post-slavery society?

**Key Developments**:
- 1889 Republic proclaimed; French iconographic model imported wholesale
- The "Effigy of the Republic" (Phrygian cap, laurel wreath) appearing on currency
- The deliberate "whitening" of the republican allegory despite Brazil's Afro-indigenous demographic majority
- The construction of Brasília (1956–1960) as modernist utopia
- Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* (1961): modernist abstraction and the missing scales

**Archival Research**:
- Museu Histórico Nacional, Rio de Janeiro
- Arquivo Nacional, Brasília
- Casa da Moeda do Brasil (monetary iconography)
- STF archives (Ceschiatti commission records, if extant)

**Analytical Focus**:
- The "Visual Racial Contract": How did republican iconography project a whitened, Europeanized Brazil?
- The relationship between visual continuity (flag colors, allegorical style) and regime rupture (Empire → Republic)
- Modernist Brasília as "transparent" democracy vs. authoritarian rupture (1964 coup four years after inauguration)
- Ceschiatti's missing scales: What does "Justice without weighing" mean?

**Lag Analysis**: 
- Republican allegory: 1889
- Women's suffrage: 1932
- **Lag: 43 years** (shorter than Europe, but still substantial)

### **E. The January 8, 2023, Brasília Attacks: Iconoclastic Unmasking and the Crisis of Visual Legitimacy**

**Research Question**: Did the January 8 attacks constitute a rupture in the visual continuity of the Brazilian Republic, or an attempted rupture that ultimately failed?

**Key Developments**:
- The defacement of Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* with "Perdeu, mané" (Barroso's partisan words)
- The use of lipstick (gendered tool) to vandalize a gendered allegory
- Physical domination: climbing on the statue, draping flags, sitting on her lap
- The vandalism of over 700 artworks, including Di Cavalcanti's *As Mulatas* and Krajcberg's *Galhos e Sombras*
- The state response: 14-year sentence for Débora Rodrigues dos Santos (the lipstick graffitist)
- Swift restoration as "ritual re-consecration"

**Archival Research**:
- STF trial records (publicly available)
- Federal Police investigation reports (partially public)
- Photographic/video documentation of the attacks (social media, news archives)
- Restoration reports (UNESCO, Ministry of Culture)
- Curatorial decisions (exhibiting damaged artworks vs. full restoration)

**Analytical Focus**:
- **Iconoclastic Ventriloquism**: How did forcing the statue to "speak" Barroso's words collapse the Body Politic / Body Natural distinction?
- **Counter-Literacy**: What sophisticated visual understanding did the rioters demonstrate?
- **The Attempted Rupture**: Why did the insurrection fail? (Lack of military support, ideological constraint within republican vocabulary, swift institutional response)
- **Comparison with 1964**: How do constitutional ruptures invoke legality rather than reject it?
- **Decolonial vs. Authoritarian Iconoclasm**: How does January 8 differ from indigenous attacks on *bandeirante* monuments?

**Theoretical Implications**:
- The fragility of visual legitimacy when counter-literacy proliferates
- The obsolescence of the Visual Sexual Contract in 21st-century polarized democracies
- The limits of symbolic violence without institutional coercive capacity
- Visual continuity as ideological mask enabling constitutional transformation

## **VI. EXPECTED CONTRIBUTIONS**

### **A. To Legal History**

1. **Redefining Legal Literacy**: Establishing visual/symbolic competence as a foundational category of legal-historical analysis, not a marginal curiosity.

2. **Images as Primary Sources**: Demonstrating that for non-literate populations, images were **not** illustrations of law but the **primary mode** through which law was encountered.

3. **Comparative Constitutional Iconography**: Providing the first systematic comparison of how different legal systems visualized authority during regime transitions.

4. **Background Knowledge as Infrastructure**: Theorizing the tacit, affective foundations of legal legitimacy that textual analysis cannot capture.

### **B. To Feminist Legal Theory**

1. **The Visual Sexual Contract**: Extending Pateman's analysis to the visual domain, explaining why female bodies proliferated as Law's face when women were excluded from Law's practice.

2. **Allegorical Hypervisibility as Exclusionary Mechanism**: Demonstrating that symbolic inclusion **substitutes for** rather than **preludes** political inclusion.

3. **Lag Analysis as Quantitative Methodology**: Providing measurable evidence (152 years France, 211 years Britain) of the gap between allegorical consolidation and political rights.

4. **Intersectional Analysis**: Demonstrating how the Visual Sexual Contract operated simultaneously as a Visual Racial Contract in colonial and post-colonial contexts (Brazil, British Empire).

### **C. To Visual Culture and Art History**

1. **Legal Pathosformeln**: Applying Warburg's methodology to legal iconography, tracing how emotionally charged forms (blindfold, scales, sword) circulate globally.

2. **Pedagogy of Encounter**: Developing a spatial-material analysis of how images generate effects depending on their placement, scale, and frequency of encounter.

3. **Iconoclasm as Critical Reading**: Theorizing vandalism as a form of visual literacy that reveals the fragility of ideological constructions.

4. **Modernist Vulnerability**: Demonstrating how 20th-century abstraction (Ceschiatti) created iconographic vulnerabilities that 19th-century neoclassicism avoided.

### **D. To Contemporary Democratic Theory**

1. **The Crisis of Visual Legitimacy**: Analyzing how 21st-century counter-literacy (enabled by digital media) destabilizes the visual infrastructure of democratic legitimacy.

2. **Attempted Rupture vs. Successful Rupture**: Distinguishing between symbolic violence (January 8) and institutional capture (1964), clarifying the conditions under which visual iconoclasm translates into constitutional crisis.

3. **Decolonial vs. Authoritarian Iconoclasm**: Providing conceptual tools to distinguish emancipatory attacks on exclusionary symbols (indigenous anti-*bandeirante* protests) from oppressive attacks on democratic symbols (Bolsonarista vandalism).

4. **The Limits of Visual Continuity**: Demonstrating that preserving monuments alone cannot sustain legitimacy when substantive inequalities and institutional dysfunctions persist.

## **CONCLUSION**

The January 8, 2023, defacement of Ceschiatti's *A Justiça*—the inscription "Perdeu, mané" in red lipstick across the stone chest of Brazil's blind, modernist Goddess of Justice—was not an isolated act of vandalism. It was the violent revelation of a tension embedded in modern legal orders since the Enlightenment: **the gap between law's universal visual pretensions and its exclusionary material practices**.

This dissertation argues that for over two centuries (1789–2023), modern states constructed legal legitimacy through a **Visual Sexual Contract**—a regime of allegorical hypervisibility that substituted symbolic female presence for real women's political exclusion. Female figures proliferated as the face of Law and Nation precisely when women were most systematically denied access to citizenship, civil capacity, and the legal profession.

This arrangement generated a transnational visual literacy: by the late 19th century, an illiterate immigrant arriving in Rio de Janeiro, Paris, or New York could recognize the state's authority through its female avatars. The woman with the scales was the court; the woman with the cap was the Republic. This was not decorative; it was **infrastructural**. Images constituted the background knowledge that made law functional for populations who could not read legal texts.

The January 8 attacks revealed both the **resilience** and **fragility** of this visual infrastructure. Resilient enough to be swiftly restored and defended through harsh prosecutions; fragile enough that a few hours of symbolic violence exposed the tenuousness of the democratic pact. When rioters forced the statue to "speak" a partisan judge's words, they collapsed the careful distinction between Law's transcendent "Body Politic" and its partisan "Body Natural." They demonstrated sophisticated counter-literacy—the ability to read and rewrite state iconography against the grain.

This project treats that moment of iconoclastic unmasking as a diagnostic crisis—one that demands a comprehensive genealogy of the visual foundations of legal legitimacy from the French Revolution to contemporary Brazil. By reconstructing how female allegories functioned as **legal Pathosformeln**—emotionally charged forms circulating globally to teach populations what law looks like and feels like—this dissertation offers new theoretical and methodological tools for understanding the past, present, and future of legal authority.

The Visual Sexual Contract is fraying. The 19th-century solution of using abstract female bodies to mask political conflict no longer effectively stabilizes legal legitimacy in 21st-century polarized democracies. When publics possess counter-literacy enabling them to read statues as partisan masks rather than universal truths, the simulacrum dissolves. The statue becomes just a rock; the court becomes just a building; the law becomes just words.

Understanding how these images were built, how they held the world together for two centuries, and why they are now cracking is essential for confronting the contemporary crisis of democratic legitimacy—not just in Brazil, but across the Atlantic world where these visual regimes originated and circulated.

**This is the work this dissertation proposes to undertake.**
