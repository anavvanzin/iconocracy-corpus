Legal Literacy and the Allegorical Body of Law: The Visual Sexual Contract in Modern State Formation (1789–1945)

1\. Background

1.1 The Crisis of Representation and the visual Sexual Contract

The transition from the *Ancien Régime* to the modern nation-state marked a fundamental rupture in the metaphysics of sovereignty. Where political authority was once incarnated in the physical body of the monarch—the "King’s two bodies," as Kantorowicz famously described it—the revolutions of the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries necessitated a new vessel for the abstraction of "the people." Paradoxically, as the emerging republics of Europe and the Americas systematically excluded women from the rights of active citizenship, they simultaneously appropriated the female form as the primary visual interface of the state (Landes 2001). From the barricades of Paris to the plazas of Rio de Janeiro, the abstraction of the State was rendered visible through the allegorical female body: *Marianne*, *Germania*, *Italia Turrita*, *Britannia*, and the *Alegoria da República*.

This doctoral project proposes a critical investigation into this constitutive contradiction of modern legal history. It posits that the ubiquity of the female allegory was not merely an aesthetic convention or a neoclassical revival, but a functional technology of political legitimacy—a **"visual sexual contract"** that stabilized the fraternal social contract by offering a symbolic compensation for political exclusion. While the textual law (the "sexual contract" described by Carole Pateman) stripped women of their civil personality through mechanisms such as *coverture* and *puissance maritale*, the visual law (the "obiter depicta" described by Peter Goodrich) elevated the female form to the status of a secular deity (Pateman 1988; Goodrich 2013).

The project defines "legal literacy" not merely as the capacity to read statutes, but as an infrastructural and visual competence—the learned ability of the citizen to recognize, trust, and navigate the authority of the state through its iconographic projections. Between 1789 and 1945, the "legal literacy" of the Western citizen was engaged primarily through the handling of coins bearing female profiles, the gaze upon courthouse pediments featuring blindfolded women, and the interaction with bureaucratic documents stamped with the seal of the Republic-as-Woman. This research asks: What kind of legal subject is formed when the "Mother of the Laws" is legally defined as a minor?

1.2 The Genealogy of the Allegorical Paradox

To understand the operation of the visual sexual contract, it is necessary to reconstruct the genealogy of the female allegory and its collision with the codification of patriarchal law in the nineteenth century.

1.2.1 The Displacement of the King’s Body

In the pre-modern era, the body of the King anchored the legal order. The King was the *fons justitiae* (fount of justice). The regicides and republican revolutions created a "void of power" (Claude Lefort) that required filling. The choice of the female allegory was strategic. As Marina Warner has argued, the female figure could embody the *Res Publica* precisely because women were historically excluded from the *Res Publica* (Warner 1985). A male figure would have implied a specific ruler or a specific class of men (e.g., a general or a merchant), thereby threatening the claim of universal abstraction. The female form, emptied of political agency, became the perfect vessel for the Universal. She could represent "Liberty" or "Justice" without threatening the actual male monopolization of liberty and judicial power.

However, this substitution created a jarring dissonance in the era of codification. As the image of the "Sovereign Woman" proliferated, the legal status of the "Concrete Woman" was being codified into a state of civil death.

1.2.2 The Napoleonic Archetype: *Marianne* vs. The Civil Code

The French case serves as the primal scene of this conflict. The French Revolution birthed *Marianne*, the fierce embodiment of Liberty and Reason. Yet, the stabilization of the revolution under Napoleon Bonaparte resulted in the *Code Napoléon* (1804), which became the template for civil law systems worldwide (France 1804).

The Code was explicitly patriarchal. Article 213 stated, "The husband owes protection to his wife, the wife obedience to her husband." Even more critical for the question of the state was the regulation of nationality. Under Article 12, "The alien woman who shall have married a Frenchman shall follow the condition of her husband," and under Article 19, a French woman marrying a foreigner was stripped of her nationality (France 1804, art. 19). The "unity of the family" doctrine required that the wife’s political identity be subsumed into the husband’s. Thus, while *Marianne* stood as the eternal guardian of the French nation, actual French women were legally transferable subjects, their bond to the nation severed by the private act of marriage. The visual regime celebrated the *Mère Patrie* (Motherland), while the legal regime enforced the *Patria Potestas* (Father's Power).

1.2.3 The Italian Case: *Italia Turrita* and the Pisanelli Code

The unification of Italy (Risorgimento) presented a similar iconographic urgency. The disparate states needed a unifying symbol, found in *Italia Turrita*—the woman crowned with a mural crown of towers, a *Pathosformel* dating back to the Roman goddess Cybele/Tyche (Wikipedia Contributors n.d.). This image circulated on the lira, stamps, and monuments, soliciting the affect of the new Italian citizen-subjects.

Yet, the *Codice Pisanelli* (Civil Code of 1865\) institutionalized the *autorizzazione maritale* (marital authorization). Under this doctrine, a married woman could not give gifts, sell real estate, take out loans, or initiate legal proceedings without her husband’s written permission (Italy 1865). The visual rhetoric of *Italia Turrita* holding a cornucopia and scepter—symbols of abundance and sovereignty—masked the material reality where women were legally incapacitated from managing their own abundance or exercising sovereignty over their own affairs. Furthermore, the 1865 Code and subsequent Law 555/1912 codified *jus sanguinis* strictly through the paternal line; women could not transmit Italian citizenship to their children, rendering the "Mother Italy" allegory a biological metaphor devoid of juridical force (Italy 1912).

1.2.4 The Brazilian Experience: Positivism and the Republic

Brazil offers a unique case study due to the strong influence of Comtian Positivism on its republican symbolism. Following the 1889 coup that ousted the Emperor, the new Republic sought to visualize itself. Artists like Décio Villares and intellectuals like Raimundo Teixeira Mendes, deeply influenced by the "Religion of Humanity," crafted an *Alegoria da República* that fused the positivist ideal of the "Mother-Humanity" with the republican liberty cap (Carvalho 1990).

However, the legal transition was fraught. The "Grande Naturalização" (Great Naturalization) decree of 1889 automatically granted citizenship to all foreigners resident in Brazil, yet the status of foreign women married to Brazilians (or Brazilian women married to foreigners) remained a site of intense contestation regarding whether they possessed an independent will to accept or reject this nationality (Brazil 1889). The Civil Code of 1916 (Law 3.071) formally codified the married woman’s status as "relatively incapable," alongside "prodigals" and "silvicolas" (indigenous peoples) (Brazil 1916). The "visual sexual contract" in Brazil was thus colored by a specific positivist theology that worshipped "Woman" as the moral center of the family while legally confining her to the domestic sphere to preserve that very morality.

1.3 Theoretical Pillars: Image, Law, and Gender

This project is grounded in a triangulation of three theoretical domains:

1. Legal Iconology (Panofsky/Warburg/Goodrich):  
   Moving beyond simple iconography (what is depicted), the project employs iconology to understand the "intrinsic meaning" or "symptom" of the image (O'Donnell 2018). It utilizes Aby Warburg’s concept of Pathosformel (emotive formula) to analyze how ancient gestures of grief, triumph, or protection were recycled to lend emotional weight to the modern state (Warburg 1999). It specifically draws on Peter Goodrich’s theory of obiter depicta, which argues that images in law are not decorative but structural—they manage the "unconscious" of the legal order and transmit the "love of law" that text alone cannot sustain (Goodrich 2013).  
2. The Sexual Contract (Pateman/Landes):  
   The project operationalizes Carole Pateman’s critique of the social contract, which exposes the fraternal pact to subordinate women. It extends this via Joan Landes’s Visualizing the Nation, which argues that the exclusion of women from the public sphere was constituted through the spectacularization of the female body (Landes 2001). The "visual sexual contract" is the mechanism that allows men to bond politically through the shared objectification/reverence of the female allegory.  
3. The Simulacrum (Baudrillard):  
   The allegory is treated as a simulacrum: a copy without an original. The "Justice" depicted in the courtroom does not refer to a reality of justice for women, but serves to mask the absence of that reality. It is a "truth effect" that sustains the system.

1.4 The Pedagogy of Legality

Central to the background of this project is the concept of **"legal literacy."** In the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, literacy rates varied wildly across the regions of study. For the illiterate or semi-literate subject, the "reading" of the law was visual and material. The encounter with the state happened through the coin in the pocket, the stamp on the letter, and the statue in the square. This project argues that these objects constituted a "pedagogy of legality" that taught citizens their place in the order of things.

* **The Lesson of the Coin:** The coin taught that value and sovereignty were guaranteed by a female figure (*Semeuse*, *Helvetia*, *Liberty*). It naturalized the association of the feminine with "value" and "trust" while separating it from "power" and "speech."  
* **The Lesson of the Courthouse:** The architectural program of the courthouse, with female caryatids supporting the structure or Justice crowning the pediment, taught that the foundation of the law was "feminine" (nature, truth, equity), while the administration of the law (judges, lawyers, jurors) was masculine. This visual pedagogy prepared the public to accept the gendered division of labor as natural.

1.5 Historical Scope: 1789–1945

The timeframe 1789–1945 frames the "Golden Age" of the female legal allegory.

* **Start (1789):** The French Revolution and the explosion of republican iconography.  
* **End (1945):** The post-WWII era marks a decisive shift. The granting of female suffrage in France (1944) and Italy (1946), and the revision of nationality laws (e.g., British Nationality Act 1948, the gradual dismantling of the Pisanelli code), coincides with the decline of the classical allegory. As women became "real" citizens, they became less useful as "symbolic" citizens. The "visual sexual contract" began to dissolve, replaced by abstract modernism or new forms of representation.

2\. Research Questions & Hypothesis

2.1 Primary Research Question

**How did the "visual sexual contract"—manifested through the ubiquitous allegorical use of the female body in state iconography (1789–1945)—function to stabilize the legitimacy of modern legal regimes, and how did this visual economy interact with, compensate for, or conceal the simultaneous juridical erasure of women’s civil personality?**

This question seeks to bridge the gap between *art history* (which studies the image) and *legal history* (which studies the exclusion), positing that they are part of a single, coherent political technology.

2.2 Secondary Research Questions

To dismantle the primary question, the project poses five sub-questions designed to guide the empirical analysis:

1. **The Media of the Contract:** Through which specific media and sites (numismatics, philately, public statuary, legal paratexts) was the visual sexual contract negotiated? How did the *mode of encounter*—the tactile intimacy of handling money vs. the distant awe of monumental statuary—shape the "legal literacy" of the citizen?  
   * *Implicit Inquiry:* Did the proliferation of these images serve as a "secular catechism" for the illiterate?  
2. **The Iconographic Grammar of Exclusion:** What were the specific attributes (scales, swords, mural crowns, broken chains, Phrygian caps) that constituted the "grammar" of these allegories? How did these attributes mutate across borders (e.g., the *fasces* moving from a symbol of republican unity in France to authoritarianism in Italy)?  
   * *Implicit Inquiry:* How did the *Pathosformel* of the "weeping woman" (e.g., *Italia Irredenta*) vs. the "armed virgin" (e.g., *Germania*) mobilize different legal affects (pity vs. obedience)?  
3. **The Mechanism of Compensation:** Did the aesthetic elevation of the female form serve as a "compensatory simulacrum" for legal dispossession? Is there a correlation between the intensity of allegorical representation and the severity of legal exclusion (e.g., the "cult of the mother" in regimes with strict *patria potestas*)?  
   * *Implicit Inquiry:* Was the visual hyper-visibility of women a necessary condition for their political invisibility?  
4. **The Crisis of Contestation:** How did the "visual sexual contract" react to challenge? How did moments of satire (e.g., Daumier’s *Republic*), iconoclasm (Suffragette vandalism), or appropriation (feminist use of *Joan of Arc*) reveal the fragility of the allegory?  
   * *Implicit Inquiry:* How did the state respond when the "symbol" started speaking?  
5. **The Nationality Nexus:** How did the specific laws regarding the "Loss of Nationality upon Marriage" (e.g., US Expatriation Act 1907, French Code Civil Art. 19, Italian Law 555/1912) interact with national personifications? How can a nation be represented by a female body (*Britannia*, *Columbia*) when actual female bodies were legally ejectable from the nation upon marriage?

2.3 Hypothesis

The project is driven by four interrelated hypotheses:

* Hypothesis 1: The Infrastructural Function of Allegory.  
  Legal literacy is not primarily textual; it is visual and infrastructural. The modern state could not have survived the transition from monarchy to republic without a "visual anchor" to replace the King’s body. The female allegory was not decoration; it was infrastructure. It provided the "face" of the state that allowed for the recognition of currency, the authority of documents, and the sanctity of public space. The success of this infrastructure depended on the "Warburgian" power of the image to elicit archaic emotional responses (filial piety, erotic desire, maternal trust) that the dry text of the constitution could not.  
* Hypothesis 2: The Visual Sexual Contract as Misdirection.  
  The "Visual Sexual Contract" operated as a technology of misdirection. By saturating the visual field with idealized women, the state masked the absence of actual women. The allegory functioned as a "screen memory" (Freud) or a "simulacrum" (Baudrillard)—a representation that hides the absence of the real. The more the state deprived women of rights (e.g., the Pisanelli Code’s autorizzazione maritale), the more it needed to elevate the female allegory (Italia Turrita) to maintain the fantasy of an inclusive, "motherly" national community.  
* Hypothesis 3: The Gendered Partition of Universality.  
  Modernity solved the problem of "Universal Rights" vs. "Male Privilege" by partitioning universality. The Abstract Universal was assigned to the female form (passive, timeless, symbolic), while the Concrete Particular (active, temporal, political) was assigned to the male. This partition allowed the state to claim it was universal (look at Liberty\!) while functioning as a patriarchy (look at the judge\!). The allegory was the linchpin that held this contradiction together.  
* Hypothesis 4: The Eclipse of Allegory by Rights.  
  The decline of the classical female allegory in the mid-20th century was not a change in artistic taste, but a consequence of the collapse of the visual sexual contract. As women gained the vote and independent nationality (e.g., US Cable Act 1922, Brazilian Decree 21.076 in 1932, French suffrage 1944), they became "concrete particulars." They could no longer function as "empty vessels" for abstract virtue. The "Real Woman" killed the "Allegorical Woman."

3\. Methodology

This project employs a **transdisciplinary methodology**, fusing the doctrinal rigor of **Comparative Legal History** with the visual decoding tools of **Iconology** and **Semiotics**.

3.1 Legal Iconology and the "Iconographic Reconstruction Protocol"

I will adopt a modified version of Erwin Panofsky’s three-strata method, filtered through Aby Warburg’s *Mnemosyne* approach and Peter Goodrich’s *Visocracy* theory (O'Donnell 2018). For every image in the corpus, I will apply the following protocol:

| Protocol Stage | Analytical Action | Example Application (Italia Turrita) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **1\. Pre-Iconographic** | Inventory of formal elements (line, color, scale, material). | A seated woman, heavy drapery, mural crown, holding cornucopia and staff. Bronze, monumental scale. |
| **2\. Iconographic** | Identification of subject via literary sources. Decoding attributes. | Identification as *Italia*. Crown \= City protection (Cybele). Cornucopia \= Abundance. Staff \= Sovereignty. |
| **3\. Iconological** | Intrinsic meaning/Cultural symptom. Connection to legal/social context. | The mural crown represents a defensive, city-state heritage unifying under the Savoyard monarchy. The cornucopia promises agricultural wealth in a time of peasant poverty. The female form solicits "filial" loyalty to the new state, masking the exclusion of women from the "family" of citizens under the Pisanelli Code. |
| **4\. Intervisual Genealogy** | Tracing the migration of the motif (*Nachleben*). | Tracing the "mural crown" from Greek *Tyche* \-\> Roman *Cybele* \-\> Renaissance *Iconologia* (Ripa) \-\> 19th Century *Italia*. |

3.2 Comparative Legal History: The Five-Case Matrix

The project avoids the trap of generalizing "Western" experience by utilizing a rigorous comparative matrix focusing on the "Loss of Nationality upon Marriage" and the status of the married woman.

* **France (The Model):**  
  * *Legal:* Code Napoléon (Arts. 12, 19, 213); Law of 1889; Law of 1927 (restoring independent nationality).  
  * *Visual:* The evolution of *Marianne* (Revolutionary virago vs. Bourgeois matron).  
* **Italy (The Paternal):**  
  * *Legal:* Pisanelli Code (1865); Law 555/1912 (strict *jus sanguinis* via father); 1948 Constitution.  
  * *Visual:* *Italia Turrita*; The fascist appropriation (adding the *fasces* to the allegory) (Addison 1726).  
* **Brazil (The Positivist):**  
  * *Legal:* "Grande Naturalização" (1889); Constitution of 1891; Civil Code 1916 (married woman as relative incapable); Decree 21.076 (1932) and Constitution of 1934 (preventing loss of nationality).  
  * *Visual:* *Alegoria da República* (Positivist influence); The flag (Order and Progress).  
* **USA/UK (The Common Law):**  
  * *Legal:* US Expatriation Act 1907; *Mackenzie v. Hare* (1915); Cable Act 1922\. UK British Nationality and Status of Aliens Act 1914\.  
  * *Visual:* *Columbia*; *Statue of Liberty*; *Britannia*.  
* **Switzerland (The Control):**  
  * *Legal:* Swiss Civil Code (1907). Late suffrage (1971).  
  * *Visual:* *Helvetia* (Vreneli) on coinage. High stability of allegory vs. high exclusion.

3.3 Data Sources and Corpus Construction

The research relies on a dual corpus:

**A. The Visual Corpus (The "Obiter Depicta")**

* **Numismatics:** 50 key coins/banknotes from the 5 jurisdictions (e.g., the French *Semeuse*, the US *Peace Dollar*, the Brazilian *Mil Réis* with the Republic’s bust).  
* **Philately:** Stamps representing the nation (especially the "Empire" vs. "Republic" series in Brazil and France).  
* **Monuments:** Major public statuary (Statue of Liberty, *Triomphe de la République* in Paris, *Monumento à Independência* in São Paulo).  
* **Legal Paratexts:** Frontispieces of the Code Napoléon, vignettes on share certificates, courthouse iconography.

**B. The Textual Corpus (The "Obiter Dicta")**

* **Legislative Debates:** Parliamentary records concerning the "Nationality of Married Women" (e.g., the debates leading to the Cable Act, the discussions in the Brazilian Constituent Assembly of 1933-34).  
* **Jurisprudence:** Key cases on the loss of nationality (e.g., *Mackenzie v. Hare*).  
* **Theoretical Texts:** Cesare Ripa’s *Iconologia* (1603) 10, legal treatises on the "nature of women" (e.g., Clóvis Beviláqua’s commentaries).

3.4 Handling Contestation

The methodology includes a specific focus on "Diagnostic Images"—images that disrupt the contract.

* **Satire:** Daumier’s caricatures of *Marianne* as a washerwoman or hag.  
* **Vandalism:** The Suffragette attack on the *Rokeby Venus* (as a proxy for the allegorical female).  
* **Fascist Mutation:** The "masculinization" of the female allegory under Mussolini (the medal of the Lateran Pacts) (Addison 1726).

Conclusions

This project aims to uncover the deep-seated complicity between the aesthetics of the state and the gendered structures of citizenship. By decoding the "visual sexual contract," the research will demonstrate that the exclusion of women was not merely a legal oversight but a foundational requirement of the modern republic’s symbolic economy. The "Allegorical Body of Law" served as a brilliant, blinding surface—a pedagogical tool that taught citizens to revere the "Idea" of Woman while legally erasing the "Fact" of women. The transition from this regime of high allegory to the era of equal citizenship marks a fundamental shift in the ontology of the state, one that this project seeks to map, analyze, and critique. The end of the allegory does not merely mean a change in style, but a change in the substance of the legal subject itself.
