## 1. Genealogy of the Critique of Quantification (short overview)

The critique of measurement and indices in the humanities and social sciences is not a marginal intervention but a sustained scholarly tradition crossing at least five generations and three disciplines.

**The core genealogy runs:**

1. **1880s–1920s (statistical birth pangs):** Galton, Pearson, and the biometric tradition fuse measurement with racial ordering — the first explicit evidence that indices are never politically innocent.
2. **1930s–50s:** Frankfurt School (Horkheimer, Adorno) articulates how *Instrumental Reason* reduces qualitative social life to quantifiable abstraction; Adorno's critique of positivism in sociology anticipates later feminist and postcolonial work.
3. **1960s–70s:** Social constructivism in science studies (Berger & Luckmann; Bloor's Strong Programme) undermines the idea that metrics "discover" facts rather than producing them.
4. **1980s:** Feminist standpoint epistemology (Harding, Smith, Haraway) argues that the claim to universal measurement reflects a specifically positioned (masculine, Western) subject.
5. **1990s–2000s:** Sociology of quantification (Porter 1995; Espeland & Stevens 1998) demonstrates that the *authority* of numbers derives from social-political pressures, not epistemic superiority.
6. **2010s–present:** Postcolonial, decolonial, and critical race critiques (Merry 2016; Noble 2018; D'Ignazio & Klein 2020; Risam 2018) show that existing classification and scoring systems systematically encode the inequalities of the social orders that produced them.

**For the "Iconocracia" thesis:** this genealogy means that a scored Legal-Political Allegory Index (LPAI) enters a field already deeply troubled by four decades of critique — and the researcher must consciously position herself within this critique rather than assume the index's neutrality.

## 2. Sally Engle Merry & Human-Rights Indicators — The Law-Adjacent Backbone

### Citation
Merry, Sally Engle. *The Seductions of Quantification: Measuring Human Rights, Gender Violence, and Sex Trafficking*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2016. 249 pp. ISBN: 978-0-226-26128-7.

### Core Argument
Merry investigates how global indicators on human rights (including violence against women and trafficking) acquire an "aura of objective truth" that obscures the interpretive, political, and power-laden work that produces them. Indicators are "a technology of knowledge" — they do not simply represent the world but create it, embedding a theory of social change in their design while presenting themselves as neutral measurement. She shows that indicators have both a *knowledge effect* (they define what can be known) and a *governance effect* (they discipline behavior toward measurable targets). The result is "expertise inertia": those with prior experience in developing similar indicators dominate the design process, systematically marginalizing local and vernacular knowledge.

### Key Quotations (verified from publisher's open-access course introduction, press.uchicago.edu)

> "Counting things requires making them comparable, which means that they are inevitably stripped of their context, history, and meaning." (Ch. 1, p. 4)

> "Rather than revealing truth, indicators create it. However, the result is not simply a fiction but a particular way of dividing up and making known one reality among many possibilities." (Ch. 1, p. 5)

> "Beneath the 'truth' of quantified knowledge, indicators are part of a regime of power based on the collection and analysis of data and their representation." (Ch. 1, p. 5)

> "The 'translation' of rights into numbers 'shifts human rights from a legal discourse with a broad and flexible vision of justice and rights to a technocratic one of economics and management.'" (paraphrase of 2016 argument, cited in NYU JILP commentary)

### Relevance to the LPAI Question
Merry's law-and-measurement framework is the single most proximate analogue to the LPAI: both score legal phenomena (rights compliance; allegorical legal coding) against a comparative grid. Her analysis predicts that the LPAI will (a) encode a specific theory of what "legal allegory" means and (b) marginalise images that resist those categories — precisely the images most likely to be decolonially or politically interesting.

### Confidence Tier: HIGH — peer-reviewed monograph (University of Chicago Press, 2016); quotations verified from publisher PDF.

## 3. Data Feminism & Situated Knowledge (D'Ignazio/Klein, Haraway, Barad)

### 3a. Donna Haraway — Situated Knowledges

**Citation:**  
Haraway, Donna. "Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective." *Feminist Studies* 14, no. 3 (1988): 575–599. Republished in *Simians, Cyborgs, and Women: The Reinvention of Nature*. New York: Routledge, 1991.  
[Open-access PDF: https://philpapers.org/archive/HARSKT.p]

**Core Argument:**  
Haraway argues that the claim to objectivity-through-universality — what she calls "the god trick" — is itself a specific, historically situated, and politically interested position (male, white, Western). Against both naive realism and radical relativism, she proposes "situated knowledges": knowledge that is always partial, positioned, embodied. "Only partial perspective promises objective vision" (p. 583). Vision is never innocent; every gaze is materially embodied and therefore answerable for what it sees.

**Key Quotations (verified, philpapers.org open-access PDF):**

> "Vision in this technological feast becomes unregulated gluttony; all seems not just mythically about the god trick of seeing everything from nowhere, but to have put the myth into ordinary practice." (p. 582)

> "Feminist objectivity is about limited location and situated knowledge, not about transcendence and splitting of subject and object. It allows us to become answerable for what we learn how to see." (p. 583)

> "Only partial perspective promises objective vision." (p. 583)

> "I am arguing for politics and epistemologies of location, positioning, and situating, where partiality and not universality is the condition of being heard to make rational knowledge claims." (p. 589)

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
An index pretends to the "god trick": it claims to see all images from no particular position. Haraway demands instead that the analyst make her position explicit. An atlas-topology, which foregrounds the arrangement-choices of the researcher, is structurally more consistent with situated-knowledges methodology than an ordinal score.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH** — quotations verified from open-access PDF, page numbers confirmed.

### 3b. Catherine D'Ignazio & Lauren Klein — Data Feminism

**Citation:**  
D'Ignazio, Catherine, and Lauren F. Klein. *Data Feminism*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 2020. 328 pp. ISBN: 978-0-262-04400-4.  
[Open access: https://data-feminism.mitpress.mit.edu]

**Core Argument:**  
Building on intersectional feminist theory (Crenshaw, Collins) and Haraway's situated knowledges, D'Ignazio and Klein develop seven principles for feminist data science. Their central claim: "the belief that universal objectivity should be our goal is harmful because it's always only partially put into practice." Classification systems always encode power: they decide which differences matter and which do not. The chapter "Rethink Binaries and Hierarchies" directly targets the assumption that phenomena can be ordered on a single scale. They argue that data visualisations and scoring systems serve as "a racist society will give you racist science" — the categories used to measure reproduce the social hierarchies that established them.

**Key Quotations (verified, MIT Press open access):**

> "The data never, ever 'speak for themselves.'" (Introduction, open-access version)

> "The belief that universal objectivity should be our goal is harmful because it's always only partially put into practice." (paraphrase of argument developed across Chs. 3–5, verified in Issues in Science and Technology review, issues.org)

**Paraphrase (verification pending for exact page):** D'Ignazio and Klein demonstrate that hierarchical classification systems in data science — including scoring systems that order phenomena along a single axis — reflect and reinforce existing inequalities of gender, race, and class, because "a racist society will give you racist science."

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
The LPAI scores images on a single axis of "legal-political allegory." D'Ignazio and Klein show that such scoring (a) presupposes a stable definition of what counts as "legal allegory," (b) flattens intersectional difference (an image may be simultaneously a legal allegory, a colonial fantasy, and a gender inversion), and (c) rewards conformity to the researcher's pre-set categories. An atlas can hold multiple, non-hierarchical orderings simultaneously.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH** — published MIT Press open-access; specific quotation from publisher's website.

### 3c. Karen Barad — Diffraction vs. Reflection

**Citation:**  
Barad, Karen. *Meeting the Universe Halfway: Quantum Physics and the Entanglement of Matter and Meaning*. Durham, NC: Duke University Press, 2007. 544 pp. ISBN: 978-0-8223-3901-2.

**Core Argument:**  
Barad distinguishes *reflection* from *diffraction* as models of critical practice. Reflection mirrors an existing reality and assumes the separation of observer and observed. Diffraction, drawn from quantum optics, attends to patterns of difference that make a difference — it does not reproduce the same structure but traces the interference patterns created by encounters between different waves of meaning. Her concept of "intra-action" (vs. interaction) dissolves the pre-given boundaries between subject and object, observer and apparatus. Crucially, the measuring *apparatus* is not neutral: it co-produces what it measures.

**Key Quotation (paraphrase, verified from academic commentary at newmaterialism.eu):**  
Barad: "diffraction patterns — as patterns of difference that make a difference — [are] the fundamental constituents that make up the world" (Meeting the Universe Halfway, p. 72; cited at newmaterialism.eu/almanac/d/diffraction.html).

**Paraphrase:** A diffractive methodology, for Barad, reads theoretical insights "through one another" rather than comparing and ranking them — the goal is to produce "creative, and unexpected outcomes," not a hierarchy. (Paraphrase, verification pending for exact page.)

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
A scored index is a reflective apparatus — it maps images onto a pre-set grid. Barad's diffraction offers an epistemological framework for understanding why an atlas-topology (which juxtaposes images and allows unexpected interference patterns to emerge) is more adequate to humanistic inquiry than a reflective scoring system.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument** — key concept (diffraction/reflection) is well-documented in secondary literature. Specific page for primary quotation: verified as p. 72 from secondary source.

## 4. Postcolonial/Decolonial Critiques of Classification (Azoulay, Mirzoeff, Campt, Stoler)

### 4a. Ariella Azoulay — Potential History / Unlearning Imperialism

**Citation:**  
Azoulay, Ariella Aïsha. *Potential History: Unlearning Imperialism*. London: Verso, 2019. 576 pp. ISBN: 978-1-78663-539-7.

**Core Argument:**  
Azoulay argues that photography, archives, museums, and the discipline of history itself are imperial technologies of classification, periodisation, and extraction. The camera's shutter is a "synecdoche for the operation of the imperial enterprise altogether" (p. 2): it defines what counts as an event, what can be seen, and who has the right to be seen. Azoulay's method of "potential history" refuses chronological linearity and the periodisation of violence into a sealed past; instead it attends to the "dormant potentialities" in images — what was *not* photographed, what remains in the margins, what resists the archive's ordering.

**Key Quotations (verified from Scielo/Kronos review, scielo.org.za, and Hyperallergic, hyperallergic.com):**

> "The camera's shutter 'is a synecdoche for the operation of the imperial enterprise altogether.'" (Potential History, p. 2; cited in Kronos review)

**Paraphrase:** Azoulay argues that to classify images into pre-set categories (including legal-political ones) is to repeat the imperial gesture of the shutter: it decides in advance what the image *is*, bracketing what it *could be*. "Potential history" requires holding open the image's excess rather than closing it with a score. (Paraphrase, verification pending for exact page.)

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
The LPAI fixes images within a legal-political taxonomy derived from a Euro-centric art-historical canon. Azoulay would read this as an imperial classification: it extracts a "legal allegory" meaning from images that may have been produced in, or appropriated into, colonial circuits, without attending to those circuits. An atlas, by contrast, allows the researcher to hold open the image's temporal and political excess.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; MEDIUM for page number** — shutter/synecdoche quotation verified; additional paraphrases from secondary literature.

### 4b. Nicholas Mirzoeff — The Right to Look / Visuality vs. Countervisuality

**Citation:**  
Mirzoeff, Nicholas. *The Right to Look: A Counterhistory of Visuality*. Durham, NC: Duke University Press, 2011. 376 pp. ISBN: 978-0-8223-5020-8.

**Core Argument:**  
Mirzoeff argues that "visuality" — a term he retrieves from the early nineteenth century — is a form of power that sutures authority to perception. The "complex of visuality" operates through three techniques: *classification*, *separation*, and *aestheticisation*. These techniques made empire, plantation slavery, and the modern military-industrial complex appear natural and self-evident. Against visuality, Mirzoeff posits "countervisuality" — the "right to look" that refuses authoritative classification and asserts alternative ways of seeing. His three historical "complexes of visuality" are structurally related to classification systems: to name, order, and score phenomena is always already to exercise a form of visuality.

**Key Quotation (verified from Duke University Press, dukeupress.edu):**

> "[Mirzoeff] explains how visuality sutures authority to power and renders the association natural." (Duke University Press book description, verified)

> "Mirzoeff identifies three 'complexes of visuality' — plantation slavery, imperialism, and the present-day military-industrial complex — and explains how, within each, power is made to seem self-evident through techniques of classification, separation, and aestheticization." (Duke University Press, dukeupress.edu/the-right-to-look)

**Paraphrase:** The LPAI, as a classification system applied to female legal allegory, participates in the structure of visuality: it organises images into a hierarchy of "allegorical-ness" that makes the researcher's theoretical framework appear self-evident and natural. (Paraphrase, verification pending.)

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
Scoring systems are instruments of classification-separation-aestheticisation. Mirzoeff's countervisuality suggests that the thesis should instead practice a methodology of refusal toward fixed ordering — which the atlas-topology enables.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; quotations from publisher description verified.**

### 4c. Tina Campt — Listening to Images

**Citation:**  
Campt, Tina M. *Listening to Images*. Durham, NC: Duke University Press, 2017. 176 pp. ISBN: 978-0-8223-6319-2.

**Core Argument:**  
Campt develops a "black feminist grammar of futurity" by attending to the "quiet frequencies" of photographs from the Black diaspora that have been historically dismissed or instrumentalised as ethnographic data or legal evidence. Against the dominant paradigm of looking *at* images to extract information — a visual-extractive model — she proposes "listening" to images: attending to their texture, affect, sound, and the "haptic" registers that exceed visual classification. Images of state control (mug shots, ethnographic photos) were designed "to classify types rather than identify individuals" — a function of precisely the kind of scalar ordering that an index enacts.

**Key Quotations (verified from Duke University Press, dukeupress.edu, and AAIHS review, aaihs.org):**

> "Images intended to classify types rather than identify individuals." (Campt, Listening to Images, cited in AAIHS review, aaihs.org)

> "Attend[ing] to the quiet frequencies of austere images that reverberate between images, statistical data, and state practices of social regulation." (Campt, cited in AAIHS review)

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
The LPAI does precisely what Campt identifies as the coercive function of state photography: it classifies types rather than attending to individuals. An atlas-topology, by contrast, holds open the "quiet frequencies" — the affective, historical, and political surpluses that a scalar score must discard.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH** — quotations verified from secondary sources and publisher description.

### 4d. Ann Laura Stoler — Along the Archival Grain

**Citation:**  
Stoler, Ann Laura. *Along the Archival Grain: Epistemic Anxieties and Colonial Common Sense*. Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press, 2009. 336 pp. ISBN: 978-0-691-14636-5.

**Core Argument:**  
Stoler reads colonial archives not for their content but for their *form*: the genre conventions, anxieties, and classificatory habits that structure what could be recorded and how. Archives are "condensed sites of epistemic and political anxiety" and "intricate technologies of rule in themselves." Her method — working "along" rather than "against" the archival grain — attends to how classification systems were assembled, revised, and contested, rather than accepting them as neutral containers.

**Key Paraphrase (verified from University of Washington lecture summary, jsis.washington.edu):**  
Stoler looks to archives as "condensed sites of epistemic and political anxiety rather than as just uneven, incomplete, or partial sources, and as intricate technologies of rule in themselves."

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
A scored index of visual allegory is itself an archival technology — it creates a retrospective order that reflects the epistemic anxieties of its designer. Stoler's method demands that the thesis attend to *how* it constructs its categories, not merely *what* it finds within them. This is a strong argument for making the researcher's analytical moves visible — as an atlas does — rather than crystallising them into a score.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; paraphrase verified from secondary source.**

### 4e. Saidiya Hartman — Critical Fabulation

**Citation:**  
Hartman, Saidiya. "Venus in Two Acts." *Small Axe* 12, no. 2 (2008): 1–14. DOI: 10.1215/08992363-2008-009.

**Core Argument:**  
Hartman confronts the archive's silences — the lives of enslaved women who appear only as names in legal records — by developing "critical fabulation": a method that uses speculative narrative to "tell an impossible story and to amplify the impossibility of its telling." Against extractive archival methods that score and quantify what is "in" the archive, Hartman insists on the ethical weight of what is absent, distorted, or violently excluded.

**Key Paraphrase (verified from LMU Digital Scholarship blog, ds.lmu.edu, and The Nation, thenation.com):**  
Critical fabulation involves "both to tell an impossible story and to amplify the impossibility of its telling" — a methodology that holds open what cannot be categorised rather than closing it with a score.

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
Female legal allegory is full of silences analogous to Hartman's archival absences: images coded as "Justice" or "Liberty" that simultaneously encoded the subordination of real women. An index scores the visible allegorical content; critical fabulation demands attention to what the image suppresses. An atlas-topological arrangement can juxtapose a scored reading with its shadow.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; paraphrase verified from secondary sources.**

## 5. Digital Humanities Critiques (Drucker, Posner, Risam)

### 5a. Johanna Drucker — Capta vs. Data / Humanities Approaches to Graphical Display

**Citation (primary):**  
Drucker, Johanna. "Humanities Approaches to Graphical Display." *Digital Humanities Quarterly* 5, no. 1 (2011). [Open access: https://dhq-static.digitalhumanities.org/pdf/000091.pdf]

**Citation (secondary):**  
Drucker, Johanna. "Humanistic Theory and Digital Scholarship." In *Debates in the Digital Humanities*, ed. Matthew K. Gold. Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press, 2012. [Open access: https://dhdebates.gc.cuny.edu/read/untitled-88c11800-9446-469b-a3be-3fdb36bfbd1e/section/0b495250-97af-4046-91ff-98b6ea9f83c0]

**Core Argument:**  
Drucker's most influential intervention is the distinction between *data* (assumed to be given, observer-independent, self-evident) and *capta* (actively taken, interpreted, constructed). All data is capta. The conventions of information visualisation — bar charts, scatter plots, ordinal scales — were designed for empirical science and carry that epistemology into humanistic inquiry as a Trojan horse. The "rendering of statistical information into graphical form gives it a simplicity and legibility that hides every aspect of the original interpretative framework on which the statistical data were constructed."

**Key Quotations (verified from DHQ open-access PDF, pp. 2, 10, 25):**

> "Such graphical tools are a kind of intellectual Trojan horse, a vehicle through which assumptions about what constitutes information swarm with potent force. These assumptions are cloaked in a rhetoric taken wholesale from the techniques of the empirical sciences that conceals their epistemological biases under a guise of familiarity." (p. 2)

> "Data are capta, taken not given, constructed as an interpretation of the phenomenal world, not inherent in it." (p. 10)

> "The rendering of statistical information into graphical form gives it a simplicity and legibility that hides every aspect of the original interpretative framework on which the statistical data were constructed." (p. 10)

> "The graphical presentation of supposedly self-evident information … conceals these complexities, and the interpretative factors that bring the numerics into being, under a guise of graphical legibility. I cannot overstate the perniciousness of such techniques for the effect of passing construction off as real, and violating the very premises of humanistic inquiry." (p. 25)

**From "Humanistic Theory and Digital Scholarship" (verified, CUNY open access):**

> "While it may seem like an extreme statement, I think the ideology of almost all current information visualization is anathema to humanistic thought, antipathetic to its aims and values."

> "Positivistic, strictly quantitative, mechanistic, reductive and literal, these visualization and processing techniques preclude humanistic methods from their operations because of the very assumptions on which they are designed: that objects of knowledge can be understood as self-identical, self-evident, ahistorical, and autonomous."

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
The LPAI is precisely the kind of index Drucker indicts: it translates complex, context-dependent images into numerical scores as if the score were self-evident. Drucker would insist that the score is capta — a construction — and that the thesis must make visible the interpretive work that produces it. An atlas makes that work legible; an index conceals it.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH** — quotations verified from open-access PDFs with page numbers.

### 5b. Miriam Posner — "What's Next: The Radical, Unrealized Potential of Digital Humanities"

**Citation:**  
Posner, Miriam. "What's Next: The Radical, Unrealized Potential of Digital Humanities." In *Debates in the Digital Humanities 2016*, ed. Matthew K. Gold and Lauren F. Klein. Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press, 2016. [Open access: https://dhdebates.gc.cuny.edu/read/untitled/section/a22aca14-0eb0-4cc6-a622-6fee9428a357]

**Core Argument:**  
Posner argues that digital humanities has borrowed its data models and visual rhetoric from corporate and government sources, which deal with categories like gender and race "with a crudeness that would never pass muster in a peer-reviewed humanities publication." The problem is not just that DH excludes marginalized communities — it is that the underlying *data models* are structurally inadequate: "the data models for identity are containers to be filled in order to produce meaning," when in fact the classification structures *themselves* constitute data about power.

**Key Quotations (verified from open-access, CUNY dhdebates.gc.cuny.edu):**

> "I would like us to start understanding markers like gender and race not as givens but as constructions that are actively created from time to time and place to place. In other words, I want us to stop acting as though the data models for identity are containers to be filled in order to produce meaning and recognize instead that these structures themselves constitute data."

> "Most of the data and data models we have inherited deal with structures of power, like gender and race, with a crudeness that would never pass muster in a peer-reviewed humanities publication."

> "It is not only about shifting the focus of projects so that they feature marginalized communities more prominently; it is about ripping apart and rebuilding the machinery of the archive and database so that it does not reproduce the logic that got us here in the first place."

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
The LPAI operates as a data model: it defines what counts as "legal" and "allegorical" in advance. Posner's critique targets precisely this: the categories are already gendered and historically Western. An atlas allows the researcher to make the structuring gesture explicit rather than naturalising it as a score.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH** — quotations verified from publisher open access, no page numbers (web publication).

### 5c. Roopika Risam — New Digital Worlds / Postcolonial DH

**Citation:**  
Risam, Roopika. *New Digital Worlds: Postcolonial Digital Humanities in Theory, Praxis, and Pedagogy*. Evanston, IL: Northwestern University Press, 2018. 176 pp. ISBN: 978-0-8101-3794-5.

**Core Argument:**  
Risam argues that the digitisation of cultural records reproduces the "inherent imperfections" of the original colonial processes of documentation — "ethical egoism, racial bias and cultural caricature" — when those records migrate into the digital domain. Postcolonial DH must therefore practise "decolonial computing": interventionist data approaches that elevate historically disadvantaged worldviews rather than scaling up the existing digital cultural record.

**Key Paraphrase (verified from DHQ review, dhq-static.digitalhumanities.org, and MSU Open Books, openbooks.lib.msu.edu):**  
Risam contends that processes which produced initial analog records were animated by racial bias, and that digital scaling of those records "precipitates the disruptions that subsist within the digital cultural record." Standard classification and scoring systems amplify rather than correct this bias.

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
The visual corpus for a study of female legal allegory is largely drawn from European canonical art history — itself a colonial archive. Scoring images against a LPAI risks scaling up the biases embedded in that canon. Risam's decolonial approach demands that the researcher interrogate whose definition of "legal allegory" is operative before assigning scores.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; paraphrase verified from secondary sources.**

### 5d. Lev Manovich — Cultural Analytics (and its critics)

**Citation:**  
Manovich, Lev. *Cultural Analytics*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 2020. 344 pp. ISBN: 978-0-262-04355-7.

**Core Argument (and critique):**  
Manovich advocates for the computational analysis of large visual corpora — measuring colour, texture, shape, and composition across millions of images. His approach is explicitly quantitative and comparative. Critics (notably reviewed in *Space and Culture*, 2021) note that Cultural Analytics exhibits a "positivism that lurks" beneath its analytical confidence; it focuses on *how to scale up* rather than on the ethical and theoretical questions that scaling raises. The tool of "media visualisation" treats images as data-spaces with geometric relations, disregarding meaning, affect, and historical context.

**Key Paraphrase (verified from Space and Culture review, spaceandculture.com):**  
The reviewer argues that Manovich's text "gives the impression of teaching cultural pattern recognition for software scientists" while Drucker's counter-position shows that "what is needed is a philosophy of representation, not a technique."

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
Manovich represents the maximalist version of what the LPAI implicitly enacts at small scale: reducing images to quantifiable properties. The critique of Manovich is therefore the critique of the LPAI — both treat images as data rather than capta, both assume the scoring criteria are self-evident.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; paraphrase verified from Space and Culture review.**

## 6. Critiques Internal to Iconography (Moxey, Holly, Bal, Didi-Huberman)

### 6a. Mieke Bal — Against Panofskian Iconology

**Citation (primary):**  
Bal, Mieke, and Norman Bryson. "Semiotics and Art History." *Art Bulletin* 73, no. 2 (1991): 174–208. DOI: 10.2307/3045790.

**Citation (secondary):**  
Bal, Mieke. *Double Exposures: The Subject of Cultural Analysis*. New York: Routledge, 1996. ISBN: 978-0-415-91704-2.

**Core Argument:**  
Bal and Bryson (1991) identify Panofskian iconology as a "vertical interpretive model focused on original context and intention" — one that pretends to decode images step by step (pre-iconographic → iconographic → iconological), as if meanings were layered and stable. Bal shows that this schema treats images as containers of pre-given meanings awaiting expert extraction. In *Double Exposures*, she argues that "differentiating modes, if not kinds of vision — multiplying perspectives, proliferating points of view — may be a more useful strategy for examining the ideological, epistemological, and representational implications of dominating modes of vision."

**Key Quotation (verified from Scribd scan of Double Exposures):**

> "Differentiating modes, if not kinds of vision — multiplying perspectives, proliferating points of view — may be a more useful strategy for examining the ideological, epistemological, and representational implications of dominating modes of vision, including their illusory monopoly in the domain of display." (*Double Exposures*, p. 9)

**Paraphrase:** Bal argues that iconology, like any classification system that pretends to decode images hierarchically, reifies an "effect of the real" — it makes the interpretive choices look like discoveries rather than constructions. (Paraphrase of *Double Exposures* argument, verification pending for exact page.)

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
An LPAI is a formalised, scored version of Panofskian iconological analysis — it claims to identify levels of "allegorical" meaning and assign them numerical weight. Bal's critique targets the vertical, hierarchical structure of this operation. Her alternative (travelling concepts; proliferated perspectives) aligns directly with atlas-topology.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; one quotation verified.**

### 6b. Keith Moxey — Visual Time / Against Teleology

**Citation:**  
Moxey, Keith. *Visual Time: The Image in History*. Durham, NC: Duke University Press, 2013. 168 pp. ISBN: 978-0-8223-5471-8.

**Core Argument:**  
Moxey argues that art history has been bound to a Hegelian teleological model of historical development — images are ordered in time according to a narrative of progress or stylistic evolution. He proposes instead the concept of "anachrony" and "heterochrony": recognizing that images carry multiple, non-synchronous temporalities. Visual studies, he argues, needs to resist the "Hegelian model of progress" and attend to what images do — their "presence" — rather than forcing them into a developmental narrative.

**Key Paraphrase (verified from Academia.edu review of Visual Time, and Journal of Art Historiography references):**  
Moxey and Karlholm argue that art history might embrace "the temporal qualities presented by the work/image such as 'anachrony' and 'heterochrony'" to become "more responsive to the heterogeneous actualities of contemporary practice, discourse and experience," rather than retaining "its traditional focus on putting art objects in their proper chronological places."

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
An ordinal index implicitly arranges images along a scale that implies a kind of progression — from "low" to "high" allegorical content, or from less to more "legal." This is a scalar version of the teleological model Moxey critiques. An atlas allows images to be placed in multiple, non-hierarchical configurations without implying a developmental sequence.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; paraphrase verified from secondary sources.**

### 6c. Georges Didi-Huberman — Anachronism / Against Linear-Teleological Reading

**Citation:**  
Didi-Huberman, Georges. *Devant le temps: Histoire de l'art et anachronisme des images*. Paris: Les Éditions de Minuit, 2000. English translation: *Before the Image, Before Time: The Sovereignty of Anachronism.* Reprinted in *Compelling Visuality: The Work of Art In and Out of History*, ed. Claire Farago and Robert Zwijnenberg. Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press, 2003.

**Citation (exhibition):**  
Didi-Huberman, Georges. *Atlas: How to Carry the World on One's Back?* Exhibition catalogue. Madrid: Museo Nacional Centro de Arte Reina Sofía, 2010.

**Core Argument:**  
Didi-Huberman, following Warburg and Benjamin, argues that images are "temporally impure, complex, overdetermined" — "polychronistic, heterochronistic, or anachronistic objects." Art history's obsession with chronological ordering and period style — its "euchronism" — systematically fails to register the survival (*Nachleben*) of forms across time. Images do not belong to a single period; they are "extraordinary montages of heterogeneous times." His "Atlas" project recuperates Warburg's Mnemosyne as a model for art-historical method: a non-linear, relational arrangement of images that makes visible their temporal and affective entanglements.

**Key Quotations (verified from Scribd scan of Didi-Huberman, "Before the Image, Before Time"):**

> "The history of images is a history of objects that are temporally impure, complex, overdetermined. It is therefore a history of polychronistic, heterochronistic, or anachronistic objects."

> "In a first approximation, then, anachronism would be the temporal way of expressing the exuberance, complexity, and overdetermination of images."

> "The sovereign of anachronism: the historian who today would confine himself to the euchronistic past — to the Zeitgeist of Fra Angelico alone — would completely miss the point of his pictorial act."

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
An ordinal index assigns images to a single conceptual axis (legal-political allegory); it is a euchronistic, reductive gesture. Didi-Huberman shows that images exceed any single classification. His atlas model — images arranged on black panels, recombineable, held together by adjacency rather than hierarchy — is the direct methodological alternative to the LPAI.

## 7. Feminist Art-History Critiques of Reading Allegory Reductively (Warner, Wenk, Schade)

### 7a. Marina Warner — Monuments and Maidens

**Citation:**  
Warner, Marina. *Monuments and Maidens: The Allegory of the Female Form*. London: Weidenfeld and Nicolson, 1985; Berkeley: University of California Press, 1985. 417 pp. ISBN: 978-0-520-06276-0.

**Core Argument:**  
Warner's foundational study demonstrates that the use of the female form to embody abstract ideals (Justice, Liberty, Truth) is not accidental but constitutive: "The female form tends to be perceived as generic and universal, with symbolic overtones; the male as individual, even when it is being used to express a generalized idea." This universalizing function of female allegory is precisely the mechanism that makes real women disappear: "Liberty is not represented as a woman … because women were or are free. In the nineteenth century, when so many of these images were made and widely disseminated, the opposite was [true]." Reading female allegory through a scoring schema that tallies its "legal-political" content without interrogating this constitutive contradiction reproduces the very reductive move Warner diagnoses.

**Key Quotation (verified from MoMA essay, moma.org, citing Warner 1985):**

> "The female form tends to be perceived as generic and universal, with symbolic overtones; the male as individual, even when it is being used to express a generalized idea." (Warner, *Monuments and Maidens*, 1985, p. 38; cited in Pejić, "Lady Rosa of Luxembourg," MoMA essay)

**Key Quotation (verified from Goodreads summary referencing Warner's argument):**  
Warner's "general paradox": "figures of women are conventionally chosen to personify an ideal, while most women are (and have been) excluded from pursuing that ideal practically."

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
An LPAI that scores images for their "legal-political allegorical" content without tracking Warner's paradox — that female legal allegory excludes real women from the law it emblematises — will produce a distorting result. The index can capture the surface iconography but not the constitutive contradiction. An atlas allows the researcher to place the emblematic image beside the historical exclusion it performs.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; key quotation verified from MoMA secondary source.**

### 7b. Silke Wenk — Allegorical Femininity and Nation-State

**Citation:**  
Wenk, Silke. "Die steinernen Frauen" [Petrified Women]. In Sigrun Anselm and Barbara Beck (eds.), *Triumph und Scheitern in der Metropole*. Berlin: Reimer, 1987.  
Wenk, Silke. *Versteinerte Weiblichkeit: Allegorien in der Skulptur der Moderne*. Cologne: Böhlau, 1996.

**Core Argument:**  
Wenk, a German feminist art historian who has produced the most sustained scholarship on female allegory in public monuments, argues that the allegorical female figure is not a survival of antique myth but a constitutively *modern* form bound to the nation-state: "The figure of allegory appears together with the constitution of the (nation) states. It works on the basis of 'moral' and has a state-building function. The allegory is oriented against the inherited myths." Crucially, female allegories are politically central precisely because women are economically and juridically *peripheral*: "The allegories are 'female' because only the images of women — who stay outside (economic and state) competition — were appropriate to embody the imagined community's interests."

**Key Quotation (verified from MoMA essay by Pejić citing Wenk 1987, moma.org/interactives/exhibitions/2011/sanjaivekovic/essays):**

> "The allegories are 'female' because only the images of women — who stay outside (economic and state) competition — were appropriate to embody the imagined community's interests." (Wenk, "Die steinernen Frauen," 1987, cited in Pejić)

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
Wenk's analysis shows that the "legal-political" content of female allegory is inseparable from the political exclusion of women from law. An LPAI that scores images on a legal-political axis without making this structural contradiction visible will produce a systematic misreading: it will measure the allegorical *claim* (Justice = female) without registering the allegorical *displacement* (women ≠ juridical subjects). No scalar score can hold this contradiction; an atlas can juxtapose emblematic image and historical record.

**Confidence Tier: HIGH for argument; quotation verified from MoMA secondary source.**

### 7c. Sigrid Schade — Feminist Art History and the Body

**Citation:**  
Schade, Sigrid, and Silke Wenk. "Zwischen Körper und Repräsentation: Neuere Tendenzen der feministischen Kunstwissenschaft." *Kunstforum International* 114 (1991): 144–151.  
Schade, Sigrid. "Charcot and the Spectacle of the Hysterical Body." *Art History* 18, no. 4 (1995): 499–517. DOI: 10.1111/j.1467-8365.1995.tb00443.x.

**Core Argument:**  
Schade and Wenk (working together in 1991 and separately) argue that feminist art history must resist the temptation to read female bodies in images as transparent indices of social meaning. The female body in Western art is always already overdetermined — it is simultaneously a site of medical, juridical, theological, and aesthetic inscription. To score it on any single axis (legal, political, allegorical) is to flatten its polysemy and to re-enact the expert gaze that produces the body as legible data. Schade's work on Charcot's hysterical body demonstrates that "scientific" visual classification of female bodies is always a performance of institutional power.

**Paraphrase (verification pending for exact pages; argument reconstructed from both articles):**  
Schade and Wenk argue that the female body in art is a site of multiple, conflicting inscriptions that resist any single interpretive scale. A method that scores these inscriptions reduces the overdetermination that constitutes the object's meaning.

**Relevance to the LPAI Question:**  
The LPAI applies a scoring scale to images that are constitutively overdetermined. Schade and Wenk's feminist art history demands a method that holds the overdetermination open — as an atlas does.

## 8. Synthesis — Five Arguments Against a Scored Index for Female Legal Allegory; Five in Favour of Atlas-Topology

### Against the LPAI (five strongest arguments)

**Argument 1 — Commensuration erases context (Espeland & Stevens; Merry)**  
Commensuration — assigning a common metric to different entities — inevitably strips phenomena of their context, history, and meaning. Merry demonstrates this for human-rights indicators: the score makes comparison easy but makes the image *less* knowable in its specificity. A female allegory of Justice in a Brazilian courtroom and one in a Viennese Sezessionist poster share a nominal category; a score that aggregates them hides more than it reveals.

**Argument 2 — Scores perform the "god trick" (Haraway; Drucker)**  
A scalar index claims to see all images from no particular position. Haraway's "god trick" names precisely this: the pretension to a view from nowhere that is actually a view from a specific, historically situated, and politically interested position. Drucker shows that standard visualisation tools are "a kind of intellectual Trojan horse" that pass interpretive construction off as natural representation. The LPAI conceals the researcher's theoretical commitments behind a veil of numerical legibility.

**Argument 3 — Indices impose progress narratives and discipline toward conformity (Espeland & Sauder; Porter)**  
Espeland and Sauder demonstrate that ordinal rankings do not measure a pre-existing reality — they *create* the social world they claim to describe (reactivity). Porter shows that the authority of numbers derives from political need for accountability, not epistemic superiority. An LPAI creates pressure toward images that "score well" on its terms, marginalising precisely those images that are most unexpected or most challenging to the researcher's framework.

**Argument 4 — Classification systems reproduce colonial and imperial orderings (Mirzoeff; Azoulay; Risam)**  
The categories for a LPAI — what counts as "legal," "political," and "allegorical" — are derived from Euro-centric art-historical and legal traditions. Mirzoeff shows that classification-separation-aestheticisation is the structure of visuality itself; Azoulay shows that the archive's ordering is always already an imperial shutter; Risam shows that digital scaling amplifies rather than corrects these biases. A scored index applied to a corpus that includes non-Western, colonial, or subaltern images will systematically rank those images as "low" on a scale designed without them.

**Argument 5 — Female allegory is constitutively overdetermined; any single scale produces systematic misreading (Warner; Wenk; Bal; Didi-Huberman)**  
Warner shows that female allegory encodes a constitutive contradiction: women embody the law from which they are excluded. Wenk shows that female allegories are peripheral politically *because* they are symbolically central. Bal shows that images exceed any single interpretive frame; Didi-Huberman shows they are temporally impure. A scalar score can capture only one of these dimensions at a time — and will systematically produce a false result by aggregating incommensurable elements into a single number.

---

### In Favour of Atlas-Topology (five strongest arguments)

**Argument 1 — The atlas makes the researcher's analytical moves visible**  
Unlike an index (which hides interpretive choices behind a score), an atlas-topological arrangement foregrounds the acts of selection, juxtaposition, and proximity that constitute the argument. This is methodologically stronger for a doctoral thesis: it shows *how* the researcher thinks, not only *what* she found.

**Argument 2 — Adjacency as argument (Warburg's Denkraum)**  
Warburg's Mnemosyne Atlas demonstrates that meaning is generated by the spaces *between* images — the "gaps" (*Zwischenräume*) in which thought happens. An atlas-topology uses adjacency rather than ranking: two images placed beside each other generate interpretive friction that a score cannot produce. This is directly generative for a thesis on female legal allegory, where the most important arguments may emerge from unexpected visual rhymes across centuries.

**Argument 3 — Topology allows multiple, non-hierarchical orderings simultaneously**  
A topological arrangement can hold multiple classifications in tension without forcing them into a single hierarchy. The same image can be simultaneously grouped by juridical context, by formal genealogy (Justice → Liberty → Marianne), and by colonial circulation — without any of these groupings claiming priority. A scalar index must collapse these into one number; a topology can hold them open.

**Argument 4 — Atlas-topology is consistent with situated-knowledges methodology (Haraway; Barad)**  
Haraway demands that knowledge be partial, positioned, and answerable. An atlas makes the researcher's positioning explicit in the arrangement itself — every juxtaposition is a signed interpretive act. Barad's diffraction means attending to patterns of interference rather than ranking outcomes; an atlas enacts diffractive method.

**Argument 5 — Methodological resonance with the strongest traditions in visual studies (Didi-Huberman; Moxey; Bal)**  
The atlas-topological model is not merely a defensive retreat from quantification — it is positively aligned with the most theoretically sophisticated methodologies in visual studies. Didi-Huberman's recuperation of Warburg, Bal's travelling concepts, and Moxey's anachrony all converge on the conclusion that images require a method that matches their temporal and semantic complexity. For a thesis on "Iconocracia" — the politics of images — an atlas is not a methodological compromise but a methodological claim.
