# Literature Review: Female Allegory on Colonial Trade Coins
## ZW-06 — British Trade Dollar × French Piastre de Commerce (1885–1935)
## Generated: 2026-03-31 | Mode: Quick lit-review (academic-research-skills)

### Research Question

How do the British Trade Dollar (Britannia standing, trident, colonial Hong Kong/Straits Settlements) and French Piastre de Commerce (Marianne seated, fasces, colonial Indochina) deploy female allegorical figures as visual instruments of imperial authority? What are the morphological differences between these two strategies of imperial endurecimento, and what does existing scholarship say about gendered allegory, colonial numismatics, and what the thesis calls the "visual sexual contract"?

## Axis 1: Colonial Numismatics and Visual Propaganda

### Core sources

**1. SCOTT, David (2005).** "Tiny transmitters of nationalist and colonial ideology: the postage stamps of Portugal and its Empire." *Nations and Nationalism*, 50 citations.

The foundational article for mass-produced state imagery as ideological transmission. Scott argues that stamps (and by extension coins) are "tiny transmitters" of dominant ideology for the imagined community. Key finding: women on Portuguese colonial stamps "confirm the patriarchal construction of Portuguese nationalism." Direct parallel to the thesis's argument about coins as carriers of the visual sexual contract.

**Relevance for ZW-06:** The Trade Dollar and Piastre are tiny transmitters calibrated specifically for colonial circulation — they carry Britannia and Marianne into territories where these figures have no cultural precedent, making the ideological function naked.

**2. Colonial Propaganda in Belgian Congo Through Postage Stamps (2025).** *Journal of Intercultural Communication*. 149 stamps, quantitative content analysis.

Methodological model: quantitative content analysis of 149 colonial stamps across phases of colonization. Key finding: "The native person appears as preferably male, vulnerable and dependent, exotic in his semi-nudity." The thesis's IconoCode indicators (especially dessexualização and apagamento_narrativo) could operationalize this same analysis for coins.

**3. Italian Colonial Stamps (2022).** *Nations and Nationalism*, 1 citation.

Shows that "an image of exoticism reinforces the idea of the nation as a new empire" — domination expressed through visual culture. The quantitative method (content analysis of propaganda themes across periods) is directly applicable to the endurecimento trajectory the thesis proposes.

**4. Zimbabwe Banknotes and Sovereignty (2025).** *Nations and Nationalism*.

Argues banknotes are "repositories of the nation‐state's collective memory" through semiotic analysis. Extends from colonial to postcolonial period, showing how banknote iconography "mirrors local and foreign policies." Useful for the thesis's argument about continuity and rupture in state imagery.

**5. RAENTO et al. / Designing the Nation (2011).** *Political Geography*, 106 citations.

Critical of the assumption that "the state controls its own representation." Argues that non-state actors (commercial banks, designers, engravers) are co-constitutive of state imagery. This complicates the thesis's model: the Trade Dollar was designed by George W. De Saulles (Royal Mint), not by Parliament. The ENDURECIMIENTO is a designer's choice within institutional constraints.

## Axis 2: Gendered Allegory in Imperial Culture

### Core sources

**6. HELMREICH / Domesticating Britannia (2003).** In *Art, Nation and Gender* (ed. Cusack), 12 citations.

Part of the key volume on gendered national allegory. Examines Britannia in *Punch* (1870–1880): how the martial Britannia was "domesticated" through satirical imagery. Key for the thesis: shows that Britannia oscillates between MILITAR and NORMATIVO regimes depending on context — the same figure hardens and softens.

**7. NIELSON, C.J. (2016).** "Erotic Attachment, Identity Formation and the Body Politic: The Woman-as-Nation in Canadian Graphic Satire, 1867–1914." *Gender & History*, 28(1), 102–126.

Argues that "the repetitive deployment of feminised and eroticised images of the nation summoned particular gender, sexual and political identities into being." Key insight: Miss Canada's "normative representation as white conflated racial whiteness and Canadian-ness" — the woman-as-nation trope constructs both gender AND racial subjectivities simultaneously. Direct theoretical support for the thesis's concept of the visual sexual contract as also a visual racial contract.

**8. Domesticating 'the heart of the wild' (1997, 25 cit.).** *Women's History Review*.

Female personifications of British white colonies (Australia, Canada, NZ, South Africa). These figures "became ideal images for attracting both male and female emigrants" and were "imperial agents for preventing racial and moral decline." The racial dimension is explicit: female allegory as guardian of whiteness.

**9. Female Bodies of (Inter)National Significance (2019, 3 cit.).** *Dix-Neuf*.

Argues that "the allegorization of the female body as a national body after 1789 fixed the concept of femininity and distinguished between the representation of universal rights and access to them." Benoist's *Portrait d'une négresse* (1800) deliberately evokes allegory to challenge the racial limits of republican universalism. Critical for the thesis: the revolutionary Marianne claims universality but her body is white.

**10. 'Brexitannia' (2020).** *Open Arts Journal*.

Contemporary update: Britannia on Brexit commemorative coins. Shows that the national allegory remains active, revealing "forms of women's agency used in the name of British nationhood." The longevity of the Pathosformel.

## Axis 3: Toward the "Visual Sexual Contract"

### Theoretical foundations

The thesis proposes the concept of the **Contrato Sexual Visual** — extending Carole Pateman's *sexual contract* (1988) into the domain of state iconography. No existing scholarship uses this exact term, but several converge on its territory:

**11. McCLINTOCK, Anne (1995).** *Imperial Leather: Race, Gender, and Sexuality in the Colonial Contest.* Routledge. ~600 citations.

The foundational text for gender + race + visual culture in imperialism. McClintock's analysis of "commodity spectacle" and "the feminization of colonial space" provides the theoretical ground for the thesis's argument that colonial trade coins feminize the imperial subject while masculinizing imperial power. The Trade Dollar's standing Britannia with trident = the imperial phallus; the Piastre's seated Marianne = the domesticated civilizing mission.

**12. SHARPE, Jenny (1993).** *Allegories of Empire: The Figure of Woman in the Colonial Text.* University of Minnesota Press. ~201 citations.

Argues that the female figure in colonial texts is always allegorical — never simply representational. The woman stands for the nation, the colony, the civilizing mission. Key for the thesis: on colonial coins, the female allegory is LITERALLY an allegory — the figure is labeled (REPUBLIQUE FRANÇAISE, BRITANNIA) and its function is explicitly juridical-political.

**13. Coloniality of Gender / LUGONES (via Coloniality at work, 2016, 70 cit.).**

The "coloniality of gender" (Lugones 2007/2010) argues that gender itself was a colonial imposition. The thesis can extend this: the female allegorical body on colonial coins exports a European gender system onto colonized territories. The Trade Dollar carries Britannia into Hong Kong; the Piastre carries Marianne into Saigon — both carry the European feminine as sovereign form.

**14. Suffrage Visual Culture (Stevenson, 2023).** *Gender & History*.

Examines how "white suffragists appropriated iconography primarily for the benefit of white women" — the bound/unbound female body as political metaphor. Relevant for the thesis's argument that the allegorical female body on coins mediates between freedom (for the metropole) and subjection (for the colony).
