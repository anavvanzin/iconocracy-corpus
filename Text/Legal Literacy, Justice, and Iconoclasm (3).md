# **The Iconocratic State and the Vandalized Goddess: A Deep Research Framework on Legal Literacy, Female Allegory, and the January 8 Iconoclasm**

## **1\. The Crisis of Representation: From the King’s Body to the Abstract State**

The storming of the Praça dos Três Poderes in Brasília on January 8, 2023, constituted a rupture in the visual continuity of the Brazilian republic. When rioters ascended the ramp of the Supreme Federal Court (STF) and defaced Alfredo Ceschiatti’s monumental sculpture *A Justiça* (1961), they engaged in a violent renegotiation of the state’s visual infrastructure. The graffiti scrawled across the statue’s chest—“Perdeu, mané” (You lost, fool)—was not merely an act of vandalism; it was a demonstration of "counter-literacy," a brutal reading that stripped the allegorical figure of her universal pretensions and reduced her to a partisan combatant in a localized political struggle. This event serves as the fulcrum for a comprehensive research project on legal literacy, necessitating a shift from the study of textual competence to an investigation of how legitimacy is staged, maintained, and destroyed through images.  
This report establishes the architectural and theoretical specifications for such a project. It posits that legal literacy must be redefined as an "infrastructural and visual competence"—the historically situated capacity to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material cues, spatial arrangements, and iconographic systems. Modern legal orders, despite their claims to be impersonal, code-based, and bureaucratic, have historically depended on dense iconographic networks to make their authority legible. From the French Revolution’s reinvention of sovereignty to the high modernist construction of Brasília, the abstract principles of the state have been consistently embodied in the female form.  
The central paradox to be explored is the "visual sexual contract". Precisely during the historical periods when women were most systematically excluded from political agency, civil capacity, and the legal profession, the female body proliferated as the omnipresent official face of the Law and the Nation. Figures such as Marianne, Britannia, Germania, and Ceschiatti’s *A Justiça* functioned as conduits for legal knowledge, teaching the public "what law looks like" while simultaneously masking the gendered exclusions of the actual legal order. The project summary detailed here seeks to reconstruct this background knowledge, tracing the genealogy of the female allegory from the 18th-century revolutionary Atlantic to the vandalized modernist plazas of 21st-century Brazil.

### **1.1 The Representational Vacuum**

To understand the gravity of the January 8 attacks, one must first understand the historical problem the statue was meant to solve. The transition to political modernity, marked by the Atlantic Revolutions of the late 18th century, created a profound crisis of representation. Under the Old Regime, the state was embodied. Ernst Kantorowicz’s theory of the "King's Two Bodies" elucidates this pre-modern logic: the Monarch possessed a body natural (mortal and fallible) and a body politic (immortal and infallible). The physical presence of the King *was* the law; his seal was the state’s guarantee; his image on a coin was a shard of his sovereignty.  
The decapitation of Louis XVI—symbolic of the broader republican turn—destroyed the "natural body" of the state. This created a terrifying representational vacuum. The new sovereigns were abstractions: "The People," "The Nation," "The Constitution," "The Law." These concepts possess no physical form. One cannot shake hands with the General Will; one cannot bow to the Civil Code. Yet, the psychological need for authority to appear in a recognizable, human form persisted. The state required a face to be loved, feared, and obeyed.  
The solution found by the 18th and 19th-century republicans was the female allegory. This choice was driven by a convergence of grammatical accident and political strategy. In Latin and Romance languages, the abstract nouns for virtues are inherently feminine: *Libertas* (Liberty), *Justitia* (Justice), *La République* (The Republic), *A Justiça* (The Justice). It was linguistically natural to personify them as women. However, the political utility of the female form was paramount. As historians Maurice Agulhon and Marina Warner have argued, a male figure represents a *ruler*. A statue of a man inevitably resembles a specific King, General, or President—a competitor for power. A female figure, historically excluded from ruling and legally defined as a minor, implies *ideals* rather than personal power. She represents the state without threatening to usurp it. She is the "Mother" of the citizens, not their master.

### **1.2 The Scope of the Inquiry**

This project tracks how this solution hardened into a global visual system. By the late 19th century, a transnational visual literacy had emerged. An illiterate immigrant arriving in Rio de Janeiro, New York, or Paris could navigate the state through its female avatars. The woman with the scales was the court; the woman with the cap was the Republic; the woman with the trident was the Empire.  
The research proposed here spans from the "representational crisis" of 1789 to the "iconoclastic crisis" of 2023\. It connects the "scythe of equality" described by Paolo Grossi, which leveled the legal subject into a naked abstraction, with the modernist abstraction of Ceschiatti’s sculpture, which stripped Justice of her scales, leaving her only with a sword and a blindfold. By examining the "background knowledge" of legality, we can understand why the attack on the statue was interpreted not as property damage, but as an assassination of the state itself.

## **2\. Redefining Legal Literacy: Beyond the Textual**

Traditional definitions of legal literacy focus on "black-letter law"—the ability to read statutes, understand rights, and navigate procedural complexities. This text-centric view, however, excludes the vast majority of historical actors. For the illiterate peasant in 19th-century France, the enslaved African in Brazil, or the working-class citizen in Victorian London, the law was not encountered in books. It was encountered in the coin exchanged in the market, the stamp on a letter, the relief on a courthouse façade, and the statue in the public square.

### **2.1 The Tripartite Definition of Legal Literacy**

This project proposes a comprehensive tripartite definition of legal literacy to guide future research, expanding the Academy's understanding of how legal knowledge is transmitted.

| Literacy Type | Definition | Primary Media | Target Demographic |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Textual Competence** | Mastery of codes, statutes, and doctrines; "Black-letter law." | Code books, case law, gazettes. | Lawyers, Judges, Elites. |
| **Infrastructural Competence** | The ability to navigate institutions; knowing where to go, who to bribe, how to file. | Forms, counter-tops, waiting rooms. | Litigants, Bureaucrats. |
| **Visual/Symbolic Competence** | The background knowledge of authority; recognizing the "face" of the state and its implicit threats/promises. | Statues, coins, stamps, architecture. | The General Public (including subalterns). |

The January 8 attacks demonstrate that this third form of literacy is not passive. The rioters possessed a high degree of visual competence; they knew exactly which symbols embodied the power they sought to dismantle. They did not burn the library of the court first; they attacked the *image* of the court. The destruction of the *A Justiça* statue, alongside the slashing of Di Cavalcanti’s *As Mulatas* and the smashing of the *Araguaia* stained glass, reveals that the "war on images" is a central front in contemporary political conflict.

### **2.2 Background Knowledge as Legal Infrastructure**

The concept of "background knowledge" is crucial here. It refers to the tacit, shared understandings that make a legal system functional. It is the instinct that tells a citizen that a building with classical columns and a statue of a blindfolded woman is a place of judgment, not a place of commerce. This knowledge is "infrastructural" because it undergirds the textual law. Without the visual cues that generate awe, respect, or fear, the text of the law remains inert.  
Research indicates that in the 19th century, states invested heavily in producing this background knowledge. They minted coins with the face of the Republic to ensure that the image of the state circulated in the most intimate sphere of daily life—the market. They placed statues of Justice on courthouse pediments to "teach" the public that the violence that happened inside was not brute force, but "Justice". This project argues that legal historians must reconstruct this visual pedagogy to understand how the law was lived by those who could not read it.

## **3\. The Visual Sexual Contract: Theory and Mechanism**

The core critical innovation of this project is the concept of the "visual sexual contract." This theoretical framework integrates legal history with feminist political theory to explain the gendered nature of state iconography.

### **3.1 The Contractual Paradox**

Carole Pateman famously argued in *The Sexual Contract* that the social contract is underpinned by a prior fraternal settlement that organizes women’s subordination. The modern state is a pact among brothers (men) that grants them equal political rights while maintaining their patriarchal right over women. This project extends Pateman's analysis to the visual field.  
The "visual sexual contract" describes a regime of symbolic hypervisibility that acts as a substitute for political inclusion. In the 19th and early 20th centuries, precisely when women were most rigorously excluded from the franchise, the jury box, and the legislature, the female body was omnipresent as the symbol of the state. This was not a contradiction; it was a mechanism of the contract.

### **3.2 Mechanisms of Operation**

The visual sexual contract operates through three distinct mechanisms, which the research will track across national contexts:

1. **Substitution:** The allegorical woman occupies the symbolic space where real women are absent. Marianne represents the French Republic, but French women cannot vote until 1944\. The image serves as a "phantom" presence, allowing the state to claim it represents "humanity" while only empowering men.  
2. **Sacralization:** The feminine figure is elevated to a plane of moral purity precisely because she is removed from the "grubby" reality of political bargaining. Justice is pure *because* she does not participate in the dirty work of politics. She sits above it, frozen in stone.  
3. **De-sexualization and Disciplining:** To function as a state symbol, the female body must be disciplined. It is often draped in heavy classical robes, maternalized, or monumentalized to strip it of erotic or threatening agency. It is the "orderly" body that replaced the "disorderly" body of the witch or the revolutionary virago.

### **3.3 The Scythe of Equality**

Paolo Grossi’s metaphor of the "scythe of equality" (*falce dell’uguaglianza*) provides the legal-historical context for this aesthetic. The French Revolution sought to eliminate the intermediate bodies of the Old Regime (guilds, estates, corporations) to create a direct relationship between the State and the Individual. This produced the "socially naked" legal subject—an abstract unit devoid of specific history or status.  
Female allegory was the perfect aesthetic correlate to this legal doctrine. Just as the Code Civil created an abstract citizen, the statue of the Republic created an abstract body—one that was "nobody in particular" and therefore could be "everybody." A statue of a specific male hero divides the populace (some love him, some hate him). A statue of an abstract woman unites them, precisely because she has no biography, no history, and no political ambition. The image bridged the gap between the messy reality of society and the pristine unity of the legal order.

## **4\. Genealogy of the Allegorical Body (1789–1945)**

To understand the target of the January 8 attacks, the project must reconstruct the lineage of the weaponized female form. This section of the research traces the transatlantic circulation of these icons, focusing on the "Golden Age" of allegory from the French Revolution to the end of World War II.

### **4.1 The Revolutionary Split: Marianne’s Two Bodies**

The decapitation of Louis XVI created the need for a new sovereign face. The choice of Marianne was immediate, but contested. The research identifies a split in the iconography of Marianne that reflects the tension between revolutionary violence and state order.

* **The Radical Marianne (The Virago):** Exemplified by Delacroix’s *Liberty Leading the People* (1830). She is bare-breasted, active, dirty, and violent. She wears the Phrygian cap (symbol of the freed slave). She holds a pike or a flag. She is leading a mob. This figure is dangerous to the established order.  
* **The Conservative Marianne (The Statue):** This became the official seal of the Republic. She is seated (static), fully clothed (decorous), and holds the scales of justice or the tablets of the law. She wears a diadem or olive wreath instead of the revolutionary cap. She is the state *stabilized*.

The project tracks how the "Conservative Marianne" became the dominant model for legal iconography, engaging in a "disciplining" of the image that paralleled the disciplining of the working class and women in the Napoleonic Code.

### **4.2 Transatlantic Variations: Britannia, Germania, Helvetia**

This visual vocabulary became the global *lingua franca* of sovereignty.

* **Britannia:** In Britain, the female allegory coexisted with a living female monarch (Victoria). Yet, Britannia served a distinct function: she represented the *Empire* and maritime dominance (trident/shield). While Victoria was the head of state, Britannia was the spirit of the state’s reach. She allowed the monarchy to remain personal while the state became imperial and abstract.  
* **Germania:** In the German lands, the allegory shifted from a romantic figure of cultural unity to a martial figure of imperial defense after the 1871 unification. She stands guard on the Rhine, holding the imperial sword. This shift militarized the female body to serve a patriarchal state, transforming the "mother" into the "valkyrie".  
* **Helvetia:** In Switzerland, Helvetia served to unite a linguistically and religiously fractured confederation. She appears on coins as a unifying "mother" figure, smoothing over the differences between cantons.

### **4.3 The Brazilian Reception and Racial Whitening**

When Brazil declared itself a Republic in 1889, it imported the French iconographic kit almost wholesale. The "Effigy of the Republic" (still seen on the Real currency) wears the Phrygian cap and laurel wreath. However, the project must address the specific racial dimension of this reception in a post-slavery society.  
The Brazilian Republic’s image was aggressively Europeanized. Despite the country’s demographic reality, the official face of the state was a white, Greco-Roman woman. This "whitening" of the allegory served to project an image of "civilization" to Europe while masking the reality of the Afro-Brazilian population. The "Visual Sexual Contract" in Brazil was thus also a "Visual Racial Contract," offering symbolic inclusion only through a whitened, Europeanized avatar.

### **4.4 Lag Analysis: Measuring the Gap**

A key methodological innovation of this project is "Lag Analysis". This involves measuring the temporal gap between the consolidation of the female allegory as the symbol of the state and the granting of political rights to women. This metric provides quantitative evidence for the hypothesis that allegorical hypervisibility compensates for political invisibility.  
**Table 1: Lag Analysis of Allegory vs. Suffrage**

| Nation | Allegory Consolidation | Women's Suffrage | Lag (Years) | Notes |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **France** | \~1792 (Marianne adopted) | 1944 | **\~152 Years** | High usage of allegory; extreme delay in rights. |
| **Switzerland** | \~1850 (Helvetia on coins) | 1971 | **\~121 Years** | Strong iconographic unity; latest suffrage in West. |
| **Brazil** | 1889 (Republic Proclaimed) | 1932 | **43 Years** | Allegory imported; rights granted earlier than France. |
| **Germany** | 1871 (Imperial Germania) | 1918 | **47 Years** | Martial allegory linked to Empire; suffrage follows WWI defeat. |
| **USA** | \~1790s (Columbia/Liberty) | 1920 | **\~130 Years** | Ubiquitous usage on currency/statuary. |

The data suggests a correlation: the nations that most heavily relied on the female form to conceptualize their sovereignty (France, Switzerland) were among the slowest to grant women political agency. The image served as a powerful mask for the exclusion.

## **5\. Brasília and the Modernist Goddess: The Case of *A Justiça***

The research pivots from the 19th century to the 20th century with the construction of Brasília. Here, the European allegorical tradition meets High Modernism, resulting in the specific artifact that was attacked on January 8\.

### **5.1 The Modernist Context**

Brasília, inaugurated in 1960, was designed as a "total work of art" (Gesamtkunstwerk). Oscar Niemeyer’s architecture sought to express the rationality, transparency, and progress of the Brazilian state. The buildings are characterized by glass façades, suggesting that power is transparent and accessible to the people. However, as the project will argue, this transparency is a form of "simulacrum". It offers visual access without political access. The citizen can *see* the workings of power through the glass, but cannot touch them. The statue of *A Justiça* mediates this relationship: she sits outside, a stone guard, marking the boundary between the profane public square and the sacred space of the Court.

### **5.2 Alfredo Ceschiatti’s *A Justiça* (1961)**

Alfredo Ceschiatti’s sculpture is the central artifact of the project. Carved from a monolithic block of Petrópolis granite, measuring 3.3 meters in height, it sits before the Supreme Federal Court. The statue represents a critical evolution in the iconography of justice. Unlike the baroque or neoclassical figures of Europe, Ceschiatti’s Justice is stripped of ornamentation. She is smooth, geometric, and imposing.  
**Table 2: Iconographic Attributes of Justice**

| Attribute | Traditional/Neoclassical | Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* (1961) | Symbolic Implication of Change |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Eyes** | Blindfolded (sometimes open in early depictions). | **Blindfolded.** | Canonizes "impartiality" and cognitive withdrawal. |
| **Posture** | Standing or Seated. | **Seated.** | Implies permanence, stability, and passivity. |
| **Right Hand** | Sword (raised or held). | **Sword (resting on lap).** | Potential for coercion; dormant but ready force. |
| **Left Hand** | Scales (Balance). | **ABSENT (No Scales).** | **Crucial Shift:** Removal of the "weighing" function. |
| **Dress** | Elaborate Robes/Armor. | **Stylized/Smooth.** | Modernist abstraction; timelessness. |

### **5.3 The Mystery of the Missing Scales**

The most significant iconographic intervention by Ceschiatti is the **omission of the scales**. In traditional iconography, the scales represent the weighing of evidence, the deliberation, the "intellectual" side of judgment. The sword represents the executive power to punish. By removing the scales, Ceschiatti creates a figure that is "all execution."  
Interpretations of this omission vary:

* **The "Unfinished" Justice:** Some suggest it implies that justice in Brazil is not yet weighed, or that the task is incomplete.  
* **The Court as Scale:** A more institutional reading suggests that the *building itself* (the STF) is the scale, and the statue is merely the guardian.  
* **Authoritarianism:** A critical reading posits that a Justice with a sword but no scales represents a legal system defined by force rather than equity.

This iconographic simplification fits the modernist ethos of Brasília—rational, uncluttered, direct. However, it also creates a symbolic vulnerability. A traditional Justice with scales reads clearly as a "neutral weigher." Ceschiatti’s Justice, enigmatic and armed, provided a blanker screen onto which the rioters of January 8 could project their narrative of a "tyrannical" judiciary.

## **6\. The January 8 Iconoclasm: Anatomy of an Attack**

The events of January 8, 2023, provide the empirical climax of the project. The attack on the statue was not collateral damage; it was a targeted assassination of a symbol. The rioters did not merely break things; they engaged in a complex semiotic performance.

### **6.1 The Act of Vandalism**

Images and reports from the event reveal the specific nature of the violence against the statue:

* **The Graffiti:** The phrase "**Perdeu, mané**" (You lost, fool/dude) was written in red (using lipstick or marker found in government offices) across the chest of the statue.  
* **The Physicality:** Rioters climbed onto the statue, sitting on her lap and hanging Brazilian flags around her neck. This physical domination reverses the hierarchy where the citizen stands in awe below the pedestal.  
* **The Context of the Phrase:** "Perdeu, mané" was a direct quote from Supreme Court Justice Luís Roberto Barroso. In November 2022, while in New York, Barroso was heckled by a bolsonarista questioning the election results. Barroso retorted, "Perdeu, mané, não amola" (You lost, fool, don't bother me).

### **6.2 Counter-Literacy and Re-Captioning**

The project interprets this act through the lens of "counter-literacy." The rioters demonstrated a sophisticated understanding of the "visual contract." They knew that the Court’s legitimacy rested on its claim to be the neutral, abstract arbiter (the blindfolded woman). To destroy that legitimacy, they engaged in an act of "ventriloquism."  
By writing Barroso’s dismissive, partisan words onto the chest of the abstract Goddess, they collapsed the distinction between the "Two Bodies of the Law." They forced the "Body Natural" of the Judge (Barroso, seen as biased and arrogant) to merge with the "Body Politic" of the Statue (Justice). They effectively captioned the statue, changing its meaning from "I am Eternal Justice" to "I am a Political Player who Mocks You."  
This was an act of iconoclastic unmasking. It rejected the "allegorical universalization" by asserting that the statue was a lie. The use of lipstick—a gendered tool—to deface the gendered allegory adds another layer of complexity, reclaiming the "female" surface for a raw, visceral message of rejection.

### **6.3 The Legal and Curatorial Aftermath**

The state's response confirms the importance of the symbol.

* **Sentencing:** The Supreme Federal Court (STF) treated the vandalism with extreme severity. **Débora Rodrigues dos Santos**, the woman identified as writing the graffiti, was sentenced to **14 years in prison** for crimes including the violent abolition of the democratic rule of law and damage to protected heritage. This harsh sentence underscores that attacking the *image* of the state is legally equivalent to attacking the *body* of the state.  
* **Restoration:** The statue was swiftly cleaned and restored. The promptness of this "healing" was a political necessity. The state could not allow the "scar" of the graffiti to remain, as it would permanently fix the partisan meaning onto the universal symbol. The restoration was a ritual of re-consecration, attempting to erase the counter-literacy and restore the silent authority of the stone.

## **7\. Comparative Case Studies: Living vs. Stone Allegories**

To fully understand the tension inherent in the female allegory, the project compares the "Stone Justice" of Brasília with incidents where the allegory "came to life."

### **7.1 The Marianne Stamp Controversy (2013)**

In 2013, France released a new stamp depicting Marianne. The artist, Olivier Ciappa, revealed that the model for the new Marianne was **Inna Shevchenko**, a leader of the radical feminist group **FEMEN**. Shevchenko was famous for topless protests, including sawing down crosses and disrupting political events.  
This sparked a massive controversy. Conservative critics were outraged that the symbol of the Republic was modeled on a "radical" and "disorderly" woman. This incident perfectly illustrates the "Visual Sexual Contract":

* The state *needs* the female image (Marianne).  
* But the state *fears* the real female political agent (Shevchenko).  
* When the "Stone Marianne" is revealed to be the "Living Shevchenko," the contract snaps. The allegory only works when the woman is abstract, silent, and contained. When she is a real, political actor, she becomes a threat to the state's dignity.

### **7.2 The Thatcher/Britannia Satire**

Similarly, the project examines how Margaret Thatcher was depicted as Britannia during the Falklands War. Initially, the association empowered her (The Iron Lady). However, as her popularity waned, the comparison became satirical and grotesque. This demonstrates that the allegory is a double-edged sword: it can confer authority, but it can also be used to mock women who step out of the "symbolic" realm into the "political" realm.

## **8\. Theoretical Synthesis and Project Methodology**

### **8.1 Methodological Protocol: Iconographic Reconstruction**

The report proposes a rigorous methodological protocol entitled **"Iconographic Reconstruction for Legal History"** to govern the research. This protocol treats images as legal texts that must be "read" with the same rigor as a statute.  
**Protocol Steps:**

1. **Iconographic Description:** Cataloging attributes (sword, scales, cap, breast, posture). What is present? What is missing (e.g., the scales in Ceschiatti)?  
2. **Pedagogy of Encounter:** Analyzing the setting. Who sees this image? Is it held in the hand (coin) or viewed from below (statue)? What does it teach about the user's relationship to the state? (Intimacy vs. Awe).  
3. **Intervisual Genealogy:** Tracing the motif. Does this Brazilian coin copy a French coin? Does Ceschiatti’s statue cite Michelangelo or Egyptian Ma’at?  
4. **Gendered Operation:** How does the femininity of the figure function? Does it solicit love? Does it promise protection? Does it mask violence?  
5. **Contestation Analysis:** How has the image been mocked, satirized, or vandalized? (e.g., Suffragette defacement of pennies, the Jan 8 graffiti).

### **8.2 The Corpus**

The project will curate a comparative corpus of visual legal artifacts from 1789 to the present, focusing on four "supports" with distinct circulation logics:

1. **Monetary Iconography:** Coins and banknotes. These represent the "intimate ubiquity" of the state. Every time a citizen buys bread, they touch the allegory.  
2. **Postal/Philatelic Iconography:** Stamps. These are "global communicators" of national virtues.  
3. **Monumental Programs:** Statues and courthouse pediments. These operate through "monumental verticality," dominating public space.  
4. **Legal Paratexts:** Official seals, letterheads, and code frontispieces. These are the "authenticating images" that give documents legal force.

## **9\. Conclusion: The Fragility of the Stone State**

The January 8 attacks revealed that the "infrastructural competence" of the modern state is fragile. The background knowledge that sustains the rule of law—the shared belief that the statue *is* Justice—can evaporate in a moment of political crisis.  
The research concludes that the "Visual Sexual Contract" is fraying. The 19th-century solution of using an abstract female body to mask political conflict is no longer effective in a 21st-century polarized environment. When the public possesses "counter-literacy"—the ability to read the statue not as a universal mother but as a partisan trick—the simulacrum dissolves. The statue becomes just a rock. The court becomes just a building. The law becomes just words.  
This project proposes that the task of legal history in the 21st century is to understand how these images were built, how they held the world together for two centuries, and why they are now cracking. It is a project about the "ghosts" in the machine of the law—the female ghosts that haunt the marble halls of power, silent until the moment someone climbs up and writes words of rage across their chest.  
By integrating the deep history of the **Visual Sexual Contract** with the sharp shock of the **January 8 Iconoclasm**, this research offers a vital new lens for understanding the crisis of democratic legitimacy in Brazil and beyond. It defines legal literacy not just as the ability to read the law, but as the power to see the structures that make the law visible—and to recognize when those structures are under attack.
