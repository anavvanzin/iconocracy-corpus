## PART 2: NARRATIVE SYNTHESIS BY CLUSTER

---

### CLUSTER 1 — Female Allegories in State/Legal Iconography

**Main Debates.** The scholarship on female national allegories has developed along three main axes since the foundational works of Agulhon (which you already know). The first axis is **country-specific iconographic history**: scholars trace the biography of individual allegories — Marianne (Agulhon and his successors), Britannia (Millat, Scott, Mellini & Matthews, Jouve), Germania (Brandt, Watanabe-O'Kelly & Colvin), and the Brazilian Republic (Carvalho, Schwarcz). The second axis is **comparative**: David Scott's "Marianne and Britannia" (1996) and the EHNE encyclopedic entry represent attempts to identify structural commonalities and differences across national traditions. The third, most theoretically ambitious axis is **gender-critical**: Hunt, Landes, and Warner (whom you know) ask *why* these allegories are female, and what work that femininity performs.

**Consensus and Findings.** There is broad consensus that the "golden age" of female national personifications stretched from the French Revolution to World War I, a period coinciding exactly with the consolidation of modern nation-states and the exclusion of women from formal citizenship. This temporal correlation is not accidental. The EHNE survey confirms that virtually every European state created a female national allegory during this period: Hispania, Hungaria, Borussia, Bavaria, Saxonia, Polonia, Helvetia, Suomi-neito, Mother Russia, and more. Millat's quantitative study of Britannia in cartoons (400+ images, 1842–1999) demonstrates that the allegory's fate closely tracked national power: "Britannia sublime" dominated during the Victorian apogee, while "Britannia grotesque" took over after 1918, reflecting imperial decline. Brandt's study of Germania shows a parallel trajectory: from pacific mother (Maximilian I) to armored warrior (1848/1871) to absence (post-1918/1945). In Brazil, Carvalho demonstrated that the republican female allegory failed to take root because the conditions that made Marianne work in France — active female participation in revolution, a strong visual public sphere — were absent. The Brazilian woman, as Carvalho memorably put it, "se pública, era prostituta" — the very concept of a "public woman" carried connotations of prostitution rather than citizenship.

**Gaps.** Despite this rich literature, three significant gaps emerge. First, **no existing study systematically compares the legal-institutional deployment** of female allegories across multiple countries — how they appeared specifically in courthouses, legal documents, stamps, and coins as instruments of *legal* (not merely national) legitimation. Studies tend to focus on either artistic/cultural or numismatic contexts separately. Second, **the relationship between the decline of female allegory and the rise of women's political rights** has been noted (Mellini & Matthews) but never systematically theorized. Your iconocracy framework could fill this gap. Third, **the Brazilian case remains understudied** relative to France, Britain, and Germany. Beyond Carvalho and Schwarcz, there is no systematic iconographic study of female allegory in Brazilian legal spaces (courthouses, legal publications, official stamps, currency).

### CLUSTER 2 — Gender, Sovereignty, and Visual Culture of Law

**Main Debates.** This cluster represents the most theoretically dense and interdisciplinary field in your mapping. The key debate centers on whether legal visual culture is merely **reflective** (representing pre-existing authority) or **constitutive** (producing authority through visual means). Peter Goodrich's work — from "Visiocracy" (2013) through *Legal Emblems* (2014) to *Retinal Justice* (2023) — argues forcefully for the constitutive position. His concept of "visiocracy" describes a regime where sovereignty is transmitted through visual emblems, judicial ceremony, and architectural grandeur. Crucially, he demonstrates that this regime operates most effectively when it goes unnoticed: the visual apparatus of law is "so embedded as to be presupposed, so familiar that they are unrecognized." The Chase & Thong experiment at NYU (2012) provides empirical support: law students consistently perceived greater legitimacy in formal courtrooms, despite their training in legal reasoning.

Martin Jay's "Must Justice Be Blind?" (1996/1999) brings critical theory and feminist analysis to bear on the most ubiquitous legal allegory. Drawing on Horkheimer and Adorno, Jay argues that Justitia's blindfold represents the suppression of visual particularity in favor of abstract equivalence — a suppression that feminist theorists identify as gendered. The blindfold closes off the "female gaze" and imposes a masculine, formalist vision of justice. Resnik and Curtis's monumental *Representing Justice* (2011) provides the most comprehensive empirical mapping of Justitia across time and space, documenting how she became a "legitimizing aspect of state power" while remaining overwhelmingly represented as a white woman. Their observation that only one known depiction shows Justice as a woman of color resonates with Schwarcz's work on racial science and national imagery in Brazil.

Joan Landes's *Visualizing the Nation* (2001) provides the crucial bridge between gender theory and visual analysis. Her argument that revolutionary visual culture was "constitutively gendered" — that the Republic was figured as female *because* the public sphere was being reconstituted as masculine — is the most direct theoretical precedent for your iconocracy framework. Landes shows that Marianne and Liberty functioned as "screen images" managing anxieties about female political agency: the allegorical woman on the pedestal simultaneously invoked and contained the threat of real women in public space.

**Consensus.** There is growing consensus across disciplines that legal visual culture is not ornamental but operative — that it does real work in producing perceptions of legitimacy, authority, and justice. The Berkeley Judicial Institute's "Are Courthouses Male or Female?" initiative (2022) reflects this awareness reaching practical/policy domains, with the striking formulation: "courthouses are gendered, because the law is gendered, and that gender is male."

**Gaps.** The most significant gap is the absence of a **unified theoretical framework** connecting the visual culture of law (Goodrich), the gendered politics of allegory (Landes, Hunt), and the critique of abstract sovereignty (Pateman, whom you know). Your "iconocracy" concept could provide exactly this synthesis. Additionally, Rosanvallon's work on democratic symbolization — his insight that "symbolization is a collective reflection" and that "the resymbolization of politics is part of a permanent questioning of social differences" — has not been systematically connected to the gendered allegory literature. The intersection of **race and gender** in legal allegory remains understudied outside the Brazilian context: how do racial hierarchies inflect which female bodies can represent the law?

### CLUSTER 3 — Iconoclasm, Image Destruction, and Political Contestation

**Main Debates.** The scholarship on modern political iconoclasm has exploded since 2020, but its intellectual roots are older. Gamboni's *The Destruction of Art* (1997) remains the foundational work, establishing the framework for understanding image destruction as a constitutive political act rather than mere vandalism. Gamboni shows that the French Revolution — paradigmatic for your period — involved both systematic *destruction* of royal/religious imagery and systematic *creation* of new republican allegory. This dialectic of destruction and creation is precisely what your thesis tracks.

The 2020 wave of monument toppling (post-George Floyd) generated a burst of new scholarship that significantly expanded the geographic and conceptual scope of iconoclasm studies. The *English Historical Review* symposium (2023) and the *Comparativ* special issue (2021) are particularly valuable because they include **Latin American cases** that earlier scholarship neglected. Chile's *estallido social* (2019–2022) resulted in the removal or transformation of approximately 400 monuments — a scale of iconoclastic activity unmatched in contemporary Latin America. Mexico City's removal of the Columbus statue, and Caribbean cases (Martinique's toppling of Schœlcher statues, Jamaican debates about colonial monuments) demonstrate that post-colonial iconoclasm has its own dynamics distinct from the BLM movement.

The Oxford Academic chapter on "Toppling Monuments: End or Means?" (2025) introduces the concept of "memorial colonization" — the idea that monument-building in colonized spaces is itself an act of symbolic violence — and argues that monument-building and monument-removal are both exercises of power rather than one being "constructive" and the other "destructive."

**Consensus.** Recent scholarship has reached broad consensus on several points: (1) monument toppling is a recurrent "spatial repertoire of contention" across centuries, not a novel phenomenon; (2) it is constitutive rather than merely destructive, creating new political meanings; (3) the destruction of symbols alone, without institutional change, is insufficient; and (4) post-colonial iconoclasm raises distinct issues from intra-European regime changes.

**Gaps.** The most striking gap for your thesis is the near-total absence of scholarship on the **iconoclasm of female allegories specifically**. The monument-toppling literature focuses overwhelmingly on statues of male political figures (Columbus, Leopold II, Confederate generals, Cecil Rhodes). When female allegories are destroyed, defaced, or replaced during regime changes — as when Germania disappeared after 1918 and 1945, or when Brazilian republican allegory replaced imperial imagery — this process is rarely analyzed through the lens of iconoclasm theory. Your thesis could open an entirely new line of inquiry: **what happens to the gendered symbolic order when regimes change?** Do female allegories get "toppled" differently from male monuments? Is there a specifically gendered form of iconoclasm?

Additionally, the **Brazilian case** is almost entirely absent from the iconoclasm literature. The symbolic transitions of 1822 (Independence), 1889 (Republic), 1937 (Estado Novo), 1964 (military dictatorship), and 1988 (new Constitution) all involved transformations of official imagery, but these have not been studied as cases of political iconoclasm.

### CLUSTER 4 — Iconographic Method and Digital Tools for Visual Corpora

**Main Debates.** This cluster divides into two sub-fields: **traditional iconographic method** and **digital humanities tools**. On the methodological side, the key development beyond Panofsky (whom you know) is Roelof van Straten's *Introduction to Iconography* (1994), which bridges Panofsky's three-level analysis (pre-iconographic, iconographic, iconological) with the practical demands of systematic classification. Van Straten includes a dedicated chapter on ICONCLASS as "a new method of research in iconography," positioning it as the operational realization of Panofsky's theoretical program.

ICONCLASS itself, developed by Henri van de Waal from the 1940s onward and published in 17 volumes (1973–1985), represents the most ambitious attempt to create a comprehensive classification system for visual content. Brandhorst's 2019 essay describes its evolution into a digital Linked Open Data resource. The system's hierarchical structure — with 10 main classes (Religion, Nature, Human Being, Society, Abstract Ideas, History, Bible, Literature, Classical Mythology, Non-representational Art) and approximately 40,000 concepts — allows systematic coding and cross-referencing of iconographic elements. For your purposes, ICONCLASS is particularly valuable because it can encode both **concrete attributes** (Phrygian cap, scales of justice, trident, fasces) and **abstract concepts** (liberty, justice, sovereignty) at different levels of specificity.

On the digital humanities front, recent work has applied machine learning to iconographic classification. Springstein et al. (2024) demonstrate hierarchical multi-label classification of art-historical images using ICONCLASS, while Kutt et al. (2025) propose a CARIS pipeline integrating YOLO object detection with ICONCLASS mapping. The key insight from both papers is that automated visual analysis can identify *visible elements* (objects, attributes), but *meaning* requires the symbolic structure provided by a classification system like ICONCLASS. This has direct methodological implications for your thesis: you could use automated tools to identify allegorical attributes (caps, scales, swords, shields) across large visual corpora, but the interpretive layer connecting these to sovereignty and gender must remain scholarly.

**Gaps.** No existing study has applied ICONCLASS or digital iconographic methods to a **systematic comparative analysis of female national allegories** across countries. The system certainly has the capacity: ICONCLASS includes codes for personifications of nations (class 44G — "symbols of the state"), virtues (class 11M and 59B), and specific allegorical attributes. But no one has used it for the kind of comparative, gendered analysis your thesis proposes. Similarly, no one has developed a **coding scheme specific to the legal-political functions** of allegorical imagery — a scheme that would encode not just *what* is depicted (Phrygian cap, scales) but *where* it appears (courthouse, coin, stamp, legal document) and *what political work* it performs (legitimation, commemoration, contestation). Your thesis could pioneer such a scheme.

Valérie Hayaert's study of Pierre Coustau's *Pegma* (2008) represents the most sophisticated application of visual analysis to legal emblems, but it focuses on a single 16th-century text rather than a comparative corpus. Goodrich's work is richly visual but interpretive rather than systematic. The field needs a methodology that combines Goodrich's theoretical ambition with the systematic rigor of ICONCLASS-based analysis.

## PART 3: GAPS AND OPPORTUNITIES — What Your Thesis Can Address

### Gap 1: The Concept of "Iconocracy" as Theoretical Synthesis
No existing framework unifies the three strands of scholarship your thesis draws on: (a) the visual culture of law (Goodrich's "visiocracy"), (b) the gendered politics of national allegory (Landes, Hunt), and (c) the feminist critique of abstract sovereignty (Pateman's "sexual contract"). Your concept of "iconocracy" — a regime where female allegorical figures produce sovereignty, organize visibility, and distribute symbolic recognition without political reciprocity for real women — fills a genuine theoretical void. Goodrich's "visiocracy" analyzes visual governance but does not foreground gender; Landes and Hunt foreground gender but do not theorize the specifically *legal* dimensions; Pateman theorizes the sexual contract but not its visual technology. Your synthesis is genuinely novel.

### Gap 2: The Brazilian Case as Anchor for Comparative Analysis
Brazil is dramatically understudied in this literature. Carvalho's *Formação das Almas* (which you know) remains virtually the only systematic study of republican allegory in Brazil, and it focuses on the 1889–1900 period. No existing study: (a) traces the full trajectory of female allegorical figures in Brazilian legal spaces (courthouses, currency, stamps, official documents) from the Empire through the Republic, Estado Novo, military dictatorship, and 1988 Constitution; (b) compares Brazilian legal allegory systematically with French and British equivalents; or (c) asks what happened to allegorical imagery during Brazil's multiple regime changes (1889, 1930, 1937, 1945, 1964, 1985/1988). Your thesis can fill all three gaps simultaneously.

### Gap 3: Female Allegory and the Paradox of Women's Political Inclusion
The literature notes but never systematically theorizes the inverse relationship between female allegory and real women's political participation. Mellini & Matthews observe that Britannia lost symbolic power as real women (Victoria, Thatcher) entered politics. The EHNE survey notes that many allegories declined after WWI — precisely when women gained suffrage in many countries. Carvalho shows that Marianne worked in France partly because of women's active participation in revolution, but the Brazilian Republic's female allegory failed partly because women were excluded from all public life. Your thesis could develop a systematic theoretical account of this inverse relationship: does iconocracy decline when the bodies it symbolically mobilizes gain actual political agency? Is there a structural incompatibility between allegorical femininity and real female citizenship?

### Gap 4: Gendered Iconoclasm — The Destruction of Female Allegories
The iconoclasm literature focuses almost exclusively on the toppling of male monuments. When female allegories are destroyed, replaced, or transformed during regime changes, this is not analyzed as iconoclasm. Yet your period (1789–1988) includes multiple such moments: the destruction of royal imagery and creation of Marianne (1789); the suppression of Marianne under Napoleon III and her restoration (1870); Germania's disappearance after 1918 and 1945; the Brazilian Republic's failed attempt to establish Marianne-like imagery (1889); the transformations of Brazilian official imagery under Vargas and the military regime. A theory of "gendered iconoclasm" — how regimes destroy, replace, or neutralize female allegories — would be a significant contribution.

### Gap 5: A Systematic Comparative Iconographic Coding Scheme
No existing study provides a replicable coding scheme for the comparative analysis of female allegories in legal/state contexts. Such a scheme would need to encode: (1) **formal attributes** (headgear, weapons, clothing, posture); (2) **medium** (coin, stamp, statute, courthouse sculpture, document, caricature); (3) **institutional location** (courthouse, parliament, ministry, public square, currency); (4) **political function** (legitimation, commemoration, contestation, satire); (5) **gender coding** (maternal, martial, erotic, domestic, abstract); (6) **racial coding** (when applicable). Combining ICONCLASS notation with a purpose-built supplementary scheme for legal-political context would be a methodological contribution usable by other scholars.

### Gap 6: The Period 1930–1988 in Latin America
The literature on national allegories concentrates heavily on the "golden age" (1789–1914) and the recent monument-toppling wave (2015–2022). The mid-20th century — precisely when Latin American states oscillated between populism, authoritarianism, and democratization — is understudied. What happened to female allegory under Vargas's Estado Novo? Under Brazilian military rule? During the transition to democracy and the 1988 Constitution? Your thesis could be the first to systematically track the fate of allegorical female figures through these regime changes in Latin America.
