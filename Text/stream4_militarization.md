## 1. Conceptual Framing: What "Militarization of Allegory" Means

### 1.1 Marina Warner's foundational argument

Warner's *Monuments and Maidens: The Allegory of the Female Form* (London: Weidenfeld & Nicolson, 1985; repr. University of California Press, 2000; 417 pp.) establishes the constitutive paradox at the heart of the research: female figures are deployed to personify the highest civic ideals precisely because women are excluded from exercising them. Her core observation is that "the female form tends to be perceived as generic and universal, with symbolic overtones; the male as individual, even when idealized" — which is why armed republics, helmeted liberties, and sword-bearing justices must be female to appear unpersonified, to seem like pure abstraction rather than political usurpation.

Warner traces how allegorical female bodies move between poles of maternity/passivity and warrior/agency without ever resolving the tension. The Statue of Liberty, constructed in 1871–86 in the shadow of France's defeat in the Franco-Prussian War and the Paris Commune, is in her reading a figure of disciplined civic militarism — torch as eternal flame *and* as torch of war — armored but not transgressive.

**Key claim (Warner 1985:66):** "When the figure is female and anonymous, when she bears no proper name but stands for something, she can transcend the particular, and achieve the condition of the universal."  
**Scope:** Western tradition, Antiquity to 1985.  
**Confidence:** High (foundational, widely cited).

### 1.2 George Mosse on nationalism, gender, and militarized bodies

Mosse's trilogy — *Nationalism and Sexuality: Respectability and Abnormal Sexuality in Modern Europe* (New York: Howard Fertig, 1985); *Fallen Soldiers: Reshaping the Memory of the World Wars* (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1990); *The Image of Man: The Creation of Modern Masculinity* (New York: Oxford University Press, 1996) — provides the structural framework for understanding how national allegory bifurcates along gender lines: male bodies carry active, martial, self-sacrificial nationalism, while female allegories carry the *permanence and continuity* of the nation — what must be defended rather than what defends.

**Key claim (Mosse, *Nationalism and Sexuality*, 1985):** "Woman as a national symbol was the guardian of the continuity and immutability of the nation, the embodiment of its respectability." This formulation explains *why* the female allegory is militarized selectively and conditionally: she may carry a sword as a token of her status as guardian, but she is not permitted to be the soldier. When authoritarian or emergency regimes break this convention — arming the Republic, putting Justitia's sword in a threatening rather than heraldic pose, dressing Pátria in uniform — they signal a rupture in the normal gendered economy of national symbolism.

**Key claim (Mosse, *Fallen Soldiers*, 1990:7):** The "Myth of the War Experience" — the process by which mass death was aestheticized, commemorated, and made sacred — required the feminization of the homeland as a passive, penetrable body to be defended by male sacrifice. Monumental female allegories on war memorials function as *the territory for which men die*, not as warriors themselves.  
**Scope:** Germany, England, France, Italy; Napoleonic Wars to 1939.  
**Confidence:** High (extensively cited in gender-nationalism scholarship).

### 1.3 Silke Wenk on "Versteinerte Weiblichkeit" (Petrified Femininity)

Wenk's *Versteinerte Weiblichkeit: Allegorien in der Skulptur der Moderne* (Cologne: Böhlau, 1996; her earlier "Die steinernen Frauen," 1987) is the most directly relevant theoretical work for the thesis. Wenk argues that female allegory appears *together with the constitution of nation-states*, serving a "state-building function" that is at once ideological and distinctly modern:

**Key claim (Wenk, as cited in Bojana Pejić, "Lady Rosa of Luxembourg," MoMA, 2001):** "The allegories are 'female' because only the images of women — who stay outside (economic and state) competition — were appropriate to embody the imagined community's interests." Wenk further holds that these are "not accompanied by narratives inherited from antiquity, but appear together with the birth of the modern state." The female body becomes a "special vehicle for transporting ideals," not myths, not religion, but the *desire for a common territory* — the nation-state. This is why militarization marks a specific historical moment: it signals that the nation-state's existence is under threat or being asserted through force, and the allegorical body absorbs that threat.

In the Nazi context, Wenk shows how Germania and related female monumental sculpture was deployed to naturalize territorial expansionism and racial hierarchy, stabilizing ideology through the apparent permanence of stone. The Fascist alliance between female allegory and the aesthetics of power was not accidental but structural.  
**Scope:** Modern European sculpture, especially German; 19th–20th centuries.  
**Confidence:** High (primary authority on German monumental allegory and femininity).

### 1.4 Eric Hobsbawm: Mass-Producing Traditions and Female Imagery

Hobsbawm's chapter "Mass-Producing Traditions: Europe, 1870–1914" in *The Invention of Tradition*, ed. Hobsbawm and Ranger (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1983), pp. 263–307, situates the explosion of female civic allegory in a specific historical moment — the period of high imperialism, mass politics, and intensified nationalism — when states required new symbolic forms to cement popular loyalties. Female allegories (Marianne, Britannia, Germania, Columbia) were mass-reproduced on stamps, coins, monuments, posters, and currency precisely because they could be standardized and repeated without appearing to represent any individual or faction. The armed variants of these figures — helmeted Britannia, sword-bearing Germania — belong to the heightened nationalist-imperialist period of 1871–1914.  
**Key claim:** Invented traditions must establish "continuity with a suitable historic past," and the armament of female allegories draws on ancient precedents (Athena, Roma militaris) to legitimate modern military nationalism as timeless necessity.  
**Scope:** Europe 1870–1914.  
**Confidence:** High.

## 2. France: Marianne from Liberty to Soldier

### 2.1 Maurice Agulhon's trilogy

Agulhon's three-volume study is the definitive authority:

- *Marianne au combat: L'imagerie et la symbolique républicaines de 1789 à 1880* (Paris: Flammarion, 1979; English trans. *Marianne into Battle*, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1981; 235 pp.)
- *Marianne au pouvoir: L'imagerie et la symbolique républicaines de 1880 à 1914* (Paris: Flammarion, 1989; 450 pp.)
- *Les métamorphoses de Marianne: L'imagerie et la symbolique républicaines de 1914 à nos jours* (Paris: Flammarion, 2001)

Agulhon traces two competing iconographic traditions running throughout French republican history: (a) the "sage and classical" Marianne — Liberty with scales or olive branch, draped, serene, associated with constitutional order and moderate republicanism; and (b) the "more radical, more red" Marianne — armed with pike, red Phrygian cap, bared breasts, associated with revolutionary republicanism, equality, popular insurrection (the *bonnet phrygien* as revolutionary helmet).

**Key claim (*Marianne au combat*, 1979/1981):** "Dans *Marianne au combat*, Maurice Agulhon avait établi l'existence d'une concurrence et d'une lutte symboliques permanentes entre deux images: l'une, sage et classique (République/liberté) et l'autre, plus radicale, plus rouge (République = égalité ou fraternité émancipatrice), dont les emblèmes étaient la pique ou le bonnet en référence à la révolte." The pike (*pique*) — a weapon — and the Phrygian cap — a revolutionary helmet — are the militarizing attributes of the populist tradition.

Agulhon also notes the militarizing effect of the Franco-Prussian War (1870) and WWI on Marianne's iconography: "l'équation entre Patrie et République devient incontournable avec la guerre de 1914. Marianne avec son bonnet phrygien représente alors la lutte pour le droit et la démocratie... La nationalisation de l'allégorie, dans le sillage de l'Union sacrée, la rend quasi unanimiste." WWI produced a near-unanimist fusion of Marianne-as-Republic with Pátria-as-war-effort.

The review in *La trilogie des Mariannes* (OpenEdition) further notes the emergence of boulangist and nationalist republicanism at the end of the 19th century in which "le drapeau et le soldat deviennent des attributs concurrents des attributs traditionnels" — the flag and the soldier compete with traditional allegorical attributes, indicating a militarization of political symbolism.

**Scope:** France, 1789–2001.  
**Confidence:** Very high (definitive authority).

**Delacroix's *Liberty Leading the People* (1830)** remains the visual paradigm of militant allegory: Marianne carries a musket with fixed bayonet in the left hand, the tricolor in the right, wears the Phrygian cap (revolutionary helmet), bared breasts evoking both the Amazon warrior tradition and maternal-civic nurture. This image is foundational for Brazilian republican iconographers (Décio Villares, Chrispim do Amaral) and Portuguese Republican imagery in Belém/Pará (see §5–6 below).

### 2.2 Lynn Hunt: Family Romance and Female Allegory

*Politics, Culture, and Class in the French Revolution* (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1984; 251 pp.) and *The Family Romance of the French Revolution* (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1992).

**Key claim (*Family Romance*, 1992:99):** "The nation as mother, La Nation, had no feminine qualities... it was not a threatening feminizing force and hence not incompatible with republicanism. La Nation was, in effect, a masculine mother, or a father capable of giving birth." Hunt argues that female allegory in the Revolution depoliticizes women by assigning them to universal, abstract space while reserving active political subjectivity for male citizens. The armed Marianne (rifle, pike) in revolutionary iconography thus performs a paradox: female form legitimates radical action while simultaneously excluding real women from it.  
**Scope:** France, French Revolution period.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 2.3 Joan Landes: Visualizing the Nation

*Visualizing the Nation: Gender, Representation, and Revolution in Eighteenth-Century France* (Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 2001).

**Key claim (Landes 2001:22, as reviewed in RACAR):** "The exclusion of women from the practice of revolution and their inclusion in representation reaffirmed the masquerade of equality within the masculine republic." Landes further argues that "the allegory of the nation's female body operated to consolidate the heterosexual investments of modern nationalism" — the erotic charge of the armed, exposed allegorical female is not incidental but structural: it positions male citizens as desiring-subjects and defenders of the feminized nation.  
**Scope:** France, late 18th century.  
**Confidence:** High.

## 3. Germany: Germania and the Armored Maiden

### 3.1 Germanic iconographic tradition

Germania is historically the most militarized of the major female national allegories. According to [EHNE, "Female Allegories of the Nation" (2020)](https://ehne.fr/en/encyclopedia/themes/gender-and-europe/gender-and-revolution-in-europe-19th-20th-century/female-allegories-nation), the attributes of Germania include:

- The *Reichsschwert* (imperial sword)
- Medieval-style shield with black eagle on gold field
- Imperial Crown of the Holy Roman Empire (post-1871: imperial crown)
- Breastplate with eagle (symbol of the German empire — strength)
- Crown of oak leaves (heroism)
- Spiked helmet (*Pickelhaube*) or Viking helmet (popularized by Richard Wagner's *Ring of the Nibelung*)

Under the German Empire (proclaimed 18 January 1871 following victory over France), Germania assumed a leading role with the imperial crown as headgear. The [Wikipedia article on Germania (personification)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germania_(personification)) notes: "In the nineteenth century, depictions of Germania were given more nationalistic and warlike traits... Defeated or dignified, she was usually represented with a manly combative spirit, presumably characteristic of her people — their *virtus bellatrix* or *virtus militum*."

**Key example:** Philipp Veit's mural for Saint Paul's Church, Frankfurt (1848–49): Germania is shown with sword and shield, symbolizing German unity aspirations during the 1848 Revolution.

**Key example:** Johannes Schilling's *Germania* monument at the Niederwalddenkmal (1871–83): monumental armored figure, sword raised, commemorating German victory over France and unification.

### 3.2 Silke Wenk on fascism and petrified femininity

Wenk's *Versteinerte Weiblichkeit* (1996) and her essay "Die steinernen Frauen [Petrified Women]" (1987) provide the theoretical framework for understanding how Nazi monumental sculpture deployed female allegory.

**Key claim (Wenk 1987, cited in Pejić 2001):** Female allegorical figures are "not accompanied by narratives inherited from antiquity, but appear together with the birth of the modern state" and carry a specifically "state-building function." In fascism, the "petrification" of femininity — making it literally stone — serves to naturalize racial hierarchy and territorial ambition as permanent, biological, geological facts.

The [EHNE article (2020)](https://ehne.fr/en/encyclopedia/themes/gender-and-europe/gender-and-revolution-in-europe-19th-20th-century/female-allegories-nation) notes that under National Socialism, the allegorical Germania was "relegated post-1918 in favor of national-socialist Führer" — she was largely replaced by the personalist charisma of Hitler — but the armored female monumental form persisted in architectural decoration, coins, and official art (e.g., Arno Breker's neoclassical female figures).

**Scope:** Modern Germany, 19th–20th centuries, with emphasis on Nazi period.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 3.3 Susanne von Falkenhausen: The gender metaphor in fascist architecture

Falkenhausen's article "The Sphere: Reading a Gender Metaphor in the Architecture of Modern Cults of Identity" (*artdok*, University of Heidelberg, 2006) analyzes Albert Speer's 1936 designs for the domed hall of Berlin as a gendered architectural-allegorical space, noting that "the 'war' of metaphors that was being fought out between fascism and democracy" organized itself around gendered visual strategies. She explicitly cites Wenk's *Versteinerte Weiblichkeit* (1996) as foundational.  
**Scope:** Nazi Germany, 1930s.  
**Confidence:** High.

## 4. Justitia Armed: The Sword, the Scales, the Blindfold

### 4.1 Judith Resnik and Dennis Curtis

*Representing Justice: Invention, Controversy, and Rights in City-States and Democratic Courtrooms* (New Haven: Yale University Press, 2011; with over 220 images). The [Yale Law School's Documents Collection Center](https://documents.law.yale.edu/representing-justice) summarizes: "By mapping the remarkable run of the icon of Justice, a woman with scales and sword, and by tracing the development of public spaces dedicated to justice — courthouses — Professors Judith Resnik and Dennis Curtis explore the evolution of adjudication into its modern form as well as the intimate relationship between the courts and democracy."

Resnik and Curtis document that:
- The **sword** is Justitia's oldest attribute, representing punitive/executive power; it predates the scales and the blindfold.
- The **blindfold** was not added until the 16th century, and originally had *negative* connotations (the blindfolded judge as a fool, from Sebastian Brant's *Ship of Fools*, 1497): a blindfolded person was a symbol of stupidity, not impartiality. It was reinterpreted as impartiality only in the 17th–18th centuries.
- The earliest Roman coins depicted Justitia *with the sword in one hand and the scale in the other, but with her eyes uncovered*.
- The first known representation of *blind* Justice is Hans Gieng's 1543 statue on the *Gerechtigkeitsbrunnen* in Bern, Switzerland.

**Key claim (Resnik & Curtis 2011, as reported in *New York Times*, December 16, 2010):** Lady Justice's familiar blindfold "did not become her fashion accessory until late in the 17th century," and medieval/Renaissance people did not view blindfolds favorably — a "hoodwink" (literal meaning: to deceive).

The armed Justitia — sword held aloft or prominently displayed — signals the coercive, punitive power of law. Under authoritarian regimes and periods of political emergency, the sword is emphasized over the scales: executive power over deliberative justice, state force over individual rights. The thesis can use Resnik & Curtis to argue that the militarized Justitia is a legal-iconographic correlate of the militarized Republic: both signal the primacy of order over rights.  
**Scope:** Global, ancient Rome to contemporary.  
**Confidence:** Very high.

### 4.2 Peter Goodrich on visual jurisprudence

Goodrich's "Visiocracy: On the Futures of the Fingerpost" (*Teoria e Critica della Regolazione Sociale*, 2013) develops a theory of "visiocracy" — law's governance through visual display, emblems, and allegorical iconography. He traces the "legal imaginary in diverse images of sovereignty, justice, amity, reverence" through the early modern emblem book (*emblemata iuris*) tradition, showing that Justitia's sword is not merely decorative but functions as a *visual jurisdiction* — the display of punitive capacity is itself an act of legal governance.

**Key claim (Goodrich 2013):** "In the left hand of nomos are the scales of justice and the sword of force and arms." The sword and scales are constitutively linked: justice without coercive power is symbolic only; force without scales is arbitrary tyranny. The militarized Justitia negotiates this tension through allegorical form.  
**Scope:** Early modern Europe; Western legal tradition.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 4.3 Historical iconography summary

| Period | Attributes | Interpretation |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Roman (Justitia) | Sword + scales, no blindfold | Punitive authority, visible |
| Medieval | Sword prominent; sometimes blindfold = foolishness | Justice as coercive power; blindfold = corrupt/ignorant judge |
| 16th c. | Blindfold gradually reinterpreted as impartiality | Transition to procedural justice ideal |
| 17th–18th c. | Blindfold = impartiality becomes standard | Enlightenment rule-of-law iconography |
| 19th–20th c. authoritarian | Sword re-emphasized; scales may disappear | Punitive state power over deliberative justice |
| Contemporary India (2024) | Blindfold removed; sword replaced by constitution | Post-colonial reinterpretation: law is not blind, constitution replaces force |

## 5. Iberian-American / Latin American Trajectories

### 5.1 General framework: gendered iconography and nation-building

Rebecca Earle, *The Return of the Native: Indians and Myth-Making in Spanish America, 1810–1930* (Durham: Duke University Press, 2007), observes that "we shall have occasion to observe not only the use of gendered iconography to construct the nineteenth-century Spanish American state, but also the place of indigenous imagery within these constructions. The Guatemalan stamp depicting the Indian princess is but one example of the framing of acceptable indigenousness within a gendered structure that linked an abstracted nation to concepts of femininity." The armed/warrior female is thus in tension in Latin America with the passive/maternal female and the indigenous/mestiza female — a three-way iconographic competition specific to postcolonial national contexts.

**Scope:** Spanish America, 1810–1930.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 5.2 Stacie G. Widdifield: Mexico

*The Embodiment of the National in Late Nineteenth-Century Mexican Painting* (Tucson: University of Arizona Press, 1996) analyzes how Mexican academic history painting of the Restored Republic (1867–1876) and Porfiriato (1876–1911) naturalized state power through allegorical female figures. The Mexican national allegory — often combining indigenous (Aztec princess) attributes with classical allegorical dress — was a contested site in which liberal Republicanism, conservative Catholicism, and positivist-inspired modernization competed for visual supremacy.

Key artworks include Juan Ortega's allegorical paintings and the sculptural programs of the Palacio de Bellas Artes. The allegory of Mexico (sometimes *La Patria* or *La República*) during the Porfiriato acquired attributes of civilizing/ordering power — scales, tablets of law — rather than explicitly militarized ones, but the underlying claim was always about the legitimacy of state power to impose order.  
**Scope:** Mexico, 1867–1911.  
**Confidence:** High (primary authority on Mexican academic painting and national allegory).

### 5.3 Magali M. Carrera: Colonial body and race in New Spain

*Imagining Identity in New Spain: Race, Lineage, and the Colonial Body in Portraiture and Casta Paintings* (Austin: University of Texas Press, 2003) and her earlier "Locating Race in Late Colonial Mexico" (*Art Journal* 57:3, 1998, pp. 36–45) document how colonial visual culture in New Spain produced a "racial imaginary" organized through bodily attributes — dress, posture, skin — that informed post-independence national iconography. The female national allegory in Mexico inherited this racially coded visual vocabulary, in which the acceptable "national body" was coded as mestiza or criollo, not indigenous.  
**Scope:** Colonial Mexico, 18th century.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 5.4 Natalia Majluf: Costumbrismo and national identity in Latin America

Majluf's curatorial work (Americas Society, *Reproducing Nations: Types and Costumes in Asia and Latin America, ca. 1800–1860*, 2006) demonstrates how *costumbrismo* — the genre of depicting local customs and dress — functioned as national iconography across Latin America in the early independence period: "This exhibition presupposed an international market for visual images, one that grew dramatically as Latin America opened up to the world after the virtual isolation imposed by the Spanish crown's restrictive policies." The typing and costuming of female national figures (indigenous princess, mestiza, criollo woman) represents a parallel but distinct mode of national feminization from the European allegorical tradition, one that is specifically postcolonial.  
**Scope:** Latin America, ca. 1800–1860.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 5.5 Jens Andermann: The Optic of the State

*The Optic of the State: Visuality and Power in Argentina and Brazil* (Pittsburgh: University of Pittsburgh Press, 2007; 272 pp.) argues that "the state of the visual form is not found in maps, museums, and photographs, but in the way of seeing that they called forth." By analyzing museums, exhibitions, landscape and history paintings, and maps, Andermann shows how "visual practices made possible" the "political metaphysics" of state consolidation in Argentina and Brazil. The book does not focus primarily on female allegory per se, but its methodology — treating visual culture as constitutive of state power rather than merely reflective of it — provides the theoretical framework for reading militarized Pátria/República imagery as an active exercise of state visual power.

**Key claim (Andermann 2007):** The "capture, classification, and display of fragments of nature and otherness constituted a comprehensive effort to establish modernizing projects as the true realizations of a broad, nationalist teleology." Female allegory participates in this capture by providing a feminized, allegorical body that can be displayed as the nation's own self-image.  
**Scope:** Argentina, Brazil, 19th–early 20th century.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 5.6 Maria Helena Capelato: Vargas and Perón — visual propaganda

*Multidões em Cena: Propaganda Política no Varguismo e no Peronismo* (Campinas: Papirus / FAPESP, 1998; 311 pp.) is the essential comparative study of visual propaganda under Brazil's Estado Novo (1937–45) and Argentina's Peronism (1946–55). Capelato's analysis of the DIP (*Departamento de Imprensa e Propaganda*) demonstrates how the Estado Novo's propaganda machine deployed an intensive iconography centered on Vargas's image — the personalist leader displacing abstract allegorical forms — alongside civic symbols (flag, family, labor) that represented the "national body" as domestic, productive, and disciplined rather than militant-female.

**Key claim (Capelato 1998, as cited in UFPI article):** "As imagens e os símbolos eram difundidos nas escolas com o objetivo de formar a consciência do pequeno cidadão" — images and symbols were disseminated in schools to form civic consciousness. The Estado Novo's visual program was extensive but the female Republic allegory was secondary to the masculine leader-cult; when Pátria appears, she appears as mother-homeland, not warrior.  
**Scope:** Brazil 1930–45; Argentina 1946–55.  
**Confidence:** High.

## 6. Brazilian Case: República, Pátria, and Estado Novo Iconography

### 6.1 José Murilo de Carvalho: The Imaginary of the Republic

*A Formação das Almas: O Imaginário da República no Brasil* (São Paulo: Companhia das Letras, 1990; 1st ed.; subsequent eds. 2003, 2023) is the foundational work. Chapter 4 — "República-Mulher: Entre Maria e Marianne" — is directly relevant.

**Key claims (Carvalho 1990):**
- "A batalha pela alegoria feminina terminou em derrota republicana. Mais ainda, em derrota do cívico perante o religioso." (p. 94) — The battle for the female allegory ended in Republican defeat, and the defeat of the civic over the religious.
- "Símbolos, alegorias, mitos só criam raízes quando há terreno social e cultural no qual se alimentarem. Na ausência de tal base, a tentativa de criá-los, de manipulá-los, de utilizá-los como elementos de legitimação, cai no vazio, quando não no ridículo." (p. 89) — Symbols only take root when there is social and cultural ground. Without it, attempts at symbolic manipulation fall into emptiness or ridicule.
- "A aceitação do símbolo na França e sua rejeição no Brasil permitem, mediante a comparação por contraste, esclarecer aspectos das duas sociedades e das duas repúblicas." (p. 14) — The acceptance of the symbol in France and its rejection in Brazil illuminate, through contrastive comparison, aspects of the two societies and the two republics.

**Key argument on militarization:** Carvalho documents the competing iconographic traditions fighting over the female Republic allegory in Brazil: the positivist vision (Clotilde de Vaux as virgin-mother, following Comte), the Jacobin/French-Republican vision (armed Marianne with pike and bare breasts), and the conservative-Catholic vision (Virgin Mary displacing Marianne). The Republic appeared armed in Brazilian caricature and political art — but often ironically, as a figure of ridicule or as a prostitute — rather than heroically armed. This ironic militarization (the Republic armed but debased) is a distinctly Brazilian variant of the phenomenon.

**Scope:** Brazil, 1889–1930s.  
**Confidence:** Very high (definitive Brazilian authority).

### 6.2 Painted militarized female allegories in Brazil (early Republic)

The USP thesis *Marianne à Brasileira* (Lima Júnior, 2020, thesis FFLCH) documents the following key works with armed/militarized female allegories:

| Work | Artist | Date | Militarizing Attributes | Location |
|------|--------|------|------------------------|----------|
| *A República* | Manuel Lopes Rodrigues | 1896 | Sword (inverted, heraldic); gorgoneion (Medusa/Athena's shield) on chest; phrygian cap with laurels; advanced right foot | Museu de Arte da Bahia, Salvador |
| *Alegoria à República* (telão) | Chrispim do Amaral | 1890 | Victory palm; military entourage with implied swords | Theatro da Paz, Belém |
| *Monumento à República* | Michele Sansebastiano | 1897 | 22m bronze; Republic with drawn sword (warrior pose); lion | Belém do Pará |
| *Triunfo da República brasileira* | Henri Rousseau | 1917 | Seated Republic surrounded by military (Deodoro on horse); indigenous figures with spears | Museu Nacional de Belas Artes, Rio |

Regarding Lopes Rodrigues' *A República* (1896): the sword + gorgoneion (Athena's aegis) signals that the Republic "terrorizes enemies" (the text invokes Ovid's description of Athena). The painting was created in the context of the 1890s federal revolts (Canudos, Federalist Revolt), and the Republic's militarization is explicitly reactive — defensive-repressive.

**Key finding:** Brazilian republican iconography shows the female Republic *armed but not triumphant* — she carries a sword but faces threats, reflecting the Republic's political fragility in the 1889–1910 period. This is iconographically distinct from Francia's triumphant Marianne or Germany's conquering Germania.

### 6.3 Lilia Moritz Schwarcz: Imperial and Republican Iconography

Schwarcz's *As Barbas do Imperador: D. Pedro II, um Monarca nos Trópicos* (São Paulo: Companhia das Letras, 1998) and *Iconografia da República* (2019) document the continuities and ruptures between imperial and republican visual culture. The key finding for the thesis: the Brazilian Republic had to create new female allegories from scratch precisely because the previous female figure of civic prestige — Princess Isabel (the "Redemptrix" who signed the *Lei Áurea*, 1888) — was associated with the Monarchy. This created what Carvalho calls a "lack of a community of imagination" (*falta de uma comunidade de imaginação*), making the female Republic allegory vulnerable to parody, debasement, and counter-appropriation.

Schwarcz's work on the *Barbas do Imperador* also documents how the Imperial period produced its own iconographic program of national power — including the feminized allegories on the arms of the aristocracy, in heraldry, and in the paintings commissioned by Pedro II. The Republican female allegory had to compete against this established Imperial visual vocabulary.  
**Scope:** Brazil, Imperial and First Republic periods.  
**Confidence:** Very high.

### 6.4 Maraliz de Castro Vieira Christo and Letícia Squeff: History painting

Christo's work (e.g., "Diálogos da História da Arte") and Squeff's chapter in *Oitocentos: Arte Brasileira do Império à Primeira República* (2008) situate Brazilian academic history painting within the broader Latin American context: "O medo à fragmentação, ocorrido na América Latina, com o esfacelamento do domínio espanhol, levou o Brasil a produzir uma iconografia que salientasse a continuidade entre passado e presente, como também a unidade, construída na defesa de seu território face ao inimigo externo" (Arbor, 2009:386). This defensive-territorial iconographic strategy explains why the Republic, when it does arm itself, carries the sword of national defense rather than the sword of conquest.

The Brazilian First Republic's allegorical program — Pedro Américo's history paintings, Décio Villares's positivist allegories, Henrique Bernardelli's sculptures — is documented in these works. Christo's analysis of *Tiradentes Esquartejado* (Pedro Américo, 1893) also illuminates the gendering of martyrdom and republic-building.  
**Scope:** Brazil, 19th–early 20th century.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 6.5 Estado Novo (1937–45): Displacement rather than militarization of female allegory

Under the Estado Novo, Vargas's DIP produced massive visual propaganda, but the female national allegory was largely *displaced* rather than *militarized*. The Estado Novo's key iconographic move was the personalist leader-cult (Vargas as "pai dos pobres") combined with labor/production imagery (the worker, the industrialized Brazil), civic ritual (Dia da Pátria, Dia da Raça, Dia da Juventude), and a sanitized maternal femininity.

Capelato (*Multidões em Cena*, 1998) documents how Estado Novo propaganda drew on Italian and German fascist propaganda techniques but adapted them to Brazilian conditions: the female figure who does appear is the *mãe trabalhadora* (working mother), not the armed warrior-republic. This represents a *de-militarization of the female allegory under authoritarianism* — precisely the opposite of what might be expected — because the Estado Novo's authoritarianism was based on productivist nationalism and personalist leadership, not on the invocation of an embattled people defending its feminized homeland.

*Exception:* Civic posters and educational materials from the Estado Novo period occasionally deploy Pátria-as-mother-with-children imagery that has latent military connotations (uniform-clad children, paramilitary youth formations), but this is indirect militarization, not the explicit armament of the female allegory.

## 7. Colonial and Authoritarian Reinscriptions

### 7.1 Anne McClintock: Imperial Leather and porno-tropics

*Imperial Leather: Race, Gender and Sexuality in the Colonial Contest* (New York: Routledge, 1995; 449 pp.) theorizes what McClintock calls the "porno-tropics" — the systematic feminization of conquered territories as a visual/discursive strategy of empire: "The interior of previously unknown lands were 'penetrated' and their treasures 'opened.' These new lands were also incorporated into what McClintock calls a 'porno-tropic' tradition of map making in which 'knowledge of the unknown world was mapped as a metaphysics of gender violence... In these fantasies, the world is feminized and spatially spread for male exploration, then reassembled and deployed in the interests of massive imperial power.'" (pp. 22–23)

**Key claim (McClintock 1995):** Imperial iconography does not simply use the female allegory to represent the nation; it uses the feminized-and-sexualized allegory of the *colonized territory* to represent domination as natural, inevitable, and erotic. The armed British Britannia standing over a prostrate India, or Columbia leading the westward expansion over indigenous peoples, is the militarized variant: the imperial female allegory as simultaneously victim (passive, needing protection) and agent (dominant, civilizing).

**Key claim (pp. 22–23):** "A triangulated, switchboard analogy thus emerged between race, class and gender deviance as a critical element in the formation of the modern, imperial imagination."  
**Scope:** British imperialism, 19th–20th centuries; South Africa.  
**Confidence:** Very high (foundational in postcolonial-feminist studies).

### 7.2 Annie Coombes: Reinventing Africa

*Reinventing Africa: Museums, Material Culture and Popular Imagination in Late Victorian and Edwardian England* (New Haven: Yale University Press, 1994) documents how British colonial exhibitions and museums constructed Africa as a feminized, primitive body requiring imperial intervention. Coombes shows that exhibition displays were "not only racist but also sexist, often portraying women as mere possessions of their savage husbands." Africa underwent a redefinition "from a land of savages requiring military intervention for redemption to a dark and enigmatic continent rife with peculiar rituals and unusual customs."

This feminization of Africa as the object of military-civilizing intervention provides the colonial counterpart to the militarized female allegory of empire: if Britannia is the armed mother/guardian, Africa is the feminized-primitive territory to be militarily civilized.  
**Scope:** Britain, 1890–1918.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 7.3 Mosse: Fallen Soldiers and the feminized homeland

*Fallen Soldiers* (1990) documents how WWI memorialization systematically feminized the homeland — "the feminine territory that men die to defend" — while masculinizing the soldier-citizen. War memorials across Germany, England, France, and Italy placed female allegorical figures (mourning mothers, winged victories, armed republics) in relation to male sacrifice: the female figure as the reason *for* which men die, not a figure who dies herself.

**Key claim (*Fallen Soldiers*, 1990):** "War memorialization became a way not just to ennoble the chaos and slaughter of war, but a promise of a kind of secular deliverance." The militarization of female allegory on war memorials — Winged Victory, armed Republicae — serves the "Myth of the War Experience" by providing a symbolic object for which sacrifice is worthwhile.  
**Scope:** Germany, England, France, Italy; WWI and aftermath.  
**Confidence:** High.

### 7.4 Authoritarian reinscriptions: comparative note

Under authoritarian and fascist regimes, the female allegory undergoes a specific pattern:
1. **Nazi Germany:** Germania is largely displaced by the Führer-cult; when she appears, she is monumentalized and petrified (Wenk), made to embody racial/territorial permanence rather than revolutionary militancy.
2. **Fascist Italy:** Italia displaces the female allegorical republic; Mussolini's cult of Romanità deploys *Roma aeterna* (the eternal female Rome) to legitimate empire, but the Duce's masculinity is the active principle.
3. **Estado Novo Brazil:** The female Republic allegory is displaced by Vargas's personalist imagery; Pátria appears as productive mother, not warrior.
4. **Peronist Argentina (Capelato):** The female figuration is split between Evita (who acquires near-allegorical status as *mãe dos pobres*, absorbing the traditional role of the civic female allegory) and the Argentine Pátria, which remains a conservative mother-figure.

The thesis can argue that authoritarianism consistently *displaces or domesticates* the armed female allegory — because the armed female republic/liberty carries revolutionary connotations that are incompatible with personalist-authoritarian rule. The militarized female allegory belongs to *republican* militarism, not to fascist or personalist authoritarianism.

## 9. Annotated Bibliography (ABNT-compatible, 17 entries)

---

**AGULHON, Maurice.** *Marianne au combat: L'imagerie et la symbolique républicaines de 1789 à 1880*. Paris: Flammarion, 1979. (English trans. Janet Lloyd: *Marianne into Battle: Republican Imagery and Symbolism in France, 1789–1880*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1981. 235 p.)

Foundational trilogy Vol. I. Documents the split between the "sage" and the "radical red" Marianne, the armed/militant republican female allegory in France. Establishes the methodology of republican "symbolic archaeology." Essential for French comparisons.

---

**AGULHON, Maurice.** *Marianne au pouvoir: L'imagerie et la symbolique républicaines de 1880 à 1914*. Paris: Flammarion, 1989. 450 p.

Trilogy Vol. II. Analyzes the consolidation and diffusion of Marianne as the official female republic allegory, and the emergence of nationalist/boulangist counter-traditions in which "le drapeau et le soldat deviennent des attributs concurrents." Documents the militarizing effect of the Franco-Prussian War and national defense on republican imagery.

---

**AGULHON, Maurice.** *Les métamorphoses de Marianne: L'imagerie et la symbolique républicaines de 1914 à nos jours*. Paris: Flammarion, 2001.

Trilogy Vol. III. Covers WWI militarization of Marianne (Marianne-as-Pátria-en-armes, Union Sacrée), the Vichy period (demotion of Marianne), and postwar metamorphoses.

---

**ANDERMANN, Jens.** *The Optic of the State: Visuality and Power in Argentina and Brazil*. Pittsburgh: University of Pittsburgh Press, 2007. 272 p.

Theorizes the state as a "visual form" — a way of seeing that naturalizes state power — analyzing museums, photographs, maps, and history paintings in 19th-century Argentina and Brazil. Essential methodological framework for reading Brazilian republican allegory as visual statecraft.

---

**CAPELATO, Maria Helena Rolim.** *Multidões em Cena: Propaganda Política no Varguismo e no Peronismo*. Campinas: Fapesp/Papirus, 1998. 311 p.

Comparative study of Estado Novo (Brazil, 1937–45) and Peronist (Argentina, 1946–55) visual propaganda. Documents the DIP's iconographic program and the displacement/domestication of the female republic allegory by personalist leader-cults. Essential for Brazilian authoritarian iconography.

---

**CARVALHO, José Murilo de.** *A Formação das Almas: O Imaginário da República no Brasil*. São Paulo: Companhia das Letras, 1990. (Ed. atual. 2023.)

Foundational study of Brazilian republican imaginary. Chapter 4 ("República-Mulher: Entre Maria e Marianne") directly addresses the failed implantation of the female republic allegory in Brazil, contrasting it with the successful French case. Key source for all sections on Brazil.

---

**COOMBES, Annie E.** *Reinventing Africa: Museums, Material Culture and Popular Imagination in Late Victorian and Edwardian England*. New Haven: Yale University Press, 1994.

Analyzes the construction of Africa as feminized-primitive in British colonial exhibitions and museums, documenting the sexist/racist iconography that defined Africa as the object of armed imperial intervention. Contextualizes the militarized Britannia as imperial guardian of feminized-colonial territory.

---

**EARLE, Rebecca.** *The Return of the Native: Indians and Myth-Making in Spanish America, 1810–1930*. Durham: Duke University Press, 2007.

Documents the gendered iconography of Spanish American nation-building, including the use of allegorical female figures (indigenous princess, mestiza) in state iconography. Notes the competing constructions of feminized national identity in postcolonial contexts.

---

**HOBSBAWM, Eric.** Mass-Producing Traditions: Europe, 1870–1914. In: HOBSBAWM, Eric; RANGER, Terence (ed.). *The Invention of Tradition*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1983. p. 263–307.

Argues that female national allegories (Marianne, Britannia, Germania) were "mass-produced" in the period of high imperialism (1870–1914) to cement popular national loyalties. Documents the standardization and mass circulation of allegorical imagery through stamps, coins, monuments, and posters.

---

**HUNT, Lynn.** *Politics, Culture, and Class in the French Revolution*. Berkeley: University of California Press, 1984. 251 p.

Analyzes the dynamic interaction between symbolic forms and political action in the French Revolution. Essential for understanding how female allegory became the default mode of republican self-representation after the dethronement of the male monarch.

---

**HUNT, Lynn.** *The Family Romance of the French Revolution*. Berkeley: University of California Press, 1992.

Argues that La Nation was "a masculine mother, or a father capable of giving birth" — the female allegory paradoxically evacuates femininity from the national body politic. Key theoretical move for the "Iconocracia" thesis.

---

**LANDES, Joan B.** *Visualizing the Nation: Gender, Representation, and Revolution in Eighteenth-Century France*. Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 2001.

Demonstrates that the female body in revolutionary visual culture "operated to consolidate the heterosexual investments of modern nationalism," linking the erotic charge of the armed allegorical female to the exclusion of real women from political rights. Essential for Section 2.

---

**McCLINTOCK, Anne.** *Imperial Leather: Race, Gender and Sexuality in the Colonial Contest*. New York: Routledge, 1995. 449 p.

Theorizes the "porno-tropics" — the feminization of conquered territories in colonial visual discourse — and the gendered iconography of empire. The armed Britannia standing over feminized-colonized territories is the militarized allegorical form of imperial power. Essential for Section 7.

---

**MOSSE, George L.** *Fallen Soldiers: Reshaping the Memory of the World Wars*. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1990.

Documents how the "Myth of the War Experience" required the feminization of the homeland as the passive body for which male soldiers sacrifice themselves. Monumental female allegories on war memorials function as the visual instantiation of the feminized nation-that-must-be-defended.

---

**MOSSE, George L.** *Nationalism and Sexuality: Respectability and Abnormal Sexuality in Modern Europe*. New York: Howard Fertig, 1985.

Argues that "Woman as a national symbol was the guardian of the continuity and immutability of the nation, the embodiment of its respectability." Explains why the selective and conditional militarization of female allegory signals regime emergency or rupture.

---

**RESNIK, Judith; CURTIS, Dennis.** *Representing Justice: Invention, Controversy, and Rights in City-States and Democratic Courtrooms*. New Haven: Yale University Press, 2011. (With over 220 images.)

Essential study of Justitia's iconographic history, tracing the evolution of sword, scales, and blindfold as legal-allegorical attributes. Documents that the sword is Justitia's oldest attribute (Roman), that the blindfold was originally negative (fool's hoodwink), and that the armed Justitia signals punitive-executive power. Appears in courts in Brazil, Zambia, Iraq, Japan, etc.

---

**WARNER, Marina.** *Monuments and Maidens: The Allegory of the Female Form*. London: Weidenfeld & Nicolson, 1985. (Repr. Berkeley: University of California Press, 2000. 417 p.)

Foundational study. Argues that "the female form tends to be perceived as generic and universal, with symbolic overtones; the male as individual, even when idealized" — explaining why abstract ideals (Justice, Liberty, Republic) are feminized. Traces the tradition from Antiquity to Thatcher-as-Britannia. Essential for the "Iconocracia" thesis.

---

**WENK, Silke.** *Versteinerte Weiblichkeit: Allegorien in der Skulptur der Moderne*. Cologne: Böhlau, 1996.

The most directly relevant theoretical study. Argues that female allegory is constitutively modern — it "appears together with the constitution of (nation) states" and carries a "state-building function." In fascism, "petrified femininity" naturalizes racial-territorial ideology. The key German-language authority on female monumental allegory and political power.
