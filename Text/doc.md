Legal Literacy and the Visual-Dogmatic Regimes: Shaping and Shattering Legal Knowledge in Brazil (1889–2023)  
Ana Vitória Vanzin Mendes\*[^1]  
*1\. Project Rationale*  
Law, like language, emerges historically from a community's shared practices, expectations, and interpretations. It can be understood as an organic[^2], knowledge-based social practice through which people collectively produce, stabilise, and contest what is considered valid norms and rights[^3]. At stake here is not only the integrity of legal discourse but also the democratic fabric that binds societies together. As these shared practices wane, the potential for democratic erosion looms, risking the unravelling of societal consensus on fundamental rights and norms. It operates through reflexivity[^4], and beneath the cold glass of modern institutions lies the fabric of text, speech, and imagery, the soul and the fate of society[^5].  
Legal historians are brought upon the uncanny task of excavating the mythological, unconscious, and structural foundations that enable a society to reproduce and establish life (*vitam instituere*)[^6]. However tempting and rewarding it is to adopt an interdisciplinary approach, one must always be methodologically careful to understand the complexities that guide actors across time and space[^7]. In this project, I will employ key disciplinary lenses, including historical sociology, legal iconology, and political theology, ensuring that the interdisciplinary approach remains analytically disciplined.  
Legal literacy is the ability to recognise, interpret, and engage with the Law. This historically contingent competence enables actors to interact with legal systems via texts, images, objects, spaces, and rituals. This competence challenges the traditional view of legality as separate from social and cultural dynamics and their agents. When it comes to the ontological instance of legal knowledge, its means of (re)construction and transmission, legal literacy is a crucial component that reflects the interplay between Law and society[^8].  
Modern constitutional power relies on a public semiotics, an economy of images and artefacts that stabilises legitimacy while allowing alternative interpretations that may challenge institutional authority. During episodes of social commotion, legal insignias, documents, and sites act as writable surfaces, turning contestation into claims to re-authorise authority itself. Much like a palimpsest, where new meanings are inscribed over old layers without entirely erasing them, the 8 January 2023 attempted coup, as an empirical anchor and "war of representation,"[^9] reveals the dynamic layering of competing regimes of reading and Law enforcement, marking a notable literacy event in legal history for the digital era.  
Brazil serves as a compelling case because the idea of a Brazilian Nation and Republic developed through post-colonial translation of European iconographic repertoires, combined with persistent tensions between imported symbols and local social realities. The episode involved a direct struggle over constitutional symbols, legal spaces, and institutional imagery. Constitutional Law professor João Carlos Souto (2023) described the events as "the most serious attack on State institutions since re-democratisation in 1985" and "the bitter fruit of a slow process that has taken hold in Brazilian society over these four years."[^10]  
Two gestures are chosen here to exemplify the dynamic: the ritual appropriation of a replica of the 1988 Constitution and the lipstick defacement of the Supreme Court's allegorical statue of Justice, *A Justiça*[^11].  
**2\. Research Questions**

1. How does a regime of visibility organise public recognition of constitutional authority in Brazil across key historical moments (1889–2023)?  
2. How does post-colonial translation shape the stability or volatility of legal iconography in Brazil?  
3. How does Tropical Iconocracy explain the fragility of imported legal symbols in Brazil's republican and constitutional imagination?  
4. How does the Visual Sexual Contract operate through feminised allegory as a technology of legitimacy and exclusion?  
5. How does the Theologico-Political Simulacrum of Legal Objects clarify the 8 January episode as a conflict over rival forms of legal authorisation in a digital-theological environment?  
6. How can legal historians reconstruct legal knowledge from visual, spatial, and material sources, including episodes of iconoclasm and idolatry?

*3\. Conceptual Structure*  
For nearly a century, the sociological interpretation of Brazil has been shadowed by Sérgio Buarque de Holanda's concept of the "cordial man", introduced in *Raízes do Brasil* (1936)[^12]. By his account, the Brazilian *ethos* is defined by a hypertrophy of the emotional sphere, a rejection of formalism, and an inability to distinguish between the private domain of the family and the public domain of the State. The "cordial" subject acts through the heart, privileging personal bonds, intimacy, and manoeuvring over the cold abstraction of the Law, infamously known as "jeitinho brasileiro"[^13], the "Brazilian little way", a culturally specific trait, associated with the adaptability of Brazilians. The failure of Brazilian institutions could be attributed to the nature of the subject: Brazil is too "warm," too informal, and too archaic to inhabit the "cold" structures of a modern liberal democracy. However, contemporary historical and legal analysis suggests that this diagnosis, while descriptively seductive regarding social manners, is structurally insufficient to explain the ontological crisis of the Brazilian regime. The cordiality idea focuses on the excess of affect in the citizen but ignores the deficit of legitimacy in the institution, thereby pathologising the populace while naturalising the State's structure.  
To understand the contingent historical processes of Brazilian democracy, with a symbolic focus on the events of the 8 January 2023, it is necessary to pivot the analytical axis through the integration of different yet complementary intellectual traditions, such as the historical sociology of José Murilo de Carvalho[^14], the dogmatic anthropology of Pierre Legendre[^15], along with the works of art historian and philosopher Marie-José Mondzain[^16] and the understanding of legal images as deontic media[^17].  
I propose the term Tropical Iconocracy to designate the volatility of legal imagination as it is absorbed into Brazil, acquiring unstable meanings through processes of translation and contestation. Unlike existing studies that primarily focus on the stability and coherence of legal symbols, Tropical Iconocracy challenges this view by emphasising the inherent instability and adaptability of iconography in Brazil's legal and cultural landscape. Rather than chaos, the 8 January manifested the collision of two epistemic communities, each claiming authority over the meaning of normativity. The riot enacted an anthropophagic[^18] reabsorption of symbols, violently devouring their established meanings to produce unstable alternatives. Such instability is not an accident; it is structural to Brazil's republican visual regime, where the sacred enemy of legality is perpetually transformed into a site of semiotic struggle. The Republic's 1889 emergence from elite and military negotiation generated a symbolic deficit, with early republican iconography struggling to stabilise civic attachment[^19]. The symbolic deficit is “a sin of origin”. Brazil is a Republic born with virtually no popular participation[^20]. The legal and aesthetic translation resulted in a “resounding failure”[^21]. The early representation of the Republic was a mock-up of Marianne and failed to resonate; the intended virgin-mother figure[^22] was quickly resignified in popular caricatures as a prostitute, mocked “even by those who had initially supported the new regime”[^23]. This fragile, contested sacred order, built on borrowed and poorly integrated symbols, forms the basis of Tropical Iconocracy.  
As historians Maurice Agulhon and Marina Warner have argued[^24], a male figure represents a ruler. This system of legal and political iconography is part of why a division in which the virtues that legitimise good and evil are feminised in image, while the institutions that wield Law remain structurally male[^25]. A statue of a man inevitably resembles a specific King, General, or President, a competitor for power. A female figure, historically excluded from ruling and legally defined as a minor, implies ideals rather than personal power. She represents the state without threatening to usurp it; she is the *mother* of the citizens, not their master. Therefore, the visual dimension of the Sexual Contract transposes feminist contract theory into the field of legal iconography[^26].  
In the early twenty-first century, this inherited symbolic inventory entered a new cycle of contestation as the Bolsonaro government presented itself as custodian of moral values, foregrounding the defence of the family, treating folklore and national festivals as sites of profanity, stigmatising African and Indigenous cultural forms, and advancing a punitive anti-drug discourse[^27]. This merger integrates political theology and simulation. Political theology provides grounds for sovereignty and authority that adopt theological forms[^28]. Simulation theory clarifies how copies and media saturation reorder relations between representation and reality[^29]. In contemporary Brazilian networks, digital religion and spiritual warfare discourse often frame institutions as sacred or demonic and ground legitimacy in divine mandate and charismatic authorisation[^30]. Within this field, legal objects (replicas, flags, constitutions, monuments) serve as privileged carriers of "true Law." Object-centred authorisation relocates legality from procedures to things, and ritual acts toward artefacts enact claims to sovereignty[^31].  
**4\. Historical Scope and Empirical Anchor**  
The construction of Brasília reconfigured the visual-legal order by deploying modernist abstraction and monumental spatial grammar[^32]. The *Praça dos Três Poderes* choreographs the separation of powers through architectural form, rendering authority as a sequence of spatial relations. Within this *tableau*, the judiciary's emblematic presence is condensed in Alfredo Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* (1961), a blindfolded granite allegory that locates authority in abstraction and scale rather than narrative figuration. This transformation recalibrates the competencies of legal literacy, as recognition is now trained on spatial and formal cues inscribed in the built environment[^33]. However, on 8 January 2023, rioters attacked legal institutions and symbol systems in Brasília. Contemporary accounts frame the episode as an assault on democratic institutions that drew energy from disinformation, electoral denialism, and transnational repertoires of anti-institutional mobilisation[^34]. Two episodes anchor the preliminary analysis:

1. Constitution replica as juridical *simulacrum* and legal object-authorisation**:** A rioter seized a replica of the 1988 Constitution and treated it as the authentic Constitution, thereby exemplifying the replica's elevation within a rival order of legality[^35].  
2. Defacement of *A Justiça as gendered (i)literacy:* Débora Rodrigues used lipstick to inscribe "Perdeu, Mané" on the statue, an act that highlights the feminised surface through which abstract legality becomes visible and contestable[^36].

**5\. Methodology and Conclusion**  
This project develops a methodology that integrates legal iconology, historiography, forensic architecture, and political theology. By reading the vandalised artefacts of 8 January as primary sources, this approach reconstructs the routes by which legal historians can interpret material evidence alongside textual statutes, tracing the resilience and fragility of the legal order. The analysis situates the 8 January 2023, attacks within the *longue durée*[^37] of Brazilian visual culture, moving beyond immediate political commentary. The close reading of the vandalised Justice statue and other targeted symbols of sovereignty reframes the crisis as a collapse of legal literacy and a rupture in the regime of images.  
The volatility is not a Brazilian genetic trait *per se*, but rather a specific historical fact of post-colonial elite imposition without popular participation. It is contingent, not genetic, not an effect of any essential Brazilian character, but of a historically produced symbolic deficit. During the period leading up to the Proclamation of the Republic in 1889, only 2% of the Brazilian population was eligible to vote, a stark reflection of the lack of participatory foundation[^38].  
The vandalism of the 8 January 2023, belongs to a prolonged struggle over the visibility of constitutional authority rather than to a self-contained episode[^39]. In a Benjaminian register, political tendency becomes legible at points of rupture, when what remains latent in art is forced into public readability[^40]. That logic matters for legal literacy because Law stabilises itself through a regime of visibility in which images and monuments train recognition of legitimacy, including the allegory of Justice as a condensed, publicly readable claim about what Law is. The sequence tightened on 13 November 2024, when Francisco Wanderley Luiz detonated explosives at the *Praça dos Três Poderes* near the STF and died at the scene; the Federal Police later concluded he acted alone and identified political extremism as the motive[^41].  
In the residence he occupied previous to his suicide-terrorist-attack, investigators and press reporters found a lipstick message linking his act to the 8 January and to Débora Rodrigues's earlier lipstick inscription on the statue *A Justiça*: "Débora Rodrigues, please do not waste lipstick. That is for making women beautiful. For a statue, one uses TNT."[^42] Therefore, Law is both the possessor and the possessed of its images.
