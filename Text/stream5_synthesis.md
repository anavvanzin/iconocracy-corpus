## 1. Didi-Huberman: Warburg Meets Foucault/Benjamin — Anachronism, Survival, Montage

### Overview

Georges Didi-Huberman is the single most important mediator between Warburg and the post-structuralist French tradition. His three key texts form a cumulative argument: *Devant l'image* (1990), *L'image survivante: Histoire de l'art et temps des fantômes selon Aby Warburg* (2002), and *Atlas, ou le gai savoir inquiet* (2011).

**The Warburg-Foucault Synthesis.** Didi-Huberman does not merge Warburg and Foucault in a single theoretical statement, but the convergence is structural and cumulative. In *L'image survivante* he positions Warburg against the "positivist" tradition of Panofsky and Gombrich in terms that recall Foucault's critique of historicism: temporal periods are "no longer fashioned according to biomorphic stages, but expressed by strata, hybrid blocks, rhizomes, specific complexities, by returns that are often unexpected and goals that are always thwarted" (*SI*, 12). The concept of anachronism — the idea that past images rupture the present as symptoms — resonates with Foucault's notion of the *dispositif* as a heterogeneous ensemble linking discourses, institutions, and material practices across discontinuous historical moments. Both Warburg's *Nachleben* and Foucault's genealogy work against teleology, against the idea that images (or power-formations) simply "progress."

In *Atlas, ou le gai savoir inquiet* (published in English as *Atlas, or the Anxious Gay Science*, University of Chicago Press, 2018), Didi-Huberman extends this by reading the atlas-form itself — in Warburg, in Gerhard Richter, in Hans-Peter Feldmann — as an epistemological device: a way of producing knowledge *by montage* rather than by argument. Montage, the collision of heterogeneous temporalities, is the method shared by Warburg, Benjamin, Brecht, and, implicitly, Foucault's archaeological juxtapositions.

**Key direct quotation (≤80 words):**
> "For the natural model of cycles of 'life and death' and 'greatness and decline,' Warburg substituted a resolutely non-natural, symbolic model, a *cultural model* of history in which temporal periods are no longer fashioned according to biomorphic stages, but, instead, are expressed by strata, hybrid blocks, rhizomes, specific complexities, by returns that are often unexpected and goals that are always thwarted." — Didi-Huberman, *The Surviving Image* (PSU Press, 2017 trans.), p. 12 [originally *L'image survivante*, 2002].

**Foucault connection — direct acknowledgement:** In [a discussion via the SITE ZONES essay "New Forms of Knowledge"](https://www.sitezones.net/from-the-vault/new-forms-of-knowledge), Didi-Huberman explicitly notes that "Foucault shows how a history of madness can produce an archaeology of knowledge. In the destructive forces of his own psychic trial Warburg arrives to find the conditions of a renewal and intensifying of his entire research." This places Foucault's archaeological method in structural parallel to Warburg's symptomatological approach to cultural memory.

**Deleuze-Guattari connection:** For Didi-Huberman (as analysed in Chirolla & Mejía, "Deleuze and Didi-Huberman on Art History"), the Warburgian *Nachleben* is made theoretically mobile through Deleuzian rhizomatics: "The concept of survival involves a rhizomatic model of time, a geological *Dynamogramm* of its constituent forces." The Mnemosyne Atlas anticipates the *plateau* structure of *A Thousand Plateaus*. This gives the Warburgian method a specifically non-hierarchical, anti-genealogical (in the evolutionary sense) quality that resonates with decolonial and feminist objections to linear history.

**Political dimension — *Peuples exposés, peuples figurants* (2012):** In this fourth volume of *L'œil de l'histoire* series, Didi-Huberman connects Warburg's concern with figures and gestures directly to political visibility: who is made visible by the image, who is reduced to a "type" or a mass, who is given singularity. This directly informs a feminist reading of legal allegories: the female body-as-Justice is precisely the kind of figural reduction Didi-Huberman critiques — woman reduced to allegorical type, deprived of singularity and political subjecthood, even as she is exposed as the image of legal power.

## 2. Griselda Pollock's Virtual Feminist Museum — An Explicit Feminist Atlas

### Overview

Griselda Pollock is the scholar who has most explicitly adapted Warburg's Mnemosyne Atlas as a feminist methodological tool. Her concept of the **Virtual Feminist Museum (VFM)** — developed across *Encounters in the Virtual Feminist Museum* (Routledge, 2007) and subsequent lectures and publications — directly invokes the atlas-form as a counter-canon strategy.

**The Warburg-Feminist Synthesis.** Pollock draws explicitly on "the models of both Aby Warburg's Mnemosyne Atlas and Freud's private museum of antiquities as well as [Bracha] Ettinger's concept of subjectivity as encounter to propose a differencing journey through time, space and archive" (*Encounters*, Google Books description). For Pollock, Warburg's atlas is not a neutral art-historical tool but a feminist resource precisely because it refuses aestheticising chronology: "Warburg detested what he called aestheticizing art history. He interrogated the archives for what he at one point called a psychological history of the image" (VFM lecture, Smithsonian, 2011).

The VFM is explicitly contrasted with Alfred Barr's modernist grid (the MoMA model): while Barr's system fixes art into period, style, oeuvre, and master — a patrilineal genealogy — Warburg's panels allow images to be placed "side by side in exhibitionary formats [to] track the movement of thought and passion and the oscillations within culture." This is the structural logic of the VFM: to re-arrange art made by women across time not according to the inherited canon but according to feminist concerns — trauma, encounter, difference, matrixiality.

**The Atlas as Feminist Epistemology.** From the Érudit article "The Virtual Museum and Female Joy in Argentina": "Following Warburg's *Mnemosyne Atlas* (2012), the virtual museum ventures a collection of images-history that reveals the emotional connections possible between images, even when those images were not originally envisioned as contiguous, or when they correspond to diverse registers and devices. Pollock's premise and inspiration, then, is that the image is not mere representation but a formula of pathos, a 'thought-feeling formula' that challenges existing definitions."

**Key direct quotation (≤80 words):**
> "Clearly inspired by Aby Warburg's legacy and his unfinished and perplexing project in his Mnemosyne BilderAtlas, the Virtual Feminist Museum [...] uses the most interesting aspect of the museum: the encounter between and with artworks arranged according to a scheme that is not identical with their making." — Griselda Pollock, *Journal of Art Historiography* (2012), citing her VFM concept. URL: https://arthistoriography.files.wordpress.com/2012/12/pollock.pdf

**Connection to *Differencing the Canon* (1999).** In this earlier book, Pollock frames the canon as a "myth of creativity" and "structure of masculine narcissism": "Feminists have shown how canons actively create a patrilineal genealogy of father-son succession and replicate patriarchal mythologies of exclusively masculine creativity." The canon critique here is effectively Foucauldian (discourse as the production of subjects) merged with Warburgian counter-memory (images survive and return, but their survival can be politically intervened upon).

**Relevance to legal allegories:** Pollock's method offers a direct model: female legal allegories can be treated as *formulae of pathos* (Warburg), placed in a virtual atlas that disrupts the patrilineal canon of state iconography, and analysed with feminist attentiveness to which bodies are made visible and for whose benefit.

## 3. Bourdieu's Field Theory Applied to Images and Iconography (Artl@s, Heinich)

### Overview

Pierre Bourdieu's field theory was not designed for the analysis of individual images or iconographic survivals; his primary objects were *producers* and *institutions* (the *champ* of cultural production, the literary field). However, two critical extensions make his framework applicable to a diachronic study of legal iconography.

**Bourdieu's Field Theory — Core Architecture.** The field concept posits that cultural production occurs within "a structure of objective relations between positions" where agents struggle for "specific symbolic capital." Crucially, Bourdieu insists that a work of art cannot be understood "purely in reference to itself" but must be situated "in terms of other points of reference in meaning and practice" — i.e., within the relational space of the field. This applies directly to legal allegories: the female Justitia image is not autonomous but exists within a *champ juridico-iconographique* — a relational space of state power, legal institutions, artistic commissions, and national ideologies that determines which image-positions are possible, dominant, or dominated at any historical moment.

**The Legal Field as an Iconographic Field.** Bourdieu developed the concept of the *champ juridique* (legal field) in "The Force of Law" (1987), arguing that the law is a field of struggle over the monopoly of the legitimate use of symbolic violence. Female legal allegories participate in this struggle: they are commissioned, displayed, and reproduced by agents within the legal field (architects, state commissions, juridical academies) to consolidate and legitimate state authority. A Bourdieusian field analysis asks: who commissions these images? What is their position in the field of power? How do position-takings in the iconographic field correspond to position-takings in the legal-political field? And — critically for diachronic study — how does the *habitus* of legal image-makers reproduce or transform the received formulae of Justitia?

**Artl@s Project.** The [Artl@s project](https://artlas.huma-num.fr/en/about/), founded in 2009 at the École normale supérieure by Béatrice Joyeux-Prunel, applies Bourdieu's field theory spatially through cartographic visualization and digital databases: "ARTL@S is a project on the spatial and trans-national history of the arts and humanities, based on the collaboration of quantitative methods and cartographic visualization. These two strategies fit into a perspective nourished by critical and sociological theory that, in short, aims to highlight not only the link between a work of art and its space, but also its underlying political, social, aesthetic or economic issues." Artl@s makes Bourdieu's field *visible* as a map — a direct precedent for the "Warburgian cartography of the juridical imaginary" proposed in Section 8.

**Heinich vs. Bourdieu.** Nathalie Heinich offers an important corrective to Bourdieu's structuralism: she insists on the "system of individuality" — that sociologists must also account for the singular values and self-understandings of agents within the field, not only structural positions. For a study of legal allegories, Heinich's pragmatist-ethnomethodological approach (derived partly from Latour) demands attention to the moment of *reception* — how specific legal actors, audiences, and citizens interact with the allegorical image — not only the conditions of its production.

**Key direct quotation (≤80 words):**
> "Fields are there as structured spaces of positions (or posts) the properties of which depend on their place in the spaces, and which can be analyzed independent of the features of the occupiers of the positions." — Pierre Bourdieu, "The Properties of Fields" (1980), in *The Field of Cultural Production* (Cambridge: Polity, 1993), p. 172.

## 4. Feminist and Decolonial Cartographies (Haraway, Rolnik, Mignolo, Braidotti, D'Ignazio/Klein)

### Overview

This cluster addresses the question of *who sees and who is seen* — the scopic politics of the image — and proposes cartographic methods that are explicitly positioned, partial, and politically accountable.

### 4.1 Donna Haraway — Situated Knowledges

Haraway's 1988 essay "Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective" (*Feminist Studies* 14:3) argues that all knowledge — including art history and iconographic analysis — is produced from a specific embodied standpoint. Against the "god trick" of view-from-nowhere objectivity, Haraway proposes "feminist objectivity" as "limited location and situated knowledge, not about transcendence and splitting of subject and object." For the study of legal allegories, this means: whose gaze produced Justitia? Whose gaze is reproduced in the allegory? Who is interpellated as the subject of the law, and who is constituted as the object to be disciplined?

Haraway is not a cartographer in the literal sense, but her epistemology generates a *cartographic ethics*: "All these pictures of the world should not be allegories of infinite mobility and interchangeability but of elaborate specificity and difference and the loving care people might take to learn how to see faithfully from another's point of view."

**Key direct quotation (≤80 words):**
> "Feminist objectivity is about limited location and situated knowledge, not about transcendence and splitting of subject and object. It allows us to become answerable for what we learn how to see." — Donna Haraway, "Situated Knowledges" (1988), *Feminist Studies* 14:3, p. 583. URL: https://philpapers.org/archive/HARSKT.pdf

**Full Citation:** Donna Haraway, "Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective," *Feminist Studies* 14:3 (1988): 575–599. Reproduced in *Simians, Cyborgs and Women* (New York: Routledge, 1991). URL: https://philpapers.org/archive/HARSKT.pdf

### 4.2 Rosi Braidotti — Nomadic Subjects and Cartographic Feminism

Braidotti's *Nomadic Subjects: Embodiment and Sexual Difference in Contemporary Feminist Theory* (Columbia UP, 1994; 2nd ed. 2011) develops an explicitly cartographic methodology derived from Deleuze and Guattari: "the nomadic subject [...] constitutes an act of resistance against methodological nationalism and a critique of Euro-centrism from within. The methodology that sustains it is derived from the feminist politics of location and figurations." Braidotti defines cartography as "a politically informed map of one's historical and social locations, enabling the analysis of situated formations of power and hence the elaboration of adequate forms of resistance."

This cartographic feminism resonates with the Warburgian project: both refuse fixed categories (epoch, style, nation) in favour of *movements*, *migrations*, and *survivals*. Braidotti's nomadic subject — in perpetual becoming, crossing established categories — is structurally analogous to the surviving Pathosformel that crosses cultures and periods. The key difference is political: Braidotti's cartography is explicitly oriented toward feminist resistance, whereas Warburg's is theoretically neutral on gender.

**Key direct quotation (≤80 words):**
> "A cartography is a theoretically-based and politically-informed reading of the present which fulfils the function of providing both analytic and exegetical tools for critical thought and also creative theoretical alternatives." — Rosi Braidotti, "Writing as a Nomadic Subject," *Comparative Critical Studies* 11:2–3 (2014): 163–184. URL: https://rosibraidotti.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Braidotti-Rosi-Writing-as-a-Nomadic-Subject.pdf

**Full Citation:** Rosi Braidotti, *Nomadic Subjects: Embodiment and Sexual Difference in Contemporary Feminist Theory*, 2nd ed. (New York: Columbia University Press, 2011). ISBN 978-0231150330.

### 4.3 Suely Rolnik — Cartografia Sentimental

Suely Rolnik's *Cartografia Sentimental: Transformações contemporâneas do desejo* (1989; 2nd ed. Porto Alegre: Sulina, 2014) is the founding text of Brazilian cartographic methodology in social psychology. Rolnik defines cartography as an ethical-aesthetic-political mode of research: "a cartography refers to the [mapping] of affections, always embedded in other geographies of power [and] knowledge." The cartographer does not map a pre-given terrain but traces the transformations of desire and affect as they move through social forces.

For Rolnik — influenced by Deleuze, Guattari, and the Brazilian *Movimento Sem Terra* — the map is never a representation of the real but a *production* of the real. This has profound consequences for a study of female legal allegories: the image of Justitia is not a *reflection* of the legal order but a productive force that shapes the affective and imaginative landscape of legal subjecthood.

The feminist cartography tradition deriving from Rolnik (traced in D'Angelo & Uziel, "Feminist Cartography in Social Psychology," *Paidéia*, 2023, https://www.scielo.br/j/paideia/a/tNxs9QzCRmpLYnwp99bS4Wb/) synthesizes Rolnik with Mohanty's decolonial critique of knowledge: "The term decolonize [...] invites a 'process of unlearning historically determined habits of privilege and privation.'"

**Key direct quotation (≤80 words):**
> "Cartography refers to the mapping of affections, always embedded in other geographies of power [and] knowledge, which are built not only in the face of events that engender barriers or borders, but also in these borders themselves." — Suely Rolnik, *Cartografia Sentimental*, cited in D'Angelo & Uziel (2023), *Paidéia* 33:e3328.

**Full Citation:** Suely Rolnik, *Cartografia Sentimental: Transformações contemporâneas do desejo*, 2nd ed. (Porto Alegre: Sulina/UFRGS, 2014 [1989]). URL: https://www.scribd.com/document/420971831/343746847-ROLNIK-Suely-Cartografia-Sentimental-pdf-pdf

### 4.4 Catherine D'Ignazio and Lauren F. Klein — Data Feminism

*Data Feminism* (MIT Press, 2020) is the most systematic attempt to articulate feminist principles for data visualization and mapping. Its six principles — Rethink Binaries, Embrace Pluralism, Examine Power, Consider Context, Legitimize Embodiment and Affect, Make Labor Visible — are directly applicable to any visual corpus study (including a digital atlas of legal allegories). D'Ignazio and Klein draw on feminist critical cartography and GIS (citing J.B. Harley's critique of the alleged neutrality of maps as expressions of Foucauldian power/knowledge) and on Haraway's situated knowledges.

For a thesis on female legal allegories, *Data Feminism* provides the methodological bridge between qualitative atlas-montage (Warburg/Pollock) and any quantitative or computational dimension: "a feminist approach to data visualization therefore considers how diverse contexts can influence the production of a visualization."

**Key direct quotation (≤80 words):**
> "Data feminism [...] draws our attention to questions of epistemology — who is included in dominant ways of producing and communicating knowledge and whose perspectives are marginalized." — Catherine D'Ignazio and Lauren F. Klein, *Data Feminism* (Cambridge: MIT Press, 2020), p. 8. URL: https://data-feminism.mitpress.mit.edu

**Full Citation:** Catherine D'Ignazio and Lauren F. Klein, *Data Feminism* (Cambridge: MIT Press, 2020). Open access at: https://data-feminism.mitpress.mit.edu. DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/11805.001.0001

**Confidence (entire Section 4):** High — all four positions are documented in primary sources and their methodological implications are explicit.

## 5. Latour / Assemblage / ANT on Image-Collectives

### Overview

Actor-Network Theory (ANT) and Deleuzian assemblage theory offer a third methodological strand distinct from both Warburg's cultural memory approach and Bourdieu's field sociology. Where Bourdieu maps positions, ANT traces *translations* and *inscriptions*; where Warburg traces survivals, ANT traces *circulations* and *networks*.

**Bruno Latour — Paris: Invisible City and Oligopticons.** Latour and Émilie Hermant's *Paris: Invisible City* (1998; French print ed. B42, 2021) is itself an atlas-like project that combines photographic montage and ANT methodology to argue that Paris cannot be captured in any single image or map: "The visible is never in an isolated image or in something outside of images, but in the montage of images, a transformation of images, a cross-cutting view, a progression, a formatting, a networking. [...] There has to be a trace linking them." Latour's concept of the *oligopticon* — "extremely narrow views of the connected whole" — directly counters Foucault's panopticon as a model of legal/state visibility. The legal apparatus does not see everything; it sees selectively, through specific inscription devices (images, seals, emblems) that constitute its oligoptic reach. Justitia is one such device.

In *An Inquiry into Modes of Existence* (AIME, 2013), Latour extends the ANT framework to include *images* as distinct actors: images have their own mode of existence, their own "felicity conditions" for success. Legal allegories, as *imagines agentes* (in Bredekamp's term), are not passive representations but *actors* in the legal field.

**Deleuze-Guattari — Assemblage Theory.** As synthesized by Manuel DeLanda (*Assemblage Theory*, Edinburgh UP, 2016), the assemblage concept frames artworks as entities that "assemble and reassemble with other works, agents, and forces throughout their continued existence." Applied to legal allegory, this means that the Justitia image is not a fixed signifier but an open assemblage whose components (scales, sword, blindfold, female body) are continually rearticulated by different social actors in different contexts. DeLanda draws on Braudel's notion of "multiplicity of flows, each with its own variable rate of change" — structurally parallel to Warburg's *Nachleben* as "strata, hybrid blocks, rhizomes."

**Relevance of assemblage to Warburg.** Chirolla and Mejía argue that "the notion of the survival of images developed by Didi-Huberman is possible thanks to the set of Deleuzian concepts that we call an 'aesthetics of force.'" The image-survival is not the survival of a meaning but of a *force*: a Dynamogramm, a "geological" persistence of affective charge. This connects directly to Latour's insistence on the agency of non-human actors.

**Key direct quotation (≤80 words):**
> "The visible is never in an isolated image or in something outside of images, but in the montage of images, a transformation of images, a cross-cutting view, a progression, a formatting, a networking. [...] There has to be a trace linking them." — Bruno Latour and Émilie Hermant, *Paris: Invisible City* (Paris, 1998), p. 29. URL: https://architecturesofspatialjustice.wordpress.com/2012/10/19/paris-invisible-city/

## 6. Distant Viewing vs. Atlas-Montage (Arnold/Tilton vs. Didi-Huberman)

### Overview

The contrast between Taylor Arnold and Lauren Tilton's *Distant Viewing* methodology and the Warburgian atlas-montage is one of the most productive methodological tensions for a doctoral project combining computational and humanistic approaches to visual corpora.

### 6.1 Arnold and Tilton — Distant Viewing

*Distant Viewing: Computational Exploration of Digital Images* (MIT Press, 2023; methodological article: *Digital Scholarship in the Humanities* 34, 2019, DOI: 10.1093/llc/fqz013) proposes a framework for analysing large visual corpora through computer vision methods. The term "distant viewing" deliberately echoes Moretti's "distant reading" but addresses the *visual semantic gap*: "one must 'view' visual materials before studying them. Viewing, which we define as an interpretive action taken by either a person or a model, is necessitated by the way in which information is transferred in visual materials."

Distant viewing is explicitly *not* a claim to objectivity: "The code system that is required for distant viewing is culturally and socially constructed." This opens it to feminist critique but also to feminist deployment (as D'Ignazio and Klein's *Data Feminism* framework could be applied to the code system design).

Applied to legal allegories, distant viewing would allow tracking of formal elements (blindfold, scales, sword, body posture, draped/undraped figure) across a large corpus of images — potentially several hundred Justitia images from different national contexts and periods — to identify patterns of formal variation and recurrence that would be impossible to detect through close reading alone.

### 6.2 Didi-Huberman — Atlas-Montage as Alternative

The Warburgian atlas refuses the quantitative code system. Instead of extracting metadata, it produces *knowledge by juxtaposition*: placing images on panels so that their differences and resonances become perceptible to a scholar who looks. The atlas generates what Warburg called an "iconology of the interval" — meaning emerges not from within individual images but in the *space between* them.

As the ["Genealogy of Visual Forms" essay](https://ceserh.hypotheses.org/997) at the Centre de Sémiotique et Rhétorique notes, Warburg and Focillon's genealogical approach "aim to study metamorphosing forms" while "computational logic works by digitizing or decomposing the whole into measurable or even irreducible characteristics." This is the fundamental tension: Warburg's *Pathosformel* is a qualitative concept (force, gesture, affect) that resists quantification; Arnold and Tilton's distant viewing operates on quantifiable features.

### 6.3 The Productive Tension

These two approaches are not mutually exclusive but complementary. A doctoral project could use distant viewing to *discover* the corpus (which images exist, where, when, in what formal configurations), then use atlas-montage to *think with* the survivals — to produce knowledge by bringing together images whose connection is not chronological but affective and political. This is precisely the hybrid suggested by the digital Mnemosyne Atlas projects (Warburg Institute digital edition; the CR/10 platform at arXiv: 2104.01663), which use digital tools to recreate the atlas as an interactive research environment.

> "Distant viewing is distinguished from other approaches by making explicit the interpretive nature of extracting semantic metadata from images. In other words, one must 'view' visual materials before studying them." — Taylor Arnold and Lauren Tilton, "Distant viewing: analyzing large visual corpora," *Digital Scholarship in the Humanities* 34, Supplement 1 (2019): i3–i16. ## 7. Jens Andermann and Latin American Visual Political Studies

### Overview

Jens Andermann's edited volume *Images of Power: Iconography, Culture and the State in Latin America* (Berghahn Books/Remapping Cultural History series, 2004; pb 2006) is the most relevant existing work that combines political iconography with Latin American state formation — directly relevant to any study of legal allegories in a non-European context.

**The Volume's Contribution.** The book is described as "the first concerted attempt by cultural, historical and visual scholars to address the political dimension of visual culture in Latin America, in a comparative perspective spanning various regions and historical stages." The case studies address: "the formation of a public sphere, the visual politics of avant-garde art, the impact of mass society on political iconography, and the consolidation and crisis of territory as a key icon of the state."

The introduction to *Images of Power* (by Andermann and Rowe) provides what the *Journal of Latin American Studies* review calls "theoretical and disciplinary eclecticism at its best" — precisely the kind of multi-methodological approach (combining political theory, visual culture studies, semiotics, and historical analysis) that a Warburgian-cartographic approach requires.

**Andermann's Broader Work.** Andermann is also author of *Mapas de poder: una arqueología literaria del espacio argentino* (Rosario, 2000) — an "archaeological" (Foucauldian in method) study of spatial and cartographic representations of Argentine national identity. The term "arqueología" is significant: it positions the work within Foucault's genealogical/archaeological tradition while the subject matter is visual/spatial rather than discursive. And *The Optic of the State: Visuality and Power in Argentina and Brazil* (University of Pittsburgh Press, 2007) extends this visual-political analysis to two paradigmatic Latin American states.

**The Female Allegory Problem in Latin America.** The volume's attention to female figures in Latin American state iconography (including allegorical representations of Liberty, Justice, and the Republic) provides a direct empirical precedent. In Latin America, where as the editors note "even today writing has remained a restricted form of expression, the task of generating consent and imposing the emergent nation-state as the exclusive form of the political, was largely conferred to the image" — the female legal allegory carries an especially heavy symbolic load. The nation is routinely feminized (as *patria*, as *república*, as *justicia*), while women as political subjects are simultaneously excluded from the legal order. This contradiction is precisely the kind of ideological tension that a Warburgian-feminist analysis can unpack.

## 8. A Proposed Synthesis Framework: "Warburgian Cartography of the Juridical Imaginary"

> **[THIS SECTION REPRESENTS THE AUTHOR'S ORIGINAL INTERPRETATION AND IS NOT FOUND AS SUCH IN ANY SINGLE EXISTING SOURCE. IT IS EXPLICITLY LABELLED AS AUTHOR'S SYNTHESIS.]**

### 8.1 The Problem

A doctoral thesis on female legal allegories as diachronic survivals faces a methodological paradox. Two of the most powerful available frameworks point in opposite directions:

1. **Warburgian Nachleben** tracks the transhistorical recurrence of visual formulae (the scales, the sword, the blindfold, the draped female body) across cultures and periods, treating this recurrence as a form of *cultural memory* that has nothing necessarily to do with institutional power.

2. **Foucauldian genealogy / Bourdieusian field theory** insists that images only make sense *within* specific configurations of power: the dispositif of state sovereignty, the juridical field's struggle over legitimate symbolic violence.

These two approaches operate at different scales and levels of abstraction. Neither, alone, can account for both the *persistence* of the Justitia formula (Warburg's question) and the *function* of that formula within specific state formations (Bourdieu/Foucault's question).

### 8.2 The Synthesis — Three Articulations

**Articulation 1: Warburg + Foucault — Diachronic Archaeology of the Legal Dispositif**

Following Didi-Huberman's reading of Warburg through Benjamin (and implicitly Foucault), the Justitia allegory can be treated as a *dispositif-image*: a visual element that participates in Foucault's *dispositif* of state sovereignty but whose historical duration exceeds any particular dispositif. The allegory persists across *discontinuous* legal orders (Roman law, medieval canon law, Enlightenment natural law, modern constitutional states) precisely because it operates not at the level of *meaning* (Panofsky's iconography) but at the level of *force*: it exerts affective-political pressure on legal subjects independently of its semantic content.

The methodological operation is: identify the *moments of rupture* in the iconographic tradition (Foucault's genealogical discontinuity) AND trace the *survivals across* those ruptures (Warburg's Nachleben). Together, these two operations produce a *differential archaeology of the juridical imaginary* — mapping both what changes in the image of justice and what, uncannily, persists.

**Articulation 2: Bourdieu + Feminist Cartography — Mapping the Field of Juridical Vision**

Bourdieu's *champ juridique* + Haraway's situated knowledges produce a question that neither framework poses alone: *from where is the Justitia image seen, and by whom?* A field analysis maps the positions of agents who commission, display, and reproduce the female allegory (state architects, legal academies, constitutional tribunals, corporate law firms). A feminist cartography makes visible the *asymmetry of this scopic regime*: the female body is constituted as the object of the legal gaze even as it is used to represent the instrument of that gaze.

The methodological operation is: construct a *Bourdieusian field map* (who occupies which position in the juridical-iconographic field at a given historical moment?) + overlay a *feminist cartographic analysis* (where does the Haraway question — "vision is better from below" — bite? which bodies are rendered invisible by the allegory of Justitia?).

The Artl@s project provides the technical model: spatial cartography + field theory. But the *feminist* inflection (Rolnik's affective cartography, Braidotti's nomadic mapping) adds the political-ethical dimension that Artl@s, as a positivist-Bourdieusian project, cannot itself supply.

**Articulation 3: Pollock's Virtual Feminist Museum as Doctoral Atlas**

Griselda Pollock's Virtual Feminist Museum provides the curatorial/pedagogical model for the doctoral thesis itself. The thesis is not just an argument; it is an *atlas* — a series of panels that juxtapose Justitia images across time, place, and commission context. Each panel enacts what Pollock calls the "encounter" between images, the revelation of the Pathosformel beneath the surface variation. The atlas is organized not chronologically but *thematically* (by formula: scales, blindfold, sword; by commission context: ecclesiastical, royal, republican, corporate; by gender trouble: bare-breasted/draped, armed/passive, seeing/blind).

The thesis-atlas thus has three dimensions simultaneously:
- **Diachronic** (Warburg): tracking the survival of the formula across centuries
- **Relational** (Bourdieu/Foucault): mapping the positions of agents who deploy the formula within specific fields of juridical power
- **Feminist-cartographic** (Haraway/Pollock/Rolnik): attending to who sees the allegory, from where, and at whose expense

### 8.3 Key Methodological Principles

| Principle | Source | Application |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Non-linear temporality / Nachleben | Warburg / Didi-Huberman | Images of Justitia survive not as meanings but as affective forces across legal orders |
| Genealogical discontinuity | Foucault | Identify ruptures: when does the image of Justice radically transform and why? |
| Relational field analysis | Bourdieu / Artl@s | Map the social positions of image-producers and consumers within the juridical field |
| Situated vision | Haraway | Ask: whose gaze constituted this image? Who is rendered invisible by it? |
| Cartographic affect | Rolnik / Braidotti | Trace the affective geography of the juridical imaginary |
| Atlas-montage | Warburg / Pollock | Organize findings not as linear argument but as juxtaposed panels / encounters |
| Assemblage agency | Latour / DeLanda | Treat the allegorical image as an actor within legal-political networks |
| Partial critique of distant viewing | Arnold/Tilton | Use computational tools for corpus discovery; use atlas-montage for interpretation |

### 8.4 The Concept — "Juridical Imaginary"

The concept *juridical imaginary* (after Cornelius Castoriadis's *social imaginary* and Pierre Legendre's *juridical-theological imaginary*) designates the ensemble of visual, textual, and affective forms through which the legal order represents itself to itself and its subjects. The female legal allegory is a central node of this imaginary: it condenses — in a single image — the masculinist symbolic order's need to represent justice as both powerful (armed) and impartial (blind), both feminine (nurturing, pure) and sovereign (sword-bearing). The Warburgian cartographic approach proposed here makes this condensation *visible* by tracing its historical depth, its relational field positions, and its feminist blind-spots simultaneously.
