# Stream 2 — Digital Atlases in Legal/State Visual History

**Prepared for:** Doctoral researcher, PPGD/UFSC — thesis "Iconocracia"  
**Research objective:** Survey digital humanities projects with atlas, plate-based, or topological (non-linear) structure that organize legal, state, civic, or juridical imagery across time and space — as precedent landscape for a digital corpus/atlas of female legal allegories.  
**Date:** 2025  
**Confidence levels:** HIGH = site visited/verified via fetch_url; MEDIUM = referenced by authoritative secondary source; LOW = mentioned in scholarly literature but not independently verified.

## 1. Warburg-Inheritance Atlases (Mnemosyne Descendants)

### 1.1 Bilderatlas Mnemosyne — The Original (HKW Berlin, 2020)
- **Institution:** Haus der Kulturen der Welt (HKW), Berlin; curated by Roberto Ohrt and Axel Heil in cooperation with the Warburg Institute, London
- **URL:** https://archiv.hkw.de/en/programm/projekte/2020/aby_warburg/partner_aby_warburg/start.php
- **Structural model:** Physical exhibition reconstructing 63 cloth panels (the 1929 version of Warburg's unfinished atlas), using original photographic reproductions from the Warburg Institute's Photographic Collection. The exhibition juxtaposed 971 individual images across all 63 panels as non-linear, associative "thought-image constellations." A companion folio volume (Hatje Cantz, 2020) provides photographic documentation of the panels in print form.
- **Scale:** 63 panels; 971 images (from the Institute's 400,000-object Photographic Collection); 20 large-scale previously unpublished photographs of earlier panel versions
- **Scholarly anchor:** Exhibition catalogue (Hatje Cantz, 2020); theoretical grounding in Warburg's Pathosformel concept; exhibition ran 4 September–30 November 2020
- **Relevance to thesis:** CORE PRECEDENT. The Mnemosyne Atlas is the intellectual ancestor of all plate-based iconological comparative methods. Warburg's plates included allegorical figures of Fortune (Fortuna), Victory (Nike/Nymph), and civic procession imagery — categories directly related to female legal allegory. The non-linear juxtaposition method is the structural model the Iconocracia thesis most directly inherits.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (URL verified via HKW archive; confirmed by Art Newspaper reporting and Engramma critical edition)

### 1.2 Engramma — Mnemosyne Atlas Digital Critical Edition
- **Institution:** Centro studi classicA, Università Iuav di Venezia; journal "La Rivista di Engramma" (Venice)
- **URL:** https://www.engramma.it/eOS/core/frontend/eos_atlas_index.php?id_articolo=5746&lang=eng
- **Structural model:** Online critical edition presenting all 63 plates of the Mnemosyne Atlas in 14 thematic pathways (α + Sections I–XII + ω). Each plate is accompanied by: title and presentation, transcribed notes by Warburg/Saxl/Bing, scholarly analyses, high-definition reproductions, and critical captions. Non-linear navigation is explicitly built in — thematic pathways are color-coded and cross-referenced. The 2024–25 "Warburg Updated" issue (no. 219) adds newly discovered Warburg notes from the Warburg Institute archive.
- **Scale:** 63 plates; hundreds of individual image entries; continuous since 2000; most recently updated December 2024
- **Scholarly anchor:** Published continuously since 2000; "Dentro Mnemosyne" (Engramma no. 26, 2003) provides methodological statement; the journal also publishes thematic issues applying Mnemosyne methods to other image corpora
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGHEST relevance. The only complete, open-access critical digital edition of the Mnemosyne Atlas. Includes plates (VII: Nike and Fortuna; V: allegorical figures) directly relevant to female civic personification. The methodology of thematic pathway reading with cross-plate links is a direct model for a legal-feminine allegory atlas. Pathway VII (Nike and Fortuna: merchants, angels, nymphs, warriors) is directly about female embodiment of civic force.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (site visited, structure verified; content of 63-plate edition confirmed)

### 1.3 PHAROS: International Consortium of Photo Archives / artresearch.net
- **Institution:** Consortium of 14 art-historical photo archives: Warburg Institute (London), Frick Art Reference Library (New York), Getty Research Institute (Los Angeles), Yale Center for British Art, Courtauld Institute of Art, Bibliotheca Hertziana (Rome), Bildarchiv Foto Marburg, Fondazione Federico Zeri (Bologna), Villa I Tatti (Florence), Kunsthistorisches Institut in Florenz, National Gallery of Art (Washington DC), Paul Mellon Centre (London), Institut National d'Histoire de l'Art (Paris), RKD (The Hague)
- **URL:** https://artresearch.net (launched November 2025); platform documentation at https://www.khi.fi.it/en/forschung/photothek/PHAROS.php
- **Structural model:** Federated linked-open-data database using CIDOC-CRM ontology and ResearchSpace software. Aggregates image collections across 14 institutions; search by artwork, artist, photograph, photographer, and repository. Computer vision image similarity search; IIIF viewer for multi-image comparison; timeline, map, and network visualizations. Aligned to Getty ULAN, TGN, and IA authority files.
- **Scale:** 2.2 million images (as of 2025 launch); eventual target ~25 million images; 14 partner institutions; scholarship-grade metadata for each image
- **Scholarly anchor:** Brockington & Ruby, "PHAROS: A Digital Research Space for Photo Archives," *Art Libraries Journal* (2020); funded by Andrew W. Mellon Foundation ($30M+ over multiple grant cycles); launched publicly November 2025
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. The Warburg Institute's own Iconographic Database is a PHAROS member. Art-historical photo archives often contain the best reproductions of works depicting Justitia, allegorical ceiling decorations, and court imagery. The image-similarity search could be used to trace visual traditions of female allegory across institutions and centuries. The Yale Center for British Art (PHAROS member) holds important iconographic collections.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (artresearch.net verified via fetch_url; confirmed as launched November 2025 with 2.2M images)

### 1.4 Warburg Institute Iconographic Database (WIID)
- **Institution:** Warburg Institute, School of Advanced Study, University of London
- **URL:** https://iconographic.warburg.sas.ac.uk/vpc/VPC_search/main_page.php (robots-blocked for direct fetch; confirmed by secondary sources)
- **Structural model:** Open-access searchable database using iconographic taxonomy derived from Rudolf Wittkower's 1930s analogue photographic collection classification. Images can be linked to Iconclass codes. Browsable by subject, period, and region.
- **Scale:** ~105,100 images (as of 2023; as documented by Iconclass consortium), primarily from the Warburg's Photographic Collection; also external images; ongoing digitization from the 400,000-item collection
- **Scholarly anchor:** Warburg Institute; published alongside the Institute's research journals; digital since 2010
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. The Warburg collection systematically documents classical survival (*Nachleben*) and allegorical traditions including Justitia, Fortune/Fortuna, Victory/Nike — the precise imagery of legal-female allegory. The database is already organized along quasi-Warburgian iconographic categories.
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM (URL blocked for robots; confirmed by Iconclass consortium documentation and multiple scholarly sources citing 105,100 images)

### 1.5 Iconclass — Classification System and Browser
- **Institution:** Henri van de Waal Foundation (Netherlands); maintained in consortium
- **URL:** https://iconclass.org
- **Structural model:** Hierarchical-decimal iconographic classification system with ~28,000 unique concepts and 14,000 entry keywords. Not itself an image database, but the primary interoperability standard linking image databases worldwide. The online browser (4th edition) includes a dataset of images, bibliography, and deep links to subscribing databases. Key linked databases include: Bildindex (Marburg), RKDimages, Rijksmuseum, Princeton Index of Medieval Art, Arkyves aggregator.
- **Scale:** 28,000 concepts; used by hundreds of institutions; Iconclass AI Test Set = 87,749 classified images freely available for ML research
- **Scholarly anchor:** Van de Waal (1970s original conception); further developed by Henri van de Waal Foundation; Iconclass Consortium formed 2024; widely used in European museum documentation standards
- **Relevance to thesis:** CRITICAL TOOL. Iconclass code 58A (Justice, Justitia) and related codes (allegorical figures of virtues, republic, liberty) can be used to search across all linked databases simultaneously. For identifying female legal allegory across time and geography, Iconclass provides the controlled vocabulary. A new atlas of legal-feminine allegory would ideally adopt Iconclass codes for cross-linking.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (iconclass.org verified; structure and scope confirmed)

### 1.6 Arkyves — Aggregator of Iconclass-Indexed Collections
- **Institution:** Arkyves (commercial, associated with Brill Publishers)
- **URL:** https://www.arkyves.org/ (subscription required)
- **Structural model:** Federated search aggregator using Iconclass codes across collections including: Rijksmuseum, Folger Shakespeare Library, Herzog August Bibliothek, Getty, Glasgow University Library, and dozens of emblem book collections. Particularly strong for emblem books (Alciato, Ripa's *Iconologia*) which are central to early modern legal iconography.
- **Scale:** Millions of images across partner collections; particularly complete for 16th–17th century emblem literature
- **Scholarly anchor:** Associated with Brill's digital humanities publication programs; used in major research projects on emblem studies
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. Ripa's *Iconologia* (Rome, 1603) — the canonical source for allegorical figure attributes including Justitia and related legal virtues — is fully indexed and searchable in Arkyves. The emblem tradition documented here is the direct genealogy of legal allegorical figures.
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM (confirmed by Iconclass documentation; Arkyves site itself is subscription-gated)

### 1.7 Princeton Index of Medieval Art
- **Institution:** Princeton University (Department of Art and Archaeology)
- **URL:** https://theindex.princeton.edu/ (open access since July 1, 2023)
- **Structural model:** Thematic and iconographic index of early Christian and medieval art objects, begun in 1917 as "Index of Christian Art." Searchable by subject, medium, location, style/culture, creator, patron. Includes manuscript illuminations, architectural sculpture, mosaics, and more. Now also includes secular subjects and Jewish/Islamic works.
- **Scale:** Covers early apostolic times through 16th century; hundreds of thousands of records; international scope
- **Scholarly anchor:** Founded 1917 by Charles Rufus Morey at Princeton; described as "the most important and largest archive of medieval art anywhere in the world"; recently made fully open-access
- **Relevance to thesis:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Medieval visual traditions of female allegorical virtue (Justice, Prudence, Fortitude, Temperance) as civic/legal personifications are foundational to later legal iconography. The corpus of medieval Justitia images in manuscript law books and architectural programs is accessible here.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (confirmed by Princeton art department announcement of 2023 open access; Rutgers/Tufts library guide descriptions)

## 2. Legal/Juridical Iconography Digital Projects

### 2.1 Representing Justice — Yale Companion Site (Resnik & Curtis)
- **Institution:** Yale Law School (Documents Collection Center); associated with Yale Lillian Goldman Law Library
- **URL:** https://documents.law.yale.edu/representing-justice
- **Structural model:** Companion website to the book *Representing Justice: Invention, Controversy, and Rights in the City-States and Democratic Courtrooms* (Yale University Press, 2011) by Judith Resnik and Dennis Curtis. The site offers a preview of images from the book and links to an online exhibit: "The Remarkable Run of a Political Icon: Justice as a Sign of the Law." A parallel Flickr album hosts images from the Yale Law Library Rare Book Collection used in the book.
- **Scale:** Over 220 images in the book; subset available online; Flickr album from Yale Law Library Rare Book Collection at https://www.flickr.com/photos/yalelawlibrary/albums/72157625368729970/
- **Scholarly anchor:** Resnik & Curtis, *Representing Justice* (Yale UP, 2011; 668 pp.); reviewed in *Journal of Legal Education* (2013); the only book-length scholarly survey tracing the full iconographic history of Justitia from city-state to modern democratic courthouse, covering over 220 representations across time and geography
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGHEST relevance. This is the primary scholarly precedent for the Iconocracia thesis. The book maps the "remarkable run" of the female figure of Justice with scales and sword; analyzes how Renaissance "rites" became democratic "rights"; traces the almost exclusive persistence of the figure as white woman; notes that of an exhaustive worldwide catalog, only ONE courthouse depicts Justice as a woman of color in plain view (St. Croix, Virgin Islands, 1993). This gap argument directly anchors the feminist/decolonial critique that the Iconocracia thesis likely makes.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (site verified via fetch_url; Flickr album URL confirmed; book confirmed via multiple reviews)

### 2.2 Yale Law Library Justitia Gallery (Flickr)
- **Institution:** Lillian Goldman Law Library, Yale University
- **URL:** https://www.flickr.com/photos/yalelawlibrary/albums/72157625368729970/
- **Structural model:** Flickr album of images from the Rare Book Collection used in *Representing Justice*. Informal image gallery supplemented by blog posts on legal iconography resources (2010–present) by law librarian Michael Widener, who has documented related databases and pointed to the Dutch NCRD database.
- **Scale:** Several dozen images; ongoing additions noted in blog
- **Scholarly anchor:** Yale Lillian Goldman Law Library; Michael Widener's legal iconography blog
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. Provides direct access to primary source images of Justitia from early printed books.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (URL confirmed by multiple Yale sources)

### 2.3 Dutch Center for Documentation in Legal Iconography (NCRD)
- **Institution:** Nederlands Centrum voor Rechthistorische Documentatie (NCRD), formerly at the Koninklijke Bibliotheek (Royal Library), The Hague
- **URL:** No longer independently accessible as of December 2021; ~12,000 images now at Memory of the Netherlands portal: https://www.delpher.nl or https://geheugen.delpher.nl
- **Structural model:** Combined image database and bibliography for legal iconography; organized by subject (legal imagery types), period, and geography. Was the most comprehensive dedicated database of legal iconographic imagery worldwide during its operation.
- **Scale:** ~12,000 images; combined bibliography of Dutch legal history
- **Scholarly anchor:** Created by the Koninklijke Bibliotheek and Dutch universities; documented in Vervaart's *Rechtsgeschiedenis* blog and Yale Law Library legal iconography resources blog (2010)
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. Although now defunct as independent database (as of December 2021), its collection is archived in the Dutch cultural heritage portal. The NCRD is the closest existing precedent to a dedicated legal iconography database — and its closure demonstrates the gap in institutional infrastructure for legal iconography research.
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM (confirmed by Vervaart's Rechtshistorie.nl and Rechtsgeschiedenis blog; URL for Memory of the Netherlands portal confirmed)

### 2.4 Visual Jurisprudence / Visual Persuasion Project (NYLS/Cardozo)
- **Institution:** New York Law School (Richard K. Sherwin, Director, Visual Persuasion Project); Cardozo School of Law (Peter Goodrich, Program in Law and Humanities)
- **URL (NYLS):** http://www.nyls.edu/centers/projects/visual_persuasion (historical; current status UNVERIFIED — no longer accessible)
- **URL (Cardozo publications):** https://larc.cardozo.yu.edu/faculty-chapters/64/ (Goodrich faculty publications, verified)
- **Structural model:** Research program rather than browsable database. The Visual Persuasion Project focused on visual evidence and advocacy in courtrooms; Goodrich's work at Cardozo produced a theory and history of legal emblems and visual governance. Key outputs: symposium "Visualizing Law in the Digital Age" (Cardozo, October 2011); NYLS Law Review vol. 57, no. 1 (2012–13) entire issue on "Visualizing Law in the Digital Age." Goodrich's online publications include images from judicial decisions and legal manuscripts.
- **Scale:** ~300 short articles in NYLS Law Review special issue; Goodrich's monograph *Legal Emblems and the Art of Law* (Cambridge UP, 2014) is the key scholarly anchor; faculty publication repository at Cardozo (LARC) contains additional digital materials
- **Scholarly anchor:** Richard K. Sherwin, *Visualizing Law in the Age of the Digital Baroque* (2011); Peter Goodrich, *Legal Emblems and the Art of Law: Obiter Depicta as the Vision of Governance* (Cambridge UP, 2014); "Visiocracy: On the Futures of the Fingerpost" (NYU); "Iconography of Nothing: Blank Spaces in the Representation of Law" (Cardozo, 2018)
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. Goodrich's "legal emblems" tradition is the theoretical framework connecting Warburg's image-knowledge to law. His work on the emblem books of Alciato and Ripa — and the allegorical female figures of Justice, Prudence, and Liberty in those books — is the direct scholarly lineage of the Iconocracia thesis. The LARC faculty publications database is freely accessible.
- **Confidence:** HIGH for Goodrich publications at LARC (verified); MEDIUM for Visual Persuasion Project website (NYLS URL cited in 2012 NYLS Law Review article but current status unclear)

### 2.5 Picturing Justice: The On-Line Journal of Law and Popular Culture

- **URL:** https://cap-press.com/sites/pj/
- **Structural model:** Archived online journal organized by medium: Silver Screen (film), Small Screen (television), News and Views (broader issues). Articles are short critical essays (not image databases). Operated 1997–2006.
- **Scale:** Over 300 articles; now a historical archive only (no new submissions)
- **Scholarly anchor:** Founded 1997; dedicated to memory of Prof. Paul Joseph; sponsored by USF Law School
- **Relevance to thesis:** MEDIUM. As a journal of law and popular culture rather than a visual database, Picturing Justice provides critical essays on the representation of legal figures in media — relevant to understanding how legal iconography is reproduced and transformed — but is not an image atlas or database.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (site verified via fetch_url)

### 2.6 Eikón/Imago — IUS ILLUMINATUM Project

- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. The journal covers legal manuscript illustration — precisely the iconographic tradition (Justitia in law books, allegorical frontispieces) that precedes and grounds courtroom iconography. The Iberian publishing venue also means it may cover Ibero-American legal imagery traditions, relevant to UFSC.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (confirmed via Medieval Art Research announcement and journal URL verified)

## 3. Political/State Iconography Archives Usable as Atlases

### 3.1 Hoover Institution Political Poster Database
- **Institution:** Hoover Institution Library & Archives, Stanford University
- **URL:** https://digitalcollections.hoover.org (general); https://digitalcollections.hoover.org/groups/formats/category/Poster (poster category)
- **Structural model:** Searchable database with keyword and advanced search; browsable by country. Two digital collections: original catalog of 33,000 digitized posters (1980s digitization, available as digital files); 20,000 "uncataloged" posters digitized 2022–23 available in "Digital Collections 2." No built-in atlas or plate-based structure; operates as a keyword-searchable relational database.
- **Scale:** 33,512 catalogued poster records in digital database; total collection over 120,000 posters from more than 100 countries; date range late 1800s to late 1980s. Top countries: USA (10,293), UK (6,331), Germany (3,784), Soviet Union (3,216), France (1,981).
- **Scholarly anchor:** Hoover Institution Library & Archives (Stanford); decades of acquisitions; described in Lafayette College and other library guides as a key resource for propaganda and political psychology research
- **Relevance to thesis:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Many political posters — particularly WWI and WWII era — deploy female allegorical figures (Columbia, Britannia, Marianne, Germania, Libertas) in state/legal contexts. Relevant for tracing how female personifications of the state are used in contexts of mobilization, justice, and sovereignty. The USSR collection (3,216 posters) includes imagery of Soviet law and justice.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (site verified via fetch_url; 33,512 poster records confirmed)

### 3.2 German Propaganda Archive (Calvin University)
- **Institution:** Calvin University (Prof. Randall Bytwerk)
- **URL:** https://research.calvin.edu/german-propaganda-archive/
- **Structural model:** Thematically organized web collection of translations and images. Nazi sections: Pre-1933 / 1933–45 / Visual Material (art, caricatures, photographs, posters) / WWII / Anti-Semitism / Propagandists' guides. East German sections: parallel structure. Search function available. Not a formal database with structured metadata; images are embedded in HTML pages.
- **Scale:** Thousands of items; ongoing updates (blog active); covers Nazi Germany and GDR
- **Scholarly anchor:** Bytwerk, Randall L., "German Propaganda Archive" (1998), *Calvin Faculty Publications and Creative Works*, 567; associated book *Bending Spines* (MSU Press, 2004); translated into multiple languages
- **Relevance to thesis:** MEDIUM. State iconography archive; visual material includes allegorical/symbolic imagery of German national identity. Female allegories (Germania, etc.) appear in this propaganda context. Less directly relevant to legal allegory than courthouse/judicial iconography, but important for understanding how state visual culture instrumentalizes female personification.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (site verified via fetch_url)

### 3.3 Library of Congress: Cartoon Prints, American (Print & Photographs Division)
- **Institution:** Library of Congress
- **URL:** https://www.loc.gov/pictures/collection/app/
- **Structural model:** Browsable and keyword-searchable collection of political cartoons and caricatures; part of the Prints & Photographs Online Catalog (PPOC). Individual cataloguing for over 800 prints; larger collection of political cartoon series. Images are digitized and freely available.
- **Scale:** 800+ individually catalogued prints (18th–19th century); broader cartoon series; covers pre-revolutionary through 19th century
- **Scholarly anchor:** Library of Congress; Prints & Photographs Division; the most authoritative American political print archive
- **Relevance to thesis:** MEDIUM. American political cartoons of the 18th–19th century routinely deployed female allegories — Columbia, Lady Liberty, Justice — in civic and legal contexts. The LOC collection is the foundational resource for this tradition.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (URL verified; description from LOC background page)

### 3.4 BnF Gallica — Bibliothèque nationale de France Digital Library
- **Institution:** Bibliothèque nationale de France (BnF)
- **URL:** https://gallica.bnf.fr
- **Structural model:** Mega-scale digital library with millions of documents (books, press, manuscripts, sound, iconographic documents, maps). Searchable by keyword, date, type, and subject. The BnF also maintains: Mandragore (iconographic database for manuscript decoration, with François Garnier's *Thesaurus Iconographique* vocabulary); Banque d'images (image licensing service with hundreds of thousands of iconographic items); and Biblissima (aggregator for medieval manuscripts with Mandragore integration). A search for "allégorie" or "Justitia" or "Marianne" yields thousands of hits.
- **Scale:** Millions of documents; the Banque d'images alone has "hundreds of thousands" of images; Mandragore covers all BnF manuscripts; Biblissima integrates Mandragore with Initiale
- **Scholarly anchor:** BnF institutional publications; Gallica has been online since 1997; the BnF also co-develops Europeana and the France-Brazil Shared Heritage digital library
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. Gallica contains the richest single-nation repository of female allegorical imagery in the Western tradition, given France's role as origin of Marianne and the modern republican allegory tradition. Searches for "allégorie de la Justice," "République française" (image), and "Marianne" yield rich iconographic materials from the Revolutionary period through the Third Republic — the golden age of female civic allegory. The France-Brazil Shared Heritage collection is directly relevant to UFSC research.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (BnF Gallica URL confirmed; Mandragore and Banque d'images confirmed in BnF institutional descriptions)

### 3.5 Europeana Collections
- **Institution:** Europeana Foundation (EU digital platform for cultural heritage)
- **URL:** https://www.europeana.eu/en
- **Structural model:** Federated aggregator from 3,500+ partner institutions across Europe; tens of millions of digitized items. Searchable by keyword, date, type, country, providing rights. Browsable by thematic collection. Advanced search supports filtering by subject terms including "allegory," "personification," "justice." Nearly 3 million items are openly licensed.
- **Scale:** Tens of millions of items; 3,500+ contributing institutions; covers all European countries and periods
- **Scholarly anchor:** European Commission digital strategy; includes national libraries, galleries, archives, and museums from all EU member states
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. Europeana aggregates iconographic collections from across Europe, enabling pan-European search for allegorical representations. A search for "Justitia" or "allegory justice" returns images from Italian, French, German, Dutch, Spanish, and Portuguese institutions — exactly the comparative pan-European scope needed for tracing the genealogy of female legal allegory before its transplantation to the Americas.
- **Confidence:** HIGH (Europeana URL confirmed; scope confirmed by EC digital strategy documentation)

### 3.6 Bildindex der Kunst und Architektur (Marburg)
- **Institution:** Deutsches Dokumentationszentrum für Kunstgeschichte — Bildarchiv Foto Marburg
- **URL:** https://www.bildindex.de/
- **Structural model:** Aggregator for ~80 German cultural heritage institutions, museums, libraries, archives — one of the largest Iconclass-indexed collections. Searchable by subject (via Iconclass), artist, medium, period, location. Millions of images with structured metadata.
- **Scale:** Millions of images; collections from ~80 German institutions; primary language German but searchable in English via Iconclass codes
- **Scholarly anchor:** Long-established German art documentation project; PHAROS member institution; one of the most complete European photographic art archives
- **Relevance to thesis:** HIGH. Particularly strong for Baroque-era German allegorical programs (ceiling paintings, court imagery, juridical iconography in legal manuscripts and courthouses). Many key images of Justitia, Fortuna, and civic virtue allegories from the Holy Roman Empire period are indexed here.
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM (confirmed by Iconclass consortium documentation and multiple scholarly references; URL confirmed)

## 4. Colonial and Postcolonial Legal Imagery Projects

### 4.1 Slavery Images: A Visual Record of the African Slave Trade (Handler-Tuite)
- **Institution:** University of Colorado Boulder (currently, under direction of Henry B. Lovejoy); originally University of Virginia
- **URL:** https://slaveryimages.org
- **Structural model:** Searchable database organized into 18 topical categories (not mutually exclusive). Images (paintings, lithographs, photographs) obtained from pre-1924 primary sources; all in public domain. Metadata includes bibliographic source, region/country, approximate date. No atlas-style spatial or non-linear organization; primarily a flat categorical database. Built on Omeka-S platform (since 2019); IIIF-compatible.
- **Scale:** ~1,280 images in 18 topical categories; launched 2000 with 150–200 images; no new additions planned but metadata regularly revised; aligned with UNESCO Slave Route Project
- **Scholarly anchor:** Handler, J.S. and Tuite, M. (creators, 1999–2018); now directed by H.B. Lovejoy (Univ. of Colorado Boulder); Lovejoy & Handler, "Legacy Websites: The Case of Slavery Images," in *Regenerated Identities: Documenting African Lives* (African World Press, 2022)
- **Relevance to thesis:** MEDIUM-HIGH. The legal dimensions of slavery are encoded in imagery of physical coercion, sale, punishment — the visual underside of juridical order. This collection provides the colonial counter-archive to the idealized female allegories of Justice in European/Brazilian legal iconography: how do images of enslaved women relate to the female figure of Justitia presiding over colonial legal systems?
- **Confidence:** HIGH (site verified via fetch_url; scope and structure confirmed)

### 4.2 Early Caribbean Digital Archive (ECDA)

## 8. Gap Analysis — What Does NOT Exist Yet (Argument for an Atlas of Legal-Feminine Allegory)

The survey of 30+ projects reveals a striking structural gap: **no existing digital project combines (a) a dedicated focus on female allegorical figures in legal/juridical contexts, (b) pan-Atlantic or Ibero-American geographic scope, (c) plate-based or atlas-style non-linear organization, and (d) feminist or decolonial analytical framework.**

### Gap 1: The Broken Institutional Chain of Legal Iconography
The NCRD (Netherlands) was the only dedicated digital database of legal iconography — and it closed in December 2021, with its 12,000 images now archived in the Memory of the Netherlands portal without the specialized legal iconographic metadata that made it functional for research. No successor institution has emerged. The Iconocracia thesis would fill this institutional gap for the Ibero-American context.

### Gap 2: No Digital Atlas of Justitia / Female Legal Allegory
Resnik & Curtis's *Representing Justice* (2011) is the only systematic scholarly survey of the Justitia iconographic tradition — and it exists only as a book (668 pages) with a minimal companion website. There is no database, no searchable corpus, no atlas. The Yale Flickr album is an informal supplement, not a structured resource. The observation that Resnik & Curtis's global survey found only ONE representation of Justice as a woman of color (St. Croix, 1993) in courthouses — after exhaustively cataloguing worldwide — demonstrates both the ideological power of the white-female-Justitia norm and the absence of any counter-atlas that would surface alternative traditions.

### Gap 3: No Ibero-American or Latin American Legal Iconography Database
All existing legal iconography projects are North Atlantic (Dutch NCRD, Yale, Cardozo). The Brazilian, Portuguese, Spanish, and broader Ibero-American tradition of visual legal culture is entirely undocumented in any dedicated digital project. The allegorical programs of Brazilian courthouses, the iconography of the República in Brazilian political satire and official imagery (1889–1945 especially), and the colonial-era visual representations of Iberian juridical sovereignty have no digital home.

### Gap 4: No Gender-Feminist Lens on Legal Visual Culture
The Erdteilallegorien database at Vienna treats continent allegories (female figures representing Europe, Asia, Africa, America) as art history, not as visual legal-political instruments. The Contested Histories database treats monuments as legal-cultural disputes, not through gender analysis. The Hoover Poster database contains thousands of images deploying female allegory in state contexts, but has no thematic organization around gender. No project asks: what does it mean that the female body has been the almost universal vessel for legal abstractions (Justice, Republic, Liberty, Law) throughout Western visual culture?

### Gap 5: No Atlas Methodology Applied to Legal Imagery
The Mnemosyne Atlas and its descendants (Engramma) demonstrate the intellectual power of the plate-based, non-linear, associative organization for image research. The Erdteilallegorien database demonstrates how this can be combined with GIS and Iconclass indexing for allegorical figures. But no one has applied this combined methodology to the specific corpus of legal allegorical imagery — and especially not to the female body as legal-allegorical vessel. The result is that legal iconography research is either: (a) done in archives without digital infrastructure, (b) published in monographs that are not machine-searchable, or (c) scattered across generic art history databases without legal-thematic organization.

### Gap 6: No Colonial/Postcolonial Counter-Archive for Legal Allegory
Slavery Images and the Early Caribbean Digital Archive document the visual culture of colonial law from below — how enslaved people were represented in European imagery. But no project triangulates this with the allegorical tradition from above — how Justice, Republic, and Liberty were depicted as white, European female figures presiding over colonial legal systems that enslaved and excluded the very populations those abstractions nominally protected. This triangulation is a core scholarly contribution of the Iconocracia thesis.

### Summary Argument for the Atlas
The Iconocracia thesis occupies a unique intersection of four underserved areas: (1) legal iconography (NCRD closed, no Ibero-American successor); (2) female allegory studies (Agulhon/Mariannologie tradition, not digitized for legal-juridical focus); (3) Brazilian/Ibero-American digital humanities (BNDigital, IMS exist but without legal-iconographic thematic organization); and (4) atlas/Mnemosyne methodology (Engramma, Erdteilallegorien exist but not for legal subjects). The Iconocracia digital atlas would be the first project to unite all four.

## 9. Technical/Structural Lessons for Designing Such an Atlas

### Lesson 1: Adopt Iconclass Coding from Day One
Every comparable project that has succeeded at cross-institutional interoperability — PHAROS, Erdteilallegorien, Bildindex, Arkyves — uses Iconclass as its shared vocabulary. For a legal-feminine allegory atlas, the key codes are:
- **11M31** (Justice, Justitia — personification)
- **11N** (allegorical figures of virtues generally)
- **44B** (State, nation, sovereignty — political allegory)
- **44G411** (Republic as female allegory)
- **44G51** (Liberty, Freedom — as allegorical figure)
- **11P** (personifications of abstract concepts)
- **34B** (female figure attributes: scales, sword, blindfold)

Adopting Iconclass from the start enables future integration with Arkyves, PHAROS, and Europeana metadata.

### Lesson 2: Use Plate-Based Organization with Non-Linear Topological Links
The Engramma Mnemosyne Atlas demonstrates that plate-based organization (each "panel" grouping related images around a visual formula) is more intellectually productive for iconographic research than a flat database. For the Iconocracia atlas, this means:
- Each "plate" = a node (e.g., Plate 1: Justitia with scales and sword, Plate 2: Justitia blindfolded, Plate 3: Republican female allegory, Plate 4: Allegorical frontispiece of Brazilian law codes, etc.)
- Each plate links thematically to related plates (pathosformeln, formula migration across time and geography)
- Non-linear navigation allows researchers to follow visual formulas across national traditions

### Lesson 3: GIS Map as One of Multiple Interfaces
The Erdteilallegorien database is the best structural model: four parallel interfaces (Map, Timeline, Index, Search). For a legal-feminine allegory atlas:
- **Map interface:** Geolocate courthouse statues, allegorical murals, printed frontispieces by city/region
- **Timeline interface:** Show chronological distribution of allegorical formulas (e.g., when does the blindfolded Justitia become dominant? When does the Republican female allegory appear in Brazil?)
- **Plate/panel interface:** Warburgian thematic groupings
- **Search interface:** Iconclass-coded full-text search

### Lesson 4: Build on Omeka-S and IIIF for Brazilian Context
Slavery Images successfully migrated to Omeka-S with IIIF integration — a platform already familiar to Brazilian digital humanities infrastructure. Given the thesis is at UFSC (a Brazilian federal university), using Omeka-S (open-source, Portuguese-language supported, widely used in Brazilian digital humanities) would ensure institutional sustainability and interoperability with BNDigital and IMS infrastructure.

### Lesson 5: Design for Multi-Source Image Aggregation
The Cornell Persuasive Maps, PHAROS, and Europeana demonstrate that the most productive digital atlases aggregate images from multiple institutions rather than cataloguing a single collection. For the Iconocracia atlas, this means designing to ingest:
- Images from BNDigital/Hemeroteca (via OAI-PMH or direct API)
- Images from IMS Brasiliana (via licensing agreement)
- Images from Gallica (via BnF API — many items are open license)
- Images from Europeana (via Europeana REST API)
- Images from Warburg Iconographic Database (via IIIF manifests)

### Lesson 6: The Scholarly Publication Must Accompany the Database
Every successful project combines a digital interface with substantial scholarly publication: Resnik & Curtis (668-page book + website); Engramma (peer-reviewed journal + atlas); Erdteilallegorien (anthologies + database). The Iconocracia thesis itself is the scholarly anchor for the digital atlas — and the thesis defense should describe the database structure and methodology as part of the original contribution, alongside the iconographic analysis.

### Lesson 7: Document the Gap (Missing Iconography)
Monument Lab's National Monument Audit demonstrates that what is ABSENT from a visual landscape is as revealing as what is present: their finding that the US monument landscape is "overwhelmingly white and male" is the thesis of their audit. Similarly, Resnik & Curtis's finding that Justice appears almost exclusively as a white woman is a structural finding about absence. The Iconocracia atlas should explicitly document: How many Brazilian courthouse statues depict Justice as a Black woman? How many as Indigenous? When and where do counter-representations appear? The database should make absences quantifiable.
