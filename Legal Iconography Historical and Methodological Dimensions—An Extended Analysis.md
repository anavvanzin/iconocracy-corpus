Legal iconography (*Rechtsikonographie*) as a formal academic subdiscipline emerged from German-speaking scholarship with the founding of the **Arbeitskreis für Rechtsikonographie** (Working Group for Legal Iconography) in **1988**—not 1989 as some sources suggest—by **Gernot Kocher** (University of Graz, Austria) and **Clausdieter Schott** (University of Zürich, Switzerland). This institutionalization marked a critical turning point in recognizing the systematic study of visual legal representations as a distinct interdisciplinary field situated within legal history but requiring methodological tools from art history, archaeology, ethnology, and folklore studies.[1][2][3][4][5]
### Historical Foundations: The Germanic Tradition (19th–Mid-20th Century)
Legal iconography's intellectual lineage extends back to the late 19th and early 20th centuries through pioneering German-speaking scholars who established its foundational concepts and archival infrastructure. The field's "founding fathers" are typically identified as:[6][7][5]
**Karl von Amira** (1848–1930), whose work on Germanic legal symbolism—particularly *Der Stab in der germanischen Rechtssymbolik* (The Staff in Germanic Legal Symbolism)—pioneered the systematic collection and analysis of legal objects and visual representations. Von Amira established the Leopold-Wenger-Institut für Rechtsgeschichte in Munich, creating the oldest German collection in legal ethnology and legal archaeology. He conceptualized *Rechtsarchäologie* (legal archaeology) as studying material traces of legal culture—courthouses, execution sites, ritual objects, monuments—recognizing that law manifested not only in texts but in physical spaces and symbolic objects.[8][7][9][6]
**Claudius Freiherr von Schwerin** (1880–1944) posthumously published von Amira's materials as *Einführung in die Rechtsarchäologie* (Introduction to Legal Archaeology, 1943), consolidating the methodological foundations for studying visual and material dimensions of legal history. However, von Schwerin's deep involvement with Nazi ideology after 1933 casts a problematic shadow over this legacy, requiring contemporary scholars to approach these foundational texts with critical awareness of their political contexts.[7][9][6]
**Hans Fehr** (1874–1961), a Swiss scholar who studied in Germany, focused specifically on representations of law in the arts, examining how legal concepts and practices were depicted in painting, sculpture, and illustrated manuscripts. Fehr's work established that visual representations were not merely decorative but constituted independent sources for understanding how legal consciousness operated in different historical periods.[6][7]
**Eberhard Georg Otto Freiherr von Künßberg** (1881–1941) concentrated on legal gestures and symbolic actions, influenced by his directorship of the *Deutsches Rechtswörterbuch* (German Legal Dictionary) project. His image collection later became part of the foundational holdings at the University of Zürich's legal iconography archive.[5][7]
These early scholars recognized what subsequent research would confirm: medieval and early modern European legal culture was profoundly **visual**. While Roman law operated largely without images, medieval legal manuscripts—particularly the illuminated *Sachsenspiegel* (Saxon Mirror) codices from the 13th–14th centuries—integrated elaborate pictorial programs that structured legal texts, aided memory, and represented legal authority. The oldest illuminated *Sachsenspiegel* dates to 1290, approximately 70 years after the text's composition, and numerous illustrated legal manuscripts followed, including the Codex Falkensteinensis (1166), various customary law compilations, and the Hamburg city law (1479).[10][8][5]
This visual richness stood in stark contrast to modern legal culture. As Klaus F. Röhl observed, "modern law is poor in images" (*Das moderne Recht ist arm an Bildern*), marking a dramatic rupture from earlier traditions when legal manuscripts were richly illustrated. Early 19th-century scholars spoke of *jurisprudentia picturata* or *jurisprudentia illustrata* (illustrated jurisprudence) to describe this medieval phenomenon, but iconographic continuity between medieval illuminated manuscripts and modern legal visualization had been severed.[8]
### Institutionalization and Conference Culture (1988–Present)
The 1988 founding of the Arbeitskreis für Rechtsikonographie created sustainable institutional infrastructure for ongoing research. The Working Group evolved into the **Internationale Rechtsikonographie-Konferenz** (International Legal Iconography Conference), which convenes biennially to present research findings and facilitate scholarly exchange. As of 2024, this conference series continues actively—the most recent documented meeting occurred April 4–5, 2024, in Heidelberg, organized by Andreas Deutsch (Heidelberg) and Heiner Lück (Halle), commemorating the late Clausdieter Schott with a thematic focus on "Legal Iconography and Legal Language".[2][3][4][11][1][10]
Research findings are published in the journal series **Signa Iuris – Beiträge zur Rechtsikonographie, Rechtsarchäologie und Rechtlichen Volkskunde** (Signa Iuris – Contributions to Legal Iconography, Legal Archaeology, and Legal Folklore), founded in 2008 under the editorship of Gernot Kocher, Heiner Lück, and Clausdieter Schott (and later Andreas Deutsch). The series title deliberately invokes *signa iuris*—the "signs of law"—emphasizing the field's semiotic dimensions and its concern with how law communicates through visual and material symbols.[11][12][7][6]
Germany houses probably the world's richest legal archaeology and iconography museum: the **Kriminalmuseum in Rothenburg ob der Tauber**, whose extensive collection documents historical instruments of punishment, execution, and legal ritual. This museum functions as both tourist attraction and research repository, preserving material artifacts that illuminate how legal power was physically enacted and symbolically represented.[6]
### The Zürich Legal Image Database: Methodological Infrastructure
A critical institutional development was the creation of comprehensive **legal image databases** (*Rechtsbilddatenbanken*) enabling systematic cataloging and analysis of visual legal sources. The most significant is the **Rechtsbilddatenbank** (Legal Image Database) of the Abteilung Rechtsvisualisierung (Department of Legal Visualization) at the University of Zürich, established in **1992**.[4][5]
This database emerged from the **Rechtsbildersammlung** (Legal Image Collection) assembled by Karl Siegfried Bader (1905–1998), who served as professor of Swiss and German legal history at Zürich from 1953–1975. Bader integrated his own collected materials with the image estates (*Bildnachlässe*) of von Künßberg and Hans Felix Pfenninger (1886–1969), creating a collection that remains **Europe's largest legal image archive with approximately 15,000 images**. The collection surpasses other European repositories in both size and thematic breadth and depth, serving as a model for subsequent archival projects.[5]
When Clausdieter Schott succeeded Bader in 1975, he inherited stewardship of the image collection. In the early 1990s, Schott's assistants Hermann Romer and Michele Luminati persuaded him to undertake systematic iconographic-iconological cataloging via a database project, initially funded by the Swiss National Science Foundation (SNF) and subsequently by the University of Zürich. The database ran initially under DOS using the F&A (Frage und Antwort / Question and Answer) 4.0 database program, later migrating to Microsoft Access with an internet-accessible version developed by programmer Anne-Chantal Mezger. Content management—the actual iconographic analysis and data entry—was conducted by Colette Brunschwig from July 1, 1992, through December 31, 2022, representing over 30 years of sustained scholarly labor.[5]
Upon Schott's retirement in 2004, Andreas Thier assumed directorship of the renamed Zentrum für Rechtsgeschichtliche Forschung (Center for Legal Historical Research) and responsibility for the database, which he retains until his anticipated retirement in 2028.[5]
Parallel to the Zürich initiative, Gernot Kocher at the Karl-Franzens-Universität Graz developed his own **Rechtsikonographische Datenbank** in the 1990s using AskSam database software. These institutional investments reflected growing recognition that historical legal images constituted sources equivalent in value to textual documents, requiring comparable systematic preservation and analysis.[4][5]
### The Iconographic-Iconological Method: Analytical Framework
Legal iconography adopted and adapted the **iconographic-iconological method** developed by art historian **Erwin Panofsky** (1892–1968) in his seminal 1939 work *Studies in Iconology*. Dutch art historian **Roelof van Straten** refined Panofsky's framework into operationally distinct phases that proved particularly practical for systematic database work.[13][14][1][5]
The method operates through **three (or four) hierarchical interpretive levels**, each answering progressively deeper questions about visual sources:[14][13][1][5]
### 1. Pre-Iconographic Description (Primary or Natural Subject Matter)
This initial phase is "purely empirical and descriptive", enumerating **everything visibly depicted** without interpretation or establishing relationships between elements. For legal images, this means identifying all legally significant visual elements: persons, objects, gestures, spatial arrangements, architectural settings, symbolic objects (staffs, swords, scales, documents), and compositional structures (foreground/background, diagonals, left/right positioning, color schemes).[14][5]
Database fields supporting pre-iconographic description include: image-internal and image-external text, color information, and keyword tagging. This phase addresses the question: **"What do I see?"**[13][5]
For example, in analyzing a *Sachsenspiegel* illumination, pre-iconographic description would note: three human figures, one seated elevated on a platform, two standing below; the seated figure wears a crown and holds a staff; objects include a document with visible text; architectural elements include columns and arches; dominant colors are red, gold, and blue.
### 2. Iconographic Description (Secondary or Conventional Subject Matter)
This phase **determines the theme** of the image by identifying depicted actions, events, objects, and persons according to established iconographic traditions. In legal contexts, this means recognizing: court scenes, execution of sentences, contractual acts, feudal ceremonies, personifications of legal virtues, specific historical legal figures (famous jurists, judges, rulers in their legal capacity).[14][5]
Crucially, this phase requires **familiarity with iconographic conventions**—"In the visual arts, most artists addressed the same themes, so that for many themes there are multiple, if not many, examples". Knowing the visual vocabulary allows identification: a woman blindfolded holding scales represents Justice (*Justitia*); two figures clasping hands signifies oath-taking or contract formation; a hand raised with specific finger positions indicates testimony or judicial pronouncement.[10][5]
This phase also involves **identifying textual and visual sources**—which biblical passages, legal codes (e.g., *Sachsenspiegel*), or earlier images served as models. It addresses: **"What is represented?"**[13][5]
Database fields include: content location/region/country, person names and legal roles (judge, lawyer, plaintiff, witness), life dates, hyperlinks to reference sources, keywords.[5]
Van Straten emphasizes that identifying **personified abstractions** (Justice, Equity, Prudence, Fortitude, Truth) belongs to the **third phase**, not this one, because such identifications require interpretation of deeper symbolic meaning.[5]
### 3. Iconographic Interpretation (Intrinsic Meaning or Content—Iconographic Level)
This phase seeks "deeper" or "secondary" meaning intentionally encoded by the artist/patron. Because such meanings are "almost always abstract," this is where **personifications and allegorical figures are properly identified**.[13][14][5]
In legal iconography, this includes recognizing:
- **Personified virtues**: *Justitia* (Justice), *Aequitas* (Equity), *Concordia* (Concord), *Prudentia* (Prudence), *Temperantia* (Temperance)[5]
- **Symbolic objects carrying legal meaning**: The market cross (*Marktkreuz*)—a stone or wooden cross marking legally designated market locations, often with a glove suspended from it signifying the market judge's judicial authority transferred to that space[5]
- **Symbolic plants**: The green rose in the Heidelberg *Sachsenspiegel* manuscript symbolizing judgment (*Urteil*)[5]
- **Symbolic animals**: The nimbed dove in the Oldenburg *Sachsenspiegel* representing the Holy Spirit inspiring Eike von Repgow, the *Sachsenspiegel*'s author[5]

This phase addresses: **"What does it mean?"** —uncovering symbolic codes that transform depicted objects into carriers of abstract legal concepts.[13]
Database fields supporting this level: image-internal/external text, person content names, symbolic location/region/country, person status, life dates, hyperlinks, keywords.[5]
### 4. Iconological Interpretation (Ultimate or Cultural Context)
The final level—sometimes treated as separate "iconology," sometimes integrated as advanced iconography—seeks to **"uncover the cultural, social, and historical background of themes in visual art and explain from this background why a particular theme was chosen by a particular person (artist or patron) in a particular place and time, and why this theme was represented in a particular manner"**.[14][13][5]
For **legal iconology** (*Rechtsikonologie*), this primarily aims to **illuminate the legal-historical and juridical background of themes (content) in legal images**. Questions include:[1][5]
- Why was a particular legal subject (e.g., personification/allegory of Justice, assembly of the Electoral College) selected in a specific temporal-spatial context?[1][5]
- Why was it represented in this manner rather than another?[1][5]
- What legal, religious, social, economic conditions shaped these representational choices?[5]
- What functions did these images serve—ordering (structuring texts), memorial (aiding memory), representational (displaying authority)?[10]

Database fields include: image-internal/external text, quotations, short quotations, additional information, literature references, hyperlinks.[5]
Van Straten notes that in practice, "a division into phases is often superfluous, and the boundaries between individual phases are in any case fluid". Database work confirms this: interpretive levels interpenetrate, and complete analysis requires moving iteratively between descriptive and interpretive modes.[5]
### Methodological Applications: The Hand as Legal Symbol
A concrete example from recent conference presentations illustrates the method's power. At the 2024 Heidelberg conference, **Gernot Kocher presented on "The Role of the Hand in Medieval Legal Life"**. He compared textual content in medieval charters from Styria with pictorial representations of legal acts from the same period, demonstrating that **the hand functioned as a multivalent legal symbol**.[10]
In medieval Germanic legal culture, hands signified:
- **Contractual agreement** (handshake, *Handschlag*)
- **Transfer of property** (symbolic handing over)
- **Oath-taking** (raised hand positions)
- **Judicial authority** (the glove suspended from market crosses representing the judge's delegated *Gerichtsgewalt* or judicial power)[5]
- **Testimony and witness** (specific finger configurations)

Kocher's iconological analysis revealed that the hand's symbolic density derived from its simultaneous materiality (actual body part performing legal acts) and abstraction (standing for concepts like fidelity, authority, agency). This exemplifies how legal iconology uncovers "deeper legal meanings embedded in symbolic elements" that pre-iconographic description alone cannot access.[5]
### Theoretical Underpinnings: Legal Semiotics and Symbolic Construction
Contemporary legal iconography increasingly integrates **legal semiotics**—the study of law through signs and symbols. This recognizes that **law is fundamentally symbolic construction**, with meaning emerging not from autonomous legal logic but through embeddedness in broader systems of signs, ideology, and everyday life.[15][16]
As Anne Wagner argues, "The law is a symbolic construction and therefore rests on a variety of undertakings. What gives law its meaning is, for some, ideology, for others, the welfare of the majority". Legal symbols—including allegorical personifications—do not possess fixed, inherent meanings but are **continuously reinterpreted through contextual factors**: who deploys them, in which media, for what purposes, to which audiences, within which power structures.[16][15]
This semiotic perspective proves essential for analyzing how **feminine allegorical personifications of Justice and Nation**—precisely your doctoral research focus—operate as **gendered technologies of legal-political communication**. These figures are not neutral representations of abstract concepts but ideologically charged symbols that naturalize particular relationships between law, power, embodiment, and gender. Legal iconology asks: Why feminine? Which qualities are attributed through feminization? How does gendered allegory shape who can access, interpret, and claim legal authority?
### Current State and Challenges (2020s)
Despite its established institutional infrastructure, legal iconography faces significant challenges. The Zürich database's internet-accessible version has been offline since approximately 2020–2021 with only a "maintenance notice" (*Wartungsmeldung*) displayed—a euphemistic placeholder suggesting institutional neglect rather than active maintenance. Colette Brunschwig, who conducted content management for over three decades, retired December 31, 2022, creating uncertainty about continued systematic development.[5]
Brunschwig's 2023 reflection articulates urgent concerns: the University of Zürich must recognize the database's immense value and provide necessary personnel, material, and financial resources for sustainable development. She advocates consulting specialists (database developers, interface designers, information scientists), partnering with modern legal database providers (Swisslex, Weblaw), and collaborating with digital humanities centers like Basel's Forschungsschwerpunkt Digital Humanities. Without such investment, this irreplaceable scholarly infrastructure risks obsolescence precisely when digital visualization of law intensifies.[5]
### Disciplinary Expansion Beyond Legal History (2001–Present)
A critical shift occurred around **2001** when the Bochum conference "Kommunikative Funktionen des Bildgebrauchs im Recht" (Communicative Functions of Image Use in Law), organized by Klaus F. Röhl, brought together scholars from diverse legal disciplines. Until then, legal iconography remained primarily a **legal history** subdiscipline practiced within the Arbeitskreis für Rechtsikonographie. The Bochum meeting demonstrated that "law and image" could and should be investigated from perspectives of multiple foundational legal fields (*rechtliche Grundlagenfächer*): legal history (iconography, iconology, archaeology), legal sociology, legal theory, legal pedagogy, and legal psychology.[5]
This interdisciplinary expansion connected historical legal iconography with emerging fields of **visual law**, **legal design**, and **legal visualization**—contemporary practices of creating visual legal communications (diagrams, flowcharts, icons, infographics) to enhance accessibility and comprehension. These connections position your historical research on legal-political iconography within broader contemporary debates about how law communicates visually across time, and how visual legal literacy functions as condition for meaningful access to justice.[17][18][19][5]
### Implications for Doctoral Research on Feminine Allegory
For your research on feminine allegories of Justice and Nation within a materialist-feminist framework, legal iconography's methodological apparatus provides essential tools:
1. **Systematic iconographic analysis** identifies conventional elements in representations across time and geography, revealing patterns, variations, and ruptures in how Justice and Nation were visualized.
2. **Iconological interpretation** situates these representations within specific legal-political contexts, asking why feminine personification emerged when and where it did, what ideological work this gendering performed, whose interests it served.
3. **Material-semiotic attention** to media and circulation patterns examines how allegories operated differently in courthouses versus printed legal texts versus coins versus revolutionary propaganda—recognizing that materiality co-produces meaning.
4. **Critical genealogy** traces not only continuities (Justice's scales, blindfold, sword) but also the **political archaeology** of how these symbols legitimated particular legal orders while naturalizing gendered hierarchies.

The German-speaking tradition's depth and methodological rigor, combined with critical feminist and materialist analytics, positions you to make significant contributions to understanding how legal-political iconography functions as gendered technology of governance—both historical phenomenon and ongoing contemporary practice requiring critical interrogation.

## Key Authors and Foundational Texts in Legal Iconography
Legal iconography has developed through contributions from scholars across three distinct generations: the founding pioneers (late 19th–mid-20th century), the institutionalizers (1970s–2000s), and contemporary theorists (2000s–present). Below is a comprehensive survey of the major figures and their essential works.
### Founding Generation: Germanic Legal Archaeology (1880s–1940s)
### **Karl von Amira (1848–1930)**
The acknowledged founder of *Rechtsarchäologie* (legal archaeology), von Amira pioneered the systematic collection and analysis of material legal culture—symbolic objects, ritual spaces, and visual representations. His work established that law could be studied not only through texts but through "Realien"—physical artifacts that embodied legal concepts and practices.[1][2][3]
**Major Works:**
- *Der Stab in der germanischen Rechtssymbolik* (The Staff in Germanic Legal Symbolism)—foundational study of legal objects[4]
- *Grundriss des germanischen Rechts* (Outline of Germanic Law)—comprehensive survey integrating visual and material sources
- *Atlas der Rechtsarchäologie*—planned pictorial atlas that was never completed; only the introduction was published posthumously by von Schwerin in 1943 as *Rechtsarchäologie: Gegenstände, Formen und Symbole des Germanischen Rechts*[3][1]

Von Amira assembled an extensive image collection at the Leopold-Wenger-Institut für Rechtsgeschichte in Munich, creating the oldest German legal archaeology collection. His methodological innovation was treating legal history as part of *Kulturgeschichte* (cultural history) rather than merely auxiliary to positive law.[5][3][4]
### **Claudius Freiherr von Schwerin (1880–1944)**
Von Amira's student who posthumously edited and published his teacher's materials, ensuring methodological continuity.[6][5][4]
**Major Work:**
- *Rechtsarchäologie: Gegenstände, Formen und Symbole des Germanischen Rechts* (1943, with Karl von Amira)—the foundational text establishing legal archaeology as systematic discipline[1]

**Critical Note:** Von Schwerin's Nazi affiliations after 1933 require contemporary scholars to approach his work critically, recognizing how political ideology may have shaped interpretations.[4]
### **Hans Fehr (1874–1961)**
Swiss scholar who specialized in representations of law in the arts—painting, sculpture, illuminated manuscripts.[5][4]
**Major Work:**
- *Das Recht im Bilde* (Law in Images, 1923)—pioneering study of how legal concepts appeared in visual art, establishing that images constituted independent historical sources for understanding legal consciousness[5]

### **Eberhard Georg Otto Freiherr von Künßberg (1881–1941)**
Focused on legal gestures, symbolic actions, and ritual performance. His image collection later became foundational holdings at the University of Zürich's legal iconography archive.[7][4]
### Second Generation: Institutionalization (1950s–2000s)
### **Karl Siegfried Bader (1905–1998)**
Professor of Swiss and German legal history at Zürich (1953–1975) who assembled the *Rechtsbildersammlung* (Legal Image Collection), integrating materials from von Künßberg and Hans Felix Pfenninger. His collection of approximately 15,000 images became Europe's largest legal image archive.[7]
### **Luis (Louis) Carlen (1919–1991)**
Swiss legal historian who contributed significantly to legal folklore studies (*rechtliche Volkskunde*) and legal iconography, particularly focusing on Swiss regional legal traditions.[8][5]
**Works:**
- *Rechtsgeschichte der Schweiz* (Legal History of Switzerland)—integrated visual and material culture analysis[9]
- Numerous studies on legal customs, pilgrimage, and symbolic practices in Swiss communities[8]

### **Gernot Kocher (1939–present)**
Austrian legal historian at Karl-Franzens-Universität Graz and co-founder (with Clausdieter Schott) of the **Arbeitskreis für Rechtsikonographie** in **1988**. Kocher established legal iconography as ongoing interdisciplinary field with regular conferences and publications.[10][11][12][4][5]
**Major Work:**
- ***Zeichen und Symbole des Rechts: Eine historische Ikonographie*** (Signs and Symbols of Law: A Historical Iconography, 1992)—the single most important comprehensive survey of legal iconography, featuring approximately 200 pages of visual documentation organized thematically. This work catalogs legal symbols across medieval and early modern Europe, providing systematic iconographic analysis of objects, gestures, personifications, and spatial arrangements.[13][14][5]

Kocher also developed the *Rechtsikonographische Datenbank* at Graz in the 1990s using AskSam software. He continues actively presenting research; his 2024 Heidelberg presentation examined "The Role of the Hand in Medieval Legal Life".[15][12][7]
**Editorial Role:** Co-editor (with Heiner Lück, Clausdieter Schott, later Andreas Deutsch) of the ***Signa Iuris*** journal series since 2008.[16][17][4][5]
### **Clausdieter Schott (1939–2023)**
Swiss legal historian at University of Zürich who succeeded Bader in 1975 and co-founded the Arbeitskreis with Kocher in 1988. Schott initiated the systematic digitization and iconographic-iconological cataloging of Bader's image collection in 1992, creating the *Rechtsbilddatenbank*.[11][12][10][7]
Schott's leadership established the infrastructure for sustained scholarly engagement with visual legal sources. Upon his retirement in 2004, Andreas Thier assumed stewardship of the database and the renamed Zentrum für Rechtsgeschichtliche Forschung. Schott's death in 2023 was commemorated by a special conference in Heidelberg organized by Andreas Deutsch and Heiner Lück.[18][15][7]
**Editorial Role:** Co-editor of ***Signa Iuris***.[17][16][4]
### **Heiner Lück (contemporary)**
Professor at Martin-Luther-Universität Halle-Wittenberg and major figure in contemporary German legal iconography. Lück focuses particularly on *Sachsenspiegel* studies and medieval German legal manuscripts.[18][15][5]
**Editorial Role:** Co-editor of ***Signa Iuris*** and co-organizer of biennial International Legal Iconography Conferences.[15][17][18]
### **Andreas Deutsch (contemporary)**
Professor at Ruprecht-Karls-Universität Heidelberg specializing in medieval legal history and iconography. Recent research examines illustrated legal texts like Ulrich Tengler's *Laienspiegel* (Laymen's Mirror), analyzing differences between editions (1509, 1511) to understand how visual programs structured legal knowledge.[18][15]
**Editorial Role:** Co-editor of ***Signa Iuris*** (succeeding Schott) and co-organizer of conferences.[17][18]
### Contemporary Generation: Visual Law and Legal Design (1990s–present)
### **Klaus F. Röhl (contemporary)**
German legal theorist and sociologist who significantly expanded legal iconography beyond purely historical concerns. Röhl's 2001 Bochum conference "Kommunikative Funktionen des Bildgebruchs im Recht" (Communicative Functions of Image Use in Law) brought together scholars from legal history, sociology, theory, pedagogy, and psychology, establishing that "law and image" constituted an interdisciplinary field spanning multiple legal disciplines.[19][20][21][22][7]
**Major Works:**
- *Bilder in historischen Rechtsbüchern* (Images in Historical Law Books)—examines medieval *jurisprudentia picturata* and its rupture in modernity[20][22]
- *Recht anschaulich: Visualisierung in der Juristenausbildung* (Law Visualized: Visualization in Legal Education, 2007, with Stefan Ulbrich)—addresses pedagogical applications[19]
- *Bildrhetorik* (Visual Rhetoric)—explores rhetorical functions of images in legal communication[21]

Röhl's key theoretical claim is that **"modern law is poor in images"** (*Das moderne Recht ist arm an Bildern*), marking a dramatic historical rupture from medieval visual legal culture. He argues that print culture and Protestant iconoclasm combined to produce modern law's "image abstinence" (*Bildabstinenz*), which contemporary visualization efforts must confront and overcome.[22][20]
### **Colette R. Brunschwig (contemporary)**
Senior researcher at University of Zürich and the preeminent theorist of contemporary **visual law** and **legal design**. Brunschwig managed the content development of the Zürich *Rechtsbilddatenbank* from July 1, 1992, through December 31, 2022—over 30 years of sustained iconographic analysis.[23][24][25][19][7]
**Major Works:**
- ***Visualisierung von Rechtsnormen – Legal Design*** (Visualization of Legal Norms – Legal Design, 2001)—doctoral dissertation that pioneered theoretical investigation of how modern legal norms can and should be visualized. This work established "legal design" as distinct scholarly domain.[24][26]
- "Visual Legal Communication Practices and Their Scholarly Exploration" (2014)—comprehensive survey distinguishing between **visual law** (law as visual phenomenon within and outside legal contexts) and **legal visualization** (practices of creating visual representations of legal content)[25][19]
- "Visual Law and Legal Design: Questions and Tentative Answers" (2021, with Rossana Ducato et al.)—synthesizes current research and methodological frameworks[27][28][29][23][7]

Brunschwig distinguishes three domains:[25]
5. **Legal visualization in the extra-legal domain** (visual legal culture: representations of law in art, literature, film, architecture)—the traditional domain of legal iconography and iconology
6. **Legal visualization in the legal framework** (diagrams, flowcharts, icons used by legal professionals)
7. **Legal design** (human-centered design applied to legal communication and systems)

Her research increasingly addresses **audiovisualization and multisensorization** of legal content, recognizing that law manifests not only visually but through multiple sensory modalities.[30][24]
### **Peter Goodrich (contemporary)**
Professor at Cardozo School of Law (New York) and leading scholar of legal emblems, visual legal semiotics, and the art-and-law movement.[31][32][5]
**Major Work:**
- ***Legal Emblems and the Art of Law: Obiter Depicta as the Vision of Governance*** (Cambridge University Press, 2014)—groundbreaking study establishing that the emblem book tradition (invented by humanist lawyer Andrea Alciato in 1531) was fundamentally a **juridical genre**. Goodrich argues that emblems depicted norms, infractions, obedience, and error in classically derived forms that became visual standards of governance. The book introduces the concept of ***obiter depicta***—"things seen along the way to judgment"—as the visual counterpart to *obiter dicta*.[32][31]

Goodrich's work is essential for understanding how early modern legal culture deployed visual rhetoric and how contemporary lawyers might recover critical apprehension of the visible.[31][32]
### **Anne Wagner (contemporary)**
Legal semiotics scholar examining how law operates as symbolic construction. Her work emphasizes that legal meaning emerges not from autonomous legal logic but through embeddedness in broader systems of signs, ideology, and everyday life.[33][34]
**Major Work:**
- *Contemporary Issues of the Semiotics of Law* (2005)—theoretical survey establishing legal semiotics as framework for understanding legal iconography[33]

Recent work addresses digital legal semiotics—how emojis, digital symbols, and platform-mediated communication transform legal interpretation and evidence.[34]
### Key Journals and Publication Series
### ***Signa Iuris – Beiträge zur Rechtsikonographie, Rechtsarchäologie und rechtlichen Volkskunde***
Founded in 2008, this is the primary journal series for legal iconography research. Edited by Gernot Kocher, Heiner Lück, Clausdieter Schott (later Andreas Deutsch), the series publishes conference proceedings and monographic studies. The title *Signa Iuris* (Signs of Law) deliberately invokes the field's semiotic dimensions.[16][4][17][5]
Multiple volumes have been published documenting conference presentations and specialized studies. The series represents the institutionalized continuation of the research tradition established by von Amira.[16][5]
### ***Rechtsgeschichte – Legal History***
Journal of the Max Planck Institute for Legal History and Legal Theory (formerly Max Planck Institute for European Legal History) that regularly publishes articles on legal iconography, particularly addressing methodological and digital humanities dimensions.[30]
### Essential Foundational Texts: Summary
For doctoral research in legal iconography, the **essential foundational readings** are:
8. **Karl von Amira & Claudius von Schwerin, *****Rechtsarchäologie: Gegenstände, Formen und Symbole des Germanischen Rechts***** (1943)**—establishes methodological foundations[1]
9. **Hans Fehr, *****Das Recht im Bilde***** (1923)**—pioneering study of law in visual art[5]
10. **Gernot Kocher, *****Zeichen und Symbole des Rechts: Eine historische Ikonographie***** (1992)**—comprehensive iconographic survey, the single most important reference work[14][13][5]
11. **Peter Goodrich, *****Legal Emblems and the Art of Law: Obiter Depicta as the Vision of Governance***** (2014)**—essential for understanding early modern visual legal culture and emblem tradition[32][31]
12. **Colette R. Brunschwig, "Visual Legal Communication Practices and Their Scholarly Exploration" (2014)** and **"Visual Law and Legal Design: Questions and Tentative Answers" (2021)**—key theoretical frameworks distinguishing historical iconography from contemporary legal design[29][23][19][25]
13. **Klaus F. Röhl, *****Bilder in historischen Rechtsbüchern***—explains rupture between medieval visual legal culture and modern "image-poor" law[20][22]

### Implications for Feminine Allegory Research
For your doctoral work on feminine allegories of Justice and Nation, these authors provide complementary resources:
- **Kocher's *****Zeichen und Symbole*** offers systematic iconographic documentation of *Justitia* and related personifications across periods and regions[13][14]
- **Goodrich's emblem studies** reveal how gendered allegorical figures functioned in early modern governance and legal pedagogy[31][32]
- **Brunschwig's visual law frameworks** help situate historical allegories within broader theories of how law communicates visually and to whom[19][25]
- **Röhl's analyses of medieval manuscripts** provide context for understanding when and why legal personifications emerged as pedagogical and mnemonic devices[22][20]

The institutional infrastructure—particularly the Zürich *Rechtsbilddatenbank*, *Signa Iuris* series, and biennial conferences—provides ongoing scholarly community and methodological resources essential for rigorous iconographic-iconological analysis of gendered legal-political symbolism.
