# **Justice Vandalized: Tropical Iconoclasm and the Crisis of the Oikonomia**

## **1\. Introduction: The Event as Epistemological Rupture**

The events of January 8, 2023, in Brasília, marked a decisive rupture in the political and visual history of the Brazilian Republic. The storming of the *Praça dos Três Poderes* (Plaza of the Three Powers) by a radicalized mob was not merely an insurrection against the electoral result; it was a violent assault on the "economy of the image" (*oikonomia*) that sustains the legitimacy of the democratic state.1 When rioters ascended the ramp of the Supreme Federal Court (STF), smashed the glass façade, and defaced the monumental sculpture *A Justiça* (The Justice) by Alfredo Ceschiatti, they were engaging in a form of "Tropical Iconoclasm"—a theological-political struggle over who possesses the power to make truth visible.1

This report investigates the January 8 attacks through a rigorous theoretical lens, synthesizing legal history, art history, and political theology. It posits that the violence cannot be understood solely through the frameworks of security failure or political polarization. Instead, it must be read as a clash between two opposing regimes of visibility: the "administrative economy" of the democratic state, which relies on the mediation of the *eikon* (icon) to represent authority, and the rising "iconocracy" of the digital mob, which demands the "total visibility" of the *idol* and rejects the "enigma" of institutional judgment.1

By integrating forensic data from the *Lacorpi* restoration project—the state’s effort to heal the material wounds of the building—with the iconological methods of Aby Warburg and Marie-José Mondzain, this research offers a comprehensive "Anatomy of a Riot." It traces the "biography of destruction" of specific legal artifacts: the blinding of the *Themis* statue, the uprooting of the Zalszupin chairs, the theft of the Coat of Arms, and the slashing of the modernist masterpiece *As Mulatas*.1

Crucially, this report argues that the violence was enabled by a specific form of "Legal Alienation" manifesting as "Negative Legal Literacy." The rioters possessed a sophisticated, albeit pathological, visual competence. They knew exactly which symbols embodied the power they sought to dismantle. They did not attack the cafeteria or the garage with the same ferocity reserved for the Plenary and the Statue. They engaged in a "war on images," driven by the magical belief that destroying the representation of the state would cause the collapse of the state itself.1 To understand this phenomenon, we must first excavate the deep theoretical foundations of how the state makes itself visible, and how that visibility has been constructed—and compromised—throughout the history of the Brazilian Republic.

## **2\. Theoretical Framework: The Warburg-Mondzain Axis**

The analysis operates on a theoretical axis connecting the "Iconology of the Interval" developed by Aby Warburg with the "Political Theology of the Image" elaborated by Marie-José Mondzain. This framework allows us to move beyond a superficial reading of the vandalism as mere property damage and understand it as a structural attack on the metaphysics of sovereignty.

### **2.1 The *Oikonomia*: The Administrative Economy of the Visible**

The central concept guiding this inquiry is *oikonomia*. Drawing on her extensive analysis of the Byzantine iconoclastic controversy (8th–9th centuries) and the Patristic tradition, Marie-José Mondzain defines *oikonomia* not in the modern financial sense, but as an "administrative economy of the visible".3 In Christian theology, the *oikonomia* refers to the management of the relationship between the invisible God and his visible incarnation (Christ) and the subsequent dissemination of that truth through the Church (the institution).

In the secularized theology of the modern republic, the *oikonomia* functions as the system that manages the relationship between an invisible authority—"The People," "The Constitution," or "The Law"—and its visible manifestations—the Judge, the Robe, the Gavel, and the Monument.1 The state cannot exist as a pure abstraction; it must appear. However, it cannot appear in its totality without overwhelming the subject. Therefore, it requires an "economy," a regulated distribution of visibility that mediates power.

The Supreme Federal Court (STF) functions as the "economist" of the Republic.1 Its architecture, rituals, and symbols are designed to produce a specific type of visibility: one that is transparent yet distant, accessible yet sacred. The legitimacy of the court depends on the successful operation of this *oikonomia*. The symbols it deploys—the statue of *A Justiça*, the Coat of Arms—must function as *eikons* (relational images) that lead the viewer from the visible object to the invisible authority of the Law.4

### **2.2 The *Eikon* and the Necessity of *Kenosis***

For the *oikonomia* to be democratic, the image must possess the quality of the *eikon*. Unlike the *idol*, which traps the gaze in the materiality of the object and refers only to itself, the *eikon* is transitive. It creates a relation. It points beyond itself. Crucially, Mondzain argues that the operation of the *eikon* requires *kenosis*—a theological term meaning "emptying" or "self-restraint".3

In the context of legal authority, *kenosis* means that the image of power must "withdraw" slightly to allow for interpretation. The judge wears a uniform to empty himself of his private personality and become the vessel of the Law. The statue of Justice is blindfolded not to indicate ignorance, but to indicate a withdrawal from the immediate visual field of passion and bias. This withdrawal creates an "interval" or "enigma"—a space where judgment, debate, and mediation can occur.1

The democratic citizen is one who can read the *eikon*. They possess the "Legal Literacy" to understand that the statue is not a god to be worshipped, but a symbol to be respected as a placeholder for a shared agreement. They understand that the judge is not a king, but a temporary occupant of the *Cathedra*.

### **2.3 The Rise of the *Iconocracy***

Opposed to the administrative economy of the *oikonomia* is the regime of the "Iconocracy".1 Mondzain identifies *iconocracy* as a system where the image seizes power for itself. It is the regime of the *idol*. In an iconocracy, the image no longer refers to a transcendent truth or an institutional process; it refers only to its own visibility and the raw exercise of force.

The January 8 insurrection was driven by an iconocratic logic. The rioters, immersed in the digital sphere of social media, memes, and algorithmic radicalization, have been habituated to a regime of "total visibility." In the digital iconocracy, there is no *kenosis*, no withdrawal, and no mediation. Everything must be seen, instantly and totally. The "enigma" of the STF—its complex procedures, its hermeneutic authority—was viewed not as a necessary function of justice, but as an intolerable opacity concealing a conspiracy.1

The mob sought to replace the *oikonomia* (mediation through law) with the *iconocracy* (immediacy of the will). They attacked the STF because its very existence as a mediating institution threatened the fantasy of direct, unmediated power promised by authoritarian populism. By storming the building, breaking the glass, and sitting in the chairs, they were attempting to eliminate the "interval" between the People and Power. They wanted to become the image, rather than relate to it.

### **2.4 Legal Literacy as Visual Competence**

This theoretical clash necessitates a redefinition of "Legal Literacy." Traditionally, legal literacy is understood as the textual competence to read statutes and understand rights.2 However, for the vast majority of historical actors—from the illiterate peasant of the 19th century to the "bestialized" masses of the 21st—the law is not encountered as text. It is encountered as image, space, and object.2

This report proposes a tripartite definition of legal literacy:

1. **Textual Competence:** Mastery of codes and statutes (Lawyers/Elites).  
2. **Infrastructural Competence:** The ability to navigate institutions and procedures (Bureaucrats/Litigants).  
3. **Visual/Symbolic Competence:** The "background knowledge" of authority; the capacity to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material cues (The Public).2

The rioters of January 8 possessed a form of "Negative Legal Literacy" or "Magical Literacy".1 They were not ignorant of the symbols; on the contrary, they were hyper-aware of them. They knew that the Coat of Arms was the certifier of the space. They knew the Chair was the seat of power. Their illiteracy lay in their inability to distinguish the *symbol* from the *thing itself*. They believed that by destroying the *Sphragis* (Seal), they were legally "decertifying" the government. This magical thinking is a hallmark of the iconocratic mind, which collapses the distance between the signifier and the signified.

## **3\. Genealogy of the Brazilian Imaginary: The Visual Sexual Contract**

To understand the specific targets and violence of January 8—particularly the gendered violence against the statue of Justice—we must excavate the historical foundations of the Brazilian legal imaginary. The Republic, proclaimed in 1889, was built on a fragile "Visual Sexual Contract" that has haunted its institutions ever since.

### **3.1 The Crisis of Representation (1789–1889)**

The transition from monarchy to republic creates a profound crisis of representation. As Ernst Kantorowicz famously articulated in *The King’s Two Bodies*, the monarch possessed both a "body natural" (mortal) and a "body politic" (eternal). The King *was* the Law. His physical presence anchored the legal order.2

The republican revolutions of the 18th and 19th centuries severed this link. They decapitated the "body natural" of the sovereign, creating a terrifying representational vacuum. Who, or what, would represent the abstraction of "The People" or "The Law"? You cannot shake hands with the General Will. You cannot bow to the Constitution. The state required a face to be loved, feared, and obeyed.2

The solution found by the Atlantic revolutions—and imported by Brazil—was the female allegory. This choice was strategic. As Marina Warner and Maurice Agulhon argue, a male figure represents a *ruler* (a specific king or general), whereas a female figure, historically excluded from power, can represent an *ideal* (Liberty, Justice, Republic) without threatening to usurp the actual political power held by men.6

### **3.2 The Brazilian Experience: Positivism and the "Mother-Humanity"**

Brazil’s entry into this visual economy was unique due to the overwhelming influence of Comtian Positivism. Following the military coup that ousted Emperor Pedro II in 1889, the new Republic needed to visualize itself urgently. The populace, described by José Murilo de Carvalho in *Os Bestializados*, watched the proclamation "bewildered," excluded from the political process.1

To bridge this gap, intellectuals like Raimundo Teixeira Mendes and artists like Décio Villares crafted the *Alegoria da República*.6 They fused the Jacobin symbols of the French Republic (the Phrygian cap) with the Positivist ideal of the "Mother-Humanity." In the Positivist theology of the "Religion of Humanity," Woman was worshipped as the moral center of the family and the spiritual anchor of the state.

However, this visual elevation masked a brutal legal reality. The "Visual Sexual Contract" functioned as a technology of compensation and misdirection. While the Republic was depicted as a Sovereign Woman on coins, stamps, and statues, actual Brazilian women were codified as "relatively incapable" in the Civil Code of 1916 (Law 3.071).6 Drafted by Clóvis Beviláqua, the Code placed married women in the same category as "prodigals" and "silvicolas" (indigenous peoples), requiring marital authorization for basic civil acts.

### **3.3 The Paradox of the Step-Mother State**

This created a "schizophrenic" visual order. The citizen was taught to look up to the "Mother Republic" as the source of rights and morality, while the state simultaneously stripped actual mothers of their civil capacity. The symbols of the Republic—including the *Themis* of the courts—functioned as *typos* (imprints from above) rather than true *eikons* of shared sovereignty.1

For the marginalized populations of Brazil, this allegory often read not as a "Mother" but as a "Step-Mother"—a distant, white, Europeanized figure that promised justice but delivered exclusion. The January 8 insurrection must be read against this long history of "Legal Alienation." When the rioters attacked the statue of Justice, they were not just attacking a neutral symbol; they were attacking the "false idol" of a Republic that had never fully included them in its *oikonomia*. They were rejecting the "Visual Sexual Contract" that asked them to worship an image of Justice that concealed a reality of power.

## **4\. The Architecture of Authority: Brasília and the Modernist *Oikonomia***

The stage for the January 8 drama was Brasília, a city designed as a "total work of art" (*Gesamtkunstwerk*) to manifest the rationality and progress of the Brazilian state. The architecture of the Supreme Federal Court (STF) by Oscar Niemeyer is a critical component of the "administrative economy" that was targeted.

### **4.1 The Transparent Temple**

Niemeyer’s design for the STF is characterized by "transparency, verticality, and silence".1 The building is a glass box, suspended by sweeping white columns. This transparency was intended to symbolize a democratic justice that is open to the people, having "nothing to hide."

However, this transparency is part of the *oikonomia*’s "mystery." It is a curated visibility. The citizen can *see* the movement of the justices inside, but they cannot *touch* them. The glass creates a boundary that is both visible and impermeable. It establishes the "interval" necessary for judgment. The building functions as a "Temple," a sacred space separated from the profane square by the ramp and the glass.1

### **4.2 The *Cathedra* and the Hierarchy of Judgment**

Inside the Plenary, the "administrative economy" is managed through furniture and spatial arrangement. The focal point is the *Cathedra*—the high-backed chairs designed by Jorge Zalszupin for the Justices.1 In legal and ecclesiastical tradition, the chair is the metonym for authority. It physically elevates the judge above the litigants and the public.

This verticality is essential to the STF’s regime of visibility. It signals that the judgment comes "from above," mediated by the Constitution and the institution. The Zalszupin chairs, with their modernist elegance and imposing stature, are the material support for this hierarchy. They are the *Sedes Materiae* (Seat of Matter) of the law.

### **4.3 The *Sphragis*: The Seal of the State**

Hanging behind the Justices is the massive Coat of Arms (*Brasão da República*). In the semiotics of law, the Coat of Arms acts as the *Sphragis* (Seal).1 It is the certifying mark that transforms the speech of the judge into the "Voice of the State." It represents the "Word and Seal" of sovereignty.1 Without the Seal, the room is just a room; with the Seal, it is a Court.

## **5\. Anatomy of a Riot: The January 8 Iconoclasm**

On January 8, 2023, the *Oikonomia* of Brasília collided with the *Iconocracy* of the mob. The insurrection was a "Tropical Iconoclasm" that followed a specific liturgical logic of desecration.

### **5.1 The Logic of the Mob: From *Eikon* to *Idol***

The rioters did not view the STF as a legitimate political adversary; they viewed it as a theological enemy. In the logic of the *Iconocracy*, the symbols of the opposing power are not *eikons* (to be respected) but *idols* (to be destroyed). The *idol* is dangerous because it exercises a false fascination; it traps the soul. Therefore, the *idol* must be physically broken to break its spell.7

The mob reclassified the symbols of the judiciary—the Coat of Arms, the Statue, the Chair—from "relational icons" into "oppressive idols".1 This reclassification authorized the violence. It transformed vandalism into a ritual of purification.

### **5.2 Digital Iconocracy vs. Analog Authority**

The riot was fueled by the "digital iconocracy." The rioters were live-streaming their actions, taking selfies, and creating content for the algorithmic feed. This behavior is crucial. In the *oikonomia*, the subject gazes at the image of authority in silence. In the *iconocracy*, the subject *becomes* the image. By placing their own faces in the frame of the destroyed Plenary, the rioters were inserting themselves into the seat of power.1

This demand for "total visibility" led to the shattering of Niemeyer’s glass façades. The "enigma" of the court—the fact that it holds secrets and deliberations—was intolerable to the iconocratic mind. Breaking the glass was an attempt to expose the "black box" of the judiciary and subject it to the direct, unmediated gaze of the "people" (the mob).1

### **5.3 Magical Literacy and Decertification**

The rioters demonstrated "Negative Legal Literacy" or "Magical Literacy." They believed that the legitimacy of the state resided *in the objects themselves*. They stole the Coat of Arms not as a souvenir, but as a capture of sovereignty. They believed that by possessing the *Sphragis*, they possessed the power to "decertify" the court.1

This reveals a profound "Legal Alienation." The rioters had no "Institutional Literacy"—they did not understand that the court exists in the laws and the gazette, not in the furniture. But they had high "Symbolic Competence"—they knew exactly which objects mattered. They did not burn the cafeteria; they burned the symbols.2

## **6\. Case Studies in Material Destruction: A Biography of Violence**

The "biography of destruction" reconstructed by the *Lacorpi* project allows us to analyze the specific meanings of the violence directed at four key artifacts: the Statue, the Chair, the Seal, and the Painting.

### **6.1 Case Study I: *A Justiça* – The Blinded and Captioned Goddess**

The most iconic image of the day was the defacement of Alfredo Ceschiatti’s *A Justiça* (1961), the granite sculpture sitting before the STF.

The Iconography of the Missing Scales:  
Ceschiatti’s Justice is a modernist radicalization of the classical Themis. She is blindfolded and holds a sword resting on her lap, but crucially, she lacks the scales.2 In traditional iconography, the scales represent deliberation, the weighing of evidence, the "intellectual" moment of judgment. The sword represents execution, the "force" of law.  
By removing the scales, Ceschiatti created a figure that is "all execution." She is a Justice who has already decided, or whose weighing is internal and invisible. To the iconoclastic eye of the mob, this abstraction was a provocation. It represented a Justice that is arbitrary, blind to their suffering, and armed only with force. She was not the "Weigher of Souls" but the "Blind Executioner."  
The "Perdeu, Mané" Graffiti:  
Rioters climbed the statue and wrote "Perdeu, mané" ("You lost, fool") across her chest in red.2 This was a "profane captioning" of the eikon.

* **Ventriloquism:** The phrase was a direct quote from STF Justice Luís Roberto Barroso, spoken to a heckler months prior.2 By inscribing these partisan, dismissive words onto the universal allegorical body, the rioters collapsed the "Two Bodies of the Law." They forced the "Body Natural" of the hated judge (Barroso) to merge with the "Body Politic" of the statue (Justice). They claimed that the statue was a lie: she is not Universal Justice; she is Barroso, and she is mocking us.2  
* **Counter-Literacy:** This was an act of "counter-literacy." The rioters read the statue against the grain of the state’s intention. They stripped her of her silence and forced her to speak the vulgar language of the political brawl.  
* **Gendered Violence:** The use of lipstick (referenced in some reports) or red marker on the breast of the female allegory reenacted the "Visual Sexual Contract." It was a violent reclaiming of the "Step-Mother" state by the "sons" of the republic, inscribing their rage onto the body that claimed to rule them.2

### **6.2 Case Study II: The *Cathedra* – Uprooting the Hierarchy**

Inside the Plenary, the mob targeted the Zalszupin chairs.

The Act of Un-Seating:  
Forensic reports describe the "uprooting of the Cathedra".1 The rioters physically tore the chairs from the floor. This was a direct attack on the vertical hierarchy of judgment.  
The oikonomia relies on the vertical elevation of the judge. By tearing out the chairs, the rioters were engaging in a ritual of "leveling." They sought to reduce the hierarchical space of the court to a horizontal plane where the "people" and the "judge" are equal.1  
The chairs were then used to build barricades. This transformed the "seat of judgment" into a "tool of war." It was the ultimate functional inversion of the object: from a symbol of stability and order to an instrument of chaos and defense.8

### **6.3 Case Study III: The *Sphragis* – Theft of the Coat of Arms**

The theft of the Coat of Arms from the Plenary wall was the supreme act of "Magical Literacy."

Decertifying the Space:  
The Coat of Arms contains the Sphragis (Seal) of the Republic. By removing it, the rioters believed they were decertifying the space.1 They were stripping the room of its legal soul.  
Videos show rioters parading with the Coat of Arms like a war trophy. This was an attempt to transfer the oikonomia to themselves. They were claiming that they now held the Seal, and therefore they were the State. It was a literal enactment of the "sovereign people" reclaiming the certifying power from the "corrupt" institution.1

### **6.4 Case Study IV: *As Mulatas* – Slashing the Racial Contract**

The destruction extended to the cultural identity of the nation. Di Cavalcanti’s mural *As Mulatas* (1962), depicting mixed-race Brazilian women in a festive, tropical modernist style, was slashed and stabbed seven times.2

The War on Modernism:  
This attack must be read through the lens of the "Visual Racial Contract." The "Iconocracy" of the Brazilian far-right is often aligned with a reactionary aesthetic that rejects the "mongrel" (mestiço) identity of modern Brazil in favor of a rigid, often white-supremacist or religious purity.  
As Mulatas represents the "Modernist Project"—the idea of Brazil as a progressive, mixed-race, tropical civilization. The rioters, looking backward to a mythical conservative past, attacked this "degenerate art." The stabbing of the painting was an attempt to kill the "body" of the multicultural nation it represented.2 It was a rejection of the "Tropical" in "Tropical Iconoclasm."

## **7\. Restoration as Re-Consecration: The *Lacorpi* Project**

The state’s response to the insurrection was the launch of the *Lacorpi* restoration project.1 This project is not merely technical; it is a theological-political operation to restore the *oikonomia*.

### **7.1 From Temple to Museum of Survival**

A central debate within the Lacorpi project was whether to erase the damage completely or to preserve the "scars." The decision to leave traces of the violence on certain objects transforms the STF. It moves from being a "Temple" (a timeless space of eternal truth) to a "Museum of Survival" (a historical space that has survived trauma).1  
This shift acknowledges the rupture. It admits that the oikonomia was broken and had to be reconstituted. The scars serve as a new eikon—a reminder of the fragility of the law and the persistence of the institution.

### **7.2 The Ritual of Cleaning**

However, for the statue of A Justiça, the restoration was immediate and total. The graffiti "Perdeu, mané" had to be erased. The state could not allow the "profane caption" to remain on the goddess. To leave the words would be to allow the Iconocracy (the partisan voice) to permanently inhabit the Eikon.  
The cleaning was a ritual of "re-silencing." The eikon must be silent to function. It must return to its kenosis. By washing away the red paint, the state re-established the "interval" between the judge and the public, reclaiming the statue for the universal order.2

## **8\. Conclusion: The Fragility of the Visual Contract**

The January 8 insurrection was a seminal event in the history of the Latin American legal imaginary. It revealed the extreme fragility of the "Visual Sexual Contract" and the "Republican *Oikonomia*" that have sustained the Brazilian state since 1889\.

The rioters, armed with a "Negative Legal Literacy" and driven by the logic of the "Iconocracy," successfully identified and attacked the nodes of the state’s visual infrastructure. They blinded the Goddess, unseated the Judge, stole the Seal, and slashed the Nation. They did this because they no longer believed in the "enigma" of the institution. They demanded the "total visibility" of the *idol*.

The "Tropical Iconoclasm" was not a random outburst; it was the inevitable collision between a 19th-century visual system (built on allegories that masked exclusion) and a 21st-century digital mob (habituated to immediacy and transparency).

The *Lacorpi* project has repaired the objects, but the "background knowledge" that allows the citizen to see a statue and feel "Justice" remains fractured. The challenge for the future is not just to restore the furniture, but to reconstruct the imaginary. The state must find a way to move from the *typos* of imposed authority to a new *eikon* of shared recognition—one that can survive the gaze of the Iconocracy without being reduced to an Idol.
