Modern legal and state authority has never been sustained solely by normative texts, but by a dense regime of visual representations that organize belief, affection, and consent. Among these, allegorical figures, particularly female personifications such as Justice, Liberty, Republic, Nation, and Victory, occupied a central and persistent role in the iconography of Western states during the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. By examining comparative iconographic corpora from selected Western states, the study asks how allegorical femininity operates as a constitutive aesthetic of authority, transforming exclusion into virtue and sovereignty into a figure with a woman's face.

## Additional Theoretical Resources to Explore
Concepts and authors that can strengthen or expand your analysis:
### Simulacrum and Hyperreality (Jean Baudrillard)
Baudrillard's simulacrum offers a lens for understanding how female allegories produce a political reality that precedes and replaces the real referent. The allegory does not represent real women; it generates a state femininity that is more real than the real, a hyper-real that organizes perception before experience. Application: coins and stamps do not imitate an existing female Republic; they produce it as an effect of material circulation.
### Dialectic of Recognition (G.W.F. Hegel)
The Hegelian dialectic of master and slave, and above all the economy of recognition, allows us to think of the female allegory as a figure that grants symbolic recognition without political reciprocity. The state recognizes itself through the female allegory, but the allegory never recognizes itself as an autonomous subject. Application: allegorical hypervisibility functions as recognition without subjectivation, offering presence without agency.
### Forms of the Visible and Scopic Regimes (various authors)
The notion of scopic regimes (Martin Jay, Jonathan Crary) and the economy of forms of the visible allows us to situate iconocracy within a long history of technologies of seeing and being seen. Each iconocratic regime (foundational, normative, military) mobilizes a distinct way of organizing the collective gaze. Application: coins produce a regime of ubiquitous and intimate visibility; monuments produce verticality and permanence; courts produce ritual theatricality.
### Purity and Danger (Mary Douglas)
Douglas' anthropology of pollution, symbolic order, and bodily limits is central to understanding why the allegorical female body must remain pure, idealized, disembodied. Allegory functions as a device of purification: it extracts from real femininity its materiality, sexuality, mortality, and converts it into an abstract and aseptic form. Application: allegories never age, menstruate, give birth, or die. They function as bodies purified of everything that threatens the patriarchal symbolic order.
### Other resources to consider
- Walter Benjamin: Baroque allegory, the origin of German tragic drama, and the distinction between symbol and allegory as modes of temporality.
- Jacques Rancière: sharing of the sensible, aesthetic dissent, and how aesthetic regimes distribute places and voices.
- Judith Butler: gender performativity applied to allegorical performativity; how ritual repetition produces state femininity.
- Giorgio Agamben: state of exception and bare life; allegories as figures of sovereignty that inhabit the threshold between zoe and bios.

The central problem addressed by this research concerns the paradox of the modern legal order's dependency on images it cannot fully control. While the state relies on the »translation of knowledge of normativity« through visual means to secure obedience and legitimacy, these visual signifiers are susceptible to acts of "counter-literacy" that expose the gap between the abstract promise of law and its material reality. This problem is particularly acute in the Brazilian context, where the importation of European allegories and the imposition of modernist abstractions have created a fragile "economy of images" or, as termed in this investigation, a Tropical Iconocracy. The insurrection of January 8 reveals a new dimension of this problem: the emergence of Fetishized Legal Literacy. In a grotesque parody of legal formalism, the rioters did not merely destroy; they engaged in a ritualistic appropriation of legal artifacts. The theft of a replica of the 1988 Constitution by a rioter draped in the national flag serves as the prime exemplar. This act was not a rejection of law, but a claim to a "higher," divinely sanctioned legality. The replica, devoid of juridical force, was elevated to the status of a sacred relic, while the actual institution of the Supreme Federal Court was desecrated as demonic. This process of fetishization transmutes the legal object into a talismanic artifact; it divests the replica of its rational normative function to reinvest it with a pneumatic, theologico-political efficacy that supersedes positive law. Furthermore, a specific gendered contradiction underpins this visual economy. The proliferation of the female body as the primary interface of the state—Justice, the Republic, the Nation—occurs precisely contemporaneous with the systematic exclusion of actual women from political agency and legal capacity. This dissonance suggests that the female allegory functions not merely as a symbol, but as a mechanism of political exclusion and substitution. The vandalism of 8 January 2023, particularly the defacement of the female allegory by a female iconoclast (Débora Rodrigues), forces a re-examination of this gendered logic. The act of marking the statue with lipstick or red pigment reenacts a patriarchal violence, functioning within the logic of 'Spiritual Warfare' to reduce the 'universal mother' to a 'whore' of the partisan state—a secular Babylon—thus exposing the inherent theological fragility of the Visual Sexual Contract. 2.2.
