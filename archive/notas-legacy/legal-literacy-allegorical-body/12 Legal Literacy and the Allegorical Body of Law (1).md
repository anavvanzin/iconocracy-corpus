**Legal Literacy and the Allegorical Body of Law: Female Personifications as Conduits and Masks of Legal Knowledge (1789–1945)**[^1]

This project debates legal literacy by treating it as infrastructural and visual competence: the historically formed capacity to recognise, trust, and navigate legality through images, spaces, and material cues, alongside textual rules. Between 1789 and 1945, modern legal orders presented themselves as impersonal and code-based, yet they simultaneously relied on dense iconographic systems, across coins, stamps, monuments, courthouse architecture, seals, and legal documents, through which sovereignty and adjudication became publicly legible. Female figures of Justice, Liberty, the Republic, and the Nation condensed constitutional claims into stable attributes and postures, producing recognisability at a glance and shaping everyday expectations about what law promises, authorises, and forbids.  
Precisely when women were constrained by civil incapacity, political disenfranchisement, and professional exclusion, the feminine body proliferated as the official face of law and nation.  This is the root where the project puts forward a critical diagnosis. Specifically, concurrently with the societal imposition of civil incapacity, political disenfranchisement, and professional exclusion upon women, the feminine form became increasingly pervasive as the official representation of both law and nation. This proliferation is politically compelling because it fuses two powerful imaginaries: the nation as a moral community and the nation as a lineage. The allegorical woman invites attachment by echoing a maternal grammar in which the polity appears as something that can be protected, loved, and reproduced, even as women’s reproductive and social labour was organised as a condition of the state’s future. Within nineteenth-century gender discourse, women were repeatedly positioned as moral incubators of coming citizens, guardians of sentiment and virtue whose authority was acknowledged precisely insofar as it remained displaced from office, adjudication, and command.  
I conceptualise this configuration as a visual sexual contract, developed in critical dialogue with Carole Pateman’s The Sexual Contract, a regime of symbolic hypervisibility that stabilises legitimacy by offering recognition without reciprocity in rights, office, or juridical authority. Allegorical femininity thus operates simultaneously as a conduit of legal knowledge and as an ideological mask. It humanises abstract universality, naturalises belonging through a feminised figure of origin, and distances legality from concrete decision-makers while leaving the gendered distribution of power intact.  
Methodologically, the project develops an iconographic reconstruction protocol for legal history and produces, as its core output, a comparative typology of iconographic grammars and their gendered operations. Through a comparative design centred on France, Germany, Britain, Switzerland, and Brazil, the project’s empirical payoff is to show how similar allegorical solutions travel across divergent regime types, how they stabilise in everyday media and institutional space, and how moments of rupture and contestation disclose the work these images perform in manufacturing modern legal legitimacy. Although the corpus ends in 1945, the analysis isolates a durable mechanism of legal visibility that remains instructive for interpreting image-saturated legal communication in the present.

	1\. **Introduction:** Embodiment, allegory, and legal literacy  
The formation of the modern nation state during the long nineteenth century constituted a profound ontological crisis for both the jurist and the artist. As the feudal bonds of personal allegiance to a monarch dissolved, they were replaced by the abstraction of the state, a political entity that required a new metaphysical foundation to sustain its legitimacy.[^2]   
The late eighteenth-century revolutions dismantled personalised royal sovereignty, yet a political demand endured: law still had to appear in a body. The transition to legal modernity followed what Paolo Grossi calls the scythe of equality, cutting through layered jurisdictions, corporate privileges, and inherited statuses. In its place emerged a "socially naked" subject, abstracted into a juridical person rather than a concrete body of flesh and blood. Once the king's body could no longer anchor authority in view, post-revolutionary regimes faced a representational vacuum. "The people", the “nation”, and the Constitution could be declared sovereign. Declarations alone, however, do not train perception. They do not teach actors how authority looks in the street, in the courtroom, or in the ledger. Revolution did not abolish embodiment. It was redistributed. That redistribution creates the space in which allegory becomes a practical pedagogy of legal literacy, culminating in Justice as the figure through which law is learned and read as what's lawful and right.  
Female allegory emerged as an especially adaptable response because “she” could carry the weight of sovereignty without claiming it as a person. Marianne and Columbia offered a human figure emptied of individual biography, a face for abstract legality able to circulate widely while remaining politically non-competitive. This matters for legal history because allegory is a compressed argument. Lady Justice with scales and sword offers a miniature theory of adjudication: disputes should be weighed; judgment should bite; force should appear as legitimate consequence rather than brute violence. The latter blindfold adds another claim, impartiality as a posture, and it also produces a methodological problem, because blindness can be performed as fairness while institutions continue to perceive class, gender, property, reputation, accent, and address.   
Justice is an extensive concept, recognizable by its iconic blindfold. However, this blindfold was not a divine endowment, but rather an imposition by human agents, specifically, the foolish and the corrupt. The earliest notable depiction of blindfolded Justice emerged in 1494, within Sebastian Brant's satirical work, Das Narrenschiff ("The Ship of Fools"). In a woodcut, Justice is shown being blindfolded by a fool. In this context, Justice is being misled, the blindfold isn't a representation of the modern interpretation of impartiality and neutrality, denoting that decisions are rendered with strict adherence to facts and the legal system, free from personal discrimination. Instead, she is rendered incapable of discerning the truth of the dispute because folly and corruption have obscured her vision. The blindfold, here, signifies error, ignorance, and invalidity, as the blindness of justice represented the demise of what is just.  
Modern legal orders presented themselves as impersonal and code-based, yet they simultaneously relied on dense iconographic systems, across coins, stamps, monuments, courthouse architecture, seals, and legal documents, through which sovereignty and adjudication became publicly legible. Female figures of Justice, Liberty, the Republic, and the Nation condensed constitutional claims into stable attributes and postures, producing recognisability at a glance and shaping everyday expectations about what law promises, authorises, and forbids.   
Here is a visible paradox in the historical construction of legal legitimacy: How did modern states make law legible to populations who could not read? The research redefines legal literacy beyond textual competence to encompass visual/symbolic literacy, the historically situated capacity to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material cues, spatial arrangements, and iconographic systems. Rather than treating images as mere illustrations of law, this project treats them as primary legal sources constituting the “background knowledge” through which most populations, illiterate, subaltern, excluded from the legal profession, actually encountered the state’s authority.  
The gendered body performs essential ideological labour. Nineteenth-century political culture associates femininity with virtue, restraint, and moral clarity, then transfers that moral credit to the state and its law. This transfer is politically convenient because real women were excluded from citizenship and office: the republic could borrow the authority of the feminine while withholding political capacity from female persons. The allegorical woman becomes universal by being unavailable for reciprocity.  
For the legal historian, this is not a decorative aside. It is an archive. Tracking where these bodies appear, which attributes become standard, and which settings repeat them, in courtrooms, schoolbooks, currency, and official stationery, enables reconstruction of what counted as legal knowledge in practice: what law sought to be taken for, which virtues it claimed, and which forms of coercion it attempted to translate into order through visibility and repetition.  
Feminine civic allegory also helped reconcile revolutionary claims to equality with enduring hierarchies of gender and class. It displaced “the people” and “the law” onto an idealised, de-sexualised female body staged as timeless and above conflict, and it became a central tool within a new myth-making machine capable of stabilising legal monism. Draped in classical forms and stripped of sexuality, maternity, labour, and desire, the allegorical body becomes an “iconic logos”, a site in which normative orders are actively constructed through visuality.  
Against this backdrop, the project is organised around a methodological and a substantive problem.

	2\. Research problem and questions  
2.1 Methodological problem  
Legal literacy is often defined as competence with rules, documents, and procedures. A legal-historical perspective points to a broader reality: actors learned legality through images, spaces, and rituals, as much as through statute books. The project asks how legal historians can reconstruct the background knowledge through which non-specialists recognised authority, anticipated institutional behaviour, and oriented themselves within legal orders.  
2.2 Substantive problem  
The project asks why modern regimes persistently selected the feminine body as a privileged interface for abstract authority, and what kind of legal literacy is produced when law is publicly learned through an idealised body rhetorically universal yet institutionally detached from women’s civic and juridical capacities.  
Core questions:

1. Through which media and sites did female legal and national allegories circulate between 1789 and 1945, and what were the conditions of encounter (scale, repetition, setting, audience)?  
2. Which iconographic grammars stabilised recognition (attributes, inscriptions, postures), and how did these grammars change across political ruptures (revolutions, unifications, regime transformations, war)?  
3. How did these images teach legality (as promise, as coercion, as neutrality), and how did they shape expectations about courts, rights, and sovereignty?  
4. How did the visual sexual contract operate as a technology of legitimacy, and through which mechanisms did symbolic femininity support universality while preserving gendered exclusions?  
5. How did contestation (satire, iconographic replacement, damage, disputes) disclose moments when allegory’s claim to neutrality became unstable?

3\. Scope, comparative design, and conceptual orientation  
3.1 Periodisation  
The project focuses on 1789 to 1945, from revolutionary refoundations of sovereignty to the post-war reorganisation of European legality.  
3.2 Cases and comparative logic  
The comparative core centres on:

* France: Marianne and the republicanisation of civic and judicial spaces.  
* German lands and Germany: Germania and the iconography of unity, constitutional aspiration, and later imperial legitimacy.  
* Britain: Britannia and the coexistence of allegory with monarchical and imperial iconography.  
* Switzerland: Helvetia and the iconography of federal unity, neutrality, and civic order.  
* Brazil: reception and adaptation of European allegorical vocabularies in republican and juridical iconography.

This selection enables controlled variation across regime types while tracking a shared representational solution to legitimacy and legal pedagogy. It supports a distinction between structural regularities (recurring operations of allegorical femininity) and context-specific deployments (local political problems addressed through particular motifs).  
3.3 Key concepts  
Legal literacy as infrastructural knowledge: legal literacy is treated as distributed competence produced through repeated encounters with institutional signs. Images and spaces shape how legality becomes credible and emotionally binding.  
Iconocracy: the project uses iconocracy to designate a regime in which images participate in the production of political reality by structuring recognition, belief, and consent. Here, iconocracy is approached as the ordinary, durable, institutionally supported circulation of symbols that organise how authority appears.  
The visual sexual contract: the visual sexual contract names the arrangement in which the feminine body serves as the public “body” of law and nation while women’s civic and juridical capacities remain constrained. The contract operates through substitution, naturalisation, sacralisation, and pedagogy.  
3.4 Simulacrum and the juridical hyperreal  
The project also mobilises Jean Baudrillard’s concept of the simulacrum as a lens for modern legal iconocracy. In this sense, the simulacrum is not reducible to false representation. It describes a situation in which the sign, here the image of Justice, Liberty, or the Republic, can mask the absence of a corresponding reality for those whom legality claims to include. Allegorical femininity is analytically central because it stages universality in a human form while separating that form from women’s concrete political and juridical capacities.  
3.4.1 Allegory as a mask of absence  
In national and judicial iconography, exalted figures of Liberty and Justice can project aesthetic perfection and moral elevation while obscuring women’s factual dispossession of rights and civic standing. In dialogue with Grossi’s account of the modern legal subject as abstracted and “socially naked”, the allegorical woman can be read as the simulacrum of formal equality, imaging a universal subject that appears available to all even when everyday legality remains structured by gendered incapacity and exclusion. In dialogue with Silvia Federici’s reconstruction of gendered violence in the formation of modern political economy, the allegorical, domesticated, and de-sexualised feminine figure also functions as a substitute for women’s embodied knowledges and social powers that were criminalised, disciplined, and redirected into reproductive labour.  
3.4.2 The precession of juridical simulacra  
Baudrillard’s claim that “the map precedes the territory” illuminates how modern law increasingly operates through abstract models, such as codes and state-centred legal categories, that actively produce social reality. Within this optic, Grossi’s diagnosis of an Enlightenment myth-making machine becomes central: legality is transformed into an object of reverence, insulated by mythology that renders contingent social foundations less visible. Iconocracy intensifies this operation. Seals, monuments, official emblems, and monetary iconography materialise a juridical hyperreality in which the image of the Republic stands in for “the people”, even when concrete needs, conflicts, and exclusions no longer operate as the effective referent of power.  
3.4.3 Legal literacy as critique of the simulacrum  
If legal literacy is approached as more than procedural competence, it can be reformulated as the capacity to decipher the simulacra that sustain patriarchal legality. Federici’s account of witches’ Sabbath iconography is exemplary: demonological imagery constructed a retrospective hallucination that criminalised real forms of collective resistance and women’s knowledges. Critical legal literacy therefore involves learning to see through visual and juridical fabrications that naturalise domination. Baudrillard’s destabilisation of the boundary between true and false has direct juridical consequences: access to rights frequently depends on conformity to an authorised simulacrum of the “legal subject”, implicitly coded as property-bearing, respectable, and racially normative. The project treats national allegories as potential fig leaves of universality and asks whose interests the universal face of law protects.

3.4.4 Implications  
Integrating the simulacrum shifts the project beyond descriptive semiotics toward a critique of visual governmentality, meaning the governance of perception as a condition of legitimacy. It also opens analytical space for counter-images and counter-figures that interrupt iconocratic transparency, including insurgent and stigmatised feminine figures that expose what allegory tends to smooth over. Finally, the simulacrum foregrounds materiality: law is lived on bodies, and modern legal mythologies can aestheticise pain into disappearance. Critical legal literacy is treated here as the disciplined reconnection of legality’s self-images to the material distribution of coercion, dependency, and exclusion.

	4\. Corpus and sources  
The corpus is organised by supports chosen for their distinct modes of encounter and social reach:

1. Monetary iconography: coins, medals, banknotes.  
2. Postal and administrative iconography: stamps, seals, official emblems, letterheads.  
3. Monumental programmes: statues, reliefs, civic squares, courthouse façades and interiors.  
4. Legal paratexts: frontispieces of codes, judicial insignia, vignettes, engravings, courtroom iconography.

Primary materials will be assembled from numismatic and philatelic collections, national and municipal repositories, architectural documentation, digitised archives of legal print culture, and, where accessible, courthouse records that clarify commissioning, placement, and reception.  
5\. Methodology: iconographic reconstruction for legal history  
The project develops an iconographic reconstruction protocol tailored to legal history. Each object will be analysed through five linked operations:

1. Iconographic description: form, attributes, dress, posture, inscription, composition.  
2. Institutional placement and audience: site, jurisdictional function, intended publics, conditions of encounter.  
3. Pedagogy of encounter: how scale, repetition, and setting shape comprehension and affect.  
4. Intervisual genealogy: motifs echoed, simplified, intensified, or replaced over time.  
5. Gendered operation: how femininity produces claims of universality, neutrality, moral elevation, or legitimation.

Contestation is treated as evidence. Satire, disputes over imagery, defacement, and iconographic replacement are analysed as diagnostic moments that disclose what the allegory does when its neutrality is challenged.

	6\. Analytical focus: what these allegories teach  
The project treats female personifications as teaching devices that translate key dimensions of legality into recognisable cues:

* Judgment: weighing, balance, evidence.  
* Coercion: enforceability and sanctioned force.  
* Impartiality: equality before law and its performed neutrality.  
* Sovereignty and belonging: the imagined community and its constitutional self-image.

The central analytical move is to show how these lessons produce a public sense of legal universality while enabling the management of exclusions.  
7\. Expected contribution  
7.1 Substantive contribution  
The project provides a gender-sensitive account of how modern legality staged universality through feminised allegory while maintaining institutional exclusions. It clarifies why female personification became a privileged interface for abstract authority and why this interface remains politically potent and vulnerable.  
7.2 Methodological contribution  
The project offers a transferable protocol for reconstructing background legal knowledge from visual sources, complementing doctrinal and archival methods and widening the evidentiary basis for studying legal consciousness.  
7.3 Contribution to the Summer Academy theme  
The project relocates legal literacy in the interface between norms and media, demonstrating how legal knowledge was transmitted, internalised, and contested through visual infrastructures that reached beyond formally educated publics.  
7.4 Contemporary resonance  
Although the empirical corpus ends in 1945, the project is designed to clarify a durable mechanism: legal authority continues to require legible embodiment, and images remain decisive for how law is perceived, trusted, and contested. In a media environment saturated by rapid circulation, branding logics, and compressed visual cues, the nineteenth-century problem of translating impersonal legality into recognisable form becomes newly visible rather than obsolete. By reconstructing how allegorical bodies once performed this work, the project offers conceptual and methodological tools for analysing present-day legal communication, including the persistence of personified symbols, the staging of neutrality through iconography, and the recurrent slippage between universality as image and exclusion as practice.

	9\. Post-1945 to the present: from allegorical unity to operational visibility  
The empirical corpus ends in 1945, yet the mechanism it isolates survives the periodisation. Modern legality continues to require public recognisability, and the visual labour of legitimacy has intensified rather than faded. In the nineteenth century, feminine personifications offered a stable emblematic solution: they condensed constitutional claims into a readable body and distributed that body through everyday media.  
After 1945, the dominant regime of legal visibility expanded. Allegorical figures persist in institutional design, architecture, seals, and professional insignia, yet legal authority increasingly presents itself through operational images: photographs, identity documents, forensic visualisations, courtroom exhibits, broadcast trials, body-worn cameras, and platform interfaces. In this shift, the image functions less as a civic emblem and more as a device within evidence-making, governance, and risk management. The legal gaze becomes infrastructural.  
This contemporary horizon refines the project’s intervention in legal literacy debates. Legal literacy can be conceptualised as competence with the visual and material infrastructures that stage legality: the ability to recognise how images solicit trust, how interfaces and archives allocate credibility, and how forms of visibility determine which claims become legible as harm, as right, or as proof. Feminine allegory becomes historically instructive because it offers an earlier, unusually durable technique for translating abstraction into recognisable form while insulating institutions from accountability for the distribution of coercion.  
The project therefore treats the nineteenth-century allegorical repertoire as a diagnostic archive for the present. It clarifies how law learns to appear neutral and universal through bodies that are rhetorically inclusive yet institutionally unavailable for reciprocity. It also equips a sharper analysis of contemporary visual regimes in which legitimacy is produced through images that present themselves as mechanical and self-evident, even when they reproduce structured exclusions.

	Annex: Research report notes and source map (18th century to the present)  
This annex consolidates a research-oriented map that extends the proposal’s logic beyond 1945 while keeping the comparative core intact. It is designed to be reusable for applications, conference abstracts, and a future methodological appendix.  
A. Research angle  
The project treats legal literacy as historically situated competence: the capacity to recognise how law becomes believable, memorable, and actionable through mediations that include images, bodies, rituals, spatial arrangements, and the authoritative staging of texts. Within that horizon, feminine allegories (Nation, Republic, Liberty, Justice) function as didactic devices and as screens: they translate abstraction into a legible body while offering a decorous surface through which the legal order can appear unified, impartial, and universally addressed.  
B. Conceptual spine

1. Revolutionary legal transformation produces an abstract, unitary subject and requires new techniques of public recognisability.  
2. Iconographic femininity supplies a depersonalised body for sovereignty, enabling continuity in visual languages of virtue while sustaining political discontinuity with monarchic embodiment.  
3. The same period intensifies disciplinary imaginaries of feminine deviance and vulnerability, producing a coupled system of exalted civic womanhood and punished feminine disorder.  
4. Iconocracy designates the organisation of the visible that secures consent by training perception. The simulacrum lens sharpens how images can stage universality while masking the absence of equivalent reality for those rhetorically included.  
5. Contemporary legal visibility shifts toward operational images and infrastructural witnessing, with legal literacy increasingly involving competence with interfaces, archives, surveillance, and evidentiary visuality.

C. Diachronic comparison  
A workable long arc can be organised into three phases, each with a distinct pedagogy of legality:

* Allegorical unity (late eighteenth to early twentieth centuries): emblems and personifications stabilise recognition and moralise authority.  
* Evidentiary operationality (nineteenth century to the present, intensifying after 1945): photography, documents, and visual traces become institutional proof-technologies.  
* Platform visibility (late twentieth century to the present): legitimacy and dispute are mediated through circulation, virality, moderation, and algorithmic attention.

D. Comparative typology (illustrative)  
France: Marianne as republican body, civic and judicial display, coins, stamps, town halls, monuments. United States: Columbia and related Liberty figures as national personification and didactic interface in print and monumental culture. Britain: Britannia as continuity figure across constitutional monarchy, empire, and monetary iconography. Switzerland: Helvetia as federal unity, neutrality, civic order, especially visible in monetary and postal repertoires. Brazil: reception and adaptation of European allegorical vocabularies within republican iconography and juridical monumentalisation.  
E. Primary-source pathways

* Numismatic and philatelic collections (state mints, national museums, digitised catalogues).  
* Court and civic architecture archives (commissioning files, plans, photographic documentation).  
* Legal print culture and paratexts (frontispieces, vignettes, judicial insignia, seals).  
* Municipal and educational materials (schoolbooks, civic catechisms, posters).

F. Micro-case studies suitable for a methods chapter

1. The blindfold of Justice as a migrating attribute: from critique to canonised impartiality.  
2. Marianne’s stabilisation as an everyday face of the Republic, with attention to site and repetition.  
3. Monetary and postal iconography as distributed pedagogy: how small-format repetition trains recognition.  
4. A Brazilian anchor object as reception and reconfiguration: how European lineages are remade in republican state iconography.

G. Contemporary extension for concluding chapters  
A concluding chapter will treat the allegorical body as a historical template for present-day legality’s demand for images.
