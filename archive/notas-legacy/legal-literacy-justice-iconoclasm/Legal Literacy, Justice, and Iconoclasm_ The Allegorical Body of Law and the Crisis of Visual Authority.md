**Legal Literacy, Justice, and Iconoclasm: The Allegorical Body of Law and the Crisis of Visual Authority**

1\. Introduction: The Iconocratic Regime of Modernity and the Target of the Image

The visual infrastructure of the modern state is not merely an ornamental supplement to the written law; it is a constitutive condition of its legitimacy. When the rioters of January 8, 2023, stormed the *Praça dos Três Poderes* in Brasília, their target transcended the judicial bureaucracy: they attacked the symbolic interface through which the state projects its authority. The defacement of Alfredo Ceschiatti’s sculpture *A Justiça*—marked with the inscription "Perdeu, mané" (You lost, fool) in red lipstick—constitutes a central event in the history of what might be termed "legal iconocracy." This act of vandalism was not merely chaotic violence, but a brutal exercise in "counter-literacy," a violent reading that sought to strip the "socially naked" abstraction of the law to reveal the partisan political body beneath (GROSSI, 2010; AGÊNCIA BRASIL, 2025).

This report investigates the historical and theoretical dimensions of this crisis, situating the Brazilian event within a genealogy of legal representation extending from the European Enlightenment to the post-colonial societies of the Americas. The core of this inquiry is the redefinition of **Legal Literacy**. Conventionally understood as the textual competence to navigate statutes and contracts, legal literacy is here reconceptualized as an *infrastructural and visual competence*: the distributed capacity of a population to recognize, inhabit, and trust the material signs of legality.1

Modern legal orders, despite their claims to textual rationality, have historically depended on dense iconographic systems to make sovereignty legible. The transition from the personal sovereignty of the monarch to the abstract sovereignty of the "Rule of Law" created a representational vacuum. The "King’s Two Bodies"—the natural (mortal) body and the political (immortal) body—were severed by the revolutions of the 18th century (KANTOROWICZ, 1957). However, the State still required a body to make itself visible, loved, and feared. The solution found by the nascent republics was the **Allegorical Body of Law**: the proliferation of idealized female figures (Justice, Liberty, Republic) to embody the abstract virtues of the State (AGULHON, 1989).

This dependence on the female form constitutes a specific technology of legitimacy that this report identifies as the **Visual Sexual Contract**, adapting the political philosophy of Carole Pateman and the art history of Marina Warner. This concept elucidates a persistent paradox: modern regimes, as they moved toward abstract governance, increasingly resorted to the female body—The Justice, The Republic (Marianne), The Nation (Germania, Helvetia)—to embody the State. This proliferation occurred precisely during historical periods when real women were systematically excluded from civil and legal capacity. The allegorical female body thus operates as a "simulacrum" (BAUDRILLARD, 1994\) of universality, a vessel that sustains the weight of the "General Will" precisely because it is emptied of concrete political agency (WARNER, 1985; PATEMAN, 1988).

Through a comparative analysis linking the consolidation of the European nation-state to the Brazilian republican experience—drawing on **Paolo Grossi**, **Steve J. Stern**, and **José Murilo de Carvalho**—it is argued that the stability of the modern legal order depends on the maintenance of this visual fiction. The iconoclasm of January 2023, therefore, signals not just a political crisis, but the collapse of the "background knowledge" that sustains the visual authority of the law, revealing the fragility of the Enlightenment promise of a neutral and universal justice.

2\. Theoretical Framework: Law, Image, and the Sediments of Time

2.1. Redefining Legal Literacy as Visual Competence

The traditional definition of legal literacy focuses on the "letter of the law." However, for the vast majority of citizens, the primary encounter with the law is not textual, but visual and spatial. The law manifests in the architecture of the courthouse, the police uniform, the document seal, and the public monument. These "cultural techniques" produce what Goodrich terms "legal emblems," serving to bind the subject to the State through an aesthetic and emotional contract (GOODRICH, 2013).

In this context, legal literacy must be understood as **infrastructural knowledge**. It is the semiotic fluency that allows a citizen to distinguish a valid decree from a forgery, largely based on the "appearance" of authority. This visual competence is what Reinhart Koselleck would describe as "sediments" of collective experience—layers of visual habituation that allow the past to structure the present (KOSELLECK, 2004). When a citizen stands before a statue of Justice, they engage with a secular "pathos formula" (in Aby Warburg's term), which commands deference and signals a dispute resolution mechanism superior to brute force.

This competence is not innate; it is learned through the "pedagogy of the street" and the "pedagogy of the coin." The circulation of images on banknotes, stamps, and monuments creates a "banal nationalism" (BILLIG, 1995\) that naturalizes state authority. As Mondzain (2005) argues, the image is an "economy"—a management of the visible that distributes power by making certain concepts (Order, Progress) visible while rendering others invisible. Critical legal literacy is, therefore, the capacity to read these invisibilities.

2.2. The Visual Sexual Contract: Presence as Absence

The dependence of modern law on the allegorical female form is not accidental, but functional. The concept of the **Visual Sexual Contract** suggests that the symbolic hypervisibility of women is a condition of their political exclusion (PATEMAN, 1988).

Marina Warner (1985) noted that "Justice is a woman, but judges are men." In the transition from the "Body of the King" to the "Body of the Law," the male figure, laden with individual interests and political competition, was inadequate as a unifying symbol. A male figure representing the Republic would inevitably resemble a specific politician or general, inviting factionalism. The female figure, legally incapacitated in the 19th century, offered a "tabula rasa" upon which the virtues of the Republic could be projected without risk of political rivalry. Thus, **Marianne** in France or **The Republic** in Brazil could represent "The People" precisely because they could not vote or judge.

This mechanism creates a **simulacrum of universality**. By presenting the State in the guise of a woman—mother, warrior virgin, or serene adjudicator—the system masks the coercive nature of its operations. The allegory functions as a "pedagogical mask," teaching the population to love the State as a nurturing mother, diverting attention from the monopoly on violence. The "Visual Sexual Contract" operates as a technology of displacement: the stone body absorbs the reverence due to the public good, while the living female body is relegated to the private sphere.

2.3. The Simulacrum and the Juridical Hyperreal

Drawing on Jean Baudrillard (1994), the modern legal image can be analyzed as a simulacrum—a copy without an original. The statue of Justice does not represent a pre-existing reality of equity; it *produces* the effect of equity in an asymmetric system. Baudrillard’s dictum that "the map precedes the territory" is apt for legal modernity: visual emblems (flag, anthem, statue) generate the reality of the Nation-State.

In the **juridical hyperreal**, the image of the law becomes more real than the law itself. An attack on the image (iconoclasm) is treated by the State not as property damage, but as treason—an assault on the body politic. This theoretical lens explains the severity of the state response to the 2023 acts, where the defacement of the statue was prosecuted as the "violent abolition of the Democratic Rule of Law" (AGÊNCIA BRASIL, 2025). The crisis of 2023 was a rupture in the simulation: the rioters refused the map, attempting to overlay it with their own partisan reality.

Furthermore, the simulacrum operates by concealing the absence of reality. The statue, with blindfold and sword, suggests that hidden in the court exists a pure and impartial decision-making process. The image ensures that "Justice" exists as a tangible substance. However, political realism suggests that the legal process is a negotiation of power. The statue hides the fact that *there is no Justice* in the ontological sense, only the imperfect administration of the law. The January 8 rioters, with the graffiti "Perdeu, mané," brutally exposed this absence, claiming that the "Justice" in the building was nothing more than the will of a specific man.1

3\. Historical Trajectories: European and Atlantic Origins of the Allegorical State (1789–1945)

To understand the Brazilian crisis, it is necessary to trace the genealogy of the visual forms imported by the young Republic in 1889\. The grammar of legal allegory is a transatlantic inheritance, forged in European revolutions and exported as a mark of "civilization."

3.1. The Crisis of Sovereignty and the Invention of the Public Image

The execution of Louis XVI in 1793 marked a representational crisis. Under the *Ancien Régime*, the King's physical body was the locus of the law. The guillotine, by severing the King's head, broke the link between the visible body and the invisible authority of the law. The Republic, being an abstract concept (*Res Publica*), needed a new body to make itself visible (KANTOROWICZ, 1957).

Maurice Agulhon (1989) details how French revolutionaries experimented with Hercules, a symbol of popular force, but ultimately opted for the female allegory, **Marianne**. Marianne offered a "soft" face for the "hard" terror of the State, representing Liberty and Reason rather than brute force. Thus began a **pedagogy of sovereignty** via coins, stamps, and letterheads, creating a "banal nationalism" (BILLIG, 1995). Legal literacy became the ability to recognize valid authority in these symbols. The "Visual Sexual Contract" was printed on the very currency.

3.2. National Personifications: Germania, Britannia, and Helvetia

The grammar of the "Woman-Nation" stabilized in 19th-century Europe, with local variations reflecting distinct legal cultures:

* **France (Marianne):** Agulhon identifies the struggle between two Mariannes: the **Revolutionary** (Phrygian cap, exposed breast, in motion) and the **Sage** (clothed, seated, with diadem or laurel wreath). The Third Republic, seeking order, favored the Sage Marianne—a crucial distinction for the Brazilian republicans of 1889\.  
* **Germany (Germania):** Born of defense and unification (1871), Germania is not a liberator, but a sentinel. In the *Niederwald* monument (1883), she guards the Rhine, sword in hand. Legal literacy here meant recognizing the duty to defend the *Reich* (WARNER, 1985).  
* **Great Britain (Britannia):** Coexisting with the monarchy, Britannia abstracted the State and Empire. With trident and shield, she mediated the "King's Peace" and the stability of Common Law, allowing the monarchy to remain personal while the State became imperial.  
* **Switzerland (Helvetia):** Performed the "legal miracle" of unifying diverse cantons. Ubiquitous in numismatics, she visualized the Federal Pact that held the nation together (WARNER, 1985).

These figures were not merely decorative; they were constitutional arguments cast in bronze, serving as an "alembic of universality" (WARNER, 1985\) to distill complex political negotiations into a legible image.

3.3. The "Scaffold of the Law": Grossi and the Reduction of Pluralism

Paolo Grossi (2010) describes the 19th century as a period of legal "mitigation," where medieval legal pluralism was crushed by the monistic State and its Codes. The "scythe of equality" severed intermediate bodies, leaving the individual "socially naked" before the State.

The female allegory filled this void, humanizing the cold rationality of the Code. However, this humanization was fictitious. Landes (2001) argues that the "visualizing of the nation" as female actively worked to exclude women from the public sphere. The more the Republic was portrayed as a woman, the less space there was for women in the Republic.

4\. Transatlantic Translations: The Brazilian Adaptation (1889–Present)

The Brazilian Republic, proclaimed in 1889, had to invent a republican tradition in a country fresh from monarchy and slavery. The importation of European visual models was central to this legitimacy project.

4.1. José Murilo de Carvalho and the "Bestializados"

José Murilo de Carvalho provides the essential framework for analyzing this transition. In *Os Bestializados* (1987) and *A Formação das Almas* (1990), Carvalho argues that the proclamation was a military coup with no popular participation. The people watched "bestializados" (stupefied).

Without a popular base, the republican elite engaged in a "battle for hearts and minds" (to use STERN's terminology, 2006\) to construct a new imaginary. Carvalho identifies the conflict between the **Jacobin** current (revolutionary Marianne) and the **Positivist** current (orderly Marianne). The Positivists triumphed, adopting a conservative allegory emphasizing Order over Liberty, reflecting the fear of the newly freed "dangerous classes."

The "whitening" of the Republic's image was literal and symbolic. The allegories were invariably white European women, despite the Brazilian demographic reality. The "Visual Sexual Contract" in Brazil was thus also a **"Visual Racial Contract."**

4.2. Modernism and the Erasure of the Scales: Ceschiatti’s *A Justiça*

The culmination of this visual trajectory is the sculpture *A Justiça* (1961) by **Alfredo Ceschiatti**, in front of the Supreme Federal Court (STF). Embedded in the modernist project of Brasília, the statue breaks with classical iconography (SALGADO, 2023).

Unlike the classical *Justitia*, Ceschiatti's Justice presents herself:

* **Seated:** Immobile and monumental.  
* **Blindfolded:** But with a stylized blindfold.  
* **With Sword:** At rest, but present.  
* **Without Scales:** The critical iconographic omission.

The absence of the scales transforms the allegory's meaning. Without the mechanism of deliberation, Justice becomes purely the power of *decision*—the force of law. She is a deity of *execution* rather than adjudication. This modernist abstraction signaled a Justice that *imposes* rather than *weighs*, creating a symbolic vulnerability that would be exploited in the 21st century.

5\. The Crisis of 2023: Iconoclasm, Ventriloquism, and Counter-Literacy

On January 8, 2023, the implicit visual pact collapsed. The invasion of the STF was an act of **iconoclasm**—a struggle for the authority to define the "true image" of power.

5.1. The Event: "Perdeu, Mané" as Iconoclastic Ventriloquism

The most visually potent act was the defacement of the statue by **Débora Rodrigues dos Santos**, who wrote "Perdeu, mané" (You lost, fool) on the figure's chest (AGÊNCIA BRASIL, 2025).

The phrase's genealogy is crucial:

* **Origin:** In November 2022, Justice Luís Roberto Barroso responded to a protester in New York with the phrase: "Perdeu, mané, não amola" (You lost, fool, don't bother me) (G1, 2022; PODER360, 2022).  
* **Transposition:** By inscribing these words on the statue, the protester performed **"iconoclastic ventriloquism."** She forced the universal and abstract body of "Justice" (Body Politic) to speak the partisan and dismissive words of a fallible judge (Body Natural).

This act collapsed the "Simulacrum of Universality," unmasking the "Visual Sexual Contract" and alleging that the serene goddess was merely a façade for the arrogance of a magistrate. The use of lipstick—a marker of femininity and vanity—re-sexualized and trivialized the allegory, reducing the "Mother of Laws" to a graffiti wall.1

5.2. The Sentence: Re-Consecrating the Image

The state response confirms that the image *is* the law in the hyperreal regime. On **April 25, 2025**, the STF sentenced Débora Rodrigues dos Santos to **14 years in prison** for crimes including the "violent abolition of the Democratic Rule of Law" (AGÊNCIA BRASIL, 2025; MIGALHAS, 2025).

Legally, this equates the defacement of the statue to the attempted overthrow of the government. The sentence acts as a ritual of **re-consecration**, stitching the "Simulacrum" and reaffirming that the Image of the Law is inviolable, for without it, the Law has no body.

5.3. Steve J. Stern and the "Memory Box" of the Coup

The violence of January cannot be understood without the specters of the authoritarian past. **Steve J. Stern** (2006), in his analysis of Chile, argues that memory is a "field of struggle." Stern identifies four memory frameworks, including "memory as salvation" (the regime saved the country from chaos).

The January 8 rioters engaged in a "memory struggle," invoking a salvationist view of the 1964 military dictatorship. The vandalism was an attempt to force open the "memory box" (STERN, 2004\) and reinstate a pre-democratic order, where the STF (and democratic Justice) is viewed as the villain.

---

6\. Synthesis: The Fragility of Visual Infrastructure and the Future of Legal Literacy

The analysis reveals a fundamental vulnerability in the modern legal project. The "Visual Sexual Contract," which managed state visibility for two centuries, is crumbling.

6.1. From Allegory to Operational Images

Although we are transitioning to the era of "Operational Images" (FAROCKI, 2004), where law is represented by data and algorithms, the 2023 crisis proves that the allegorical regime is not dead. The "Pathos Formula" (Warburg) survives. When rioters wanted to attack the Law, they attacked the female body of the statue.

6.2. The Failure of the "Socially Naked" Subject

Grossi (2010) warned that the "socially naked" individual created by the modern state depended on an unsustainable myth of equality. The "Visual Sexual Contract" was the aesthetic covering of this nakedness. The "Perdeu, mané" incident signifies the failure of this promise: for the rioters, the State ceased to be a universal mother to become a partisan agent.

6.3. Conclusion: Toward a Critical Legal Literacy

It is concluded that the crisis of legality is fundamentally a crisis of **Visual Authority**. A critical **Legal Literacy** must include "iconological self-defense": the ability to deconstruct the visual myths of the state and recognize the violence hidden beneath the serene mask of the allegory. The red lipstick on the granite is now an indelible part of this history, the scar where the "Simulacrum" met the "Real" (AGÊNCIA BRASIL, 2025; CNN BRASIL, 2024).
