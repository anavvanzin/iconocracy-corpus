## I. RESEARCH QUESTION AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

### The Central Paradox

This project investigates a fundamental paradox in the historical construction of legal legitimacy: **How did modern states make law legible to populations who could not read?** The research redefines legal literacy beyond textual competence to encompass **visual/symbolic literacy**—the historically situated capacity to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material cues, spatial arrangements, and iconographic systems. Rather than treating images as mere illustrations of law, this project treats them as primary legal sources constituting the "background knowledge" through which most populations—illiterate, subaltern, excluded from the legal profession—actually encountered the state's authority.

The empirical fulcrum for this investigation is the January 8, 2023 attack on Brazil's Supreme Federal Court (*Supremo Tribunal Federal*, STF), when supporters of former President Jair Bolsonaro stormed the *Praça dos Três Poderes* (Plaza of the Three Powers) in Brasília. Rioters defaced Alfredo Ceschiatti's monumental sculpture *A Justiça* (1961)—a 3.3-meter carved granite figure dominating the court's entrance—by scrawling "Perdeu, mané" (You lost, fool) across its chest in red lipstick. This was not collateral vandalism. The attack was carefully targeted. The phrase itself was a direct quotation from Justice Luís Roberto Barroso, spoken dismissively to a Bolsonaro supporter in New York in November 2022: "Perdeu, mané, não amola" (You lost, fool, don't bother me).

By inscribing Barroso's partisan, contemptuous words onto the body of the abstract, universal Goddess of Justice, the rioters performed an act of what this project terms **iconoclastic unmasking**—a violent revelation that the statue's universal pretensions were a mask concealing partisan judicial politics. They demonstrated sophisticated **counter-literacy**: the ability to read and rewrite state iconography against the grain, to collapse the careful distinction between the Law's immortal "body politic" (transcendent Justice, blind and impartial) and its mortal "body natural" (fallible judges with biases and contempt).

This act forces legal historians to confront a troubling question: On what material, affective, and visual foundations rests the legitimacy of modern legal orders? If that legitimacy can be destroyed by forcing a stone statue to speak a partisan judge's voice, how stable are the legal systems built upon such symbolic infrastructure?

### Theoretical Scaffolding: From Pateman to the "Visual Sexual Contract"

Building upon Carole Pateman's foundational concept of "the sexual contract," this research proposes the existence of a **visual sexual contract**—a regime of symbolic hypervisibility functioning as a sophisticated substitute for political inclusion. Pateman demonstrated that the Enlightenment's celebrated "social contract" was undergirded by a prior fraternal settlement organizing women's systematic subordination. The modern liberal state emerged not from a universal contract among equals but from a pact among brothers (men) granting them equal political rights while maintaining their patriarchal right over women. This project extends Pateman's analysis into the visual field, arguing that the omnipresent female allegory was not decorative but foundational to how patriarchal modernity operated.

The central empirical observation is startling: **precisely when women were most rigorously excluded from political agency (the franchise, jury service, the legal profession, property ownership), the female body proliferated as the omnipresent official face of Law, Justice, and the Nation.** Figures such as Marianne (France), Britannia (Britain), Germania (Germany), Columbia (United States), and Brazil's Republican effigy taught citizens across the Atlantic world "what law looks like" while simultaneously masking the gendered exclusions of actual legal orders. The image served as what we might call a "phantom presence," allowing the state to claim it represented universal humanity while empowering only half the population.

### Why This Matters for Legal History

Legal historians have long recognized that law operates through more than written texts. As law and society scholars have demonstrated, legality is embedded in institutions, practices, and performances. Yet the comprehensive iconographic infrastructure through which states constructed legitimacy has been remarkably understudied. This is particularly striking given that:

1. **For most historical populations, law was not a written reality but a visual and spatial one.** The 19th-century peasant in France could not read the Code Civil; the enslaved African in Brazil could not access written law; the working-class woman in Victorian London encountered law primarily through encounters with state symbols, not statute books.

2. **The female allegory was uniquely effective at masking the exclusions it enabled.** A statue of a male hero inevitably resembles a specific ruler—a competitor for power, a figure around which factions form. A female figure, historically excluded from ruling and legally defined as a perpetual minor, implies ideals rather than personal power. She unites the populace precisely because she appears to have no biography, no history, no political ambition.

3. **The visible legitimacy of modern law rests on foundations that are more fragile than contemporary legal theory acknowledges.** The January 8 attacks revealed that when populations gain counter-literacy—the ability to read state symbols critically—the simulacrum can be shattered in minutes.

## II. LEGAL LITERACY RECONCEPTUALIZED: A TRIPARTITE MODEL

### The Problem with Traditional Definitions

Conventional scholarship on legal literacy concentrates narrowly on what we might call "black-letter literacy"—the ability to read statutes, understand legal doctrines, and navigate procedural complexity. This text-centric view has several profound limitations:

- It excludes the vast majority of historical actors who were illiterate
- It treats literacy as individual competence rather than as historically and socially situated
- It privileges the professional domain while marginalizing how subaltern populations engaged with law
- It assumes that law is primarily a phenomenon of written texts rather than material culture, spatial arrangement, and affective performance

This project proposes a comprehensive **tripartite definition of legal literacy** to address these limitations and align with the Max Planck Summer Academy's interest in diverse forms of legal knowledge managed by laypeople, subaltern actors, and historical populations:

### Type 1: Textual Competence

**Definition**: Mastery of codes, statutes, and legal doctrines; "black-letter law"  
**Primary Media**: Law books, case reports, legal gazettes, commentaries  
**Target Demographic**: Lawyers, judges, educated elites  
**Circulation Logic**: Restricted to professional community; requires formal training

Textual competence is what traditional legal history has primarily studied. It requires literacy in a formal sense—the ability to read written language—and access to legal literature. This form of competence allows its possessor to interpret law doctrinally, to understand precedent, to construct arguments within legal logic. It is the competence of legal professionals.

### Type 2: Infrastructural Competence

**Definition**: The practical ability to navigate institutions; knowing where to go, whom to approach, how to file, what forms to complete, what informal protocols to follow  
**Primary Media**: Forms, counters, waiting rooms, bureaucratic procedures, administrative spaces  
**Target Demographic**: Litigants, petitioners, bureaucrats, those navigating the legal system  
**Circulation Logic**: Embedded in institutional practice; learned through experience and informal socialization

Infrastructural competence describes the practical knowledge required to move through a legal system without necessarily understanding its doctrinal foundations. A petitioner may not understand the civil code, but they know which office to approach, which form to file, when to wait, whom to bribe. Legal anthropologists and socio-legal scholars have documented this form of competence, though often without theorizing it as a distinct form of literacy.

### Type 3: Visual/Symbolic Competence

**Definition**: Background knowledge of authority; the recognition of the "face" of the state and its implicit threats and promises  
**Primary Media**: Statues, coins, stamps, architectural forms, official seals, legal paratexts  
**Target Demographic**: The general public, including illiterate populations, subaltern actors, those excluded from the legal profession  
**Circulation Logic**: Ubiquitous, intimate, non-textual; learned through everyday encounter and habituation

This third category constitutes the project's primary contribution to legal history. It is the form of legal literacy that has been most systematically neglected despite constituting the primary mode through which most historical populations actually encountered the state's authority.

The concept of "background knowledge" is crucial here. It refers to the tacit, shared understandings that make a legal system functional. It is the instinct that tells a citizen walking through a city that a building with classical columns topped by a blindfolded female figure is a courthouse, not a marketplace. It is the knowledge that allows one to "read" a coin and understand that its imagery represents state authority. It is the competence that makes an illiterate person bow slightly upon entering a space marked with judicial symbols.

This knowledge is "infrastructural" because it undergirds the textual law. Without the visual cues that generate awe, respect, or fear, the text of the law remains inert. A constitution is just words. A statute is just ink. But a statue of Justice dominating a public plaza teaches—wordlessly, affectively, through embodied encounter—that violence enacted within a particular building is "justice" rather than mere brutality.

### The Evidence: Coins, Stamps, Stones

Research indicates that in the 19th century, states invested heavily in producing this background knowledge. Consider the following examples:

**Monetary Iconography**: Nations minted coins with the face of the Republic to ensure that the image of the state circulated in the most intimate sphere of daily life—the market. Every time a peasant purchased bread, they touched Marianne. Every time a Brazilian purchased sugar or cloth, they held the face of the Republic in their palm. This was not decoration; it was pedagogy. The coin taught what the nation looked like.

**Postal Iconography**: Stamps performed a similar function across greater distances. A letter bearing Britannia carried British imperial authority across seas. A stamp with Helvetia unified a linguistically fractured confederation. These small adhesive images were global communicators of national virtues.

**Monumental Programs**: Statues and courthouse pediments operated through what we might call "vertical domination." The citizen stood below; the allegory dominated. This physical arrangement encoded a message: justice is superior to individual will, the state is eternal while citizens are mortal.

**Legal Paratexts**: Official seals, letterheads, and code frontispieces functioned as "authenticating images"—they signaled that a document came from legitimate authority and therefore deserved obedience.

### Counter-Literacy: The Emerging Third Consciousness

The January 8 attacks demonstrate that visual literacy is not passive reception but active interpretation and contestation. The rioters possessed a high degree of visual competence; they knew precisely which symbols embodied the power they sought to dismantle. They understood that the Court's legitimacy rested on its claim to be the neutral, abstract arbiter—the blindfolded woman. To destroy that legitimacy, they engaged in an act of **ventriloquism**: they forced the statue to speak in Barroso's voice, thereby revealing that the "universal goddess" was actually a partisan political player.

This represents the emergence of what this project terms **counter-literacy**—sophisticated visual competence deployed not to sustain but to delegitimize state authority. Counter-literacy is not simply illiteracy or lack of understanding. Rather, it is a form of hyper-literacy: the ability to read visual texts against the grain, to expose the ideological work they perform, to rewrite them with alternative meanings.

The graffiti "Perdeu, mané" performed an act of iconoclastic unmasking. It collapsed the distinction between the Two Bodies of the Law. It forced the eternal, abstract Body Politic (Justice herself) to merge with the mortal, fallible Body Natural (Barroso, seen as biased and contemptuous). It effectively captioned the statue, changing its meaning from "I am Eternal Justice" from "I am a partisan player who mocks you."

The state's response confirms the violence of this unmasking. Débora Rodrigues dos Santos, identified as writing the graffiti, was sentenced to 14 years in prison—treated as legally equivalent to attacking the body of the state itself. The statue was swiftly cleaned and restored; the state could not allow the "scar" to remain, as it would permanently inscribe the counter-literacy into the public symbol.

## III. THE VISUAL SEXUAL CONTRACT: MECHANISMS AND HISTORICAL EVIDENCE

### The Paradox Stated

The central paradox to be explored through this research is the "visual sexual contract"—a regime of representation that appears to contradict itself: **During the precise historical periods when women were most systematically excluded from political agency, civil capacity, and the legal profession, the female body proliferated as the omnipresent official face of the Law and the Nation.**

Consider the following chronological data:

- **France**: Marianne consolidated as the symbol of the Republic c. 1792 → Women's suffrage: 1944 (152-year lag)
- **Switzerland**: Helvetia consolidated on coins c. 1850 → Women's suffrage: 1971 (121-year lag)
- **United States**: Columbia/Liberty ubiquitous from 1790s → Women's suffrage: 1920 (130-year lag)
- **Germany**: Imperial Germania consolidated 1871 → Women's suffrage: 1918 (47-year lag)
- **Brazil**: Republican allegory 1889 → Women's suffrage: 1932 (43-year lag)

This is not coincidence. The nations that most heavily invested in the female form as the symbol of sovereignty were among the slowest to grant women political rights. The image served as a powerful mask, creating the illusion of feminine presence and power while the actual legal and political order systematically denied women agency.

### Three Mechanisms of Operation

The visual sexual contract operated through three distinct mechanisms, which this research traces across national contexts:

#### Mechanism 1: Substitution

The allegorical woman occupied the symbolic space where real women were absent. Marianne represented the French Republic, but French women could not vote until 1944—152 years after Marianne's consolidation as the official symbol of the state. The image served as a "phantom presence," allowing the state to claim it represented universal humanity while empowering only half the population.

This mechanism was particularly effective because it allowed the state to solve a representation problem without actually changing power relations. The Republic needed a face, a body, something the people could see and touch (on coins, stamps, architectural reliefs). The female form provided this visibility. But because women themselves possessed no political power, their symbolic omnipresence could coexist indefinitely with their political exclusion.

#### Mechanism 2: Sacralization

The feminine figure was elevated to a plane of moral purity precisely because she was removed from the "grubby" reality of political bargaining. Justice is pure because she does not participate in the dirty work of politics. She sits above it, frozen in stone, eternal and incorruptible. This sacralization paradoxically depended upon women's exclusion from actual political power; a Justice who could speak, vote, or legislate would threaten the very purity she was meant to embody.

The comparison with the male allegory is instructive. A statue of a specific male hero inevitably divides the populace. Some love him, others hate him. Some see him as a competitor for power. A female figure, historically excluded from ruling and legally defined as a perpetual minor, appears to unite everyone precisely because she seems to have no biography, no history, no political ambition. She promises to represent everyone's interests because she appears to have no interests of her own.

#### Mechanism 3: Disciplining and De-sexualization

To function as a state symbol, the female body required careful management. Allegories were typically draped in heavy classical robes, maternalized, monumentalized to strip them of erotic or threatening agency. The contrast between the "radical Marianne" of Delacroix's *Liberty Leading the People* (1830)—bare-breasted, active, dirty, violent, leading a mob—and the "conservative Marianne" adopted for official state seals is instructive.

The official Marianne is:
- Seated (static, not leading anything)
- Fully clothed (decorous, not erotic)
- Holding tablets of law or scales of justice (intellectual tools, not weapons)
- Wearing a diadem or olive wreath instead of the revolutionary cap (order, not revolution)

This disciplining process paralleled the disciplining of the working class and women themselves in the Napoleonic Code and subsequent legal regimes. Just as women's bodies were to be controlled, regulated, and confined to the domestic sphere, the allegorical female body was controlled, regulated, and confined to the symbolic sphere.

The "orderly" allegorical body replaced the "disorderly" body of the witch, the revolutionary virago, or the uncontrolled woman. It represented what Patricia Simons and other art historians have called the "domestication of the female form" within patriarchal visual regimes.

### The Racial Dimension: Brazil's Visual Racial Contract

When Brazil declared itself a Republic in 1889, it imported the French iconographic kit almost wholesale. The "Effigy of the Republic" (still seen on Brazilian currency) wears the Phrygian cap and laurel wreath of Marianne. However, the research must address the specific racial dimension of this reception in a post-slavery society.

The Brazilian Republic's image was aggressively Europeanized. Despite the country's demographic reality—a majority population of African or mixed descent—the official face of the state was a white, Greco-Roman woman. This "whitening" of the allegory served to project an image of "civilization" to Europe while masking the reality of Brazil's Afro-Brazilian population.

This reveals that the "Visual Sexual Contract" was simultaneously a **Visual Racial Contract**, operating through the same logic: symbolic inclusion of a whitened, idealized feminine form functioning as substitute for the political exclusion and racial subordination of actual women, particularly Afro-Brazilian women.

The January 8 attacks targeted not only Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* but also Di Cavalcanti's *As Mulatas* (1962), displayed in the Planalto Palace. The painting depicts mixed-race women conversing by the sea—a modernist attempt to construct national identity through representations of mestiçagem (racial mixture). The rioters stabbed this painting repeatedly, creating five puncture wounds in the canvas. This targeted attack reveals that the rioters understood Brasília's artistic program as a unified system of racialized and gendered symbolism requiring systematic destruction.

### Lag Analysis: Quantifying the Paradox

A key methodological innovation of this project is what it terms "Lag Analysis"—measuring the temporal gap between the consolidation of the female allegory as the symbol of the state and the granting of political rights to women. This metric provides quantitative evidence for the hypothesis that allegorical hypervisibility served as substitute for political invisibility.

The data suggest a clear pattern:

| Nation | Allegory Consolidation | Women's Suffrage | Lag (Years) | Notes |
|--------|----------------------|-----------------|-------------|-------|
| France | ~1792 (Marianne adopted) | 1944 | 152 | Highest allegorical usage; extreme delay in rights |
| Switzerland | ~1850 (Helvetia on coins) | 1971 | 121 | Strong iconographic unity; latest suffrage in West |
| USA | ~1790s (Columbia/Liberty) | 1920 | 130 | Ubiquitous usage on currency/statuary |
| Germany | 1871 (Imperial Germania) | 1918 | 47 | Martial allegory linked to empire |
| Brazil | 1889 (Republic Proclaimed) | 1932 | 43 | Allegory imported; rights granted earlier than France |

The data is suggestive rather than determinative. It points toward a correlation: the nations that most heavily relied on the female form to conceptualize their sovereignty were among the slowest to grant women political agency. France and Switzerland, which saturated their visual cultures with female imagery, were the slowest. Germany and Brazil, which adopted the allegorical tradition later and less extensively, were faster.

One interpretation is that the allegorical image served as a powerful mask. The state could claim it represented "humanity" and "universal values" while actually empowering only men. The presence of the image short-circuited the logical demand for female political participation. Why demand the right to vote when women already symbolically dominated the state's imagery?

## IV. BRASÍLIA AND THE MODERNIST GODDESS: THE CASE OF *A JUSTIÇA*

### Modernism, Transparency, and Simulacrum

Brasília, inaugurated in 1960, was designed as a total work of art. Oscar Niemeyer, the communist architect who had served as an apprentice to Lúcio Costa and was influenced by Le Corbusier, sought to express through architecture the rationality, transparency, and progress of the modern Brazilian state. The buildings are characterized by glass façades—vast expanses of transparent glass allowing citizens to see inside the spaces of power.

This architectural choice was ideologically freighted. The glass promised transparency: power would be visible, accessible, knowable to the people. Unlike the dark interiors of colonial palaces or monarchical courts, the modernist court would operate in plain sight. Citizens could see government happening.

However, scholars of architecture have noted that this transparency functions as what Jean Baudrillard would call a "simulacrum." It offers visual access without political access. The citizen can see the workings of power through the glass, but cannot touch it. The architecture creates an illusion of accessibility that masks actual political distance. You can watch, but you cannot participate. You can observe, but you cannot interfere.

Within this architectural mise en scène, the statue of *A Justiça* mediated the relationship between citizen and court. She sat outside, a stone sentinel, marking the boundary between the profane public square (where citizens gather) and the sacred interior space of juridical authority (where judges decide). She was the guardian at the threshold, the embodiment of what citizens would encounter inside.

### Ceschiatti's *A Justiça*: Iconographic Innovation and Vulnerability

Alfredo Ceschiatti (1918-1974) was one of Brazil's most important modernist sculptors. His *A Justiça*, carved from a monolithic block of Petrópolis granite, measures 3.3 meters in height and sits before the Supreme Federal Court. The statue represents a critical evolution—some would argue a distortion—of the traditional allegorical vocabulary.

Unlike baroque or neoclassical representations of Justice in European courthouses, Ceschiatti's figure is stripped of ornamentation. She is smooth, geometric, imposing. She wears no elaborate robes; her clothing is stylized into abstract planes of stone. Her attributes are minimal: a blindfold covering her eyes, a sword resting across her lap. That is all.

This modernist simplification is iconographically significant. Compare it to traditional representations:

| Attribute | Traditional/Neoclassical | Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* | Implication |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------|-------------|
| Eyes | Blindfolded (sometimes open in early depictions) | Blindfolded | Canonizes "impartiality" and cognitive withdrawal |
| Posture | Standing or Seated | Seated | Permanence, stability, passivity |
| Right Hand | Sword (raised or held) | Sword (resting on lap) | Potential for coercion; dormant but ready force |
| Left Hand | Scales (for weighing evidence) | ABSENT | CRUCIAL: Removal of deliberative function |
| Dress | Elaborate robes/armor | Stylized/smooth | Modernist abstraction; timelessness |

The most significant iconographic intervention is the **omission of the scales**. In traditional iconography, the scales represent the weighing of evidence, the deliberation, the intellectual or comparative side of judgment. The sword represents the executive power to punish. By removing the scales, Ceschiatti creates a figure that is "all execution"—a Justice defined purely by force, with the deliberative function excised.

Multiple interpretations circulate among art historians and legal scholars:

1. **The "Unfinished" Reading**: Some interpret the absence as signaling that justice in Brazil is not yet fully developed, that the weighing of evidence has not yet occurred, that the task remains incomplete.

2. **The Institutional Reading**: A more sophisticated institutional reading suggests that the Supreme Federal Court building itself functions as the scale—the institution performs the deliberation, and the statue is merely the guardian, the symbol of executive authority.

3. **The Authoritarian Reading**: The most critical interpretation reads the missing scales as symptomatic of authoritarianism. A Justice armed with a sword but lacking scales represents a legal system defined by force rather than equity, by punishment rather than deliberation. This reading notes that Ceschiatti created the statue in 1961, during the early years of the Jango Goulart government, a period of increasing political instability that would culminate in the 1964 military coup. Some scholars have speculated that the statue's severity reflects the period's political tensions.

### The Modernist Abstraction and Its Symbolic Vulnerability

Ceschiatti's modernist abstraction—reducing the allegory to its barest formal elements—created a particular kind of symbolic vulnerability. A traditional Justice holding scales reads unambiguously as a neutral arbiter carefully weighing competing claims. The scales communicate deliberation, balance, fairness. A figure holding scales alone, without a sword, might seem merely indecisive.

Ceschiatti's Justice, enigmatic and armed, with no visible scales, provided a much blanker screen onto which different viewers could project contested meanings. To those sympathetic to the judiciary, she represented impartial force—force in service of law, not personal will. To critics, she represented brute power untempered by deliberation.

When the January 8 rioters defaced her, this iconographic ambiguity became a vulnerability. The statue was not so densely packed with traditional allegorical meaning that alternative readings were impossible. Her modernist abstraction—intended to convey timeless rationality and transcendence—became instead a site for contested meaning-making. The rioters could write Barroso's dismissive words across her chest, and she could not resist this reinterpretation through her formal properties. There was no weight of tradition, no classical richness, to anchor her against the graffiti.

## V. THE ANATOMY OF ICONOCLASTIC UNMASKING: JANUARY 8, 2023

### The Event: Specificity of Violence

To understand the January 8 attacks as iconoclastic rather than merely destructive, one must examine the precision of the violence. The rioters did not randomly vandalize the plaza. They engaged in a carefully targeted semiotic performance.

Video and photographic evidence reveals the specificity:

**The Graffiti**: The phrase "Perdeu, mané" was inscribed in red (using lipstick or marker found in government offices) across the chest of the statue. The choice of lipstick is significant—lipstick as tool of feminine adornment, lipstick as writing instrument, lipstick's red color echoing both blood and revolutionary imagery.

**The Physical Domination**: Rioters climbed onto the statue, sitting in her lap and draping Brazilian flags around her neck. This physical act reversed the normal hierarchy where the citizen stands below, gazing upward in awe. By sitting on her, they performed a kind of violation, a physical reversal of the respectful distance normally maintained toward monumental figures.

**The Contextualization**: The phrase itself was crucial. "Perdeu, mané" was not a random insult but a direct quotation from Justice Luís Roberto Barroso. In November 2022, while in New York, Barroso was accosted by a Bolsonaro supporter questioning the election results. Barroso retorted dismissively: "Perdeu, mané, não amola" (You lost, fool, don't bother me). This phrase circulated widely on social media among the Bolsonaro movement, becoming a rallying cry of sorts. By inscribing it onto the statue, the rioters connected the abstract allegory to the specific, partisan judge.

### Counter-Literacy and Ventriloquism

The act must be understood as a sophisticated form of "counter-literacy"—the deployment of visual competence to delegitimize rather than sustain state authority. The rioters possessed a high degree of visual literacy; they knew exactly which symbols embodied the power they sought to dismantle. They understood the architecture of legal legitimacy.

Specifically, they engaged in what this project terms **iconoclastic ventriloquism**. In ventriloquism, a performer throws their voice, making it appear that a dummy or puppet is speaking. The rioters performed a similar semiotic operation: they inscribed Barroso's voice onto the body of the statue, forcing Justice to speak as a partisan player rather than an abstract ideal.

This act accomplished something remarkable: it **collapsed the distinction between the Two Bodies of the Law**—a concept this research extends from Kantorowicz's medieval political theology.

The Law, like the medieval Monarch, possesses two bodies:

- **The Body Politic**: Abstract, eternal, incorruptible. In this case, the ideal of Justice itself—blind, impartial, above the fray.
- **The Body Natural**: Concrete, mortal, fallible. In this case, specific judges like Luís Roberto Barroso—human beings with biases, resentments, partisan allegiances.

Modern legal systems depend on maintaining the distinction between these two bodies. Citizens are meant to obey the law (the Body Politic) even when they disagree with specific judges' decisions (the Body Natural). The judge is merely the vehicle through which eternal Justice speaks.

By writing Barroso's contemptuous words onto Justice's chest, the rioters forcibly merged these two bodies. They revealed that the "eternal Justice" was actually dependent on the partisan judge. They exposed that what appeared to be transcendent authority was actually located in mortal, fallible human beings.

The effect was devastating to the statue's legitimacy. It was no longer possible to see *A Justiça* as merely representing abstract justice. She had become a proxy for Barroso, a representation of his arrogance, his dismissal of citizens' concerns.

### The Gendered and Raced Dimensions

The choice to use lipstick as the instrument of graffiti added layers of meaning. Lipstick is a tool associated with feminine self-fashioning and adornment. By using lipstick on a female figure, the rioters reclaimed the "feminine surface" for a raw, visceral message of rejection. They refused the passive role of beholding beauty and instead used femininity as a tool for writing.

Moreover, lipstick is a tool of visible, public self-presentation. It leaves a mark; it announces itself. In contrast to other forms of graffiti using spray paint or paint markers, lipstick is associated with the intimate and the personal, yet here it was deployed for a very public political message.

The targeting of Di Cavalcanti's *As Mulatas* (stabbed repeatedly) and Marianne Peretti's *Araguaia* stained glass (damaged) reveals that the rioters understood Brasília's artistic program as a unified system of gendered and racialized symbolism. They were not merely attacking individual objects but the comprehensive visual ideology that legitimated the state—an ideology that had depended on the female form, the white allegorical body, and modernist abstraction to make authority appear natural and inevitable.

### The State's Response: Restoration as Re-consecration

The Brazilian state's response to the vandalism confirms the statue's centrality to legal legitimacy.

Débora Rodrigues dos Santos, identified as the woman who inscribed the graffiti, was arrested and prosecuted. The charges were extraordinary: crimes including "violent abolition of the democratic rule of law," "coup d'état," "damage to protected heritage," and "deterioration of listed property." She was sentenced to 14 years in prison.

This extraordinarily harsh sentence—far exceeding typical penalties for vandalism or property damage, comparable to sentences for violent assault—underscores a crucial legal reality: **attacking the image of the state is prosecuted as equivalent to attacking the state itself.** The lipstick graffiti was not treated as property damage but as an act of political violence, a threat to the very foundations of state authority.

The statue was swiftly cleaned and restored. The restoration was rapid, professional, ritualized. The government could not allow the "scar" of the graffiti to remain permanently visible, as it would have transformed the statue from a closed, authoritative symbol into an open, contested text. The scar would have fixed an alternative meaning onto the stone.

The restoration can be understood as a ritual of re-consecration—an attempt to erase the counter-literacy and restore the silent authority of stone, to return the statue to its position as a symbol speaking only in the language of abstract justice rather than partisan politics.

However, the restoration was not entirely successful. Images of the defaced statue circulated globally through digital media. These images now form part of the statue's meaning. Even after cleaning, the "ghost" of the graffiti haunts the restored stone, teaching new generations to read state iconography suspiciously and to recognize that such symbols can be transformed through acts of counter-literacy.

## VI. COMPARATIVE ICONOCLASMS: WHEN THE ALLEGORY COMES TO LIFE

### The Marianne Stamp Controversy (2013)

The tension inherent in the female allegory becomes most visible in moments when the "stone allegory" encounters the "living woman." A particularly illuminating case study is the 2013 French stamp controversy.

In 2013, the French postal service released a new postage stamp depicting Marianne, the embodiment of the French Republic and one of the world's most iconic images. The artist, Olivier Ciappa, revealed that the model for the new Marianne was Inna Shevchenko, a leading activist of FEMEN, the radical feminist protest group known for topless demonstrations.

Shevchenko had achieved international notoriety for her activism, including actions such as sawing down Orthodox crosses in Ukraine and disrupting various political and religious events across Europe. She used her body as a weapon—literally baring her breasts, which are often marked with slogans or painted messages, to make her political points.

The revelation that the symbol of the French Republic was modeled on Shevchenko sparked immediate controversy. Conservative critics erupted in fury. How could the sacred symbol of the Republic be modeled on a "radical," "disorderly," "indecent" woman? How could a woman known for topless protests embody the values of the French state?

The controversy perfectly crystallizes the logic of the visual sexual contract:

1. **The state needs the female image.** Marianne must appear on stamps, coins, and official seals to make the Republic visible, recognizable, present to the people.

2. **But the state fears the real female political agent.** When the abstract, controlled, "Stone Marianne" is revealed to be the embodied, political, transgressive "Living Shevchenko," the contract fractures.

3. **The allegory only functions when the woman is abstract, silent, and contained.** A real woman who is vocal, political, and willing to transgress the boundaries of feminine decorum threatens the entire system. She cannot be safely monumentalized, controlled, or rendered eternal.

The controversy reveals that there is a fundamental incompatibility between the allegorical woman and the political woman. The state can tolerate women as symbols; it cannot tolerate women as agents. When a living woman claims the iconic role, the symbolic order breaks down.

### The Thatcher/Britannia Satire

Similarly, the project examines how Margaret Thatcher, Britain's Prime Minister from 1979 to 1990, was depicted as Britannia during the Falkland Islands War (1982). During that period, when Thatcher's popularity was high and her military decisiveness was celebrated, she was frequently depicted in political cartoons as Britannia—the female warrior figure with trident and shield, symbol of British naval power and imperial dominance.

The association empowered her. As "Britannia," Thatcher was elevated to the status of national symbol, eternal and transcendent. She was no longer merely a politician but the embodiment of British greatness itself.

However, as her popularity waned in the latter 1980s and especially after she was ousted from office in 1990, the Britannia comparison became ironic and satirical. Cartoonists would depict her as a grotesque, aging Britannia, suggesting she had overstayed her allegorical role, that she was clinging to a symbolic position to which she had no claim.

This demonstrates the double-edged nature of the allegorical weapon: **it can confer temporary authority, but it can also be used to mock women who dare to step from the symbolic realm into the political realm.** The message is clear: women can represent power, but they must not actually exercise it. When they do, they become subject to grotesque satire.

## VII. ICONOGRAPHY RECONSTRUCTED: METHODOLOGY FOR LEGAL HISTORY

### The Iconographic Reconstruction Protocol

This project proposes a rigorous methodological protocol for legal historians to analyze images as primary legal sources. The protocol treats visual artifacts with the same intellectual rigor traditionally applied to written texts.

#### Step 1: Iconographic Description

The first step is careful, detailed description of what is present and, critically, what is **absent**.

For *A Justiça*, this means:
- **Present**: Blindfold, sword, seated posture, smooth geometric form, monumental scale
- **Absent**: Scales (historically integral to Justice iconography)

This difference between traditional Justice imagery and Ceschiatti's version is not minor. It is iconographically significant. The absence of the scales removes the visual representation of deliberation, judgment, weighing of evidence—the intellectual and comparative dimensions of justice.

Similarly, in analyzing coins and stamps, one notes:
- What objects are depicted?
- What colors are used?
- What inscriptions appear?
- What is the relationship between figures (are they central or peripheral)?
- What is notably missing from the image?

#### Step 2: Pedagogy of Encounter

The second step analyzes the material and spatial conditions of encounter. How and where does the citizen encounter this image?

A coin is encountered in the palm of the hand—intimate, tactile, repeated. A citizen touches Marianne hundreds of times across a lifetime of market transactions. The image enters the body through touch and habituation.

A statue is encountered from below, requiring the viewer to look upward—creating an architectural and affective relationship that positions the allegory as superior, dominating, worthy of upward gaze.

A stamp is encountered on personal correspondence—small, portable, global. It travels across oceans, carrying the image of the nation into foreign spaces.

A courthouse pediment is encountered upon arrival at a specific institutional space—signaling entry into a domain governed by law.

These different modalities of encounter produce different effects. The coin teaches intimacy and ubiquity. The statue teaches superiority and awe. The stamp teaches global reach. The pediment teaches institutional authority.

#### Step 3: Intervisual Genealogy

The third step traces the motif across time and space. Does the Brazilian coin copy a French model? Does Ceschiatti's abstract seated figure cite Renaissance representations of Justice? Does the blindfold reference ancient representations of Themis?

Tracing genealogies reveals how iconographic vocabularies circulate globally, how local variations encode specific political meanings, how national allegories are adaptations of international templates.

#### Step 4: Gendered (and Raced) Operation

The fourth step analyzes how the femininity (and in some cases, racial characteristics) of the figure functions. Does it solicit love? Does it promise protection? Does it mask violence through the association with motherhood?

For instance, the "maternalization" of Marianne and Britannia—depicting them as maternal, nurturing figures—serves specific ideological functions. It suggests that the state cares for its citizens as a mother cares for children. It naturalizes subordination (citizens are like children; the state is like a mother). It creates an affective relationship that exceeds mere allegiance to law.

#### Step 5: Contestation Analysis

Finally, one analyzes how the image has been contested, mocked, satirized, or vandalized. These acts of counter-literacy reveal how the image's meaning is unstable, how it can be rewritten, how its authority is vulnerable.

The January 8 graffiti is an act of contestation analysis—it reveals that Ceschiatti's Justice can be transformed into a partisan political figure through a simple inscription. The Marianne/Shevchenko controversy reveals the incompatibility between allegorical woman and political woman. The Thatcher/Britannia satire reveals how the allegorical weapon can turn against those who wield it.

### The Corpus: Four "Supports" with Distinct Circulation Logics

This research constructs a comparative corpus examining four distinct media through which legal-allegorical knowledge circulated:

#### 1. Monetary Iconography (1789-present)

Coins and banknotes represent the "intimate ubiquity" of the state. Every time a citizen buys bread, they touch the allegory. Every market transaction reproduces state symbolism at the most intimate level. The numismatic evidence reveals which allegorical forms were considered most important to circulate, how these forms changed over time, and how local variations encoded political meaning.

Research includes:
- French francs and euros featuring Marianne
- British pounds featuring Britannia
- Swiss francs featuring Helvetia
- Brazilian reais and earlier currency featuring the Republican allegory

#### 2. Postal/Philatelic Iconography (1850-present)

Stamps are "global communicators" of national virtues. They travel across borders, carrying state imagery into foreign spaces. They are small enough to be portable but formal enough to carry official authority.

Research includes:
- French stamps depicting Marianne in different eras
- British stamps with Britannia
- The controversial 2013 Marianne stamp modeled on Shevchenko
- Brazilian stamps and postal imagery

#### 3. Monumental Programs (1789-present)

Statues and courthouse pediments operate through "vertical domination." They occupy public space, demanding attention, encoding hierarchy through physical presence.

Research includes:
- Ceschiatti's *A Justiça* (Brazil, 1961)
- Marianne monuments across France
- Britannia monuments in Britain
- Germania monuments in German-speaking lands
- Courthouse architectural programs in multiple nations

#### 4. Legal Paratexts (1789-present)

Official seals, letterheads, and code frontispieces are the "authenticating images" that give documents legal force. They appear on legal instruments, making them official and authoritative.

Research includes:
- Frontispieces of the Napoleonic Code and subsequent legal codes
- Official seals of courts and legal institutions
- Letterheads of legal bodies
- Stamps and authenticating marks on documents

## VIII. CONTEMPORARY RELEVANCE: THE FRAGILITY OF VISUAL LEGITIMACY

### The Crisis of the Two Bodies

The January 8 attacks revealed what scholars of iconoclasm have long understood: **images constitute power; they do not merely represent it.** When rioters defaced *A Justiça*, they demonstrated that the "infrastructural competence" sustaining modern legal orders is profoundly fragile.

The background knowledge that causes citizens to trust, obey, and navigate legal systems—the shared belief that the statue is Justice, that the building is the Court, that violence enacted within those walls is legitimate rather than criminal—can evaporate in moments of political crisis.

Moreover, the age of digital reproduction has transformed the politics of iconoclasm. In previous eras, vandalism to a statue was localized, visible primarily to those in the immediate vicinity. Now, images of the defaced statue circulate globally through social media. These images become part of the statue's permanent meaning. Even after restoration, the "ghost" of the graffiti haunts the stone, teaching new generations to read state symbols as contested rather than settled.

### Counter-Literacy and the Erosion of Consensus

The emergence of counter-literacy—sophisticated visual competence deployed to delegitimize rather than sustain state authority—represents a significant historical shift. In the 19th and early 20th centuries, most populations lacked the visual competence to "read" state symbolism critically. They accepted the statue as Justice because they lacked the conceptual tools to interrogate it.

Now, particularly among populations that have received formal visual education (through media literacy, digital culture, art history instruction), the ability to decode and rewrite visual messages is widespread. Citizens understand that images are constructed, that they serve ideological functions, that they can be transformed.

When populations gain this counter-literacy, the allegorical system becomes vulnerable. The statue ceases to be a sacred embodiment of justice and becomes legible as a political trick, a gendered performance, a simulacrum concealing rather than revealing power operations.

### The Racial Reckoning

The targeting of Di Cavalcanti's *As Mulatas* and the emphasis on Ceschiatti's white, Europeanized *A Justiça* reveals another dimension of the contemporary crisis: a racial reckoning with the allegorical system itself.

The female allegory was never truly universal. It was always Europeanized, whitened, Greco-Roman. In colonial and postcolonial contexts, this meant that the face of the nation was explicitly a face that excluded most of the population from recognition.

Contemporary anti-racist politics increasingly demands that the state's visual infrastructure be confronted and transformed. The vandalism of the statues was, in some sense, a demand that the state stop representing itself through the bodies of white women and begin to grapple with the question: **What would a truly democratic, inclusive visual representation of the state look like?**

## IX. CONCLUSION: LEGAL HISTORY'S VISUAL TURN

### The Task Before Us

This project argues that legal historians must fundamentally reconceptualize their object of study. **Legal literacy extends far beyond the ability to read statutes.** It encompasses the historically situated capacity to recognize, trust, and navigate legality through material encounters with images, spaces, and objects.

The female allegory was not decorative; it was foundational. It taught citizens "what law looks like" while simultaneously masking who law actually served. For two centuries, the stone goddess stood at the entrance to courts across the Atlantic world, mute, timeless, promising that the violence enacted within was justice rather than brute force.

That two-century reign is ending. The goddess has been made to speak—to utter partisan words, to reveal her complicity with specific judges rather than transcendent ideals. She cannot be unmade to speak. Even after restoration, the graffiti haunts her.

### The Fragility of the Stone State

The project concludes that the foundations of modern legal legitimacy are more fragile than contemporary legal theory acknowledges. Law requires more than written texts and institutional structures. It requires **belief**—belief materialized in stone, circulated on coins, stamped on letters, carved into courthouse pediments. It requires the kind of visual, affective literacy that makes citizens willingly subject themselves to legal authority.

When that belief fractures—when populations gain counter-literacy, when the statue can be rewritten with alternative meanings, when the "Two Bodies" collapse into singular, mortal form—the legal order becomes nakedly visible as a structure of force rather than authority.

The question that haunted the revolutionaries of 1789 returns with renewed urgency: **Without a visible, believable sovereign, how can power be recognized as legitimate?**

The old answers—the allegorical women who stood guard for two centuries—can no longer bear the weight of the question. They have been vandalized, defaced, interrogated. The statue becomes just a rock. The court becomes just a building. The law becomes just words.

The task of legal history in the 21st century is to understand how these visual-allegorical-affective infrastructures were built, how they held the political world together across two centuries of colonial and postcolonial rule, how they masked gendered and racial exclusions while claiming universality, and why they are now cracking under pressure from populations who have learned to read the goddesses back.
