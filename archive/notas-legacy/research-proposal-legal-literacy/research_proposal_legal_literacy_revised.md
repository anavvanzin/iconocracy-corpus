# Iconographic Simplification and Allegorical Universalization
## Female Allegories as Media of Legal Knowledge in Brazil and Europe (c. 1789–Present)

## Introduction

This proposal advances a legal-historical inquiry into a recurring modern scene: the law appearing as a woman. In Europe and in Brazil, Justice, Liberty, and the Nation have been rendered as feminized figures on coins, banknotes, seals, court buildings, civic monuments, and state pedagogical materials. These images do more than decorate institutions. They teach: they train expectations about impartiality, sovereignty, equality, sacrifice, and belonging. They also invite a specific kind of recognition, in which the authority of legal order is encountered through a body that is rhetorically “for everyone” precisely because it is allegorical.

The project treats this pedagogical work as a core problem of legal literacy. Legal literacy is understood here as a cluster of capacities and dispositions through which actors navigate normative life: knowledge of formal rules and rights, familiarity with institutional practices, and a background sense of what law is and what it demands. Such literacy is cultivated through diverse media, not through statutes alone. The proposal therefore asks how feminized allegories functioned as media of legal knowledge from the era of revolutionary codification to the present, in a comparative frame that moves between Europe and Brazil.

A contemporary Brazilian episode clarifies why the inquiry should reach the present. During the attacks on institutions in Brasília on 8 January 2023, works of art and symbolic objects associated with the judiciary and the constitutional order were vandalized. The granite statue *A Justiça* (1961), a seated, blindfolded female figure placed in front of the Supremo Tribunal Federal (STF), was defaced with graffiti. The incident is analytically important because it foregrounds a basic premise: legal authority is negotiated in the field of images as much as in the field of norms. A legal literacy approach can read such moments as crises of recognition and transmission, in which the destruction or profanation of legal personifications becomes a political intervention into the public’s sense of legality.

## Background of Legal Literacy

Legal literacy is often reduced, in everyday discourse, to an ability to understand written law. That reduction is itself part of the phenomenon under study. In modern legal culture, the prominence of codes, constitutions, and statutory language encourages a “text-first” imagination of legality, while masking the extent to which law is learned through rituals, spatial arrangements, symbols, and narratives. The project adopts a threefold view of legal knowledge.

First, black-letter law: the enacted or officially recognized norms that can be quoted, invoked, and challenged in formal registers. Second, practitioners’ law: the operational knowledge produced in institutional settings, such as courts, administrative agencies, police practices, and legal professions, including procedural routines and styles of authority. Third, background knowledge: the diffuse, socially shared sense of what counts as lawful, legitimate, or just, which is cultivated through education, public ceremonies, material culture, and visual regimes.

The central claim is that female allegories are a privileged vehicle of background legal knowledge, and that they often mediate between black-letter law and lived perceptions of legality. They translate abstraction into legibility. They also shape the affective tone of law, producing trust, awe, fear, reverence, or contempt.

## Importance of Female Allegories in Legal Knowledge

Female allegories perform two operations that are especially relevant to legal literacy.

The first is iconographic simplification. Modern legal orders had to make themselves graspable as “one” order in contexts in which legal relations were previously fragmented across corporate bodies, estates, confessional jurisdictions, and local privileges. Paolo Grossi describes the French revolutionary transformation as a drastic reduction of a complex polity to a compact, unitary structure, carried out by what he calls the “scythe of equality.” In his account, equality cuts through stratifications and differentiations by imposing simplified models of the legal person and the legal good, thereby sketching a new legal landscape marked by compactness and simplicity. This legal operation has a visual counterpart: the condensation of legality into stable figures and attributes that can circulate widely and endure.

The second is allegorical universalization. Allegory produces an abstract subject by presenting a body that is rhetorically available to all because it is not “social” in the ordinary sense. Marianne is not a French woman; she is “France” as a civic and legal form. Lady Justice is not a judge; she is the promise that judging can be impartial. This universality, however, is never neutral. Grossi himself notes that revolutionary equality made individuals “as if they were statues cast from the same mould,” while preserving as a salient distinction the division between men and women. The project takes that tension as structurally revealing: the feminized allegory offers the appearance of inclusive universality while enabling the management of sexed hierarchy in the distribution of power, authority, and political voice.

In short, female allegories are not ancillary to law. They are a stabilizing medium through which legal knowledge is expressed, transmitted, contested, and sometimes attacked.

## Historical Context: From Revolutionary Abstraction to Contemporary Contestation

The project’s historical arc begins in the late eighteenth century because that moment crystallizes a new style of legal and political abstraction. Revolutionary egalitarianism, codification, and nation-building demanded a legal subject stripped of corporate thickness and reinserted into a national macro-community. Across the nineteenth century, liberal constitutionalism, imperial projects, and the consolidation of state bureaucracies expanded the need for public-facing representations of legality and sovereignty. National personifications proliferated: Marianne, Germania, Britannia, Italia Turrita, and others. Simultaneously, the allegory of Justice migrated and stabilized across legal architectures and printed legal cultures.

Brazil offers an especially productive comparative partner because it presents a distinct trajectory of constitutional form and political rupture combined with intense iconographic borrowing and adaptation. The transition from colony to empire (1822), the proclamation of the Republic (1889), twentieth-century authoritarian and democratic cycles, and the invention of Brasília as a new capital in the mid-twentieth century each reorganized the relationship between law, public space, and symbolic authority. The modernist aesthetics of Brasília, including Ceschiatti’s *A Justiça* in front of the STF, condense a specific promise: that the law can be impersonal, rational, and above faction. When such an image is profaned in a political assault on institutions, the act stages a conflict over the very readability of law.

Extending the timeline to the present allows the project to test a crucial hypothesis: that legal allegories remain operative as background knowledge even after the decline of classical allegorical language in many artistic domains. They persist because they are embedded in institutions, reproduced in civic rituals, and reiterated through material supports that structure public perception.

## Literature Review

### Existing Research on Legal Literacy

Work on legal literacy has moved from narrow definitions of rule-awareness toward broader accounts of how legal knowledge guides actors in daily life. Socio-legal studies have examined how people learn to “speak law” through encounters with institutions, through media narratives, and through community practices. In legal history, scholarship has traced the circulation of legal ideas through manuals, schooling, catechisms of citizenship, and popular print. Yet visual media are still often treated as illustrative rather than constitutive. This project treats images as a site in which legal knowledge is made operational.

### Female Allegories in Art History, Political Theory, and Legal Studies

Art historical scholarship has offered rich inventories of allegorical motifs and has mapped the iconographic grammar of national personifications and Lady Justice. Marina Warner’s work remains central for understanding how feminine personifications legitimize authority while constraining women’s political agency. Iconological approaches, from Panofsky onward, provide tools to connect formal elements to cultural meanings and to reconstruct the historical conditions of intelligibility.

In legal studies, scholarship on “law and the visual” has argued that images do not merely represent law but participate in legal epistemology and authority. Peter Goodrich’s work on legal emblems and legal aesthetics, as well as research in legal iconography more broadly, has shown that visual forms can be normative devices, shaping the way law is perceived and remembered.

The project’s originality lies in its synthesis: it treats female allegory as a privileged medium in the formation of legal literacy, and it moves beyond a Europe-only narrative by treating Brazil as a full comparative site rather than a derivative appendix.

### Comparative Work: Brazil and Europe

Brazilian historiography has examined republican symbols, the circulation of French models, and the cultural politics of nation-building, including the adoption and adaptation of European iconographic repertoires. Comparative work that directly links these visual forms to transformations in legal knowledge remains limited. This project addresses that gap by reconstructing pathways of transfer, translation, and institutional embedding across the Atlantic, while resisting a one-directional diffusion story. Brazil is treated as a site of creative resemanticization, in which allegories are retooled to address local constitutional imaginaries, racialized and classed social orders, and distinct narratives of modernity.

## Research Objectives

### Primary Objectives

1. To reconstruct how female allegories (Justice, Liberty, Nation/Republic) operated as media of legal knowledge across institutional settings in Brazil and selected European contexts.
2. To theorize iconographic simplification and allegorical universalization as two mechanisms through which visual forms cultivate legal literacy as background knowledge.
3. To analyze the gendered politics of legal universality in the interaction between feminized symbols and the distribution of legal and political power.

### Secondary Objectives

1. To map transatlantic circulations of motifs (attributes, poses, inscriptions, materials, spatial placements) between Europe and Brazil.
2. To trace moments of contestation, appropriation, or iconoclasm, including contemporary attacks on legal symbols, as indicators of conflict over legal authority.
3. To produce a curated corpus and an interpretive protocol that can be used for further research and teaching.

## Research Questions

### Key Questions to Explore

1. How do female allegories translate abstract legal concepts into legible public knowledge, and which forms of legal knowledge do they primarily cultivate (formal, professional, background)?
2. How does revolutionary and post-revolutionary simplification of legal personhood and property find a visual counterpart in the stabilization of allegorical repertoires?
3. How does the feminization of legal universality function politically: what does it authorize, obscure, or displace in terms of inclusion, representation, and authority?

### Sub-questions for In-depth Analysis

1. Which attributes and compositional choices recur across contexts (blindfold, scales, sword, Phrygian cap, laurel, fasces, shields, inscriptions), and what legal meanings do they communicate?
2. How do institutional settings (courthouses, parliamentary buildings, city halls, currency systems, schoolbooks) shape the interpretive horizon of allegorical figures?
3. How do legal reforms (constitutions, codes, judicial reorganizations) coincide with shifts in allegorical imagery, and how do images anticipate, echo, or stabilize those reforms?
4. How are European motifs translated in Brazil: what is preserved, what is altered, and what new tensions emerge in a postcolonial and later republican setting?
5. How do episodes of iconoclasm or profanation (such as the defacement of *A Justiça* in 2023) illuminate crises of legal literacy and struggles over legitimacy?

## Methodology

### Research Design

The project combines comparative legal history with iconological analysis. It treats visual artefacts as primary sources for legal knowledge formation, and it reads them in relation to normative texts and institutional practices. The comparative design pairs European and Brazilian case clusters around moments of constitutional refoundation, codification, regime change, or intensified public pedagogy.

The European component is organized around a focused set of jurisdictions and symbols, selected for their centrality to modern legal iconography and for the richness of available sources: France (Marianne and Justice), a German-speaking context (Germania and related legal personifications), and one further comparator (Britain’s Britannia or Italy’s Italia Turrita), depending on source accessibility for the Summer Academy timeframe.

The Brazilian component is organized around republican imagery (the effigy of the Republic on currency and seals), the iconography of Justice in institutional architecture and printed legal culture, and Brasília’s symbolic landscape, including *A Justiça* as a contemporary hinge.

### Data Collection Methods

1. Visual sources: coins, banknotes, seals, coats of arms, courthouse programmes, official prints, monuments, and architectural iconographies. Sources will be gathered from museums and digital repositories, numismatic collections, state archives, and institutional websites, with careful recording of provenance, commissioning bodies, and circulation.
2. Textual sources: constitutional texts, civil codes, legislative debates, decrees, court ceremonial guidelines, archival correspondence about commissions and iconographic programmes, and contemporaneous press commentary on public monuments and symbols.
3. Contemporary documentation: reliable journalistic and institutional records concerning the 8 January 2023 attacks, with attention to how legal symbols were described, targeted, and interpreted in public discourse.

### Data Analysis Techniques

The analysis proceeds through a structured interpretive protocol that keeps comparison disciplined while remaining qualitative.

1. Systematic description: each object is described in a consistent format (material, scale, composition, attributes, inscriptions, spatial placement, intended audience). A descriptive sheet ensures comparability across media.
2. Contextual reconstruction: for each object, the institutional setting is reconstructed (commissioning authority, intended function, legal and political moment of installation or circulation). The aim is to prevent the allegory from floating free of legal history.
3. Iconological interpretation: the project reconstructs how the object teaches legality. Here the two central mechanisms are operationalized.
   4. Iconographic simplification is assessed by identifying what complexities are condensed and which features are rendered salient for public recognition.
   5. Allegorical universalization is assessed by examining how a universal legal subject is staged, which exclusions are masked, and how gender operates as a structural remainder.

The project incorporates Georges Didi-Huberman’s methodological attention to temporal montage. Motifs are treated as historically active reactivations rather than timeless survivals: when an older figure returns, the analysis asks what work that return performs under new legal and political pressures.

### Conceptual Framework: Iconocracy and Iconoclasm

To interpret moments in which legal symbols become targets, the project draws on Marie-José Mondzain’s analysis of Byzantine iconoclasm and her concept of iconocracy as an economy of images. The key insight is not that images are mere instruments of persuasion, but that struggles over images are struggles over the distribution of authority, truth, and visibility. From this perspective, iconoclasm is not simply destruction; it is an intervention into the public’s regime of intelligibility. When a legal allegory is defaced, the act can be read as an attempt to interrupt the transmission of legal knowledge that the image supports, or to replace that knowledge with another account of legitimacy.

In the Brazilian 2023 episode, the defacement of *A Justiça* can therefore be approached as a conflict over the symbolic conditions under which law is recognized as binding. The framework allows the project to connect long legal histories of personification to present-day struggles over institutions, without treating the contemporary as merely anecdotal.

## Anticipated Results

### Expected Contributions to the Field

First, the project proposes a legal-historical account of legal literacy that takes visual media seriously as a formative site of legal knowledge. It shows how law becomes “learnable” through images embedded in material infrastructures of public life.

Second, it offers two transferable analytical concepts, iconographic simplification and allegorical universalization, designed for legal history rather than for art history alone. These concepts illuminate how modern legal orders produce legibility and consent, while also identifying the gendered contradictions within that production.

Third, it develops a genuinely comparative transatlantic narrative. By reconstructing transfers and resemanticizations between Europe and Brazil, it adds empirical depth to claims about global modernity and the circulation of constitutional symbols.

Finally, by extending to the present, it demonstrates that legal allegories remain active in the public sphere and can become flashpoints in conflicts over authority. Contemporary iconoclasm becomes, in this framework, a diagnostic of legal literacy crises: what is attacked is not only stone or bronze, but a publicly taught image of legality.

### Implications for Legal Education and Literacy

The project’s findings have implications for civic education and for the public communication of law. If legal literacy is cultivated through symbols and spaces as well as through texts, then legal education can profit from a critical visual pedagogy that teaches audiences how legal authority is staged. Such pedagogy can also expose exclusions that have been naturalized through universalizing imagery. In practical terms, the project can inform museum interpretation, heritage policy, and public-facing legal institutions that rely on symbolic environments to cultivate trust.

## Conclusion

### Summary of Key Points

This proposal argues that female allegories are a stabilized medium through which legal knowledge has been expressed, transmitted, and contested from the age of revolutionary abstraction to contemporary crises of legitimacy. By theorizing iconographic simplification and allegorical universalization, and by comparing European and Brazilian trajectories, the project reconstructs legal literacy as a historically produced relation between norms, institutions, and visual regimes. The inclusion of contemporary contestation, including the defacement of *A Justiça* in 2023, strengthens rather than dilutes the historical argument by showing that legal personifications remain operative in the public understanding of law.

### Future Research Directions

The Summer Academy project is designed as a focused study with expandable avenues. Future work could extend the inquiry to popular visual cultures (caricature, advertising, film, digital memes), to other postcolonial contexts, and to the contemporary politics of online image economies. Such expansions would further test how legal literacy is cultivated, contested, and reconfigured as media environments change.
