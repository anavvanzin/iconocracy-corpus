Title

The Allegorical Body of Law: Legal Literacy, Civic Pedagogy, and Female Personifications from Revolutionary France to Republican Brazil

Abstract

This proposal approaches legal literacy as a historically situated repertoire of competences through which actors recognise, interpret, and negotiate legal authority in everyday life. Such competences are not acquired solely through the mastery of statutes or doctrinal formulations. They are also cultivated through repeated encounters with institutional images, civic rituals, material artefacts, and the spatial dramaturgy of public power. Female allegories such as Justice, Marianne, and Germania constitute a particularly resilient visual lexicon of legality. Across media and sites, they condense complex norms into stable, reiterable forms that invite immediate recognition and, in so doing, help to naturalise specific configurations of authority.

The core argument is that these allegorical bodies operate as a gendered technology of modernity. Modern political imagination recurrently mobilises the feminine as a privileged surface upon which universality, rationality, equilibrium, and civic virtue can be inscribed. The allegorical woman becomes rhetorically shareable precisely because she is neither socially determinate nor biographically legible. Yet that very abstraction coexists with, and may even help to occlude, historically uneven distributions of citizenship and institutional voice. Rather than treating this tension as a mere irony, the project treats it as a diagnostic of how legal knowledge is stabilised, circulated, and contested.

The study advances a comparative legal-historical inquiry across France, Germany, and Brazil. Empirically, it will analyse, in each setting, a small but telling set of objects and supports through which legality becomes publicly legible: in France, Marianne and Justice across town-hall and school iconographies as well as everyday state media such as stamps and coinage; in Germany, Germania in unification-era monumental programmes and reproducible commemorative media; in Brazil, republican personifications of the Republic and Justice in courthouse statuary and in reproducible official artefacts such as seals, banknotes, and educational materials. It concentrates on formative episodes of civic pedagogy in which regimes deliberately codified their visual languages, then tracks the afterlives of those codifications across subsequent political rearticulations. The aim is to reconstruct how allegorical personifications function as media of legal knowledge, how they shape background expectations about legality, and how their claims to neutrality become politically vulnerable when the meaning of the legal order is disputed.

1. Introduction: Legal literacy beyond the statute book

Legal literacy is often reduced, in policy and pedagogical discourse, to the ability to read the law in a narrowly textual sense. A legal-historical perspective suggests a broader, and analytically more fruitful, account. In most modern polities, the public’s orientation to legality is mediated by a composite of cues that are visual, spatial, and ritual as much as they are textual. People learn what law is, what law promises, and what law authorises through repeated exposure to institutional scenography: courthouse façades and interiors, municipal iconography, civic ceremonies, schoolroom emblems, currency, stamps, seals, and the everyday material culture of the state.

This proposal isolates a specific vehicle within that composite: the allegorical female body. From the late eighteenth century onward, Justice, the Republic, Liberty, and the Nation are repeatedly personified as women. Their iconographic attributes produce a grammar of recognisability: the scales and blindfold as the promise of impartial adjudication; the sword as coercive capacity and sovereign decision; the Phrygian cap as revolutionary legitimacy; the laurel as civic virtue; the seated posture as permanence and institutional continuity. Such figures teach legality at a glance, functioning as mnemonic devices through which complex political settlements become socially intelligible.

The inquiry is thus twofold. First, why does modern legality persistently select the feminine body as a privileged interface for abstract authority? Second, what kind of legal literacy is produced when the public learns legality through an idealised body that is rhetorically universal yet socially detached from the lives of actual women?

2. Conceptual orientation: modernity, gender, and the allegorical interface

2.1 Gender as a symbolic grammar of modern political narratives

A central premise is that modernity is not only a chronological designation but also a narrative form, articulated through recurrent symbolic oppositions that are themselves gender-coded. Accounts of modern life frequently align public reason, order, progress, and universality with a masculine-coded register, while assigning affect, tradition, particularity, and the domestic to a feminine-coded register. In such configurations, gender functions less as demographic description than as a semiotic resource through which political authority becomes legible. For the purposes of this project, the relevance of this insight is methodological: it clarifies why the feminine can operate as a vehicle of universality in political iconography. The allegorical woman is a figure of abstraction, offered as common property precisely because she is detached from social specificity.

2.2 Iconographic simplification and the jurisprudence of equality

The project situates allegorical universalisation within the broader process by which modern legal orders simplify social complexity into a limited set of juridical categories. Revolutionary and post-revolutionary regimes are often characterised by the reduction of corporate, territorial, and professional stratifications in favour of standardised individual subjecthood. This reduction is frequently described through metaphors of cutting through inherited differentiations to produce a compact legal landscape governed by equality and unitary sovereignty. Iconographic simplification is the visual correlate of that operation. The more law seeks to present itself as universal, the more it depends on forms that are stable, transferable, and immediately recognisable.

In this sense, allegory does not merely adorn legality. It participates in the production of a public understanding of what legality is supposed to look like. The allegorical body becomes a visual analogue of the equal subject, and the reiteration of that body across civic media trains expectations about legitimacy, representation, and institutional neutrality.

2.3 Images as infrastructures of expectation

The proposal treats images as infrastructures of expectation rather than passive reflections. Reiterated visual forms can precede and shape what actors anticipate from institutions, providing a template through which the legal order is judged, trusted, or rejected. The project formulates this claim with restraint. It does not attribute causal primacy to images over legal practice. It argues, instead, that images stabilise background knowledge: shared, often tacit expectations regarding authority, procedure, and the normative promises attached to institutions.

2.4 Sequence, persistence, and afterlife

Comparative work of this kind requires a disciplined approach to temporal scale. The project therefore treats allegories as sequential solutions to recurring political problems. Recognisable motifs persist, migrate across media, and are periodically recalibrated in response to shifts in sovereignty, regime type, and political theology. Reading allegory as a sequence allows continuity and rupture to be analysed without collapsing historical specificity.

3. Scope and case selection

The comparative design is organised around three sites in which civic pedagogy is both visible and institutionally consequential.

3.1 France: the Third Republic as a programme of civic instruction

The French Third Republic provides the project’s primary laboratory because it explicitly teaches republican legality through an integrated visual programme. The Republic is installed in municipal architecture, school settings, civic ceremonies, stamps, coins, monuments, and the quotidian objects through which the state becomes familiar. Marianne thus operates as a public interface of legality, mediating between constitutional abstraction and everyday recognition. 

This case also permits contestation to be treated as an ordinary feature of political life rather than as an exceptional spectacle. Debates over secularism, clerical influence, republican legitimacy, and competing conceptions of the people shape the staging, regulation, and interpretation of republican imagery. The project will analyse these disputes as moments in which the work performed by allegory becomes conceptually visible.

3.2 Germany: Germania and the aesthetics of unity and memory

Germania offers a distinct configuration of personification. Her iconographic life intersects with unification, empire, and the production of national memory. This makes Germania a comparative counterpoint to Marianne, illuminating how a feminine national body can mediate sovereignty, unity, and historical time under different institutional arrangements. Germania allows the project to test whether allegorical legality is best understood as specifically republican pedagogy or, more broadly, as a technology by which modern political orders render authority publicly legible.

3.3 Brazil: early republican iconography and the afterlife of legitimacy

Brazil adds a third axis: a republic that inherits European iconographic repertoires while transforming them in a context marked by contested citizenship, institutional centralisation, and uneven access to political voice. The corpus will emphasise early republican representations of the Republic and Justice in monuments, official media, and educational materials, then trace how these forms persist and mutate across later political cycles. This design permits a controlled extension towards the present without making the analysis dependent upon a single contemporary incident.

4. Research objectives

Primary objective

To reconstruct how female allegories function as media of legal literacy by translating legal authority into a public visual vocabulary learned through repetition in civic space and everyday material culture.

Secondary objectives

To theorise how gendered symbolism helps modern law appear universal, rational, and impartial, and to analyse how this symbolic operation coexists with historically uneven distributions of citizenship and institutional voice.

To compare civic pedagogies of legality across France, Germany, and Brazil through a shared analytic focus on iconographic form, institutional placement, and material circulation.

To trace the persistence and recalibration of allegorical motifs across media and political conjunctures, treating continuity and contestation as joint features of legal meaning-making.

5. Research questions

Core question

How do female allegories teach legality, and why does modern law repeatedly select a feminine body as the public face of authority?

Guiding questions

How does Third Republic civic pedagogy mobilise Marianne across municipal, educational, and everyday state media to produce shared background knowledge of republican legality?

How does Germania mediate unity, sovereignty, and memory, and what does this reveal about personification beyond republicanism?

How does Brazilian republican imagery adapt and transform European repertoires, and how do these forms persist as legal-cultural cues across subsequent political rearticulations?

When allegories are contested, what is the object of contestation: the symbol, the institution it anchors, or the promise of universality the symbol performs?

6. Sources and corpus

The corpus will be curated to remain analytically tractable while permitting cross-media comparison. It will be organised in three categories.

Institutional images

Civic and judicial architecture, public monuments, town halls, school buildings, and their iconographic programmes, including placement, scale, and the choreography of encounter.

Everyday state media

Coins, stamps, official seals, paper money, civic emblems, educational and commemorative materials, and other reproducible artefacts that circulate legality in ordinary life.

Legal-cultural texts

Constitutional and statutory preambles, civic manuals, parliamentary and municipal debates relating to symbolic programmes, and press materials that interpret, defend, or dispute the figures.

7. Methodology and analytic protocol

The method combines close iconographic description with legal-historical contextualisation and controlled comparison.

First, each object will be described in terms of form and attribute: posture, dress, bodily gesture, tools, inscriptions, setting, and institutional placement.

Second, the object will be situated within a pedagogy of encounter: who sees it, in what setting, under what institutional conditions, and with what implied lesson about authority and legality.

Third, the object will be read as part of a sequence: what prior motifs it echoes, what it simplifies, what it intensifies, and what political problem it appears to address.

Fourth, the analysis will specify the gendered operation: how femininity is mobilised to perform universality, to naturalise authority, or to aestheticise rule as equilibrium.

Fifth, contestation will be treated as a diagnostic register. Satire, reappropriation, replacement, and defacement will be analysed as practices that disclose the work performed by allegory when its claim to neutrality becomes untenable.

8. Operational concepts and reconstruction strategy

Iconographic simplification

The condensation of complex legal and political ideas into a restricted repertoire of stable figures and attributes that can be recognised across media and repeated over time.

Allegorical universalisation

The production of an abstract legal subject through a body that is rhetorically available to all precisely because it is allegorical rather than socially determinate.

Civic pedagogy

The institutional distribution of signs of legitimacy across public space and everyday objects, producing background expectations about authority that operate prior to doctrinal comprehension.

Background knowledge of legality

The shared, often tacit expectations that guide how actors recognise lawful authority, evaluate institutional legitimacy, and interpret what law is for.

9. Comparative plan

The comparison proceeds through shared analytic questions rather than through forced equivalences. Each case will be treated as a distinct civic pedagogy of legality.

France

Analysis of Third Republic municipal and educational iconographies, the distribution of Marianne across everyday state media, and contemporary interpretive disputes that illuminate how republican legality is taught and policed.

Germany

Analysis of Germania’s repertoire in relation to unification, imperial sovereignty, and memory production, mapping how a feminine national body mediates unity and legitimacy.

Brazil

Analysis of early republican personifications of the Republic and Justice across monuments, official media, and educational materials, then a controlled tracing of afterlives in later republican cycles.

10. Expected contribution and feasibility

Contribution

The project contributes to legal history by specifying legal literacy as a public competence grounded in visual and material cues, supplementing text-centred accounts of legal knowledge. It also offers a gender-sensitive explanation of how modern legality stages universality, showing why the feminine allegory becomes a privileged interface for abstract authority and why that interface remains politically vulnerable.

Feasibility

The corpus is manageable because it draws on reproducible public media, much of which is accessible through national archives, museum collections, numismatic databases, and digitised civic and educational sources. The analytic protocol is scalable. Within a summer academy format, the project can produce a focused paper based on a bounded corpus while also generating an expandable catalogue of images that can be redeployed in longer doctoral writing.
