Legal Literacy and the Visual-Dogmatic Regimes: Shaping and Shattering Legal Knowledge in Brazil (1889–2023)  
Project Summary by Ana Vitória Vanzin Mendes[^1]  
1. Project Rationale  
Law, like language, emerges historically from the shared practices, expectations, and interpretations of a community, so it can be understood as a knowledge-based social practice through which people collectively produce, stabilise, and contest what is considered valid norms and rights (Savigny 1814), (Silbey 2019, 1–18), (von Benda-Beckmann and Turner 2018, 255–274)[^2]. It operates through reflexivity (Legendre 2018, 107), and beneath the cold glass of modern institutions, lies the fabric of text, speech and imagery, *the soul and the fate* of society (Goodrich 1997, 258)[^3] (Legendre 1997, 143)[^4].   
Legal historians are brought upon the uncanny task of excavating the mythological, unconscious, and structural foundations that enable a society to reproduce and establish life (*vitam instituere*)[^5]. 
Undoubtedly, however tempting and rewarding it is to adopt an interdisciplinary approach, one must always be methodologically careful to understand the complexities that guide actors across time and space (Duve 2022a, 2022b)[^6].  
When it comes to the ontological instance of legal knowledge, its means of (re)construction and transmission, legal literacy is a historically contingent competence through which actors recognise, interpret, and authorise Law via texts, images, objects, spaces, and rituals (Martyn and Sontag 2021, 697–748)[^7] (Goodrich 2013, 90)[^8] and directly challenges the traditional view of legality as separate from social and cultural dynamics and their agents.   
Modern constitutional power relies on a public semiotics, an economy of images and artefacts that stabilises legitimacy while allowing alternative interpretations that may challenge institutional authority. During episodes of social commotion, legal insignias, documents, and sites act as writable surfaces, turning contestation into claims to re-authorise authority itself. The 8 January 2023 attempted coup, as an empirical anchor and “war of representation”, shows a clash between competing regimes of reading and Law enforcement, marking a notable literacy event in legal history for the digital era (Legendre 1997, 253)[^9].   
Brazil serves as a compelling case because the idea of a Brazilian Nation and Republic developed through post-colonial translation of European iconographic repertoires, combined with persistent tensions between imported symbols and local social realities. The episode involved a direct struggle over constitutional symbols, legal spaces, and institutional imagery (Souto 2023; STF 2023; Ramos 2023; Garrossini et al. 2023). Constitutional Law professor João Carlos Souto (2023) described the events as “the most serious attack on State institutions since re-democratisation in 1985” and “the bitter fruit of a slow process that has taken hold in Brazilian society over these four years.”   
 Two gestures are chosen here to exemplify the dynamic: the ritual appropriation of a replicaof the 1988 Constitution (Folha de São Paulo 2025) and the lipstick defacement of the Supreme Court’s allegorical statue of Justice, *A Justiça* (BBC News 2025). 

2. Research Questions

1. How does a regime of visibility organise public recognition of constitutional authority in Brazil across key historical moments (1889–2023)?   
2. How does post-colonial translation shape the stability or volatility of legal iconography in Brazil?   
3. How does Tropical Iconocracy explain the fragility of imported legal symbols in Brazil’s republican and constitutional imagination?   
4. How does the Visual Sexual Contract operate through feminised allegory as a technology of legitimacy and exclusion?    
5. How does the Theologico-Political Simulacrum of Legal Objects clarify the January 8 episode as a conflict over rival forms of legal authorisation in a digital-theological environment?    
6. How can legal historians reconstruct legal knowledge from visual, spatial, and material sources, including episodes of iconoclasm and idolatry?   

3. Conceptual Structure  
For nearly a century, the sociological interpretation of Brazil has been shadowed by Sérgio Buarque de Holanda’s concept of the “cordial man”, introduced in *Raízes do Brasil* (1936)[^10]. By his account, the Brazilian *ethos* is defined by a hypertrophy of the emotional sphere, a rejection of formalism, and an inability to distinguish between the private domain of the family and the public domain of the State. The "cordial" subject acts through the heart, privileging personal bonds, intimacy, and the manoeuvring over the cold abstraction of the Law (in)famously known as “jeitinho brasileiro”[^11], “*Brazilian little way”,* the direct translation. The failure of Brazilian institutions could be attributed to the nature of the subject: Brazil is too "warm," too informal, and too archaic to inhabit the "cold" structures of a modern liberal democracy. 
However, contemporary historical and legal analysis suggests that this diagnosis, while descriptively seductive regarding social manners, is structurally insufficient to explain the ontological crisis of the Brazilian regime. The cordiality idea focuses on the excess of affect in the citizen but ignores the deficit of legitimacy in the institution, thereby pathologising the populace while naturalising the State's structure.   
To understand the contingent historical processes of Brazilian democracy, with a symbolic focus on the events of 8 January 2023, it is necessary to pivot the analytical axis through the integration of different yet complementary intellectual traditions, such as the historical sociology of José Murilo de Carvalho[^12], the dogmatic anthropology of Pierre Legendre[^13], along with the works of art historian and philosopher Marie-José Mondzain[^14] and the understanding of legal images as deontic media (Siniscalchi 2019, 150–159) (Mondzain 2005) (Carvalho 1990) (Legendre 1974, 1989, 2019).   
I propose *Tropical Iconocracy* as a term to designate a volatility of legal imagination as it is absorbed into Brazil, acquiring unstable meanings through processes of translation and contestation. Rather than chaos, January 8 manifested the collision of two epistemic communities, each claiming authority over the meaning of normativity. The riot enacted an *anthropophagic* reabsorption[^15] of symbols, violently devouring their established meanings to produce unstable alternatives. Such instability is not an accident; it is structural to Brazil’s republican visual regime, where the sacred enemy of legality is perpetually transformed into a site of semiotic struggle. The Republic’s 1889 emergence from elite and military negotiation generated a symbolic deficit, with early republican iconography struggling to stabilise civic attachment (Carvalho 2017; Jurt 2012). The symbolic deficit is “a sin of origin”. Brazil is a Republic born with virtually no popular participation (Carvalho 2017, 15–20). The legal and aesthetic translation resulted in a “resounding failure” (Carvalho 1990, 88–89) shortly. The early representation of the Republic was a mock-up of Marianne and failed to resonate; the intended virgin-mother figure[^16] was quickly resignified in popular caricatures as a prostitute, mocked “even by those who had initially supported the new regime” (Carvalho 1990, 89). This fragile, contested sacred order, built on borrowed and poorly integrated symbols, forms the basis of Tropical Iconocracy.  
As historians Maurice Agulhon and Marina Warner have argued (Agulhon 1981; Warner 1985), a male figure represents a ruler. This system of legal and political iconography is part of why a division in which the virtues that legitimise good and evil are feminised in image, while the institutions that wield Law remain structurally male (Goodrich 2013, 75–80). A statue of a man inevitably resembles a specific King, General, or President, a competitor for power. A female figure, historically excluded from ruling and legally defined as a minor, implies ideals rather than personal power. She represents the state without threatening to usurp it; she is the *mother* of the citizens, not their master.  Therefore, the visual dimension of the Sexual Contract transposes feminist contract theory into the field of legal iconography.   
In the early twenty-first century, this inherited symbolic inventory entered a new cycle of contestation as the Bolsonaro government presented itself as custodian of moral values long associated with Brazilian Protestantism since the nineteenth century, foregrounding the defense of the family, treating folklore and national festivals as sites of profanity, stigmatizing African and Indigenous cultural forms, and advancing a punitive anti-drug discourse (Kibuuka 2020, 289). This merger integrates political theology and simulation. Political theology provides grounds for sovereignty and authority that adopt theological forms (Schmitt 2005). Simulation theory clarifies how copies and media saturation reorder relations between representation and reality (Baudrillard 1994). In contemporary Brazilian networks, digital religion and spiritual warfare discourse often frame institutions as sacred or demonic and ground legitimacy in divine mandate and charismatic authorisation (Løland 2020; The Revealer 2023). Within this field, legal objects (replicas, flags, constitutions, monuments) serve as privileged carriers of “true Law.” Object-centred authorisation relocates legality from procedures to things, and ritual acts toward artefacts enact claims to sovereignty (Douzinas 2000; Baudrillard 1994).

4. Historical Scope and Empirical Anchor  
The construction of Brasília reconfigured the visual-legal order by deploying modernist abstraction and monumental spatial grammar (Holston 1989). The *Praça dos Três Poderes* choreographs the separation of powers through architectural form, rendering authority as a sequence of spatial relations. Within this *tableau*, the judiciary’s emblematic presence is condensed in Alfredo Ceschiatti’s *A Justiça* (1961), a blindfolded granite allegory that locates authority in abstraction and scale rather than narrative figuration (Holston 1989; Mulcahy 2011). This transformation recalibrates the competencies of legal literacy, as recognition is now trained on spatial and formal cues inscribed in the built environment (Mulcahy 2011; Resnik and Curtis 2011).  
But on 8 January 2023, rioters attacked legal institutions and symbol systems in Brasília. Contemporary accounts frame the episode as an assault on democratic institutions that drew energy from disinformation, electoral denialism, and transnational repertoires of anti-institutional mobilisation (Souto 2023; Garrossini et al. 2023; de Ramos 2023; STF 2023).   
Two episodes anchor the analysis:

1. Constitution replica as juridical simulacrum and legal object-authorisation. A rioter seized a replica of the 1988 Constitution and treated it as the authentic Constitution, which exemplifies the copy’s elevation within a rival order of legality (Folha de São Paulo 2025; Baudrillard 1994).  
2. Defacement of *A Justiça* as gendered counter-literacy. Débora Rodrigues used lipstick to inscribe “Perdeu, Mané” on the statue, an act that highlights the feminised surface through which abstract legality becomes visible and contestable (BBC News 2025; Warner 1985; Pateman 1988; Fruzzetti and Pérez 2002).

5. Methodology and Conclusion   
This project constructs a methodology that weaves together legal iconology, forensic architecture, and political theology. By reading the vandalised artefacts of January 8 as primary sources, this approach reconstructs the routes by which legal historians can interpret material evidence alongside textual statutes, tracing the resilience and fragility of the legal order. The analysis situates the January 8, 2023, attacks within the *longue durée* of Brazilian visual culture[^17], moving beyond immediate political commentary. The close reading of the vandalised Justice statue and other targeted symbols of sovereignty reframes the crisis as a collapse of legal literacy and a rupture in the regime of images. The volatility is not Brazilian nature but the specific historical fact of post-colonial elite imposition without popular participation. It's contingent, not geneti  
The vandalism of January 8, 2023, belongs to a prolonged struggle over the visibility of constitutional authority rather than to a self-contained episode (Reuters 2024). In a Benjaminian register, political tendency becomes legible at points of rupture, when what remains latent in art is forced into public readability (Benjamin 2005, 26). That logic matters for legal literacy because Law stabilises itself through a regime of visibility in which images and monuments train recognition of legitimacy, including the allegory of Justice as a condensed, publicly readable claim about what Law is. The sequence tightened on November 13, 2024, when Francisco Wanderley Luiz detonated explosives at the *Praça dos Três Poderes* near the STF and died at the scene; the Federal Police later concluded he acted alone and identified political extremism as the motive (Polícia Federal 2025).   
In the residence he occupied previous to his suicide-terrorist-attack, investigators and press reportsers found a lipstick message linking his act to January 8 and to Débora Rodrigues’s earlier lipstick inscription on the statue *A Justiça*: “Débora Rodrigues, please do not waste lipstick. That is for making women beautiful. For a \[*insult in the original*\] statue, one uses TNT.” (Rosa 2024).  Law is both the possessor and the possessed of its images.
