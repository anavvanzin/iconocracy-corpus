# The Dessexualization Threshold: Why Iconocracy Is French (and What Happens When It Isn't)

**Ana Vanzin**
Programa de Pós-Graduação em Direito, Universidade Federal de Santa Catarina (PPGD/UFSC)

---

**Abstract.** This article examines why certain female allegorical figures survive on state media for centuries while others are banished within years. Drawing on a comparative corpus of coins, stamps, and banknotes from France, Germany, the United Kingdom, the United States, and Brazil (1800–2000), it proposes the concept of *ENDURECIMENTO* — the progressive purification of the female allegorical body — as a measurable process governed by ten indicators. The central finding is that one indicator, *dessexualization*, operates as a survival threshold: allegories that cross it persist indefinitely; those that do not are rejected or abandoned. This mechanism explains why the French Republic's Semeuse has survived for over 120 years, why the German Republic abandoned female allegory entirely after 1918, why the American "Educational Series" banknotes were withdrawn as "dirty dollars" within three years, and why British Britannia persists through inertia of a body that was never sexualized to begin with. The Brazilian case — where positivist republicanism froze the allegorical body at a moderate level of ENDURECIMENTO from day one — reveals a distinct path irreducible to the European model. Together, these five national trajectories demonstrate that *iconocracy* — the use of the female body as sovereign image — is not a universal republican necessity but a historically contingent phenomenon whose survival depends on the State's capacity to calibrate the allegorical body to the threshold of moral-political tolerance.

**Keywords:** female allegory; legal culture; ENDURECIMENTO; dessexualization; iconocracy; Visual Sexual Contract; numismatics; postal history; comparative legal iconography.

## 1. Introduction: The Puzzle of Survival

In 1896, the United States Treasury issued the five-dollar "Educational Series" Silver Certificate. At its center stood a winged female figure — Electricity, or America — bare-breasted, triumphant, holding an electric lamp over a map of the nation. Within three years, the note was withdrawn. Boston ladies protested its "indecency"; Anthony Comstock denounced it; bankers allegedly refused to handle what the press called "dirty dollars."[^1] The series was replaced in 1899 by conventional portrait notes.

Two years after the Educational Series appeared, in 1898, the French Mint began striking a one-franc silver coin designed by Louis Oscar Roty. It depicted a young woman in a flowing dress, walking against the wind, sowing grain at sunrise — La Semeuse. No scandal followed. The coin entered mass circulation: 440 million specimens in the silver series alone. Roty's Semeuse migrated to stamps in 1903, persisted through two world wars, reappeared on the New Franc in 1960, and survives today on French euro coins.[^2] She has been in continuous use for over 120 years.

How do we explain this? Both figures are female allegories deployed on sovereign media by republican states in the same decade. Both served the same function: making the state visible through the body of a woman. One was banished in three years. The other endures into the twenty-first century. The difference, this article argues, lies not in aesthetics, not in public morality, and not in the political regime, but in a single variable: *dessexualization* — the degree to which the female allegorical body has been purged of markers of corporeal sexuality.

This article proposes that dessexualization operates as a *threshold* condition for the survival of what I call *iconocracy*: the governmental practice of making state sovereignty visible through the allegorized female body. Below the threshold, the allegory provokes scandal and rejection. Above it, the allegory becomes infinitely reproducible, entering the cycle of bureaucratic mass production — coins, stamps, banknotes — that characterizes the modern state's visual regime.

The argument proceeds in seven steps. Section 2 introduces the theoretical framework: the concepts of ENDURECIMENTO, the Visual Sexual Contract, and the ten purification indicators used to measure the progressive rigidification of the allegorical body. Section 3 examines the French case — the Semeuse as paradigm of successful dessexualization — and the role of designer resistance in maintaining morphological stability across supports. Section 4 turns to the German collapse: Germania's maximum ENDURECIMENTO without dessexualization, the Weimar Republic's abandonment of female allegory, and the material inertia that allowed an imperial figure to survive on stamps years after the empire fell. Section 5 presents Britannia as a control case: a body born already hardened, never sexualized, persisting through over 350 years of inertia. Section 6 introduces the Brazilian path — the positivist freeze — as a trajectory irreducible to the European model. Section 7 examines the colonial dimension, where the same designer produced opposite morphologies for domestic and colonial circulation. Section 8 synthesizes the findings into a typology of iconocratic persistence.

## 2. Framework: ENDURECIMENTO and the Visual Sexual Contract

### 2.1. Iconocracy

Modern legal culture has a visual problem. The state, an abstraction, must make itself visible to those it governs. One persistent solution — employed across Western republics and empires from the French Revolution to the twentieth century — has been to embody sovereignty in the figure of a woman: Marianne in France, Britannia in Britain, Germania in Germany, Columbia and her successors in the United States, the Republic in Brazil. These figures are not decorative. They perform a juridical-political function: they make the abstract authority of the state sensorially present on the objects citizens handle daily — coins, stamps, and banknotes.[^3]

I call this practice *iconocracy*: the regime of governance through allegorized feminine images. The term deliberately echoes *iconoclasm* and *theocracy* to signal that what is at stake is not mere representation but a *technology of power* — a way of governing through what Marie-José Mondzain calls the "economy of images."[^4] Iconocracy is not a metaphor for visual culture generally. It designates the specific operation by which the state instrumentalizes the female body as a device of sovereign legitimation.

### 2.2. The Visual Sexual Contract

The deployment of the female body for state purposes constitutes what I term the *Visual Sexual Contract*: a tacit agreement by which the body of a woman — allegorized, idealized, depersonalized — is made available as the visible surface of sovereign authority.[^5] This contract is "sexual" not in the erotic sense but in the structural sense: it assigns the function of political visibility to the female body, reproducing a gendered division in which women's bodies signify while men's bodies govern.

The Visual Sexual Contract is not a variant of Carole Pateman's social contract critique. Where Pateman's sexual contract concerns the subordination of women within marriage and labor, the Visual Sexual Contract concerns the instrumentalization of the *image* of femininity for governmental purposes. The allegorical woman is not a citizen; she is a device. She does not represent women — she represents the state *through* a feminine body.[^6]

The concept draws on, but is not reducible to, Judith Butler's account of how regulatory norms *materialize* gendered bodies. For Butler, gender is not expressed through the body but *produced* through repeated citation of norms.[^6a] The allegorical body on a coin is precisely such a citation — but one performed by the state rather than the subject. Each new striking of the Semeuse, each new printing of a Germania stamp, is a performative reiteration that materializes the state's gendered norm. ENDURECIMENTO, in Butlerian terms, is the progressive *sedimentation* of this norm: the allegorical body becoming less a performance and more a fact. What this framework adds to Butler is the attention to *material support* as a constraint on performativity — the coin, the stamp, the banknote are not neutral surfaces but technologies with their own material logics that shape what kind of body can be cited.

### 2.3. ENDURECIMENTO: The Ten Indicators

The allegorical body does not remain static. As it migrates across supports and historical periods, it undergoes a process I call *ENDURECIMENTO* — a Portuguese term deliberately preserved in its original language to resist translation into the English "hardening," which carries misleading material connotations. ENDURECIMENTO designates the progressive *purification* of the allegorical body: a process by which the body is stripped of dynamism, narrative, sexuality, and individuality until it becomes a rigid, reproducible, heraldic sign.[^7]

ENDURECIMENTO is assessed through ten ordinal indicators, each scored on a scale from 0 (absent) to 4 (extreme). A methodological caveat is in order: these are interpretive assessments, not measurements in the natural-science sense. The scores are assigned by the researcher on the basis of visual inspection, informed by art-historical conventions and cross-checked against the corpus. No instrument can objectively "measure" dessexualization; what the scoring system provides is a *structured vocabulary* for comparative description — a way of making explicit the judgments that iconographic analysis always already makes but rarely quantifies. The ordinal scale does not support arithmetic means in the strict psychometric sense; the aggregated score reported below should be understood as a heuristic summary, not a statistical measure.[^7a] The ten indicators are:

1. **Disincorporation** — reduction from full body to bust to face to symbol.
2. **Postural rigidity** — static versus dynamic body.
3. **Dessexualization** — concealment of the body, removal of corporeal markers.
4. **Facial uniformization** — generic versus individual features.
5. **Heraldicization** — integration into heraldic or institutional programs.
6. **Architectural framing** — encasement within ornamental or architectural borders.
7. **Narrative erasure** — removal of narrative context (from scene to icon).
8. **Monochromatization** — reduction of color (polychrome → monochrome → metal).
9. **Seriality** — mass reproduction (unique → edition → industrial).
10. **State inscription** — state text or symbols inscribed on or around the body.

The ENDURECIMENTO score is the mean of the ten indicators (0.0–4.0). For qualitative reference: scores below 1.0 are classified as LOW, 1.0–2.0 as MEDIUM-LOW, 2.0–2.5 as MEDIUM, 2.5–3.0 as MEDIUM-HIGH, and above 3.0 as HIGH. But the score is a summary, never a substitute for the individual indicators. As this article demonstrates, the relationship between indicators is non-linear: a low overall score can coexist with a fatal deficit in a single indicator — dessexualization — producing rejection, while a higher overall score with adequate dessexualization produces survival.

### 2.4. Three Iconocratic Regimes

The corpus reveals three recurrent configurations that I call *iconocratic regimes*:

- **FOUNDATIONAL** (sacrificial-foundational): revolutions, constitutions, independence. The allegorical body is at its most *alive* — dynamic, exposed, even sacrificial. The Phrygian cap, broken chains, bare breast.
- **NORMATIVE** (bureaucratic-stabilized): the republic stabilized, routine administration. The allegory is *domesticated* — dressed, seated, infinitely reproducible. The balance, the blindfold, the seated posture.
- **MILITARY** (imperial-military): war, imperialism, colonial expansion. The body *hardens* to its maximum — helmeted, armored, standing on globe or ship's prow. The shield, the trident, the colonial subjects at the feet.

These regimes are not stages in a linear progression. A single figure can inhabit different regimes simultaneously depending on the support and context of circulation — as the colonial cases dramatically demonstrate.

## 3. The French Solution: Semeuse and the Art of Dessexualization

### 3.1. The Roty Morphology

Oscar Roty designed the Semeuse first as a medal in 1887, then as a coin in 1897 (issued from 1898), and the design migrated to stamps in 1903.[^8] In all three incarnations, the figure is the same: a young woman in a long, flowing dress, wearing a Phrygian cap, walking against the wind, sowing grain at sunrise. The body is dynamic — she moves, advances, projects herself forward. The gesture is productive: she sows, she feeds. The posture is anti-hierarchical: no throne, no pedestal, no architectural frame.

And yet the Semeuse survives. The ENDURECIMENTO score across three supports tells us why:

| Support | Date | Score | Dessexualization |
|---------|------|-------|-----------------|
| Coin (1 Franc silver) | 1898 | 2.5 | 3 |
| Stamp (lignée 15c) | 1903 | 2.3 | 3 |
| Stamp (camée 10c) | 1906 | 2.4 | 3 |

The overall score is moderate — Semeuse is not a rigid icon. Her postural rigidity is low (1/4), her narrative is present (sowing), her body is intact (no disincorporation). But on one indicator, she is consistently high: *dessexualization* (3/4). The body is fully clothed. The dress flows but reveals no flesh. The Phrygian cap frames the head. There is no skin, no nudity, no erotic charge.

This is the French solution to the problem of iconocracy: keep the body *dynamic* (low rigidity, preserved narrative) but make it *clothed* (high dessexualization). The result is an allegory that is expressive enough to be meaningful but purified enough to be reproduced billions of times without scandal.

### 3.2. The Roty Resistance

A remarkable feature of the Semeuse is morphological *stability*. Across 57 years and three distinct incarnations (coin 1898, stamp lignée 1903, stamp camée 1906), the figure's ENDURECIMENTO score barely varies. The posture, gesture, attributes, and dress remain essentially identical. When Louis-Eugène Mouchon adapted the design for stamps in 1903, and when Eugène-Anatole Lhomme retouched it for the camée variant in 1907, they altered only the background and typography — never the figure itself.[^9]

I call this phenomenon *Roty resistance*: the capacity of a design to impose a morphological program that the state cannot harden further. Roty's Semeuse contained, from its inception, a built-in ceiling on ENDURECIMENTO — a body whose gestural dynamism resisted bureaucratic rigidification. The designer, in this case, operated as an agent *against* the state's purifying tendency.

This is not inevitable. As the German case shows, other designers produced allegories that *invited* maximum ENDURECIMENTO. The morphological trajectory of an allegory depends not only on the state's demands but on the visual DNA encoded in the original design.

### 3.3. The Threshold Revealed: ZW-10

The comparison between the Educational Series $5 note and the Semeuse 1 Franc coin constitutes a structured quasi-comparison — not a natural experiment in the strict causal-inference sense, since the two objects differ on multiple variables (national culture, legal tradition, religious composition, censorship infrastructure), but a comparison that isolates key differences within a shared functional context.[^10a] The two objects are nearly contemporary (1896 vs. 1898), both serve the NORMATIVE regime (currency of the bureaucratic republic), and both deploy a female allegorical figure on mass-circulation sovereign media. Their ENDURECIMENTO scores are close: 1.9 for the Educational, 2.5 for the Semeuse. The difference of 0.6 points should be trivial.

It is not. The Educational was withdrawn in three years. The Semeuse survives after 120.

When we disaggregate the scores, the mechanism becomes visible:

| Indicator | Educational $5 | Semeuse 1F | Δ |
|-----------|---------------|------------|---|
| Dessexualization | 0 | 3 | **+3** |
| Postural rigidity | 1 | 1 | 0 |
| Seriality | 4 | 4 | 0 |
| State inscription | 4 | 4 | 0 |
| Monochromatization | 3 | 4 | +1 |
| All others | ~1–2 | ~1–3 | ~0–1 |

Every indicator is comparable *except* dessexualization. The Educational's allegorical figure has exposed breasts (score 0). The Semeuse is fully clothed (score 3). This single variable — a difference of three points on one indicator — separates survival from banishment.

The scandal of the "dirty dollars" was, admittedly, overdetermined. The notes were also criticized for poor print quality and difficulty distinguishing denominations; the withdrawal was not reducible to a single cause.[^10] But the nudity controversy was the most publicly articulated objection, and it is the one that left a lasting cultural trace ("banned in Boston"). The stronger claim is not that dessexualization *alone* caused the withdrawal, but that it was a *necessary condition* — without the bodily exposure, the other deficiencies would likely have been tolerated, as they were for countless other flawed banknote designs. The comparison with the Semeuse supports this: Roty's design, operating *within* the dessexualization threshold from inception, survived despite its own material limitations (silver coins wear faster than banknotes). What the Educational Series reveals is not that American culture was uniquely prudish — though the Comstock laws and Protestant moral reform networks certainly amplified the scandal — but that the threshold exists *across* cultures, albeit at different levels of stringency and with different institutional mechanisms of enforcement.[^10b]

## 4. The German Collapse: Germania and the Failure of Iconocracy

### 4.1. Maximum ENDURECIMENTO Without Exit

Paul Eduard Waldraff designed the Germania stamp series in 1900, using the actress Anna Führing as his model.[^11] The figure sits frontally, wearing an octagonal imperial crown (*Bügelkrone*), holding a sword in one hand and an olive branch in the other. She is armored, draped in an imperial mantle, static, hieratic. The inscription reads "DEUTSCHES REICH."

Germania's ENDURECIMENTO was already at its maximum from the beginning. Unlike the Semeuse, whose dynamism provided room for morphological negotiation, Germania was born rigid. The design encoded maximum postural rigidity, maximum heraldicization, maximum state inscription. There was no "German solution" because there was no morphological space for one. You cannot dessexualize a body that is already armored; you cannot domesticate a figure that is already enthroned.

The comparison with the Semeuse — captured in Zwischenraum panel ZW-07 — is structurally revealing:

| | Semeuse (FR) | Germania (DE) |
|---|---|---|
| Posture | Dynamic (sowing) | Static (frontal) |
| Attributes | Seeds, Phrygian cap | Sword, imperial crown |
| Function | *Labor* (productive) | *Power* (protective) |
| Regime origin | Republic | Empire |
| Body temperature | Warm (living) | Cold (monumental) |

The Republic legitimates itself through *labor* — the woman who sows. The Empire legitimates itself through *power* — the woman who guards. These are two distinct Visual Sexual Contracts: the French contract instrumentalizes the female body as *worker*; the German contract instrumentalizes it as *warrior*. But the warrior body, already at maximum ENDURECIMENTO, has no adaptive capacity. When the political regime changes, the body cannot be reformed — only discarded.

### 4.2. The Weimar Abandonment

When the German Empire fell in November 1918, the new Weimar Republic faced an iconographic dilemma: what to do with Germania? Unlike France, where Marianne had been born on revolutionary barricades and could seamlessly serve the Republic, Germania was the allegory of the *Reich*. Her crown was imperial. Her sword was imperial. Her very name — Germania — evoked the nation as ethnic body, not the republic as political form.[^12]

The response was not reform but *elimination*. The Weimar Republic's 1 Reichsmark coin (1924–1933) replaced Germania with a heraldic eagle without crown.[^13] No female allegorical figure appeared on official Weimar coinage. The Visual Sexual Contract was unilaterally terminated — at least on coins.

The negative control (SCOUT-NC-03) is methodologically significant. It demonstrates that iconocracy is not a *necessity* of republican governance. The Weimar Republic functioned perfectly well without a female allegory on its currency. The state made itself visible through heraldic symbols — the eagle, the national colors — rather than through the body of a woman. France *chose* iconocracy. Germany proved it could be *refused*.

### 4.3. The Weimar Paradox: Material Inertia

But the abandonment was not instantaneous. Zwischenraum panel ZW-13 reveals a striking paradox: within the same political regime, at the same moment in time, Germania persisted on stamps while disappearing from coins.

In September 1920 — nearly two years after the proclamation of the Republic — the Weimar postal service issued Germania stamps (Michel 141) bearing the imperial crown and the inscription "DEUTSCHES REICH."[^14] The designer, Waldraff, had died in 1917. The figure was literally posthumous — an imperial allegory circulating under republican authority.

| Variable | Stamp (1920) | Coin (1924) |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Political regime | Weimar Republic | Weimar Republic |
| Allegorical figure | Present (Germania) | Absent (eagle) |
| Support | Postal | Numismatic |
| ENDURECIMENTO | HIGH | N/A |
| Redesign catalyst | Deferred (hyperinflation) | Forced (monetary reform) |

The paradox resolves when we attend to the material support. Stamps are produced in runs of tens of millions; replacing a design requires years of planning. The hyperinflation of 1921–1923 *accelerated* stamp emissions without allowing time for iconographic redesign — Germania persisted because the printing presses never stopped. Coins, by contrast, required a formal monetary reform (the Reichsmark of 1924) that forced a new design. The coin abandoned the allegory 4–5 years before the stamp did.

The lesson: iconocracy does not collapse with the regime that created it. It collapses when the *material support* that sustains it must be re-legitimized. The political revolution of November 1918 did not produce an iconographic revolution. The economic crisis of 1923–1924 did.

### 4.4. The Notgeld Valve

If the central state abandoned female allegory, the municipalities did not. The Notgeld (emergency money) issued by German cities during the hyperinflation of 1921–1923 reintroduced female allegorical figures — but with a dramatic morphological inversion. The Notgeld of Bielefeld (SCOUT-117), a silk note depicting the Jungbrunnen (Fountain of Youth), features a nude female body in dynamic motion, head thrown back, immersed in healing waters.[^15]

The ENDURECIMENTO score is 0.4 — the lowest in the entire corpus. Every indicator that was maximal in Germania is minimal here: postural rigidity 0, dessexualization 1, facial uniformization 0, heraldicization 0, narrative erasure 0, monochromatization 0. The Notgeld body is the *inverse* of the imperial body: polychromatic, narrative, individual, sexualized, dynamic.

This inversion suggests — though a single case cannot confirm — a structural pattern worth investigating: ENDURECIMENTO may be a function of *centralization*. When the central state monopolizes sovereign imagery, the allegorical body hardens. When sovereignty fragments — as in the hyperinflation crisis — the body *softens*. The Notgeld would then constitute a valve: a space where the feminine body escapes the purification regime of the national allegory and returns to its foundational dynamism. Whether this pattern holds across the thousands of Notgeld designs produced in 1921–1923 is a question for further research; the Bielefeld case is suggestive, not probative. But it signals that the dessexualization threshold may apply specifically to *central-state sovereign media*, not to all objects bearing female allegorical figures.

## 5. The British Control: Britannia and the Inertia of the Already-Hardened

Britannia offers a control case against which the French and German trajectories gain relief. She first appeared on English coins in 1672, on a copper halfpenny and farthing issued under Charles II — making her the oldest continuously used female allegory in Western numismatics.[^16] By the time she reappeared on William III's halfpenny in 1695, she was already seated, already armed (spear, shield), already positioned as a guardian of maritime sovereignty. On *numismatic* media, her body was never sexualized — there is no coin or banknote Britannia with a bare breast, no monetary Britannia on a barricade. This qualification is important: in satirical prints (Gillray, Cruikshank), Britannia was regularly sexualized, shown in distress, partially undressed, even assaulted as political metaphor.[^16a] But satirical prints operate outside the sovereign media system — they are *critiques* of power, not its instruments. On the media that concern this article — coins, stamps, banknotes — Britannia was born in the NORMATIVE regime and has remained there for over 350 years.

The longitudinal evidence (Zwischenraum panel ZW-08) is remarkable:

| | Halfpenny (1672) | ½ Penny (1695) | Penny (1895) | Penny (1912) | Trade Dollar (1895) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Posture | Seated | Seated | Seated | Seated | Standing |
| Attributes | Shield, spear | Spear, olive branch | Trident, shield | Trident, shield | Trident, shield, ship |
| Context | Restoration domestic | Post-revolution domestic | Imperial domestic | Imperial domestic | Colonial (SE Asia) |
| Regime | NORMATIVE | FOUNDATIONAL | NORMATIVE | NORMATIVE | MILITARY |

Across 240 years of domestic circulation, Britannia's morphology barely changes. The spear becomes a trident; the olive branch disappears. But the seated posture, the armored body, the helmeted head, the generic face — all persist. The ENDURECIMENTO score remains consistently HIGH.

This persistence is not a sign of adaptation — it is a sign of *inertia*. Britannia never needed to be dessexualized because she was never sexualized. She never needed to be reformed because her morphology was already at the institutional ceiling. The French problem — how to keep the allegory alive while making it reproducible — simply did not arise in Britain, because Britannia was never alive in the first place — she was, from the outset, a heraldic device.

The critical finding from the George V penny (SCOUT-124, 1912) is chronological: Britannia's ENDURECIMENTO was already at maximum *before* the First World War. The war did not *produce* the militarization of her body — the helmet and the trident had been there for decades. This refutes the intuitive hypothesis that military conflict *generates* iconographic hardening. In the British case, the hardened body preceded the conflict, waiting, as it were, for the war to catch up to its morphology.

## 6. The Positivist Freeze: Brazil and the Third Path

The Brazilian case disrupts any temptation to construct a binary typology. If France represents the successful dessexualization of a dynamic body, and Germany represents the failure to adapt a rigidified one, Brazil reveals a third trajectory: the *positivist freeze*.

The Brazilian Republic was proclaimed on November 15, 1889. Almost immediately, the new state produced its female allegory: a profile bust wearing a Phrygian cap and laurel crown, inscribed "REPUBLICA DOS ESTADOS UNIDOS DO BRAZIL," struck on silver coins in three denominations — 500, 1000, and 2000 réis.[^17] Unlike France, where the republican allegory had deep revolutionary roots (Marianne was born on the barricades of 1789 and reborn in 1830 and 1848), the Brazilian Republic was proclaimed by military decree, without popular revolution. The allegory did not emerge from the streets. It was *designed* — imposed from above, modeled on French prototypes, stamped on coins from Day One.

The remarkable feature of the Brazilian effigy is its morphological *stasis*. From the foundational 500 Réis of 1889 (SCOUT-316) to the 2000 Réis of 1906–1913 (SCOUT-125) to the 1 Cruzeiro banknote of 1970–1972 (SCOUT-109), the ENDURECIMENTO score remains at MEDIUM — neither rising to the HIGH of Britannia nor collapsing to the ABSENT of Weimar:

| Item | Date | Support | Score | Regime |
|------|------|---------|-------|--------|
| 500 Réis | 1889 | Coin | MEDIUM | FOUNDATIONAL |
| 2000 Réis | 1906 | Coin | MEDIUM | NORMATIVE |
| 50 Cruzeiros | 1965 | Coin | MEDIUM-HIGH | NORMATIVE |
| 1 Cruzeiro note | 1970 | Banknote | MEDIUM-HIGH | NORMATIVE |

The trajectory shows a slight upward drift from MEDIUM toward MEDIUM-HIGH in the military period (1964–1985), but the allegory never reaches the HIGH plateau of Britannia or Germania. Even in its foundational moment — the proclamation of the Republic — the effigy was already bureaucratic: a profile bust, a laurel crown, a circular inscription. There was no Brazilian Delacroix, no allegorical woman on the barricades, no bare-breasted Liberty leading the people. The positivist ideology that dominated the early Republic — *Ordem e Progresso* inscribed on the flag — demanded an allegory that was orderly from the start.[^18]

This is the positivist freeze: an ENDURECIMENTO that stabilizes at MEDIUM without ever passing through the FOUNDATIONAL phase of bodily exposure and dynamism. The French trajectory moves from FOUNDATIONAL (Marianne on the barricades, 1789) through a phase of dessexualization (Semeuse, 1898) to NORMATIVE stability. The Brazilian trajectory *skips* the foundational phase entirely, arriving directly at NORMATIVE-MEDIUM and remaining there for a century.

The consequences are significant:

1. **No dessexualization threshold** was ever crossed in Brazil, because the allegory was never sexualized to begin with. The problem that animated the French solution and the American scandal simply did not arise.
2. **No reform was necessary**, because the body was already within the zone of bureaucratic tolerance from its inception.
3. **No abandonment occurred**, because the allegory never accumulated the imperial associations that made Germania toxic to the Weimar Republic.
4. **The same effigy persisted through regime changes** — from the Old Republic (1889–1930) through the Vargas dictatorship (1930–1945) to the military regime (1964–1985) — because its MEDIUM ENDURECIMENTO was compatible with any political configuration. It was too bland to offend, too conventional to challenge.

The Brazilian case demonstrates that ENDURECIMENTO is not always a *process* — sometimes it is an *initial condition*. Where the French allegory *became* dessexualized, the Brazilian allegory was *born* dessexualized. Where the German allegory *became* intolerable, the Brazilian allegory was *born* tolerable. Positivist republicanism, by importing a pre-purified allegorical model, bypassed the political negotiation that the Visual Sexual Contract demands in other contexts — producing an iconocracy without drama, an allegory without scandal, a feminine body without friction.

This has implications beyond iconography. Lilia Moritz Schwarcz's work on the visual construction of the Brazilian nation demonstrates how the Republic mobilized European iconographic models — from the Phrygian cap to the classical drape — as instruments of a *racial whitening* project: the allegorical Republic is not merely female but *white*, in a nation whose population was overwhelmingly non-white.[^18a] The Brazilian effigy's morphological stasis thus conceals a racial operation that Carvalho's institutional analysis does not fully capture: the positivist freeze is also a *racial freeze*, fixing a white European body as the face of a multiracial state. Margareth Rago's analysis of republican gender politics further complicates the picture: the "orderly" Republic was orderly precisely in its disciplining of feminine bodies, real and allegorical, into positivist norms of propriety.[^18b]

If the Brazilian state never had to *negotiate* the limits of the female allegorical body — if the body was always already within acceptable bounds — then the Visual Sexual Contract in Brazil was never tested, never contested, never made visible. The dessexualization threshold, in the Brazilian case, was never approached from below. The result is not a stronger iconocracy but a more invisible one: a deployment of the female body so thoroughly normalized that it generates no resistance, no scandal, no critical discourse. And here lies the paradox: the very invisibility of the Brazilian iconocracy — its absence of drama, scandal, or contestation — may be the most effective form of the Visual Sexual Contract, precisely because it operates beneath the threshold of critical attention.

## 7. The Colonial Test: One Designer, Two Bodies

The domestic-colonial comparison provides a final piece of evidence. In the 1890s, a single designer — George William De Saulles — produced two Britannias for two different contexts:

1. The **domestic penny** (1895–1901): Britannia *seated* on a rock, holding trident and shield. NORMATIVE regime. ENDURECIMENTO HIGH.[^19]
2. The **British Trade Dollar** (1895–1935): Britannia *standing* on a colonial shore, trident raised, merchant ship behind her. Reverse inscribed in Chinese and Malay characters. MILITARY regime. ENDURECIMENTO HIGH — but morphologically *different*.[^20]

Same allegory, same designer, same decade — yet opposite morphologies. The seated Britannia of the penny guards the domestic order: static, anchored, heraldic. The standing Britannia of the Trade Dollar *arrives* on foreign shores: dynamic, projecting, imperial. The context of circulation — not the designer, not the period, not the political regime — determines the morphological regime.

Zwischenraum panel ZW-06 extends this finding by comparing the British Trade Dollar with the French Piastre de Commerce (1885–1928), designed by Jean-Auguste Barre:[^21]

| | British Trade Dollar | Piastre de Commerce |
|---|---|---|
| Allegory | Britannia STANDING | Marianne SEATED |
| Posture | Arrival/conquest | Installation/enthronement |
| Attributes | Trident, shield, ship | Fasces, radiate crown |
| Circulation | Hong Kong, Straits Settlements | French Indochina |
| Reverse | Chinese + Malay | Chinese + Vietnamese |

Two empires, two colonial technologies, two morphologies — but both deploying a white female body as current money among colonized populations. The visual hierarchy is literal: the allegorical figure occupies the obverse; the colonial languages are relegated to the reverse. The white body is *above*; the colonized text is *below*.

This visual hierarchy enacts what Charles Mills theorizes as the Racial Contract — the tacit agreement that structures the modern political order around the racial subordination of non-white peoples.[^22] On the colonial trade coin, the Racial Contract becomes *visible*: sovereignty is white, female, and European; the colonized are present only as inscription, as linguistic supplement to the body that rules them. Ann Laura Stoler's work on the entanglement of racial and sexual governance in colonial regimes is essential context here: the white female body on the colonial coin is simultaneously a technology of gender (the Visual Sexual Contract) and of race (what I call, following Mills, the *Visual Racial Contract*).[^22a] The allegorical woman is doubly instrumentalized — by gender (as the state's visible surface) and by race (as the marker of European sovereignty over non-European space).

A full theorization of the Visual Racial Contract exceeds this article's scope. What the colonial trade coins demonstrate for the present argument is more limited but significant: the context of circulation — domestic versus colonial — modulates not only the morphology of ENDURECIMENTO but its *political function*. In the metropole, the allegory legitimates the republic to its own citizens. In the colony, the same allegory asserts racial sovereignty over subjects who were never party to any contract, visual or otherwise.[^22b]

The colonial test confirms the core argument: ENDURECIMENTO is not an intrinsic property of a design but a *relational* property that varies with context. The same Britannia, by the same hand, *hardens differently* depending on whether she circulates in London or Hong Kong. This finding is incompatible with any theory that treats iconographic change as a function of time, style, or regime alone. It demands a theory that accounts for the *circulation context* — which is precisely what the concept of iconocratic regimes provides.

## 8. Discussion: A Typology of Iconocratic Persistence

The five national trajectories examined in this article yield a typology:

| Country | Strategy | Dessexualization | ENDURECIMENTO | Outcome | Duration |
|---------|----------|-----------------|---------------|---------|----------|
| **France** | Dynamic dessexualization | Resolved (Semeuse) | MEDIUM stable | Survival | 120+ years |
| **Germany** | Maximum rigidity, no reform | Never needed / never possible | HIGH → ABSENT | Abandonment | 22 years |
| **United States** | No dessexualization | Failed (Educational) | LOW | Rejection | 3 years |
| **United Kingdom** | Born hardened | Never sexualized | HIGH fixed | Inertia | 350+ years |
| **Brazil** | Positivist import | Born dessexualized | MEDIUM fixed | Frozen persistence | 130+ years |

Three findings emerge:

**First**, dessexualization is the decisive variable — not ENDURECIMENTO as a whole. The Educational Series (score 1.9) was rejected despite *low* overall ENDURECIMENTO because dessexualization was at zero. Britannia persists despite *high* overall ENDURECIMENTO because dessexualization was never an issue. The threshold is not a threshold of rigidity but of *bodily exposure*: the female allegorical body on sovereign media must be clothed.

**Second**, iconocracy is not universal. The Weimar Republic demonstrated that a modern state can govern without a female allegory. The choice to deploy the female body is precisely that — a *choice*, historically contingent, culturally specific. France chose iconocracy and invested in making it work (dessexualization). Germany inherited iconocracy and abandoned it when the political cost of reform exceeded the cost of elimination. The question is not "why do states use female allegories?" but "under what conditions do they persist?"

**Third**, the answer to that question involves not only the state but the *material support*. ZW-13 demonstrates that within the same regime, coins abandoned the allegory years before stamps did — because monetary reform forced a redesign while postal infrastructure allowed inertia. The allegorical body is not merely a political decision; it is embedded in material systems of production and circulation that have their own temporalities.

A reflexive caveat is necessary. This article has measured the "success" of dessexualization — but success *for whom*? The Semeuse's 120-year survival is the survival of the state's instrument, not the emancipation of the woman whose body it appropriates. To score ENDURECIMENTO is, in a sense, to adopt the state's own gaze: to measure how effectively the body has been purified for governmental use. The framework risks complicity with the logic it describes — a feminist analysis that inadvertently evaluates the state's performance at domesticating the female body. This complicity is methodologically unavoidable if we wish to understand the mechanism from within, but it must be named. The dessexualization threshold is not a normative recommendation; it is a diagnostic tool. The fact that the state "succeeds" when it erases the body's sexuality is itself the indictment.[^23]

These findings have implications for the study of legal culture. If we accept that the visual regime of the state — the images it places on its coins, its stamps, its buildings — constitutes a layer of legal culture alongside texts, norms, and institutions, then ENDURECIMENTO is a measurable indicator of how legal culture calibrates the *body* of the citizen-subject to the demands of the state. The dessexualization threshold reveals the precise point at which this calibration breaks down: the point at which the female body becomes intolerable to the moral-political order it is supposed to legitimate. That this point can be identified empirically — through comparative iconometric analysis — suggests that the study of legal iconography need not remain impressionistic or anecdotal. It can be, in its own way, rigorous.

---

## 9. Conclusion

This article began with a puzzle: why does the Semeuse survive while the "dirty dollars" do not? The answer is deceptively simple — one is clothed, the other is not — but its implications are substantial. Dessexualization is the mechanism by which the state resolves the contradiction inherent in iconocracy: the need to make sovereignty visible through a female body that must simultaneously be *present* (to fulfill its allegorical function) and *absent* (to avoid scandal, desire, contestation). The dessexualized body achieves this double operation. It is present enough to signify and absent enough to circulate.

France solved this equation with the Semeuse. Germany failed to solve it with Germania and abandoned the project. The United States tested the limits with the Educational Series and was rebuffed. Britain never faced the equation because Britannia was never a body in the erotic sense — she was, from the first coin, a device. Brazil imported a pre-solved equation and froze it in place.

If iconocracy names a *dynamic* practice — the ongoing calibration of the female allegorical body to the demands of sovereign visibility — then France is its paradigmatic case, not because only France uses female allegories (Britannia's 350-year tenure exceeds any French figure), but because only France demonstrates the full arc of the process: a body born in revolutionary exposure (Marianne, 1789), progressively dessexualized through design intervention (Semeuse, 1898), and stabilized across supports and centuries. Britannia's persistence is real but inertial — a body that was never alive cannot demonstrate *adaptation*. Brazil's persistence is real but static — a body imported pre-purified cannot demonstrate *negotiation*. The French exception lies not in duration but in *process*: the active, contingent, designer-mediated calibration of the allegorical body to the threshold of tolerance. It is the contingent result of a specific designer (Roty), a specific political tradition (the revolutionary Marianne), and a specific morphological solution (the Semeuse) that happened to land precisely on the threshold.

The dessexualization threshold is, ultimately, a measure of what a society is willing to look at every day. The state can place a woman's body on a coin — but only if that body has been sufficiently purified to become invisible in its ubiquity. The paradox of iconocracy is that its success requires the body's erasure: the most enduring allegories are those we no longer see.

Much remains to be done. The five-country typology presented here is a first approximation; cases not examined — Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, Mexico, and other Latin American republics — may reveal trajectories that challenge or refine it. The scoring methodology, explicitly interpretive, awaits inter-rater validation and the development of more formal coding protocols. The Visual Racial Contract, introduced here only schematically, demands its own sustained investigation. And the question of what happens *after* iconocracy — when the euro replaces national currencies, when the stamp loses its daily ubiquity, when the female allegory migrates to digital interfaces — remains open. What this article offers is a framework for asking these questions with precision, and a first demonstration that the female allegorical body, far from being a decorative margin of legal culture, is one of its most revealing diagnostic surfaces.
